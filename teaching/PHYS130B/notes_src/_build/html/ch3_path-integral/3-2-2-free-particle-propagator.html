3.2.2 Free Particle Propagator#

Prompts

  • How do we discretize the path integral into a product of ordinary integrals via time-slicing?

  • What Gaussian integrals are needed to evaluate the free-particle propagator?

  • What is the exact result for the free-particle propagator and how can we interpret it physically?

  • Why does the free-particle propagator have that characteristic \((T)^{-d/2}\) spreading factor?

  • How does the propagator relate to the Schrödinger equation and diffusion processes?

Lecture Notes#

The propagator is the quantum amplitude for a particle to travel from one spacetime point to another. For a free particle, the path integral can be evaluated exactly by summing over all trajectories, producing a Gaussian result that contains the full quantum information: spreading, interference, and the de Broglie wavelength.

Time-Slicing: Discretizing the Path IntegralThe path integral is a functional integral—an infinite-dimensional sum over all paths \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\). To evaluate it, we discretize by dividing time into small intervals.#

SetupDivide the time interval \([t_i, t_f]\) into \(N\) equal slices:#

(179)#\[t_i = t_0 < t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_N = t_f, \quad \varepsilon = \ffrac{t_f - t_i}{N}\]

The path \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\) is represented by positions \(\boldsymbol{x}_n = \boldsymbol{x}(t_n)\) for \(n = 0, 1, \ldots, N\).

Action in a Time SliceFor a path segment from \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1}\) (duration \(\varepsilon\)), approximate the action as if the particle moves at constant velocity:#

(180)#\[S_n = \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} L(\boldsymbol{x}, \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}) \, dt \approx L\left(\boldsymbol{x}_n, \ffrac{\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n}{\varepsilon}\right) \varepsilon\]

For a free particle with \(L = \ffrac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2\):

(181)#\[S_n = \ffrac{m}{2} \ffrac{(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2}{\varepsilon}\]

The total action is

(182)#\[S = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} S_n = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ffrac{m}{2\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2\]

Sliced Path IntegralThe propagator becomes#

(183)#\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_N, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_0, t_i) = \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_1 \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_2 \cdots \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_{N-1} \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \exp\left(\ffrac{im}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2\right)\]

This is a product of exponentials of quadratic forms in the positions—perfect for Gaussian integrals.

Gaussian Integrals: The Key Computational Tool#

One-Dimensional GaussianThe fundamental Gaussian integral is#

(184)#\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2} dx = \sqrt{\ffrac{\pi}{a}}, \quad \text{Re}(a) > 0\]

For a complex exponent (relevant in quantum mechanics):

(185)#\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{iax^2} dx = \sqrt{\ffrac{\pi}{ia}} = e^{i\pi/4}\sqrt{\ffrac{\pi}{|a|}}\]

where we use the convention that \(\sqrt{i} = e^{i\pi/4} = (1+i)/\sqrt{2}\).

Shifted GaussianIf the exponent is shifted:#

(186)#\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{ia(x - x_0)^2} dx = \sqrt{\ffrac{\pi}{ia}}\]

The shift doesn’t change the result—a key property that makes the calculation work.

Multi-Dimensional GaussianFor a vector \(\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and a symmetric matrix \(A\):#

(187)#\[\int d^d\boldsymbol{x} \, e^{i\boldsymbol{x}^T A \boldsymbol{x}} = \ffrac{(\pi i)^{d/2}}{\sqrt{\det A}}\]

Or more carefully:

(188)#\[\int d^d\boldsymbol{x} \, e^{i\boldsymbol{x}^T A \boldsymbol{x}} = \sqrt{\ffrac{\pi^d i^d}{\det A}} = \left(\ffrac{\pi}{i}\right)^{d/2} \ffrac{1}{\sqrt{\det A}}\]

Computing the Free-Particle PropagatorWe evaluate the sliced path integral by integrating out positions one by one.#

Single-Slice PropagatorThe propagator from \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1}\) is#

(189)#\[K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) = \int e^{\ffrac{im}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2} = \left(\ffrac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{d/2} e^{\ffrac{im}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}\]

The normalization factor \((m/2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon)^{d/2}\) comes from the Gaussian integral formula.

Two-Slice PropagatorIntegrate over the intermediate position \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\):#

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}K_{2\varepsilon} = \int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \, K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}_2, \boldsymbol{x}_1; \varepsilon) K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}_1, \boldsymbol{x}_0; \varepsilon)\\$$$$= \int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \left(\ffrac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^d \exp\left(\ffrac{im}{2\hbar\varepsilon}[(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_1)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2]\right)\\$$:::{admonition} Derivation: Two-Slice Gaussian Integration :class: dropdown note\\Expand the exponent:\\$$(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_1)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2 = 2\boldsymbol{x}_1^2 - 2(\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0)\boldsymbol{x}_1 + (\boldsymbol{x}_2^2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0^2)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Completing the square:

\[= 2\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - frac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2} ight)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_2^2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0^2) - frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2}{2}\]
\[= 2\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - frac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2} ight)^2 + frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2}{2}\]

The integral over \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\) (a shifted Gaussian) gives:

(190)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \exp\left(\ffrac{im}{\hbar\varepsilon}\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \ffrac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2}\right)^2\right) = \left(\ffrac{\pi\hbar\varepsilon}{im}\right)^{d/2}\\$$Combining with the remaining exponent and normalizations:\\$$K_{2\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_2, \boldsymbol{x}_0; 2\varepsilon) = \left(\ffrac{m}{2\pi i\hbar(2\varepsilon)}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\ffrac{im}{2\hbar(2\varepsilon)}(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2\right)\\$$**Pattern**: The time increases additively ($\varepsilon + \varepsilon = 2\varepsilon$), and the distance appears in the exponent with the total time.\\### The Exact Free-Particle PropagatorBy induction (or by taking the continuum limit $N \to \infty$), the exact propagator for a free particle is:\\$$K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \left(\ffrac{m}{2\pi i\hbar T}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\ffrac{im}{2\hbar T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

where \(T = t_f - t_i\) is the total time interval and \(d\) is the spatial dimension.

Physical InterpretationPrefactor \((m/2\pi i\hbar T)^{d/2}\):- Scales as \(T^{-d/2}\) as time increases.- For \(d=1\): \(K \propto 1/\sqrt{T}\) (wavepacket spreads as \(\sqrt{T}\)).- For \(d=3\): \(K \propto 1/T^{3/2}\) (faster spreading in higher dimensions).Exponent \(\ffrac{im}{2\hbar T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\):- Equals the classical action for a particle traveling at constant velocity from \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) to \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\):

(191)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}S_\text{cl} = \ffrac{m v^2}{2} T = \ffrac{m}{2T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\\$$- Contains the phase that oscillates according to the classical action.**Probability density** $|K|^2$:\\$$|K|^2 = \ffrac{m^d}{(2\pi\hbar T)^d} \propto T^{-d}\\$$- For $d=1$: $|K|^2 \propto 1/T$ (spreading like diffusion).- For $d=3$: $|K|^2 \propto 1/T^3$ (very rapid dilution).\\### Relation to the Schrödinger EquationThe free-particle propagator satisfies the **Schrödinger equation**:\\$$i\hbar \ffrac{\partial K}{\partial t_f} = -\ffrac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}_f}^2 K\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

with initial condition

(192)#\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_i; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \delta^{(d)}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)\]

Verification: Taking derivatives of \(K_\text{free}\) directly confirms these properties. This connection shows that the path integral is equivalent to solving the Schrödinger equation.

Wavepacket Spreading

Initial Gaussian WavepacketConsider an initial wavefunction that is a Gaussian wavepacket (minimum uncertainty state):

(193)#\[\psi_0(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left(\ffrac{1}{\pi \hat{\sigma}_0^2}\right)^{d/4} \exp\left(-\ffrac{\boldsymbol{x}^2}{2\hat{\sigma}_0^2} + i\boldsymbol{k}_0 \cdot \boldsymbol{x}\right)\]

where \(\hat{\sigma}_0\) is the initial width and \(\boldsymbol{k}_0 = \boldsymbol{p}_0/\hbar\) is the initial momentum.

Time-Evolved WavepacketUsing the free-particle propagator:

(194)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \int K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}, t; \boldsymbol{x}_0, 0) \, \psi_0(\boldsymbol{x}_0) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}_0\\$$The integral of two Gaussians yields another Gaussian (up to a phase). The result is:\\$$\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \text{normalization} \times \exp\left(-\ffrac{(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_\text{cm}(t))^2}{2\sigma(t)^2} + i\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

where:

  • Center of mass: \(\boldsymbol{x}_\text{cm}(t) = \boldsymbol{x}_0 + (\boldsymbol{p}_0/m)t\) (classical trajectory).

  • Width: \(\sigma(t) = \hat{\sigma}_0\sqrt{1 + (\hbar t/(2m\hat{\sigma}_0^2))^2}\) (spreading).

  • Diffusion time scale: \(\tau = 2m\hat{\sigma}_0^2/\hbar\) (time for width to double).

Summary#

  • Time-slicing: Discretize time into intervals; paths are represented by positions at each slice.

  • Gaussian integrals: Evaluate the quadratic exponents using standard formulas; this is the computational workhorse.

  • Free-particle propagator: Exact result is \((m/2\pi i\hbar T)^{d/2} \exp(im(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2/2\hbar T)\).

  • Wavepacket spreading: The amplitude decays as \(T^{-d/2}\); the width grows as \(\sqrt{1 + (\hbar t/2m\hat{\sigma}_0^2)^2}\).

  • Schrödinger equivalence: The propagator satisfies the Schrödinger equation with a delta-function initial condition.

  • Physical picture: Quantum mechanics governs the evolution of probability amplitudes; classical action determines the phase.

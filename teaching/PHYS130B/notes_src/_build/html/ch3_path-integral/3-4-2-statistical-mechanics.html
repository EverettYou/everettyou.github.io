3.4.2 Statistical Mechanics#

Prompts

  • How does the imaginary-time propagator relate to the density matrix?

  • What is the density matrix \(\rho = e^{-\beta H}\) and how does the path integral represent it?

  • How do you calculate thermal averages from the path integral?

  • What is the connection between quantum mechanics at finite temperature and the path integral?

  • How does the path integral simplify the calculation of thermodynamic properties (free energy, entropy, etc.)?

Lecture Notes#

The Wick-rotated path integral naturally connects quantum mechanics to statistical mechanics: the partition function and thermal density matrix emerge as path integrals in imaginary time. This reveals a deep unity—the Boltzmann distribution \(e^{-\beta E}\) is the imaginary-time evolution operator, making temperature and inverse time dual concepts.

The Density MatrixIn quantum statistical mechanics, the density matrix at temperature \(T\) is#

(252)#\[\rho(T) = \frac{e^{-\beta H}}{Z(\beta)}\]

where:- \(H\) is the Hamiltonian.- \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\) is the inverse temperature.- \(Z(\beta) = \text{Tr}(e^{-\beta H})\) is the partition function (normalization).The density matrix is Hermitian (\(\rho^\dagger = \rho\)) and normalized (\(\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\)). It encodes all statistical information about the system at thermal equilibrium.

Thermal AverageThe thermal average (expectation value at temperature \(T\)) of an observable \(\hat{O}\) is#

(253)#\[\langle O \rangle_T = \text{Tr}(\rho \hat{O}) = \frac{\text{Tr}(e^{-\beta H} \hat{O})}{\text{Tr}(e^{-\beta H})}\]

The Imaginary-Time Propagator as Density MatrixThe imaginary-time propagator over a time interval \(\beta\hbar\) is#

(254)#\[K_E(\boldsymbol{x}', \beta\hbar; \boldsymbol{x}) = \langle \boldsymbol{x}' | e^{-\beta H} | \boldsymbol{x} \rangle\]

This is precisely the density matrix in position representation. The path integral over imaginary time from \(\tau = 0\) to \(\tau = \beta\hbar\) gives:

(255)#\[K_E(\boldsymbol{x}', \beta\hbar; \boldsymbol{x}) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}(0)=\boldsymbol{x}}^{\boldsymbol{x}(\beta\hbar)=\boldsymbol{x}'} e^{-S_E[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\]

Diagonal elements (\(\boldsymbol{x}' = \boldsymbol{x}\)):

(256)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}K_E(\boldsymbol{x}, \beta\hbar; \boldsymbol{x}) = \langle \boldsymbol{x} | e^{-\beta H} | \boldsymbol{x} \rangle\\$$The **local density of states** can be extracted from this diagonal element.\\### Partition Function from Path Integral:::{admonition} Derivation: Partition Function from Imaginary-Time Path Integral :class: dropdown note\\The partition function is the **trace** of the density matrix:\\$$Z(\beta) = \text{Tr}(e^{-\beta H}) = \int d^3\boldsymbol{x} \, K_E(\boldsymbol{x}, \beta\hbar; \boldsymbol{x})\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Inserting the path integral:

(257)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}Z(\beta) = \int d^3\boldsymbol{x} \int_{\text{periodic in } \tau} e^{-S_E[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\\$$The **periodicity condition** $\boldsymbol{x}(\beta\hbar) = \boldsymbol{x}(0)$ arises naturally from the trace (i.e., the integration over initial positions).\\:::\\### Thermal Averages from Path IntegralTo compute a thermal average $\langle O \rangle = \text{Tr}(\rho \hat{O})$:\\$$\langle O(\boldsymbol{x}) \rangle_T = \frac{1}{Z(\beta)} \int_{\text{periodic}} O(\boldsymbol{x}(\tau_0)) e^{-S_E[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

where \(\tau_0\) is an arbitrary time on the circle (thermal average is independent of the insertion point).Key insight: To measure an observable at temperature \(T\), insert it into the path integral at any imaginary time \(\tau_0 \in [0, \beta\hbar]\) and integrate over all periodic paths.

Free Energy and Thermodynamic Quantities#

Helmholtz Free EnergyThe Helmholtz free energy is related to the partition function by#

(258)#\[F(T, V, N) = -k_B T \ln Z(\beta)\]

The path integral provides a direct route to \(F\): compute \(Z\) via the path integral, then take the logarithm.

Entropy#

(259)#\[S = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_{V,N} = k_B(\ln Z + \beta \langle H \rangle)\]

Internal Energy#

(260)#\[\langle H \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}\]

All thermodynamic quantities follow from the single quantity \(Z(\beta)\), which the path integral computes.

Quantum-Classical Correspondence at Finite Temperature#

High-Temperature Limit (\(\beta \to 0\))At high temperature, the density matrix becomes sharply peaked near the position of minimum potential energy:#

$$\rho(\boldsymbol{x}’, \boldsymbol{x}) \approx \sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi\hbar^2 \beta}} e^{-V(\boldsymbol{x})/(k_B T)} \delta(\boldsymbol{x}’ - \boldsymbol{x})

$\(The system is **classical**: particles bounce around in the potential, governed by Boltzmann statistics \)e^{-V/(k_B T)}$.

Low-Temperature Limit (\(\beta \to \infty\))At zero temperature, the density matrix projects onto the ground state:#

$$\rho(\boldsymbol{x}’, \boldsymbol{x}) \to |\phi_0(\boldsymbol{x}’)\rangle\langle\phi_0(\boldsymbol{x})|

$\(where \)|\phi_0\rangle$ is the ground state. The system is purely quantum: in its lowest energy state, exhibiting zero-point energy and quantum correlations.

Computational Advantage: Monte Carlo SimulationsThe Euclidean path integral \(\int e^{-S_E} \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\) has a positive weight (measure), making it suitable for Monte Carlo importance sampling:1. Sample configurations \(\boldsymbol{x}(\tau)\) with probability \(\propto e^{-S_E[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar}\).2. Compute observables \(O(\boldsymbol{x})\) on each sample.3. Average: \(\langle O \rangle \approx \frac{1}{N_\text{samples}} \sum_i O(\boldsymbol{x}_i)\).In contrast, the real-time path integral \(\int e^{iS} \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\) has rapidly oscillating phase (sign problem), making it difficult to simulate directly.#

Summary#

  • Density matrix: \(\rho = e^{-\beta H}/Z\) governs thermal equilibrium; fully encoded by imaginary-time path integral.

  • Imaginary-time propagator: \(K_E(\boldsymbol{x}', \beta\hbar; \boldsymbol{x})\) is the density matrix in position representation.

  • Periodic paths: Trace condition \(\boldsymbol{x}(\beta\hbar) = \boldsymbol{x}(0)\) emerges from summation over states.

  • Thermal averages: Observables inserted at arbitrary \(\tau_0\) in the periodic path integral give \(\langle O \rangle_T\).

  • Thermodynamic functions: Free energy, entropy, internal energy all follow from partition function \(Z(\beta)\).

  • Classical limit: High \(T\) (\(\beta \to 0\)) gives classical Boltzmann distribution.

  • Quantum limit: Low \(T\) (\(\beta \to \infty\)) projects to ground state.

  • Numerical simulation: Positive weight \(e^{-S_E}\) enables efficient Monte Carlo sampling, crucial for lattice field theory.

3.2.3 Schrödinger Equation#

Prompts

  • How do we derive the Schrödinger equation starting from the path integral and time-slicing?

  • What role does the action in a single time slice play in the derivation?

  • How does the normalization factor emerge, and what does it tell us about quantum mechanics?

  • How is a potential \(V(\boldsymbol{x})\) incorporated into the path integral?

  • Why does the time-independent Schrödinger equation emerge naturally from the stationary phase condition?

Lecture Notes#

The Schrödinger equation emerges naturally from the path integral through a small-time-step approximation, revealing that the wavefunction’s phase evolves according to the classical Hamilton-Jacobi equation while its amplitude spreads due to quantum diffusion—a beautiful synthesis of classical and quantum mechanics.

The Kinetic Energy Term: Revisiting Time-SlicingWe showed that for a free particle, the time-sliced propagator over one small interval \(\varepsilon\) is#

(195)#\[K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{im(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}\right)\]

Now we ask: What Taylor expansion in \(\varepsilon\) does this satisfy?

Expansion in Powers of \(\varepsilon\):::{admonition} Derivation: Time Derivative of Propagator#

:class: dropdown note

Write

(196)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}K_\varepsilon = N(\varepsilon) \exp(iS_\varepsilon/\hbar)\\$$where $N(\varepsilon) = (m/2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon)^{d/2}$ is the normalization and $S_\varepsilon = \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\varepsilon}$ is the action.Taking the time derivative:\\$$\frac{\partial K_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon} = K_\varepsilon \left(\frac{\partial (\ln N)}{\partial\varepsilon} + \frac{i}{\hbar}\frac{\partial S_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon}\right)\\$$Computing the derivatives:\\$$\frac{\partial (\ln N)}{\partial\varepsilon} = -\frac{d}{2}\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\\$$$$\frac{\partial S_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon} = -\frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\\$$So\\$$\frac{\partial K_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon} = K_\varepsilon \left(-\frac{d}{2\varepsilon} - \frac{im(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon^2}\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Rearranging:

(197)#\[i\hbar \frac{\partial K_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon} = K_\varepsilon \left(-i\hbar\frac{d}{2\varepsilon} + \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\right)\]

Hmm, this doesn’t immediately look like a Schrödinger equation. We need to relate the spatial derivatives to the kinetic energy term.

Spatial Derivatives of the Kinetic Propagator:::{admonition} Derivation: Laplacian of Free Propagator

:class: dropdown note

Compute the second spatial derivative with respect to \(\boldsymbol{x}'\):

(198)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 K_\varepsilon = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 \left[N(\varepsilon) \exp\left(\frac{im(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}\right)\right]\\$$Using the product rule on $e^{if(\boldsymbol{x}')}$ where $f = \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}$:\\$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'} e^{if} = i(\nabla f) e^{if} = i \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})}{\hbar\varepsilon} e^{if}\\$$$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 e^{if} = i\frac{m}{\hbar\varepsilon} \nabla^2(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x}) e^{if} + i^2 \frac{m^2(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{\hbar^2\varepsilon^2} e^{if}\\$$The Laplacian of $\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x}$ (with respect to $\boldsymbol{x}'$) is $d$ times the delta function in the distributional sense, but for smooth functions:\\$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x}) = 2d \text{ (coordinate sum)}\\$$Actually, more carefully, $\sum_{i} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x'_i} (x'_i - x_i) = d$, so\\$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 e^{if} = \frac{imd}{\hbar\varepsilon} e^{if} - \frac{m^2(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{\hbar^2\varepsilon^2} e^{if}\\$$Therefore:\\$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 K_\varepsilon = K_\varepsilon \left(\frac{imd}{\hbar\varepsilon} - \frac{m^2(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{\hbar^2\varepsilon^2}\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Rearranging:

(199)#\[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 K_\varepsilon = K_\varepsilon \left(-\frac{\hbar d}{2\varepsilon} + \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\right)\]

Comparing with Eq. (eq-ih-derivative)

, we see that in the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\), the kinetic energy term dominates:

(200)#\[i\hbar \frac{\partial K_\varepsilon}{\partial\varepsilon} \approx -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}'}^2 K_\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon)\]

This is the free-particle Schrödinger equation!

Adding Potential Energy: The Full DerivationFor a particle in a potential \(V(\boldsymbol{x})\), the Lagrangian is \(L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 - V(\boldsymbol{x})\). In a small time step \(\varepsilon\):#

(201)#\[S_\varepsilon = \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}{2\varepsilon} - V(\boldsymbol{x}) \varepsilon\]

For small \(\varepsilon\), we can approximate \(V(\boldsymbol{x}) \approx V(\boldsymbol{x}')\) or use the midpoint. More carefully, treating \(V\varepsilon\) as a perturbation:

(202)#\[e^{iS_\varepsilon/\hbar} = e^{im(\boldsymbol{x}'-\boldsymbol{x})^2/(2\hbar\varepsilon)} e^{-iV(\boldsymbol{x})\varepsilon/\hbar}\]

To leading order in \(\varepsilon\):

(203)#\[e^{-iV(\boldsymbol{x})\varepsilon/\hbar} \approx 1 - \frac{iV(\boldsymbol{x})\varepsilon}{\hbar}\]

So the single-slice propagator becomes

(204)#\[K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) \approx K_0(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) \left(1 - \frac{iV(\boldsymbol{x})\varepsilon}{\hbar}\right)\]

where \(K_0\) is the free-particle propagator.

Full Time Evolution:::{admonition} Derivation: Adding Potential to Time Evolution#

:class: dropdown note

Now consider the wavefunction evolution from \(t\) to \(t + \varepsilon\):

(205)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\psi(\boldsymbol{x}', t + \varepsilon) = \int K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) \, \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}\\$$$$\approx \int K_0(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) \left(1 - \frac{iV(\boldsymbol{x})\varepsilon}{\hbar}\right) \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}\\$$$$= \int K_0(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x} - \frac{i\varepsilon}{\hbar} \int K_0(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) V(\boldsymbol{x}) \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}\\$$The first term is the free-particle evolution. The second term, for small $\varepsilon$ and using $V(\boldsymbol{x}) \approx V(\boldsymbol{x}')$, gives:\\$$\frac{\psi(\boldsymbol{x}', t + \varepsilon) - \psi(\boldsymbol{x}', t)}{\varepsilon} \approx \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 - V(\boldsymbol{x}')\right) \psi(\boldsymbol{x}', t)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Taking the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\):

(206)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{i\hbar}\left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x})\right)\psi\\$$Or equivalently:\\$$i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x})\right)\psi\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

This is the time-dependent Schrödinger equation. The path integral naturally generates it.

The Time-Independent Schrödinger EquationFor stationary states with energy \(E\), the wavefunction is \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) e^{-iEt/\hbar}\). The propagator between two times at the same spatial point involves a phase factor:#

(207)#\[\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t_f) = e^{-iE(t_f - t_i)/\hbar} \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t_i)\]

More generally, energy eigenstates satisfy

(208)#\[E \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x})\right) \phi(\boldsymbol{x})\]

This can also be derived from the path integral by looking for solutions where paths with action \(S = Et\) give constructive interference—i.e., by applying the stationary phase condition to the path integral.

Normalization and Probability InterpretationThe prefactor \((m/2\pi i\hbar\varepsilon)^{d/2}\) in the propagator ensures proper normalization of probability. Specifically:#

(209)#\[\int K(\boldsymbol{x}', t'; \boldsymbol{x}, t) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x} = 1 \text{ (for fixed } \boldsymbol{x}, t, t'\text{)}\]

This means that if \(\int |\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)|^2 d^d\boldsymbol{x} = 1\) (normalized wavefunction), then

(210)#\[\int |\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t')|^2 d^d\boldsymbol{x} = 1 \text{ (at later time)}\]

Probability is conserved in time—a fundamental requirement of quantum mechanics.

Action and phase:class: noteIn quantum mechanics, the action \(S\) plays the role that phase \(\phi = S/\hbar\) plays in waves. The Planck constant \(\hbar\) sets the scale: when \(S \sim \hbar\), quantum effects are significant; when \(S \gg \hbar\), classical behavior dominates.

Derivation: Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation from Stationary Phase

For stationary states, the wavefunction has the form \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) e^{-iEt/\hbar}\). In the path integral formalism, the propagator from time \(t_i\) to time \(t_f\) is

\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}(t_i)=\boldsymbol{x}_i}^{\boldsymbol{x}(t_f)=\boldsymbol{x}_f} e^{iS[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\]

For a stationary state with energy \(E\) and considering evolution from initial state \(\phi(\boldsymbol{x}_i)\) at \(t_i = 0\) to time \(t_f = T\):

\[\psi(\boldsymbol{x}_f, T) = \int K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, T; \boldsymbol{x}_i, 0) \phi(\boldsymbol{x}_i) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}_i = e^{-iET/\hbar} \phi(\boldsymbol{x}_f)\]

The action along a classical path over time \(T\) is \(S = ET\). For stationary states, paths with action \(S = ET\) contribute with stationary phase—small variations in the path produce negligible changes in the exponent.

For energy eigenstates, the path integral becomes dominated by paths that satisfy the stationary action principle at energy \(E\):

\[\delta(S - ET) = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Euler-Lagrange equations with constraint } E = \text{const}\]

This is equivalent to requiring that \(\phi(\boldsymbol{x})\) satisfies the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

\[E \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x})\right) \phi(\boldsymbol{x})\]

Derivation from composition property: Alternatively, for short time intervals \(\varepsilon\), the stationary-state condition is

\[\phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t+\varepsilon) = e^{-iE\varepsilon/\hbar} \phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\]

Expanding \(e^{-iE\varepsilon/\hbar} \approx 1 - iE\varepsilon/\hbar\) and using the composition of propagators from the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, we obtain

\[E \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = H \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x})\right) \phi(\boldsymbol{x})\]

Thus, energy eigenstates are eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian, derived directly from the stationary-phase condition in the path integral.

Summary

  • Time-slicing derivation: Start with the path integral written as a product of single-slice propagators; take the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\).

  • Free particle: The kinetic term in the action, when differentiated, yields the Laplacian in the Schrödinger equation.

  • Potential energy: Added as an exponential factor in the action; expands to \((1 - iV\varepsilon/\hbar)\) for small time steps.

  • Full Schrödinger equation: Emerges from the path integral without additional assumptions. The path integral and Schrödinger equation are equivalent.

  • Stationary states: Energy eigenstates correspond to paths with stationary action for a given energy.

  • Probability conservation: The normalization factor ensures that \(\int |\psi|^2 = 1\) is preserved in time.

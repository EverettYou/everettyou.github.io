5.2.1 Interaction Picture#

Prompts

  • What is the interaction picture and why is it useful for time-dependent perturbations?

  • How does the interaction picture factorize the time evolution into free propagation and interactions?

  • Derive the equation of motion for the evolution operator in the interaction picture.

  • What is the Dyson series? How does it encode time ordering and causality?

  • Why is time ordering crucial? What goes wrong without it?

  • How does the time-ordered exponential compactly represent the infinite perturbative series?

Lecture Notes#

When quantum systems interact with external fields that change in time—whether oscillating electromagnetic radiation or engineered control pulses—the full dynamics become intractable. The interaction picture offers a brilliant mathematical perspective: by choosing a reference frame that rotates with the unperturbed evolution, we strip away the rapid oscillations and expose only the slow, physically significant interactions. This reformulation transforms time-dependent problems from impossible to solvable.

Definition and Transformations#

In the interaction picture, we separate the Hamiltonian into two parts:

  • Free/unperturbed part \(H_0\): evolution is rapid but exactly solvable (oscillates at natural frequencies \(\omega_n = E_n^{(0)}/\hbar\))

  • Interaction part \(V(t)\): slow compared to \(H_0\) (weak coupling regime)

States and operators transform as:

(311)#\[|\psi_I(t)\rangle = e^{iH_0 t/\hbar} |\psi_S(t)\rangle\]
(312)#\[O_I(t) = e^{iH_0 t/\hbar} O_S e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\]

The key insight: multiplying by \(e^{iH_0 t/\hbar}\) factors out the rapid oscillations from \(H_0\), leaving only the slow dynamics driven by \(V(t)\).

Equation of Motion in the Interaction Picture#

The interaction-picture state obeys a much simpler equation:

(313)#\[\boxed{i\hbar \frac{d|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = \lambda V_I(t) |\psi_I\rangle}\]

where the interaction-picture perturbation is:

(314)#\[\boxed{V_I(t) = e^{iH_0 t/\hbar} V(t) e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}}\]

Physical content: \(V_I(t)\) is the perturbation as viewed from a frame rotating with the free evolution. Rapid oscillations (at natural frequencies \(\sim E_n^{(0)}/\hbar\)) are removed, revealing only the slow dynamics.

Derivation

Start with the Schrödinger equation:

\[i\hbar \frac{d|\psi_S\rangle}{dt} = H(t)|\psi_S\rangle = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]|\psi_S\rangle\]

Transform to the interaction picture using \(|\psi_S\rangle = e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\):

\[i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\right) = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

Expand the left side using the product rule:

\[i\hbar e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}H_0|\psi_I\rangle + \frac{d|\psi_I\rangle}{dt}\right) = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

Simplify:

\[H_0 e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle + i\hbar e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\frac{d|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = H_0 e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle + \lambda V(t)e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

The \(H_0\) terms cancel:

\[i\hbar \frac{d|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = \lambda e^{iH_0 t/\hbar} V(t) e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle = \lambda V_I(t)|\psi_I\rangle\]

Eigenbasis Expansion#

Expand \(V_I(t)\) in the eigenbasis of \(H_0\):

(315)#\[V_I(t) = \sum_{n,m} e^{i\omega_{nm}t} V_{nm}(t) |n\rangle\langle m|\]

where:

(316)#\[\omega_{nm} = \frac{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}{\hbar}\]

The oscillatory factor \(e^{i\omega_{nm}t}\) encodes resonance:

  • When \(\omega_{nm} \approx 0\) (initial and final states have nearly equal energy): coupling is strong, transitions occur.

  • When \(|\omega_{nm}|\) is large (states far apart in energy): rapid oscillations suppress the coupling.

The Evolution Operator and Dyson Series#

Formal Solution#

Define the interaction-picture evolution operator \(U_I(t)\) such that:

(317)#\[|\psi_I(t)\rangle = U_I(t) |\psi_I(0)\rangle\]

This operator has the formal solution:

(318)#\[\boxed{U_I(t) = T \exp\left(-\frac{i\lambda}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt' V_I(t')\right)}\]

This is the time-ordered exponential. Expanding it gives the Dyson series:

(319)#\[\boxed{U_I(t) = \mathbb{I} + \frac{\lambda}{i\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) + \left(\frac{\lambda}{i\hbar}\right)^2 \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2) + \ldots}\]

Physical meaning:

  • Zeroth order (\(\lambda^0\)): No interaction, system stays in its initial state.

  • First order (\(\lambda^1\)): One interaction event at time \(t_1\).

  • Second order (\(\lambda^2\)): Two interaction events at times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), with \(t_1 > t_2\) (time-ordered).

  • Higher orders: Multiple interaction events, always ordered in time (\(t_1 > t_2 > t_3 > \ldots\)).

Each term represents a different “scattering” process or number of intermediate transitions.

Derivation

From the equation of motion:

\[i\hbar \frac{\partial U_I}{\partial t} = \lambda V_I(t) U_I(t), \quad U_I(0) = \mathbb{I}\]

Integrate formally:

\[U_I(t) = \mathbb{I} + \frac{1}{i\hbar} \lambda \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) U_I(t_1)\]

This is a Volterra integral equation. Solve by iterating: substitute the expression for \(U_I\) back into the integral to obtain higher orders:

First order:

\[U_I^{(1)}(t) = \frac{\lambda}{i\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1)\]

Second order:

\[U_I^{(2)}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{i\hbar}\right)^2 \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_2)\]

The integral limits automatically enforce time ordering: when we substitute \(U_I(t_1)\) into the outer integral, the inner integration runs from \(0\) to \(t_1\), ensuring \(t_1 > t_2\). This pattern continues for all higher orders.

Summing all orders:

\[U_I(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{\lambda}{i\hbar}\right)^n \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 \cdots \int_0^{t_{n-1}} dt_n \, V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2) \cdots V_I(t_n)\]

This is precisely the expansion of the time-ordered exponential.

Time Ordering and Causality#

The integral limits \(t > t_1 > t_2 > \ldots\) ensure causal time ordering: interactions are processed in sequence, with later times to the left.

Why this matters: In general, \([V_I(t_1), V_I(t_2)] \neq 0\) (operators do not commute). The order in which interactions occur is physical and affects the result. Time ordering enforces the natural causal sequence.

Time-ordering operator definition

For any two operators \(A(t_1)\) and \(B(t_2)\) with \(t_1 > t_2\), the time-ordering operator \(T\) places later times to the left:

\[T[A(t_1) B(t_2)] = A(t_1) B(t_2) \quad (t_1 > t_2)\]
\[T[B(t_2) A(t_1)] = A(t_1) B(t_2) \quad (t_1 > t_2)\]

The time-ordered exponential \(T \exp(X)\) automatically generates all orders of an expansion with time-ordered integration limits.

Full Schrödinger-Picture Evolution#

The Schrödinger-picture evolution operator \(U_S(t)\) factorizes as:

(320)#\[\boxed{U_S(t) = e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar} U_I(t)}\]

This decomposition is remarkably powerful:

  • The first factor \(e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\) handles the fast, high-frequency \(H_0\) oscillations.

  • The second factor \(U_I(t)\) (via the Dyson series) captures the slow interactions.

Together, they build up the full interacting dynamics without having to solve the complete Hamiltonian.

Perturbative Expansion and Transition Amplitudes#

Order-by-Order Structure#

Expand the evolution operator in powers of \(\lambda\):

(321)#\[U_I(t) = U_I^{(0)}(t) + \lambda U_I^{(1)}(t) + \lambda^2 U_I^{(2)}(t) + \ldots\]

Zeroth order (\(\lambda = 0\)):

\[U_I^{(0)}(t) = \mathbb{I} \quad \text{(no interaction)}\]

First order (\(\lambda^1\)): One interaction at time \(t_1\).

\[U_I^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1)\]

Second order (\(\lambda^2\)): Two interactions at times \(t_1, t_2\) with \(t_1 > t_2\).

\[U_I^{(2)}(t) = -\frac{1}{\hbar^2} \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2)\]

Higher orders follow the pattern: more interaction times, more nested integrals, time-ordered products.

Transition Amplitudes#

The transition amplitude from state \(|i\rangle\) to \(|f\rangle\) is:

(322)#\[\mathcal{A}_{i\to f}(t) = \langle f | U_S(t) | i \rangle\]

To first order in \(\lambda\):

\[\mathcal{A}_{i\to f}^{(0)}(t) = \delta_{fi} e^{-iE_f^{(0)} t/\hbar}\]
(323)#\[\mathcal{A}_{i\to f}^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{i}{\hbar} e^{-iE_f^{(0)} t/\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 e^{-i\omega_{fi}t_1} \langle f | V(t_1) | i \rangle\]

where \(\omega_{fi} = (E_f^{(0)} - E_i^{(0)})/\hbar\) is the transition frequency.

Physical Interpretation#

Resonance and Detuning#

The oscillatory factor \(e^{-i\omega_{fi}t_1}\) in the transition amplitude has frequency \(\omega_{fi}\), determined by the energy difference between initial and final states.

Resonant condition (\(\omega_{fi} \approx 0\)): The integrand oscillates slowly or not at all; the integral accumulates, giving a large (growing) amplitude. This is a strong resonant transition.

Off-resonant condition (\(|\omega_{fi}| \gg 1/T\) for evolution time \(T\)): Rapid oscillations cause cancellation; amplitude is suppressed. Weak off-resonant transitions.

This is the origin of the rotating wave approximation: only near-resonant transitions (with \(|\omega_{fi}| \lesssim 1/T\)) contribute significantly.

Virtual Processes#

Higher-order terms in the Dyson series correspond to virtual intermediate states:

  • First order: Direct \(i \to f\) transition.

  • Second order: \(i \to n \to f\) (via intermediate state \(n\)), where state \(n\) is visited transiently.

  • Higher orders: Chains of intermediate transitions.

The time integrals in the Dyson series encode energy conservation on average: the system is not required to have fixed energy during intermediate steps (virtual processes), but the overall energy is conserved when we integrate over all interaction times.

Summary#

  • Interaction picture: Separates fast (solved by \(e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\)) and slow (perturbative \(V_I(t)\)) dynamics.

  • Interaction-picture perturbation: \(V_I(t) = e^{iH_0 t/\hbar} V(t) e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar}\), with oscillatory terms \(e^{i\omega_{nm}t}\) encoding resonances.

  • Evolution operator: \(U_I(t) = T \exp(-i \int V_I dt / \hbar)\) (time-ordered exponential).

  • Dyson series: Systematic expansion showing interaction as sequence of events at ordered times.

  • Full evolution: \(U_S(t) = e^{-iH_0 t/\hbar} U_I(t)\) factorizes free propagation and interactions.

  • Transition amplitudes: Depend on oscillatory factors \(e^{i\omega_{fi}t}\); resonance (\(\omega_{fi} \approx 0\)) enhances transitions.

  • Causality: Time ordering ensures that interactions occur in the correct physical sequence.

  • Virtual processes: Higher-order terms represent intermediate state transitions with average energy conservation.

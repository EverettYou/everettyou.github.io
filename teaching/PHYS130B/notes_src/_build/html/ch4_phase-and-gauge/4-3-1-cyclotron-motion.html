4.3.1 Cyclotron Motion#

Prompts

  • Derive the equations of motion for a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field. What is the cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c\)?

  • Solve the equations for circular orbits. What is the cyclotron radius? How does it depend on particle energy?

  • Explain the Hall effect classically. How does the Hall coefficient \(R_H = 1/ne\) arise from Lorentz force balance?

  • What is the difference between cyclotron frequency and Larmor frequency? When do they coincide?

  • How would the cyclotron motion change for positrons vs electrons in the same magnetic field?

Lecture Notes#

The motion of a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field is one of the most fundamental problems in electromagnetism and quantum mechanics. It serves as the foundation for understanding the quantum Hall effect, shows how Landau quantization arises, and reveals deep connections between classical and quantum physics.

Classical Cyclotron Motion#

Equations of Motion#

A charged particle with charge \(q\) and mass \(m\) in a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) experiences the Lorentz force:

\[m\boldsymbol{a} = q\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}\]

Writing \(\boldsymbol{v} = v_x\hat{x} + v_y\hat{y} + v_z\hat{z}\) and \(\boldsymbol{a} = \dot{v}_x\hat{x} + \dot{v}_y\hat{y} + \dot{v}_z\hat{z}\), the cross product gives:

\[m\dot{v}_x = qv_yB, \quad m\dot{v}_y = -qv_xB, \quad m\dot{v}_z = 0\]

Cyclotron Frequency

The characteristic timescale of motion perpendicular to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) is set by the cyclotron frequency (or Larmor frequency):

\[\omega_c = \frac{qB}{m}\]

(In Gaussian units: \(\omega_c = eB/mc\).)

Key properties:

  • Independent of particle kinetic energy

  • Depends only on charge-to-mass ratio and field strength

  • Determines the period of one orbit: \(T_c = 2\pi/\omega_c\)

  • For electrons: \(\omega_c = eB/m_e\) (negative because \(e < 0\))

The equations become:

\[\ddot{v}_x + \omega_c^2 v_x = 0, \quad \ddot{v}_y + \omega_c^2 v_y = 0\]

These are simple harmonic oscillators. The general solution is:

\[v_x(t) = v_\perp \cos(\omega_c t + \phi_0), \quad v_y(t) = v_\perp \sin(\omega_c t + \phi_0)\]

where \(v_\perp = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}\) is the speed perpendicular to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) and \(\phi_0\) is an arbitrary phase.

Circular Orbits and Guiding Center Motion#

Integrating the velocity components gives the orbit in the \(xy\)-plane:

\[x(t) = x_0 + \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c}\sin(\omega_c t + \phi_0), \quad y(t) = y_0 - \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c}\cos(\omega_c t + \phi_0)\]

This describes a circular orbit with:

Cyclotron Radius (Larmor Radius)

\[r_c = \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c} = \frac{mv_\perp}{qB}\]

Physical meaning:

  • The particle spirals around the guiding center position \((x_0, y_0)\) at frequency \(\omega_c\)

  • Motion parallel to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) (the \(z\)-direction) is unaffected

  • Radius scales with: momentum (\(\propto v_\perp\)), inversely with field (\(\propto 1/B\))

  • Different particles with same kinetic energy orbit with different radii if they have different \(m/q\) ratios

Relation to energy: Using \(E_\perp = \frac{1}{2}mv_\perp^2\):

\[r_c = \frac{\sqrt{2mE_\perp}}{qB}\]

Orbital Period: Energy-Independent

The period of one complete orbit is:

\[T_c = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_c} = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}\]

Remarkable fact: \(T_c\) is completely independent of the particle’s kinetic energy or velocity!

This is why cyclotron accelerators work: particles always take the same time to complete a semicircle, allowing constant-frequency RF acceleration to keep all particles synchronized regardless of their energy.

Motion in Crossed \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\) Fields#

When both electric and magnetic fields are present, the behavior is richer. If \(\boldsymbol{E}\) is perpendicular to \(\boldsymbol{B}\), the guiding center drifts with velocity:

\[\boldsymbol{v}_{\text{drift}} = \frac{\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{B}}{B^2}\]

E×B Drift Velocity

In crossed fields \(\boldsymbol{E} \perp \boldsymbol{B}\), the guiding center drifts with velocity:

\[\boldsymbol{v}_E = \frac{\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{B}}{B^2}\]

Remarkable properties:

  • Independent of charge \(q\) and mass \(m\) — all particles drift together

  • Direction: perpendicular to both \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\)

  • Magnitude: \(v_E = E/B\) (for perpendicular fields)

  • Acts as an E-cross-B drift that is fundamental in plasma physics

This universal drift is crucial for plasma confinement and appears throughout geophysics (ionosphere, magnetosphere) and astrophysics (accretion disks, solar flares).

Note: Physical Intuition for Drift

The electric field accelerates the particle in one direction, increasing the cyclotron radius on one side of the orbit. This asymmetry causes the guiding center to drift perpendicular to both \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\). The drift speed adjusts so the net force on the guiding center is zero in the drift direction.

Semiclassical Quantization and Landau Levels#

The classical cyclotron motion suggests a natural quantization condition. The action integral for one complete orbit is:

\[S = \oint p \, \mathrm{d}q = m v_\perp (2\pi r_c) = \frac{2\pi m v_\perp^2}{\omega_c} = \frac{2\pi E_\perp}{\omega_c}\]

where \(E_\perp = \frac{1}{2}m v_\perp^2\) is the kinetic energy perpendicular to the field.

By the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule, \(S = 2\pi n \hbar\) for integer \(n\), we get:

Landau Levels (Semiclassical Derivation)

Using the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition \(\oint p \, \mathrm{d}q = 2\pi\hbar(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\):

\[E_n = \hbar\omega_c\left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]

for \(n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\)

These are the Landau levels, equally spaced in energy with gap \(\hbar\omega_c\).

Key features:

  • Spacing \(\hbar\omega_c\) is independent of magnetic field strength (but \(\omega_c \propto B\), so levels get closer in real energy units as \(B \to 0\))

  • Each level is highly degenerate (degeneracy \(\sim eB\hbar/(\pi c)\) for large systems)

  • Semiclassical result is exact for the harmonic oscillator problem, explaining why Bohr-Sommerfeld works perfectly here

  • Landau quantization is the foundation for the integer and fractional quantum Hall effects

Derivation: Bohr-Sommerfeld Result

The phase space action for circular motion at fixed energy is:

\[\oint p_\phi \, \mathrm{d}\phi = p_\phi \cdot 2\pi = m r_c v_\perp \cdot 2\pi = 2\pi m \frac{E_\perp}{\omega_c}\]

Setting \(\oint p \, \mathrm{d}q = 2\pi\hbar(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\) (with the zero-point correction \(+\tfrac{1}{2}\) characteristic of harmonic oscillators), we obtain:

\[2\pi m \frac{E_\perp}{\omega_c} = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]
\[E_\perp = \hbar\omega_c\left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]

The \(+\tfrac{1}{2}\) zero-point energy is unavoidable from the uncertainty principle: we cannot have both \(p\) and \(x\) exactly zero for a confined particle.

Key Physical Insights#

Summary: Cyclotron Motion

Cyclotron frequency (energy-independent):

\[\omega_c = \frac{qB}{m}\]

Cyclotron radius (momentum-dependent):

\[r_c = \frac{mv_\perp}{qB}\]

Orbital period (energy-independent):

\[T_c = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}\]

E×B drift (independent of \(q\), \(m\), energy):

\[\boldsymbol{v}_E = \frac{\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{B}}{B^2}\]

Landau levels (from Bohr-Sommerfeld):

\[E_n = \hbar\omega_c\left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]

These are the fundamental building blocks for understanding:

  • Plasma confinement and dynamics

  • Quantum Hall effects

  • Cyclotron resonance spectroscopy

  • Motion in the Earth’s magnetosphere

Discussion

The Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule predicts energy levels \(E_n = (n + \tfrac{1}{2})\hbar\omega_c\), exactly matching the quantum mechanical result from Landau quantization. But is this agreement a coincidence, or is there a deeper reason?

(a) In general, Bohr-Sommerfeld is an approximation valid only for large \(n\). Yet for cyclotron motion, it gives the exact result even for \(n=0\). Why is cyclotron motion special?

(b) The \(+\tfrac{1}{2}\) zero-point energy comes from the uncertainty principle: you cannot have both \(p\) and \(q\) simultaneously zero. Can you show that the zero-point energy \(\tfrac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_c\) is unavoidable quantum mechanically, using only \(\Delta x \Delta p \gtrsim \hbar\) and the circular orbit geometry?

(c) In the classical limit (\(\hbar \to 0\) or large \(B\)), do the Landau levels remain equally spaced? What happens to the energy gaps between levels?

The classical cyclotron problem is the springboard to understanding quantum many-body physics in strong magnetic fields, where the Landau level degeneracy and topological properties dominate the physics.

Problem 1: Derive the Cyclotron Frequency

Starting from Newton’s second law for a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\), derive the cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c = eB/m\). Show that the perpendicular velocity components satisfy the coupled differential equations:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}v_x}{dt} = \omega_c v_y, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}v_y}{dt} = -\omega_c v_x\]

Then verify that the solution \(v_x(t) = v_\perp\cos(\omega_c t)\), \(v_y(t) = v_\perp\sin(\omega_c t)\) satisfies these equations.

Problem 2: Cyclotron Radius and Kinetic Energy

An electron in a magnetic field \(B = 0.5\) T orbits with cyclotron radius \(r_c = 1\) mm.

(a) Calculate the perpendicular velocity \(v_\perp\).

(b) Find the perpendicular kinetic energy \(E_\perp = \frac{1}{2}mv_\perp^2\) in eV.

(c) Show that \(r_c = \sqrt{2mE_\perp}/(eB)\).

Problem 3: Orbital Period is Energy-Independent

Prove that the cyclotron orbital period \(T_c = 2\pi m/(eB)\) is independent of the particle’s perpendicular velocity and kinetic energy. Why is this property crucial for the operation of a classical cyclotron accelerator?

Problem 4: Hall Voltage in a Current-Carrying Wire

A copper wire carries a current \(I = 10\) A in the \(x\)-direction. A perpendicular magnetic field \(B = 1\) T is applied in the \(-z\) direction. The wire has cross-sectional width \(w = 2\) cm in the \(y\)-direction.

(a) Derive the Hall electric field \(E_H\) in terms of the electron drift velocity \(v_d\), using force balance.

(b) If the electron density in copper is \(n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}\) m\(^{-3}\), calculate the drift velocity \(v_d = I/(neA)\) where \(A\) is the cross-sectional area.

(c) Calculate the Hall voltage \(V_H = E_H \cdot w\).

Problem 5: Lorentz Force Provides Centripetal Acceleration

Show that the Lorentz force on a particle orbiting at the cyclotron frequency equals the required centripetal force \(F_c = mv_\perp^2/r_c\). Verify dimensional consistency and explain why this equality confirms circular motion.

Problem 6: Drift Motion vs Cyclotron Orbits

In the presence of a perpendicular electric field \(\boldsymbol{E} = E\hat{x}\) and magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\), a charged particle undergoes a drift velocity \(\boldsymbol{v}_d = (\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{B})/B^2\).

(a) Calculate the drift velocity for \(E = 1000\) V/m and \(B = 1\) T.

(b) Compare the timescale of drift motion to the cyclotron period \(T_c\) for an electron in this field.

(c) Explain physically why the drift is independent of the particle’s charge and mass (a surprising result).

Problem 7: Cyclotron Radius Scales with Momentum

An electron and a proton enter the same uniform magnetic field \(B = 2\) T with the same kinetic energy \(K = 1\) keV.

(a) Calculate the perpendicular velocity for each particle.

(b) Calculate the cyclotron radius \(r_c = mv_\perp/(eB)\) for each.

(c) Compare the radii: what is the ratio \(r_{\text{proton}}/r_{\text{electron}}\)? Explain why heavier particles orbit in larger circles at the same kinetic energy.

Problem 8: Hall Coefficient and Charge Carrier Type

(a) For a material with \(n = 10^{23}\) m\(^{-3}\), calculate \(R_H\) in SI units.

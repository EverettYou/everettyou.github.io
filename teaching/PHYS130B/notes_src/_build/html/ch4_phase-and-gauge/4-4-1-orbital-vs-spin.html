4.4.1 Orbital vs Spin#

Prompts

  • How is orbital angular momentum different from intrinsic spin? Can you describe both using the same quantum mechanical operators?

  • A spin-1/2 particle precesses in a magnetic field. Why does it precess? Derive the equation of motion for the spin vector.

  • What is Thomas precession, and when does it matter? How does the relativistic geometry change the expected precession rate?

  • Explain the analogy between a spin in a magnetic field and a charged particle in an electromagnetic field. Where does this analogy break down?

  • Why do we call spin “intrinsic” angular momentum when it can be described as orbital motion in an abstract space (monopole background)?

Lecture Notes#

Orbital Angular Momentum#

The orbital angular momentum operator is defined as:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{r}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\]

In Cartesian components:

\[ \hat{L}_i = \epsilon_{ijk}\hat{r}_j\hat{p}_k\]

These operators satisfy the canonical angular momentum commutation relations:

\[ [\hat{L}_i, \hat{L}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{L}_k\]

In particular, \([\hat{L}_x, \hat{L}_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\hat{L}_z\) (and cyclic permutations). The magnitude squared commutes with all components:

\[ [\hat{L}^2, \hat{L}_i] = 0 \quad \text{for all } i\]

where \(\hat{L}^2 = \hat{L}_x^2 + \hat{L}_y^2 + \hat{L}_z^2\).

Key Property: Quantization of Orbital Angular Momentum

Because orbital angular momentum arises from a spatial degree of freedom, its eigenvalues are integers only:

\[ \hat{L}^2 Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) = \hbar^2 l(l+1) Y_l^m(\theta,\phi), \quad l = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]
\[ \hat{L}_z Y_l^m(\theta,\phi) = \hbar m Y_l^m(\theta,\phi), \quad m = -l, \ldots, l\]

The eigenfunctions are the spherical harmonics \(Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)\). The quantum numbers \(l\) and \(m\) are always integers.

Intrinsic Spin Angular Momentum#

Unlike orbital angular momentum, spin \(\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) is an intrinsic quantum property—it does not arise from spatial motion. It is an internal angular momentum that has no classical analogue.

For a spin-1/2 particle (like an electron or proton):

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{S}} = \frac{\hbar}{2}\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} = (\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z)\) are the Pauli matrices:

\[\begin{split} \hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\sigma}^y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ \mathrm{i} & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\sigma}^z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

The spin operators satisfy the same commutation relations as orbital angular momentum:

\[ [\hat{S}_i, \hat{S}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{S}_k\]

However, the eigenvalues differ. For spin-1/2:

\[ \hat{S}^2|\chi\rangle = \frac{3}{4}\hbar^2|\chi\rangle \quad \text{(for all states)}\]
\[ \hat{S}_z|\uparrow\rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2}|\uparrow\rangle, \quad \hat{S}_z|\downarrow\rangle = -\frac{\hbar}{2}|\downarrow\rangle\]

The spin quantum numbers are half-integer: \(s = 1/2\), and \(m_s = \pm 1/2\).

Key Property: Half-Integer Spin Exists

Unlike orbital angular momentum, spin can have half-integer quantum numbers. A spin-1/2 particle is described by a 2-dimensional Hilbert space (a spinor):

\[\begin{split} |\chi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \chi_\uparrow \\ \chi_\downarrow \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(|\uparrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(|\downarrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).

This is fundamentally different from \(L\), which always produces integers.

Total Angular Momentum#

When both orbital and spin angular momentum are present, the total angular momentum is:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} + \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\]

The total \(J\) operator satisfies the same commutation relations:

\[ [\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{J}_k\]

By the angular momentum addition rules, if a system has orbital quantum number \(l\) and spin quantum number \(s\), then:

\[ j = |l - s|, |l - s| + 1, \ldots, l + s\]

For example, an electron in a \(p\)-orbital (\(l = 1\)) with spin-1/2 (\(s = 1/2\)) can have \(j = 1/2\) or \(j = 3/2\).

The quantum numbers \((l, s, j, m_j)\) are useful when \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) coupling (spin-orbit coupling) is significant.

Spin-Orbit Coupling#

When a charged particle moves in a potential with a radial force (such as a nucleus), relativistic effects lead to a coupling between spin and orbital angular momentum.

Derivation: Spin-Orbit Coupling Energy

In the rest frame of the electron orbiting a nucleus with charge \(Ze\), the nucleus appears to move, creating an effective magnetic field. This field couples to the electron’s magnetic moment:

\[ \boldsymbol{B}_\text{eff} = -\frac{1}{c^2m}(\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{v})\]

For a central potential \(V(r) = -\frac{Ze^2}{r}\), the electric field is \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla V\), so:

\[ \boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{v} = -\frac{1}{r}\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}r}(\hat{r} \times \boldsymbol{v})\]

The electron’s magnetic moment is \(\boldsymbol{\mu} = -\frac{g e}{2m c}\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) (with \(g \approx 2\) for the electron), so the energy is:

\[ H_{SO} = \boldsymbol{\mu} \cdot \boldsymbol{B}_\text{eff}\]

After accounting for Thomas precession (a relativistic geometric effect that introduces a factor of 1/2), one obtains:

\[ H_{SO} = \frac{1}{2m^2c^2r}\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}r}\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\]

For the Coulomb potential, \(\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}r} = \frac{Ze^2}{r^2}\), giving:

\[ H_{SO} = \frac{Ze^2}{2m^2c^2r^3}\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\]

The strength of spin-orbit coupling is parametrized as:

\[ H_{SO} = \xi(r)\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\]

where \(\xi(r) = \frac{1}{2m^2c^2r}\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}r}\) depends on the potential. For hydrogen-like atoms, \(\xi(r) \propto 1/r^3\).

Key Formula: Dot Product in Terms of Total Angular Momentum

The dot product \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) can be written in terms of the total angular momentum:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}^2 - \hat{L}^2 - \hat{S}^2)\]

Since \(\hat{J}^2\), \(\hat{L}^2\), and \(\hat{S}^2\) are diagonal in the \(|l, s, j, m_j\rangle\) basis, this formula greatly simplifies perturbation theory calculations.

For a given \(l\), \(s\), and \(j\):

\[ \langle \hat{L} \cdot \hat{S} \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}[j(j+1) - l(l+1) - s(s+1)]\]

Physical consequence: Spin-orbit coupling splits energy levels. For example, in hydrogen’s \(2p\) state:

  • Without coupling: \(2p\) is 3-fold degenerate (three \(m_l\) values with any spin)

  • With coupling: the state splits into \(2p_{3/2}\) (\(j=3/2\), 4-fold degenerate) and \(2p_{1/2}\) (\(j=1/2\), 2-fold degenerate)

This fine structure splitting was a key piece of evidence that spin-orbit coupling is real.

Discussion: Spin-Orbit Coupling and Energy Splitting

Spin-orbit coupling ∝ L·S mixes eigenstates of L_z and S_z.

Compute the energy splitting between j=3/2 and j=1/2 states for hydrogen 2p states.

How does this fine structure reveal that spin and orbital angular momentum are not independent degrees of freedom in the presence of a nucleus?

Representations of the Rotation Group#

A fundamental distinction between orbital and spin angular momentum emerges from group theory:

  • Orbital angular momentum generates integer representations of \(SO(3)\) (the rotation group in 3D)

  • Spin generates both integer and half-integer representations of \(SU(2)\) (the double cover of \(SO(3)\))

This distinction is topological. Under a rotation by \(2\pi\) around any axis:

  • A state with integer \(l\) returns to itself: \(|\psi\rangle \to |\psi\rangle\)

  • A state with half-integer \(s\) picks up a minus sign: \(|\chi\rangle \to -|\chi\rangle\)

This is observable only for half-integer spin; under two consecutive \(2\pi\) rotations, any state returns to itself. This is why interference experiments can distinguish half-integer from integer angular momentum.

  1. Angular momentum decomposition for a spinning coin

    A spinning coin with moment of inertia \(I_a\) about its symmetry axis and \(I_t\) about any transverse axis has total angular momentum \(\boldsymbol{L}\).

    (a) Write the kinetic energy in terms of \(L_\parallel = \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \hat{z}\) and the perpendicular component, where \(\hat{z}\) is the spin axis direction.

    (b) For a coin with \(I_a = 0.1 I_t\), describe qualitatively how the coin prefers to spin: is it more energetically favorable to spin about its symmetry axis or about an axis perpendicular to it?

  2. Equation of motion for spin axis on the sphere

    The spin axis \(\boldsymbol{n}\) evolves according to \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{n}}{dt} = \boldsymbol{n} \times \boldsymbol{\Omega}(\boldsymbol{n})\), where \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) is an effective angular velocity.

    (a) Show that this equation preserves the constraint \(|\boldsymbol{n}|^2 = 1\) (i.e., motion stays on the unit sphere).

    (b) If \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) is a constant vector, describe the geometry of trajectories: what shape does \(\boldsymbol{n}\) trace out on the sphere?

  3. Lorentz force analogy in spin dynamics

    A charged particle in a magnetic field obeys \(m\boldsymbol{a} = q(\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B})\). The spin axis satisfies \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{n}}{dt} = \boldsymbol{n} \times \boldsymbol{\Omega}\).

    (a) Identify the physical correspondence: what plays the role of charge, velocity, magnetic field?

    (b) A charged particle moving on a sphere (e.g., on Earth’s surface with constant angular velocity) traces a circle. By analogy, what does a spin axis do if \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) is constant?

  4. Larmor precession from first principles

    A magnetic dipole with moment \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) in a field \(\boldsymbol{B}\) experiences torque \(\boldsymbol{\tau} = \boldsymbol{\mu} \times \boldsymbol{B}\). For a spin, \(\boldsymbol{\mu} = -\gamma\boldsymbol{S}\) with gyromagnetic ratio \(\gamma = g/(2m)\).

    (a) Write the equation of motion for \(\boldsymbol{S}\) and identify the Larmor frequency \(\omega_L\).

    (b) For \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\), show that \(S_z\) is conserved while \(S_x\) and \(S_y\) rotate with angular frequency \(\omega_L\).

    (c) What is the Larmor frequency in rad/s for an electron in a magnetic field \(B = 1\) Tesla? (Use \(g = 2\), \(m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\) kg, \(e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\) C, SI units.)

  5. Magnetic energy and spin orientation

    The magnetic energy of a spin in a field is \(U = -\boldsymbol{\mu} \cdot \boldsymbol{B} = \gamma\boldsymbol{S} \cdot \boldsymbol{B}\).

    (a) For a spin-1/2 particle with \(|\boldsymbol{S}| = \hbar/2\), calculate the energy splitting between spin-up (aligned with \(\boldsymbol{B}\)) and spin-down (anti-aligned).

    (b) A spin initially anti-aligned with the field is placed in the field. Describe qualitatively what happens: does the spin remain anti-aligned, or does it dynamically respond?

    (c) In ESR spectroscopy, radio-frequency radiation at frequency \(\nu\) causes spin flips. What is the relationship between \(\nu\) and the magnetic field \(B\)?

  6. Thomas precession and spin-orbit coupling

    In the rest frame of a non-relativistic electron orbiting a nucleus, the nucleus appears to move, creating an effective magnetic field. This leads to Thomas precession—a modification of the naïve spin-orbit coupling by a factor of 1/2.

    (a) Explain qualitatively why a moving nucleus (in the electron’s rest frame) produces a magnetic field.

    (b) The naive spin-orbit coupling would give an energy \(-\boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{B}_\text{eff}\). Why does the factor of 1/2 from Thomas precession matter for the fine structure of hydrogen?

  7. Potential energy and effective field geometry

    Suppose the spin axis experiences a potential energy \(V(\boldsymbol{n}) = -\alpha n_z\), where \(n_z\) is the \(z\)-component of the unit vector \(\boldsymbol{n}\).

    (a) Compute the effective angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}(\boldsymbol{n}) = \frac{1}{I_t}(\nabla V \times \boldsymbol{n})\).

    (b) Describe the equilibrium orientations: where does the spin axis settle if released from rest?

    (c) Is motion near the equilibrium stable or unstable? Explain.

  8. Precession cone geometry

    A spin precesses around a magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) at the Larmor frequency. Suppose the spin is initially at angle \(\theta_0\) from the \(z\)-axis.

    (a) As the spin precesses, does the angle \(\theta\) from the \(z\)-axis change?

    (b) Describe the 3D trajectory: what surface does the spin vector trace out?

  9. Quantization of precession

    (b) If the spin starts in a superposition \(|\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|\uparrow\rangle + |\downarrow\rangle)\), how does it evolve in a magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\)?

    (c) What is the physical interpretation of Rabi oscillations in terms of this precession picture?

  10. Spin vs. orbital angular momentum in an atom

    An electron in an atom can have orbital angular momentum \(\boldsymbol{L}\) (from its orbit) and spin angular momentum \(\boldsymbol{S}\) (intrinsic).

    (a) Why is the classical picture of spin (as a tiny sphere spinning) incompatible with the fact that a spin-1/2 electron has only two possible outcomes when measured along any axis?

    (b) In fine-structure splitting, the term \((\boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S})\) couples orbital and spin angular momentum. Explain why this coupling should exist from a relativistic viewpoint (hint: Thomas precession).

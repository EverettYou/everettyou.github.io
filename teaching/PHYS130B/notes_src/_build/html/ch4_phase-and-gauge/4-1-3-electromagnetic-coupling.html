4.1.3 Electromagnetic Coupling#

Prompts

  • How does the Hamiltonian change when we couple a charged particle to an electromagnetic field? Derive the minimal coupling prescription from first principles.

  • What is the canonical momentum \(\boldsymbol{p} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\) versus kinetic momentum \(\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A}\)? Why do they differ in a magnetic field?

  • Derive the quantum Lorentz force from the Hamiltonian using the Heisenberg equation of motion. Does it match the classical formula?

  • Prove that expectation values of physical observables are gauge-invariant. Why can different gauge choices give the same physics?

  • What is the significance of the quantum kinetic momentum \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\)? How does it relate to particle velocity?

Lecture Notes#

Coupling a charged particle to an electromagnetic field is the next step in understanding gauge theory. We move from the abstract principle of gauge invariance to concrete physical consequences: how charges respond to fields, how energy and momentum are redefined, and how the kinetic momentum emerges as the physically relevant quantity.

Minimal Coupling Prescription#

From Classical to Quantum#

In classical mechanics, a charged particle in an electromagnetic field obeys the Lorentz force:

\[ m\frac{\mathrm{d}^2\boldsymbol{r}}{\mathrm{d}t^2} = q(\boldsymbol{E} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B})\]

The Hamiltonian formulation expresses this through the canonical momentum:

\[ H_{\text{cl}} = \frac{(\boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2}{2m} + q\Phi\]

where \(\boldsymbol{p}\) is the canonical momentum (different from \(m\boldsymbol{v}\) when fields are present), and \(\boldsymbol{A}\), \(\Phi\) are the vector and scalar potentials.

To quantize a charged particle in an electromagnetic field, we use the minimal coupling prescription:

Minimal Coupling Prescription

Replace the canonical momentum operator with:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \to \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}(\hat{\boldsymbol{r}})\]

The quantum Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 + q\Phi\]

This is equivalent to the gauge-covariant Schrödinger equation where ordinary derivatives are replaced by covariant derivatives \(D_i = \partial_i + \mathrm{i}qA_i\).

This prescription is not arbitrary—it is the unique choice that preserves gauge covariance of the Schrödinger equation.

Expansion of the Hamiltonian#

Expand the kinetic energy term:

\[ (\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2 - q(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}) + q^2\boldsymbol{A}^2\]

Since \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\):

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \nabla + \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A})\]

In the Coulomb gauge where \(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\):

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -2\mathrm{i}\hbar\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \nabla = 2\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A}\]

Hamiltonian in Coulomb Gauge

In the Coulomb gauge (\(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\)):

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2}{2m} - \frac{q}{m}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} + \frac{q^2}{2m}\boldsymbol{A}^2 + q\Phi\]

  • First term: kinetic energy (field-free)

  • Second term: linear coupling to magnetic field (diamagnetic coupling)

  • Third term: quadratic response to field (important only for strong fields)

  • Fourth term: Coulomb potential from scalar field

Canonical Momentum vs. Kinetic Momentum#

A crucial distinction in quantum mechanics with electromagnetic fields is between two types of momentum:

Canonical vs. Kinetic Momentum

The canonical momentum is:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\]

It satisfies the canonical commutation relation:

\[ [\hat{p}_i, \hat{x}_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\delta_{ij}\]

The kinetic momentum (or physical momentum) is:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\]

It determines the particle’s actual velocity and obeys:

\[ [\hat{\pi}_i, \hat{x}_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\delta_{ij} - q\partial_j A_i\]

The correction term depends on how the magnetic field varies in space.

Why the distinction? The canonical momentum commutes with the Hamiltonian (in certain gauges), making it a useful quantum number. But the kinetic momentum represents the actual velocity and appears in physical observables like current and energy flux.

Discussion

In an experiment, can we directly measure the canonical momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\) of a particle in an electromagnetic field? Why or why not? How does the existence of multiple gauge choices (Coulomb, Lorenz, etc.) reflect this? Which momentum—canonical or kinetic—determines observable quantities like the velocity distribution in a momentum eigenstate?

Lorentz Force from Quantum Mechanics#

Heisenberg Equation of Motion#

Use the Heisenberg equation to derive the force law from the Hamiltonian:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}, \hat{H}]\]

In the Coulomb gauge with time-dependent fields, the calculation yields:

\[ m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\boldsymbol{v}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = q\langle\boldsymbol{E}\rangle + q\langle\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}\rangle\]

Derivation: Quantum Lorentz Force

Start with:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}, \hat{H}] = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}, -\frac{q}{m}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} + q\Phi + \ldots]\]

The commutator \([\hat{p}_i, A_j\hat{p}_j]\) requires the product rule:

\[ [\hat{p}_i, A_j\hat{p}_j] = [\hat{p}_i, A_j]\hat{p}_j + A_j[\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j]\]

Since \([\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j] = 0\) and \([\hat{p}_i, A_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\partial_i A_j\):

\[ [\hat{p}_i, A_j\hat{p}_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(\partial_i A_j)\hat{p}_j\]

Similarly, \([\hat{p}_i, \Phi] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\partial_i\Phi\).

Collecting all terms:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = q\langle\nabla_{\boldsymbol{r}}\Phi\rangle - \frac{q}{m}\langle(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{r}} A_i)\hat{p}_i\rangle + q\frac{\partial\langle\boldsymbol{A}\rangle}{\partial t}\]

Using \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \partial_t\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) (which relates \((\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i)\) to \(\boldsymbol{B}\)):

\[ m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\boldsymbol{v}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = q\langle\boldsymbol{E}\rangle + q\langle\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}\rangle\]

This is the correspondence principle in action: expectation values of quantum observables obey classical equations of motion.

Discussion

The Coulomb gauge (\(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\)) is convenient for atomic physics calculations. However, in a region where \(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} \neq 0\), that gauge choice is not available. Does this mean different physical regimes require different gauges? Or should all physical predictions be independent of which gauge we choose?

Gauge Invariance of the Schrödinger Equation#

The Schrödinger equation with electromagnetic fields is manifestly gauge-covariant:

Gauge Covariance of the Schrödinger Equation

If \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\) satisfies:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H}[\boldsymbol{A}, \Phi]\psi\]

then \(\psi'(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)}\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\) satisfies:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{\partial\psi'}{\partial t} = \hat{H}[\boldsymbol{A}', \Phi']\psi'\]

where \(\boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi\) and \(\Phi' = \Phi + \frac{1}{q}\partial_t\chi\).

This means: different gauge choices yield wavefunctions that differ by a phase, but produce identical predictions for all physical observables.

Probability Current with Electromagnetic Field#

A fundamental consequence of gauge invariance is how probability current transforms. The continuity equation is:

\[ \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{J} = 0\]

where \(\rho = |\psi|^2\) is probability density. With an electromagnetic field, the probability current is:

Probability Current in Electromagnetic Field

\[ \boldsymbol{J} = \frac{1}{m}\text{Im}(\psi^*\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\psi) = \frac{1}{m}\text{Im}(\psi^*[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}]\psi)\]

The kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\) appears naturally in the current, not the canonical momentum. This makes physical sense: the current describes actual particle flow, which is determined by kinetic velocity \(\boldsymbol{v} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}/m\), not canonical velocity.

Summary#

  • The minimal coupling prescription \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \to \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) is the unique choice preserving gauge covariance.

  • The kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) represents actual particle momentum; canonical momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\) is a mathematical tool.

  • Expectation values obey the quantum Lorentz force, confirming the correspondence principle.

  • The Schrödinger equation is gauge-covariant: different gauges give phase-related wavefunctions with identical physics.

  • Probability current naturally involves kinetic momentum, reflecting the physical meaning of particle flow in electromagnetic fields.

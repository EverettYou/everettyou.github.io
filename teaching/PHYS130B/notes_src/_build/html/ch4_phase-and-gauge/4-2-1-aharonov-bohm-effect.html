4.2.1 Aharonov-Bohm Effect#

Prompts

  • Describe the Aharonov-Bohm experimental setup. Why is there a phase shift despite zero magnetic field outside the solenoid?

  • Derive the vector potential for a solenoid with internal field \(B\) and radius \(R\). Show that \(\oint \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l} = \Phi_B\).

  • Compute the phase difference between paths above and below the solenoid in a double-slit setup. How does this shift the interference pattern?

  • Why is the Aharonov-Bohm effect a demonstration of the non-locality of quantum mechanics? What makes it fundamentally different from classical effects?

  • How would you design an experiment using electron holograms to measure the Aharonov-Bohm phase shift? What precision is required?

Lecture Notes#

The Aharonov-Bohm (AB) effect is one of the most celebrated phenomena in quantum mechanics. It demonstrates that the quantum phase of a particle can be affected by a vector potential even in regions where the electromagnetic field (the field strength tensor) vanishes completely. This effect reveals the fundamental importance of gauge fields in quantum mechanics and hints at deep geometric structure underlying quantum theory.

The Aharonov-Bohm Experimental Setup#

Physical Configuration#

Imagine a solenoid (a coil wound tightly on a cylinder) producing a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B}\) inside the solenoid but approximately zero outside. An electron beam is split into two paths that encircle the solenoid—one path passing above it and one passing below. The two beams then recombine at a distant observation screen.

In classical mechanics, since the electron experiences no Lorentz force (\(\boldsymbol{F} = q\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}\)) in the field-free region outside the solenoid, both paths would follow identical trajectories. The interference pattern at the screen should show no dependence on the solenoid’s magnetic field.

In quantum mechanics, something startling happens:

Aharonov-Bohm Phase Shift

Even though the magnetic field is zero along the electron’s path, the quantum phase of the electron wavefunction changes depending on the solenoid’s flux. This phase shift shifts the interference pattern—a purely quantum effect with no classical analog.

The phase difference between the upper and lower paths is:

\[ \Delta\phi = \frac{q\Phi_B}{\hbar c}\]

where \(\Phi_B = \int \boldsymbol{B} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}\) is the total magnetic flux through the solenoid (not experienced by the particle!).

Vector Potential in a Solenoid#

To understand the AB effect, we must think in terms of the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\), not the field strength \(\boldsymbol{B}\).

Outside the Solenoid#

For a solenoid of radius \(R\) with uniform internal field \(B\) along the \(z\)-axis, the vector potential outside (in cylindrical coordinates) is:

\[ \boldsymbol{A}(r > R) = \frac{\Phi_B}{2\pi r}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\]

where \(\Phi_B = \pi R^2 B\) is the total flux. Note:

  • \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is nonzero outside the solenoid

  • The magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A} = 0\) outside (the curl of \(\boldsymbol{A}\) vanishes)

Circulation Integral#

The key property is that the vector potential’s circulation around any closed loop encircling the solenoid equals the enclosed flux:

\[ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l} = \Phi_B\]

This result holds for any closed path enclosing the solenoid, regardless of its shape. It is a topological fact—a consequence of Stokes’ theorem:

\[ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l} = \int_{\text{surface}} (\nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \int_{\text{surface}} \boldsymbol{B} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \Phi_B\]

Discussion

The vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is nonzero outside the solenoid, but the magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A} = 0\). In classical mechanics, only \(\boldsymbol{B}\) couples to a moving charge. How can the nonzero \(\boldsymbol{A}\) produce a physical effect (phase shift) if no classical force acts? Does this mean quantum mechanics sees something in the vector potential that classical mechanics misses?

Phase Accumulation Along a Path#

The Phase Integral#

When a charged particle travels along a path \(\mathcal{C}\) through a region with vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\), the quantum phase acquired is:

\[ \phi[\mathcal{C}] = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\int_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\]

This arises from the covariant derivative in the Schrödinger equation: as the wavefunction propagates, the local vector potential “rotates” its phase continuously.

Two-Path Interference#

Consider two paths: the upper path (encircling the solenoid from above) and the lower path (from below). Both start and end at the same points, but their relative phase depends on the solenoid’s flux.

The phase difference is:

\[ \Delta\phi = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\left(\int_{\text{upper}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l} - \int_{\text{lower}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\right) = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\oint \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l} = \frac{q\Phi_B}{\hbar c}\]

The two wave packets acquire a relative phase that manifests as a shift in the interference pattern.

Gauge Invariance of the Aharonov-Bohm Effect#

A crucial check: is the AB effect gauge-invariant?

Gauge Invariance of AB Phase

Under a gauge transformation \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi\) and \(\psi \to \psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi}\psi\):

The accumulated phase changes as:

\[ \phi'[\mathcal{C}] = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\int_{\mathcal{C}} (\boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l} = \phi[\mathcal{C}] - \frac{q}{\hbar c}[\chi(\text{end}) - \chi(\text{start})]\]

But the wavefunction also acquires an overall phase factor \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q(\chi_{\text{end}} - \chi_{\text{start}})}\). These precisely cancel when we compare relative phases of two paths with the same endpoints:

\[ \Delta\phi' = \phi'_1 - \phi'_2 = (\phi_1 - \Delta\chi_1) - (\phi_2 - \Delta\chi_2) = \phi_1 - \phi_2 = \Delta\phi\]

The relative phase difference between two interfering beams is gauge-invariant.

Discussion

We showed that the relative phase difference \(\Delta\phi' = \Delta\phi\) is gauge-invariant. But the individual phases \(\phi_1'\) and \(\phi_2'\) on each path do change under a gauge transformation. How can we experimentally measure the relative phase in a way that reveals the gauge-invariant quantity, not the gauge-dependent individual phases?

Why This Matters: The Non-Classical Nature of the Vector Potential#

The Aharonov-Bohm effect reveals something profound:

The Vector Potential is Physical in Quantum Mechanics

In classical electromagnetism, the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is regarded as a mathematical convenience—only the field strengths \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\) have direct physical meaning.

In quantum mechanics, \(\boldsymbol{A}\) appears explicitly in the Schrödinger equation via the covariant derivative. The AB effect demonstrates that \(\boldsymbol{A}\) produces observable consequences even when \(\boldsymbol{B} = 0\) everywhere on the particle’s path.

This is not a contradiction: \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is gauge-dependent (unobservable in isolation), but the circulation \(\oint \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\) is gauge-invariant (observable). Only gauge-invariant combinations appear in interference patterns.

Topological Nature and Flux Quantization#

The AB effect has a topological character: it depends only on the total flux enclosed, not on the detailed field configuration.

Topology: Shape-Independence

Imagine deforming the two paths continuously without breaking them. As long as they remain separated and encircle the solenoid, the phase difference \(\Delta\phi = q\Phi_B/(\hbar c)\) remains unchanged. This is a topological property: it is invariant under continuous deformation.

Only discontinuous changes (like one path “jumping over” the solenoid) would change the phase.

This topological structure connects to flux quantization in superconductivity: when a superconductor forms a ring, quantum interference enforces that the flux through the ring is quantized in units of the flux quantum:

\[ \Phi_q = \frac{\pi \hbar c}{e} \approx 4.1 \times 10^{-15} \text{ Wb}\]

When the enclosed flux is exactly \(\Phi_q\), the phase shift is \(2\pi\), returning the electron wavefunction to itself with a complete cycle.

Summary#

  • The Aharonov-Bohm effect demonstrates that quantum phases depend on the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\), even in regions where the field strength (magnetic field) is zero.

  • The phase shift around a closed path equals \(q\Phi_B/(\hbar c)\), where \(\Phi_B\) is the enclosed flux.

  • The effect is gauge-invariant: relative phases between interfering beams are independent of gauge choice.

  • The AB effect reveals that the vector potential has genuine physical significance in quantum mechanics, not merely mathematical convenience as in classical theory.

  • The topological nature of the AB effect connects to flux quantization and appears in superconductivity, quantum Hall effect, and modern topological materials.

Problem 1: Vector Potential Circulation

A solenoid of radius \(R = 1\) cm carries a uniform internal magnetic field \(B = 0.1\) T along the \(z\)-axis. Outside the solenoid, \(\boldsymbol{B} = 0\).

(a) Compute the total magnetic flux \(\Phi_B\) through the solenoid.

(b) Using the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}_{\text{out}} = \frac{BR^2}{2r}\hat{\theta}\), verify by direct integration that \(\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l} = \Phi_B\) for a circular path \(C\) of radius \(r = 2R\) encircling the solenoid.

(c) Show that this result holds for any closed path encircling the solenoid, not just circles. (Hint: Use Stokes’ theorem.)

Problem 2: Gauge Freedom and Circulation

The vector potential is not unique; adding \(\boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\chi\) for any scalar function \(\chi\) gives the same magnetic field.

(a) Show that a different gauge choice \(\boldsymbol{A}'\) gives \(\oint_C \boldsymbol{A}' \cdot d\boldsymbol{l} = \oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\) for any closed path.

(b) Explain why this is consistent with the fact that the Aharonov-Bohm phase shift is physical (gauge-invariant) even though individual vector potentials are gauge-dependent.

Problem 3: Aharonov-Bohm Phase (Quantitative)

An electron (charge \(-e\), mass \(m\)) travels around a solenoid of flux \(\Phi_B = 10^{-15}\) Wb.

(a) Compute the Aharonov-Bohm phase shift \(\phi_{\text{AB}} = \frac{e\Phi_B}{\hbar c}\) (use SI units).

(b) If the electron de Broglie wavelength is \(\lambda = 0.1\) nm, what is the phase shift in terms of wavelengths crossed? (Relate phase to path length divided by wavelength.)

(c) Would this phase shift be observable (i.e., shift an interference pattern by a detectable amount)? Justify your answer.

Problem 4: Double-Slit Interference Pattern Shift

Two electrons pass through two slits positioned symmetrically above and below an infinitesimally thin solenoid carrying flux \(\Phi_B\). They recombine at a distant screen.

(a) Explain why the upper path acquires phase \(+\phi_{\text{AB}}/2\) and the lower path acquires phase \(-\phi_{\text{AB}}/2\) relative to a reference (not including geometric phase from propagation distance).

(b) Write the two-slit intensity formula \(I(x) = |\psi_1(x) + \psi_2(x)|^2\) and show that the phase difference between the two amplitudes is \(\Delta\phi = e\Phi_B/(\hbar c)\).

(c) By how many fringe spacings does the interference pattern shift if the solenoid current (and thus \(\Phi_B\)) is increased by \(\Delta\Phi_B = 2\pi\hbar c/e\)? (This quantum is called the flux quantum in superconductivity.)

  • Path C: A highly irregular, loopy curve

(a) If a charged particle travels along each path, what are the respective phase shifts?

(b) Why does the phase shift depend only on the enclosed flux and not on the detailed shape of the path? (Hint: Use Stokes’ theorem and think about what property of the paths is actually relevant.)

(c) How would the result change if the solenoid were replaced by a solenoid with a non-uniform internal field? Would the phase shift still depend only on total flux, or on the detailed distribution?

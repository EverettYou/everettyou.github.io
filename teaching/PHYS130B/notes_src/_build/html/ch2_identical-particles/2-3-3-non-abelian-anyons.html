2.3.3 Non-Abelian Anyons#

Prompts

  • What distinguishes non-abelian from abelian anyons?

  • Explain fusion rules and their role in anyonic systems.

  • What are braiding matrices? How do they encode non-abelian operations?

  • Describe F-symbols (6j symbols) and their significance.

  • How could non-abelian anyons be used for quantum computation? What is topological protection?

  • What are Fibonacci anyons? Where might they be found physically?

Lecture Notes#

Non-abelian anyons represent a deeper layer of topological order, where particle exchange operations do not commute and can perform quantum computations. Their potential for fault-tolerant quantum computing has made them targets of intense experimental effort, offering a path to quantum information protected by topology itself.

What Makes Them Non-Abelian?#

Non-abelian anyons are anyonic quasiparticles whose braiding operations don’t simply multiply the state by a phase factor. Instead, braiding can mix different quantum states.

More precisely: when two non-abelian anyons exchange, the operation is represented by a unitary matrix acting on the Hilbert space of the system, not just a phase factor.

\[\text{Abelian}: \quad R |\psi\rangle = e^{i\phi} |\psi\rangle\]
\[\text{Non-abelian}: \quad R |\psi_1\rangle = U_{11} |\psi_1\rangle + U_{12} |\psi_2\rangle + \ldots\]

where \(U\) is a unitary matrix with multiple non-zero entries.

Why “Non-Abelian”?#

The braiding operations form a representation of the braid group \(B_N\). For abelian anyons, this representation is 1-dimensional (just phases). For non-abelian anyons, the representation is higher-dimensional.

The composition of braiding operations doesn’t commute: \(R_1 R_2 \neq R_2 R_1\) in general. This non-commutativity is what makes the algebra “non-abelian.”

Topological quantum computation

Non-abelian anyons are the basis for topological quantum computation: quantum gates are implemented by braiding anyons, and the topological nature of braids makes the computation inherently fault-tolerant against local perturbations.

Important

Non-Abelian Braid Group

Non-abelian anyons obey non-abelian braid relations: BᵢBⱼ ≠ BⱼBᵢ in general. The braiding of multiple anyons can create unitary transformations in a protected subspace, enabling topological quantum computation.

Note

Topological Qubits

Non-abelian anyons can encode quantum information in their collective braiding patterns. Crucially, this encoding is topological: small perturbations do not corrupt the information, offering built-in error protection.

Caution

Majorana Fermions

Proposals for creating non-abelian anyons (e.g., Majorana zero modes in topological superconductors) remain experimentally challenging. Definitive signatures require demonstrating non-abelian braiding phases.

Fusion Rules#

Basic Concept#

In a system with anyons, two anyons can fuse (come close together and combine) into a single anyon of a different type. The fusion rule specifies which fusion channels are allowed:

(129)#\[a \times b = \sum_c N_{ab}^c \, c\]

where:

  • \(a, b\) are anyon types (can be the same).

  • \(c\) ranges over all possible fusion outcomes.

  • \(N_{ab}^c \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) is the fusion multiplicity: how many independent ways \(a\) and \(b\) can fuse into \(c\).

Example: Fibonacci Anyons

Fibonacci anyons are the simplest non-abelian anyons. There are two types: the identity \(I\) (vacuum) and the non-trivial anyon \(\tau\).

Fusion rules:

\[I \times I = I\]
\[I \times \tau = \tau\]
(130)#\[\tau \times \tau = I \oplus \tau\]

The last rule is the key: two \(\tau\) anyons can fuse into either \(I\) (vacuum) or \(\tau\). There are two channels.

Multiplicity and Degeneracy#

When \(N_{ab}^c > 1\), there are multiple independent ways to fuse \(a\) and \(b\) into \(c\). Each way corresponds to a different quantum state.

For Fibonacci anyons at \(\tau \times \tau = I \oplus \tau\):

  • One way fuses to \(I\).

  • One way fuses to \(\tau\).

These are orthogonal outcomes.

Modular Tensor Categories and TQFT#

The Mathematical Framework#

The complete description of anyonic systems is captured by topological quantum field theory (TQFT), or equivalently, modular tensor categories (MTCs).

The Key Data: F-Symbols#

Beyond fusion rules, we need to specify how to rearrange fusion trees. This is encoded in the F-symbols (or 6-j symbols):

\[F^{d}_{abc}\]

These are unitary matrices that relate different ways of fusing three anyons \(a, b, c\) into \(d\).

\[\text{(fusion order 1:} (a \times b) \times c\text{)} \leftrightarrow \text{(fusion order 2:} a \times (b \times c)\text{)}\]

The R-Matrices: Braiding#

The R-matrices (braiding matrices) specify how to exchange two anyons:

\[R^c_{ab}\]

These are unitary matrices acting on fusion channels. When two anyons \(a\) and \(b\) exchange, the state in different fusion channels gets mixed by \(R^c_{ab}\).

Consistency: Yang-Baxter Equation#

The F-symbols and R-matrices must satisfy consistency equations, most notably the Yang-Baxter equation:

\[R_{12} R_{23} R_{12} = R_{23} R_{12} R_{23}\]

This ensures that different ways of braiding multiple anyons give the same result.

Example: Fibonacci Anyons in Detail

Fusion Rules (Recap)

\[\tau \times \tau = I \oplus \tau\]

Two \(\tau\) anyons can fuse to \(I\) or \(\tau\)—two orthogonal fusion channels.

Quantum Dimension

The quantum dimension of the \(\tau\) anyon is determined by:

\[d_\tau^2 = \sum_c N_{\tau\tau}^c d_c^2\]

With \(N_{\tau\tau}^I = N_{\tau\tau}^\tau = 1\) and \(d_I = 1\):

\[d_\tau^2 = 1 + d_\tau^2\]

Wait—this is circular! The resolution: Fibonacci anyons satisfy

\[d_\tau = \phi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618 \quad \text{(golden ratio!)}\]

Indeed: \(\phi^2 = \phi + 1\), so \(\phi^2 = 1 + \phi^2\) in the above (modulo subtlety with the \(d_I\) term).

R-Matrix

The braiding of two \(\tau\) anyons in their \(\tau \times \tau \to \tau\) channel gives phase:

\[R^\tau_{\tau\tau} = e^{4\pi i/5}\]

This is a rotation by \(144°\).

Why Fibonacci?

The name “Fibonacci” comes from the fact that the quantum dimensions follow Fibonacci numbers in a generalized sense. The golden ratio naturally appears in their structure.

Topological Quantum Computing#

Qubits from Anyons#

A topological qubit can be encoded in the fusion channel of two non-abelian anyons:

\[|0\rangle_\text{topo} = |\text{anyon pair fuses to } I\rangle\]
\[|1\rangle_\text{topo} = |\text{anyon pair fuses to } a \neq I\rangle\]

The qubit is topological: encoded in a non-local property (fusion outcome) rather than a local quantum state.

Quantum Gates via Braiding#

Quantum gates are implemented by braiding anyons:

  1. Braiding two anyons around each other applies a unitary transformation (the R-matrix) to the qubit.

  2. Different braiding paths give different gates. The gates are determined purely by topology, independent of precise trajectories.

  3. Example: For Fibonacci anyons, certain braiding sequences implement universal quantum gates (enough to do any computation).

Topological Protection#

Error correction by topology: Since the qubit is encoded in a topological property (fusion channel), local errors cannot change it.

  • A qubit in the “fuses to \(I\)” state remains in that state as long as the two anyons are well-separated.

  • To flip the qubit, an anyon would need to pass through infinity (a topologically nontrivial operation).

  • Errors from local noise are exponentially suppressed (decay as \(e^{-d/\xi}\) where \(d\) is anyon separation and \(\xi\) is a correlation length).

Advantage: Quantum information is automatically protected without needing active error correction codes (though passive protection requires sufficient topological gap and separation).

Universality and Computational Power#

Universal Gate Sets#

Not all anyon types are “universal” for quantum computation. Universal anyons allow us to implement an arbitrary quantum gate (up to approximation).

Fibonacci anyons are universal: Certain sequences of braiding can approximate any unitary gate with arbitrary precision.

Abelian anyons are not universal: Braiding of abelian anyons only produces abelian gates (diagonal in some basis), not full unitary operations.

Computational Complexity#

With non-abelian anyons:

  • The Hilbert space dimension grows as \(d^n\) where \(d > 1\) is the quantum dimension.

  • For Fibonacci anyons: \(d = \phi \approx 1.618\), so \(n\) qubits have \(\approx 1.618^n\) states.

  • This exponential scaling, combined with topological protection, offers computational advantage.

Challenges#

  • Creating and isolating anyons: Requires extreme conditions (low temperature, strong fields).

  • Manipulating them: Braiding must be done slowly (adiabatically) to avoid exciting unwanted states.

  • Reading out results: Measuring topological charge is non-trivial.

  • Proof of concept: No unambiguous experimental observation of anyons yet (though strong evidence in FQH systems).

Physical Realizations of Non-Abelian Anyons#

Fractional Quantum Hall at \(\nu = 5/2\)#

The FQHE at filling factor \(\nu = 5/2\) is believed to host non-abelian Moore-Read state:

  • Quasihole excitations are Fibonacci anyons (or similar).

  • Evidence from quasiparticle interference experiments and numerical simulations.

  • Still no definitive experimental confirmation, but strong indicators.

Topological Insulators / Superconductors#

Topological superconductors with appropriate symmetries host non-abelian anyons:

  • Majorana fermions (a special non-abelian anyonic mode) emerge at vortex cores.

  • Platforms: p-wave superconductors, iron pnictides, proximity-induced on topological insulators.

  • Braiding of Majorana modes implements quantum gates.

Quantum Spin Liquids#

Certain quantum spin liquids (exotic spin systems with no magnetic order) have anyonic excitations.

Engineered Cold Atoms#

Using synthetic gauge fields in ultra-cold atomic systems, one can create analog environments where non-abelian anyons might exist.

Topological Codes#

Toric code and related spin models defined on 2D lattices have non-abelian generalizations with anyonic excitations that can be simulated in quantum computers.

Braiding Matrices and Computation#

Encoding Information in Fusion Channels#

Consider \(n\) Fibonacci \(\tau\) anyons. They can fuse in pairs:
\((\tau_1 \times \tau_2) \to c_{12}\), then \((c_{12} \times \tau_3) \to c_{123}\), and so on.

At each step, there are two fusion channels (to \(I\) or \(\tau\)). With \(n\) anyons, there are \(F_{n-2}\) different fusion outcomes, where \(F_n\) is the \(n\)-th Fibonacci number.

Quantum Dimension of Multi-Particle Spaces#

  • \(n\) Fibonacci \(\tau\) anyons: dimension \(\sim F_{n-2} \times (d_\tau)^{n-1} = F_{n-2} \times \phi^{n-1}\).

  • This grows as \(\phi^{n-1}\), much faster than the linear Hilbert space dimension per qubit.

Gate Implementation#

A quantum gate is a sequence of braiding moves:

  1. Label which pairs to braid and in what order.

  2. The resulting unitary \(U = R_{i_1} R_{i_2} \cdots R_{i_k}\) (composition of R-matrices) performs the gate.

  3. Topological invariance: small deviations in trajectories don’t change the gate (only the final topology matters).

Research Frontiers#

Observing Non-Abelian Statistics#

Direct experimental evidence for non-abelian anyons remains elusive:

  • Charge measurements (via shot noise) in FQHE are consistent with fractional charge.

  • Shot noise also provides evidence for fractional statistics (abelian).

  • Non-abelian braiding statistics are harder to measure directly.

Majorana Zero Modes#

Recent experimental programs focus on Majorana fermions in topological superconductors:

  • Claims of observation in nanowires, but interpretation debated.

  • If confirmed, braiding experiments could test non-abelian statistics.

Digital Simulation#

A near-term approach: simulate anyonic systems on quantum computers and test braiding rules computationally.

Materials Discovery#

Search for natural or engineered materials with non-abelian anyonic excitations (topological superconductors, spin liquids, etc.).

Summary#

  • Non-abelian anyons: Braiding mixes different quantum states (R-matrices are non-diagonal).

  • Fusion rules: Specify allowed fusion outcomes and multiplicities; form algebraic structure.

  • F-symbols: Encode rearrangement of fusion trees; must satisfy Yang-Baxter equation.

  • Fibonacci anyons: Quantum dimension \(\phi\) (golden ratio); universal for quantum computation.

  • Topological qubits: Encoded in fusion channels; protected from local decoherence by topology.

  • Quantum gates: Implemented via braiding; topologically protected and fault-tolerant by design.

  • Physical systems: FQHE at \(\nu = 5/2\), topological superconductors, spin liquids (candidate platforms).

  • Challenge: Creating, isolating, and manipulating anyons experimentally remains open.

  • Promise: Fault-tolerant quantum computation without active error correction.

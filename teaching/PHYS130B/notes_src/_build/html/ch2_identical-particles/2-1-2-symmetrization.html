2.1.2 Symmetrization#

Prompts

  • What does it mean for particles to be identical in quantum mechanics? Why is indistinguishability a fundamental principle, not a practical limitation?

  • Why does indistinguishability constrain the wavefunction to be either symmetric or antisymmetric? Why are these the only two options?

  • How do you construct a properly symmetrized (bosonic) or antisymmetrized (fermionic) state from single-particle wavefunctions?

  • What are the insertion and deletion rules? How do they define the procedure for adding or removing a particle while preserving symmetry?

  • State the Pauli exclusion principle. How does it follow from antisymmetry?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Particles of the same type (electrons, photons, etc.) are absolutely identical in quantum mechanics—they carry no identity tags and are completely interchangeable. This indistinguishability is not a practical limitation but a fundamental principle that restricts which quantum states are physical: multi-particle states must be either symmetric (bosons) or antisymmetric (fermions) under particle exchange.

Analogy. Money in a bank account is identical. You cannot ask “Is this the dollar I deposited today or yesterday?”—dollars are indistinguishable. Identical particles are the same: two electrons have no labels. This simple observation has profound consequences for the structure of matter.

Identical Particles#

Identical Particles

Particles of the same type have identical mass, charge, spin, and no inherent labels. This indistinguishability is a fundamental principle that constrains which quantum states are physical.

Physical requirement. Let \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) be the permutation operator that swaps particles \(i\) and \(j\). For identical particles, all observables must be invariant under permutation: \(\langle\Psi\vert \hat{O}\vert\Psi\rangle = \langle\Psi\vert \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^\dagger \hat{O}\,\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle\) for any observable \(\hat{O}\). This requires the state to be an eigenstate of every transposition:

\[\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = \lambda\,\vert\Psi\rangle\]

Since \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^2 = \hat{I}\), the eigenvalue satisfies \(\lambda^2 = 1\), so \(\lambda = \pm 1\).

Symmetry Constraint

Under particle exchange, the many-body state must satisfy:

  • Symmetric (\(\lambda = +1\), bosons): \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = +\vert\Psi\rangle\)

  • Antisymmetric (\(\lambda = -1\), fermions): \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = -\vert\Psi\rangle\)

The choice is determined by particle spin (integer → boson, half-integer → fermion) via the spin-statistics theorem.

Discussion: Indistinguishability and Entanglement

A symmetrized or antisymmetrized state is always an entangled superposition—you cannot assign a definite single-particle state to a specific particle. Is this “entanglement” the same as in a Bell pair, or is it fundamentally different? Can you design an experiment that distinguishes indistinguishability-entanglement from preparation-entanglement?

Symmetric and Antisymmetric States#

Let \(\{\vert\alpha\rangle\}\) be an orthonormal basis for the single-particle Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). A generic \(N\)-particle state in \(\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}\) is

(34)#\[\vert\Psi\rangle = \sum_{\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N} \Psi_{\alpha_1 \cdots \alpha_N}\;\vert\alpha_1\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_N\rangle\]

where \(\Psi_{\alpha_1 \cdots \alpha_N}\) is the many-body amplitude. For identical particles, only the symmetric or antisymmetric subspace of \(\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}\) is physical.

Bosonic (Symmetric) States#

For \(N\) bosons placed in distinct single-particle states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_N\rangle\), the symmetrized state is:

(35)#\[\vert\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N\rangle_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(2)}\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle\]

The sum (all signs positive) is the ket-space analog of the permanent. Multiple bosons can occupy the same state.

Example: Two Bosons

Problem. Construct the symmetric state for two bosons in orthogonal states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \vert\alpha_2\rangle\).

Solution.

\[\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \bigl(\vert\alpha_1\rangle\vert\alpha_2\rangle + \vert\alpha_2\rangle\vert\alpha_1\rangle\bigr)\]

If both bosons occupy the same state \(\vert\alpha\rangle\): \(\vert\alpha, \alpha\rangle_+ = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\) (already symmetric).

Normalization with Repeated States

More generally, when \(n_\alpha\) bosons share the same single-particle state, the permanent acquires \(n_\alpha !\) identical terms, which should be further normalized by \(\sqrt{n_\alpha !}\). So for \(N\) bosons with occupation numbers \(\{n_\alpha\}\), the normalization factor is:

\[\mathcal{N}_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N! \prod_\alpha n_\alpha!}}\]

Fermionic (Antisymmetric) States#

For \(N\) fermions placed in distinct single-particle states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_N\rangle\), the antisymmetrized state is:

(36)#\[\vert\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \mathrm{sgn}(\pi)\;\vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(2)}\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle\]

This is the ket-space analog of the Slater determinant. Antisymmetry is automatic: swapping any two particle labels flips the sign. If \(\vert\alpha_i\rangle = \vert\alpha_j\rangle\) for \(i \neq j\), the state vanishes—this is the Pauli exclusion principle.

Example: Two Fermions

Problem. Construct the antisymmetric state for two fermions in states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \vert\alpha_2\rangle\).

Solution.

\[\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \bigl(\vert\alpha_1\rangle\vert\alpha_2\rangle - \vert\alpha_2\rangle\vert\alpha_1\rangle\bigr)\]

Check: Swap particle labels gives \(-\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_-\). Set \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle = \vert\alpha_2\rangle\) gives zero.

Pauli Exclusion Principle#

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two identical fermions can occupy the same single-particle quantum state. The fermionic occupation number satisfies \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1\}\).

(Bosons have no such restriction: \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\))

This follows directly from antisymmetry: if \(\vert\alpha_i\rangle = \vert\alpha_j\rangle\), the antisymmetrized state has two identical columns in the Slater determinant and vanishes. The consequences are far-reaching: atomic shell structure, chemical bonding, degeneracy pressure in white dwarfs and neutron stars, and the stability of matter itself.

Insertion and Deletion Rules#

To add or remove a particle while preserving symmetry, we define insertion (\(\rhd_\pm\)) and deletion (\(\lhd_\pm\)) operations on first-quantized states. Let \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) be a single-particle state, let \(\mathbb{1}\) be the tensor identity (generator of the zero-particle space \(\mathbb{C}\), satisfying \(\vert\alpha\rangle \equiv \mathbb{1} \otimes \vert\alpha\rangle \equiv \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes \mathbb{1}\)), and let \(\vert\Psi\rangle = \vert\alpha_1\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_2\rangle \otimes \cdots\) be a generic tensor product state. The subscript \(\pm\) denotes symmetrization (+, bosons) or antisymmetrization (−, fermions).

Insertion and Deletion Rules

Insertion \(\rhd_\pm\) (adds a particle symmetrically/antisymmetrically):

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm \mathbb{1} = \vert\alpha\rangle, \qquad \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle) = \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes \vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm \vert\Psi\rangle)\]

Deletion \(\lhd_\pm\) (removes a particle symmetrically/antisymmetrically):

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm \mathbb{1} = 0, \qquad \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle) = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\,\vert\Psi\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm \vert\Psi\rangle)\]

Both operations are linear in the state being acted on.

The recursive structure means insertion places \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) at every possible position with alternating signs (for fermions) or all-positive signs (for bosons). Deletion removes \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) from every position where it appears, with the same sign pattern.

Example: Insertion into a Two-Particle State

Problem. Compute \(\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\gamma\rangle)\).

Solution. Apply the recursive relation twice:

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\gamma\rangle) = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm \vert\gamma\rangle)\]

For the inner insertion, use \(\vert\gamma\rangle = \vert\gamma\rangle \otimes \mathbb{1}\):

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm \vert\gamma\rangle = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle \pm \vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\]

So:

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle) = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\]

(dropping \(\otimes\) for brevity). For bosons (+): sum of all 3 cyclic insertions. For fermions (−): alternating signs \(+1, -1, +1\).

Boson Creation and Annihilation Operators#

The boson creation operator \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha\) and annihilation operator \(\hat{b}_\alpha\) are defined by normalizing the insertion and deletion operations acting on an \(N\)-particle state \(\vert\Psi\rangle\):

Definition: Boson Creation and Annihilation

(37)#\[\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N+1}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_+ \vert\Psi\rangle, \qquad \hat{b}_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_+ \vert\Psi\rangle\]

The factor \(1/\sqrt{N+1}\) (resp. \(1/\sqrt{N}\)) normalizes the \((N{+}1)\)-particle (resp. \((N{-}1)\)-particle) state produced by symmetric insertion (resp. deletion).

Example: \(\hat{b}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \sqrt{2}\,\vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle\)

Problem. Create one more boson in state \(\vert 1\rangle\), starting from \(\vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = (\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\).

Solution. Apply the definition \(\hat{b}^\dagger_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+\) (since \(N=2\), so \(N+1=3\)):

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle \right)\]

Compute each insertion using the recursive rule:

\[\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 2\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]
\[= 2\,\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]
\[\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \rhd_+ \vert 1\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]
\[= \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + 2\,\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]

Sum:

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}} (\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle)\]

Recognizing \(\vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} (\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle)\):

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{6}}\;\vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle = \sqrt{2}\;\vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle\]

This confirms \(\hat{b}^\dagger_1 \vert n_1, n_2\rangle = \sqrt{n_1+1}\;\vert n_1+1, n_2\rangle\) with \(n_1 = 1\).

Example: \(\hat{b}_1 \vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle = \sqrt{2}\,\vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle\)

Problem. Annihilate a boson from state \(\vert 1\rangle\), starting from \(\vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle)\).

Solution. Apply the definition \(\hat{b}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+\) (since \(N=3\)):

\[\hat{b}_1 \vert 2_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{3}\bigl(\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\bigr)\]

Compute each deletion using \(\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_+ (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle) = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\vert\Psi\rangle + \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_+ \vert\Psi\rangle)\):

\[\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle(\vert 2\rangle + 0) = 2\,\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\]
\[\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 1\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle) = \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 1\rangle(0 + \vert 2\rangle) = \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\]
\[\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle = 0 + \vert 2\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \lhd_+ \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle) = \vert 2\rangle(\vert 1\rangle + \vert 1\rangle) = 2\,\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]

Sum: \(\frac{1}{3}(3\,\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + 3\,\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \sqrt{2}\,\vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle\).

Action on single-mode Fock states. Starting from the vacuum \(\vert 0_\alpha\rangle = \mathbb{1}\) and applying \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha\) repeatedly, one can derive the action on any Fock state:

Boson Operators on Fock States

(38)#\[\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \sqrt{n_\alpha + 1}\;\vert n_\alpha + 1\rangle, \qquad \hat{b}_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \sqrt{n_\alpha}\;\vert n_\alpha - 1\rangle, \qquad \hat{b}_\alpha \vert 0\rangle = 0\]

Derivation: Action on Single-Mode Fock States

The single-mode Fock state with \(n\) bosons is \(\vert n_\alpha\rangle = \vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes n} / \sqrt{n!}\) (after normalization of the permanent). From the definition:

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}} \;\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_+ \frac{\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes n}}{\sqrt{n!}}\]

Since all \(n\) particles are in the same state \(\vert\alpha\rangle\), the symmetric insertion \(\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_+ \vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes n}\) produces \((n+1)\) identical copies of \(\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n+1)}\) (one for each insertion position, all with \(+\) sign). So:

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_+ \vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes n} = (n+1)\;\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n+1)}\]

Therefore:

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \frac{(n+1)}{\sqrt{(n+1)\cdot n!}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n+1)} = \sqrt{n+1}\;\frac{\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n+1)}}{\sqrt{(n+1)!}} = \sqrt{n+1}\;\vert n+1\rangle\]

Similarly for annihilation, the symmetric deletion \(\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_+ \vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes n}\) removes \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) from each of the \(n\) positions (all giving \(+\) overlap), producing \(n\;\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n-1)}\):

\[\hat{b}_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \frac{n}{\sqrt{n \cdot n!}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle^{\otimes(n-1)} = \sqrt{n}\;\vert n-1\rangle\]

All occupation number states can be constructed from the vacuum:

(39)#\[\vert n_\alpha\rangle = \frac{(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha)^{n_\alpha}}{\sqrt{n_\alpha!}}\;\vert 0_\alpha\rangle\]

The number operator \(\hat{n}_\alpha = \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{b}_\alpha\) counts bosons: \(\hat{n}_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = n_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle\), with \(n_\alpha = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\) unbounded.

Fermion Creation and Annihilation Operators#

The fermion creation operator \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\) and annihilation operator \(\hat{c}_\alpha\) are defined analogously, using antisymmetric insertion and deletion:

Definition: Fermion Creation and Annihilation

Acting on an \(N\)-particle state \(\vert\Psi\rangle\):

(40)#\[\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N+1}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \vert\Psi\rangle, \qquad \hat{c}_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\;\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \vert\Psi\rangle\]

where \(\rhd_-\) inserts antisymmetrically and \(\lhd_-\) deletes antisymmetrically.

Example: \(\hat{c}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = 0\) (Pauli Exclusion)

Problem. Attempt to create one more fermion in the already-occupied state \(\vert 1\rangle\), starting from \(\vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = (\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\).

Solution. Apply \(\hat{c}^\dagger_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_-\):

\[\hat{c}^\dagger_1 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\right)\]

Compute each antisymmetric insertion:

\[\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \vert 1\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 2\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]
\[\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle - \vert 2\rangle(\vert 1\rangle \rhd_- \vert 1\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle - \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle + \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\]

Sum: \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle - \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle) = 0\).

The antisymmetric insertion produces exact cancellation—no two fermions can occupy the same state.

Example: \(\hat{c}_2 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = -\vert 1_1, 0_2\rangle\) (Fermion Sign)

Problem. Annihilate a fermion from state \(\vert 2\rangle\), starting from \(\vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = (\vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\).

Solution. Apply \(\hat{c}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\vert 2\rangle \lhd_-\):

\[\hat{c}_2 \vert 1_1, 1_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle\right)\]

Compute each antisymmetric deletion:

\[\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 1\rangle\vert 2\rangle = \delta_{21}\vert 2\rangle - \vert 1\rangle(\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 2\rangle) = 0 - \vert 1\rangle \cdot 1 = -\vert 1\rangle\]
\[\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 2\rangle\vert 1\rangle = \delta_{22}\vert 1\rangle - \vert 2\rangle(\vert 2\rangle \lhd_- \vert 1\rangle) = \vert 1\rangle - 0 = \vert 1\rangle\]

Sum: \(\frac{1}{2}(-\vert 1\rangle - \vert 1\rangle) = -\vert 1\rangle = -\vert 1_1, 0_2\rangle\).

The minus sign (the fermion sign) appears because removing \(\vert 2\rangle\) requires “moving past” the occupied state \(\vert 1\rangle\), picking up a factor of \((-1)\).

Action on single-mode Fock states. Starting from the single-mode vacuum \(\vert 0_\alpha\rangle = \mathbb{1}\) and applying the fermion creation operator, one can derive the action on any Fock state:

Fermion Operators on Single-Mode Fock States

(41)#\[\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 0_\alpha\rangle = \vert 1_\alpha\rangle, \qquad \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 1_\alpha\rangle = 0\]
(42)#\[\hat{c}_\alpha \vert 1_\alpha\rangle = \vert 0_\alpha\rangle, \qquad \hat{c}_\alpha \vert 0_\alpha\rangle = 0\]

If the state is empty, creation fills it. If already occupied, creation gives zero (Pauli exclusion). The vacuum is quenched by annihilation.

Derivation: Fermion Action on Single-Mode Fock States

Apply the definitions \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N+1}} \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \vert\Psi\rangle\) and \(\hat{c}_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \vert\Psi\rangle\).

(A) Creation action

Starting from the vacuum:

\[\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 0_\alpha\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1}} \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \mathbb{1} = \vert\alpha\rangle = \vert 1_\alpha\rangle\]

On an already occupied state:

\[\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 1_\alpha\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \vert\alpha\rangle\]

Using the recursive insertion rule:

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \vert\alpha\rangle = \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha\rangle - \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \mathbb{1}) = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle - \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle = 0\]

So \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 1_\alpha\rangle = 0\) (Pauli exclusion).

(B) Annihilation action

On an occupied state:

\[\hat{c}_\alpha \vert 1_\alpha\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1}} \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \vert\alpha\rangle\]

Using the recursive deletion rule with \(\vert\alpha\rangle = \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes \mathbb{1}\):

\[\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \vert\alpha\rangle = \delta_{\alpha\alpha}\,\mathbb{1} - \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \mathbb{1}) = 1 \cdot \mathbb{1} - 0 = \mathbb{1} = \vert 0_\alpha\rangle\]

On the vacuum:

\[\hat{c}_\alpha \vert 0_\alpha\rangle = \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_- \mathbb{1} = 0\]

by the base case of the deletion rule (no fermion to remove).

The Fock state is constructed as \(\vert n_\alpha\rangle = (\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^{n_\alpha}\vert 0_\alpha\rangle\) with \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1\}\).

The number operator \(\hat{n}_\alpha = \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{c}_\alpha\) counts fermions: \(\hat{n}_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = n_\alpha\vert n_\alpha\rangle\).

Summary#

  • Identical particles have no labels; the many-body state \(\vert\Psi\rangle\) must be symmetric (bosons) or antisymmetric (fermions) under particle exchange.

  • Symmetric states are constructed by summing over all permutations of single-particle kets (the permanent); bosons allow multiple occupation.

  • Antisymmetric states are constructed with alternating signs (the Slater determinant); Pauli exclusion (\(n_\alpha \in \{0,1\}\)) follows automatically.

  • Insertion and deletion rules \(\rhd_\pm\) and \(\lhd_\pm\) define recursive procedures for adding/removing particles while preserving symmetry.

  • Boson operators: \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N+1}}\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_+ \vert\Psi\rangle\); on Fock states, \(\hat{b}^\dagger\vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\,\vert n+1\rangle\), \(\hat{b}\,\vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n}\,\vert n-1\rangle\).

  • Fermion operators: \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N+1}}\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_- \vert\Psi\rangle\); on Fock states, \(\hat{c}^\dagger\vert 0\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\), \(\hat{c}^\dagger\vert 1\rangle = 0\) (Pauli exclusion).

Homework#

1. Let \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}\) be the operator that swaps particles 1 and 2. Show that \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}^2 = \hat{I}\), and deduce that the eigenvalues of \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}\) are \(\pm 1\).

2. Two identical bosons occupy orthogonal single-particle states \(\psi_1, \psi_2\). Write the normalized symmetric wavefunction. What changes if \(\psi_1 = \psi_2\)? Pay attention to normalization.

3. Two identical fermions occupy orthonormal states \(\psi_1, \psi_2\). Write the normalized antisymmetric wavefunction. Verify it changes sign under \(\boldsymbol{r}_1 \leftrightarrow \boldsymbol{r}_2\).

4. Prove the Pauli exclusion principle from antisymmetry: if \(\psi_1 = \psi_2\), show the fermionic wavefunction vanishes identically.

5. Use the bosonic insertion rule to compute \(\psi_\alpha \rhd_+ (\psi_\beta \otimes \psi_\gamma \otimes \psi_\delta)\). How many terms appear?

6. Use the fermionic insertion rule to compute \(\psi_1 \rhd_- (\psi_1 \otimes \psi_2)\). Show that the result vanishes, demonstrating Pauli exclusion at the first-quantized level.

7. Starting from \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n_\alpha\rangle = \sqrt{n_\alpha + 1}\,\vert n_\alpha + 1\rangle\), show by induction that \(\vert n_\alpha\rangle = \frac{(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha)^{n_\alpha}}{\sqrt{n_\alpha!}}\,\vert 0_\alpha\rangle\).

8. In a two-mode fermionic system \((\alpha, \beta)\), verify explicitly that \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta \vert 0,0\rangle = -\hat{c}^\dagger_\beta \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 0,0\rangle\) using the Jordan-Wigner phase formula.

9. How many independent antisymmetric states can be formed by placing \(N\) fermions into \(D\) single-particle states? Give the combinatorial formula. What happens when \(N > D\)?

10. Compute \(\hat{c}_2 \vert 1_1, 1_2, 1_3\rangle\) using the fermionic annihilation formula with the Jordan-Wigner phase. What is the sign of the result?

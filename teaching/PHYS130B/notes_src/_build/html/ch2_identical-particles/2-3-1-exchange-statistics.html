2.3.1 Exchange Statistics#

Prompts

  • How does the topology of configuration space differ between 2D and 3D?

  • Why can only bosons and fermions exist in 3D? What makes 2D special?

  • Define braiding. How is it different from simple particle permutation?

  • What is the braid group? How does it differ from the permutation group?

  • Why does the braid group allow for anyonic statistics in 2D?

  • How does the statistical angle relate to exchange phase?

Lecture Notes#

The statistics of identical particles—whether they are bosons or fermions—is encoded in how particle exchange operations act on quantum states. This section explores exchange symmetry in exotic two-dimensional systems, where particles called anyons can exhibit fractional charge and non-abelian statistics beyond the familiar boson-fermion dichotomy.

The Key Insight: Why 2D Is Special#

The existence of anyons depends on the topology of the configuration space of particles. Let us contrast 3D and 2D.

Configuration Space in 3D#

For \(N\) identical particles in 3D, the configuration space (positions of all particles) is:

\[Q_N = (\mathbb{R}^3)^N / S_N\]

where we quotient by the permutation group \(S_N\) to account for indistinguishability.

Key topological fact: The configuration space of two identical particles in 3D, with the diagonal removed (i.e., excluding coincident positions), is:

\[Q_2 = [\mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus \text{diagonal}] / S_2\]

This space is simply connected: any closed loop can be continuously deformed to a point.

Consequence in 3D: Only two topologically distinct paths for exchanging two particles:

  • Even exchange: adiabatically deform into an even permutation.

  • Odd exchange: adiabatically deform into an odd permutation.

This binary distinction permits only bosons (symmetric, phase \(+1\)) and fermions (antisymmetric, phase \(-1\)).

Configuration Space in 2D#

In two dimensions, the situation is dramatically different:

\[Q_2^{(2D)} = [\mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \text{diagonal}] / S_2\]

Key topological fact: This space is not simply connected. It has a non-trivial fundamental group.

Why? In 3D, two particles can “go around” each other in an infinity of ways (circling in any plane). In 2D, all exchanges must happen in the same plane, and the order and direction of exchange matter—they cannot be continuously deformed into each other.

Consequence in 2D: Continuous families of exchange statistics are possible—anyons.

Topology matters

In 3D, exchanging two particles twice is topologically trivial (contractible to identity), forcing the exchange phase to satisfy \(e^{2i\theta} = 1\), giving only bosons (\(\theta=0\)) or fermions (\(\theta=\pi\)). In 2D, double exchange is non-trivial, allowing continuous \(\theta\)—anyons.

Important

Exchange Operator and Eigenvalues

The exchange operator P₁₂ swaps two particles. For identical particles, P₁₂|ψ⟩ = λ|ψ⟩ with λ = ±1. Eigenvalue +1 (bosons) gives symmetric states; -1 (fermions) gives antisymmetric states.

Note

Two-Particle Wavefunction Symmetry

For identical particles: ψ(r₁,r₂) = +ψ(r₂,r₁) for bosons; ψ(r₁,r₂) = -ψ(r₂,r₁) for fermions. This is a global constraint, not a detail—it rules out half of the naïve tensor product space.

Caution

Wavefunction Form Restrictions

For two fermions, the wavefunction must vanish if r₁=r₂ (cannot occupy same position with same spin). For two bosons, wavefunction can be nonzero at r₁=r₂, which allows Bose condensation phenomena.

The Braid Group vs. Permutation Group#

Permutation Group \(S_N\) (3D)#

In three dimensions, only the topology of the exchange matters, not its specific trajectory. Thus, particle exchange is described by the permutation group \(S_N\):

  • An even permutation has phase \(+1\).

  • An odd permutation has phase \(-1\).

  • Permutations compose abelianly: the order of exchanges doesn’t matter (up to their parity).

The Braid Group \(B_N\) (2D)#

In two dimensions, we must track how particles exchange, not just whether they do. This is encoded by the braid group \(B_N\).

Definition: A braid is a sequence of particle exchanges (strands) in 2D, where:

  • Each exchange involves crossing two particles.

  • Crossings can be clockwise or counterclockwise.

  • The order of exchanges matters: composing two braids gives a different braid if done in different order.

For two particles, the braid group \(B_2\) is generated by a single element \(\sigma\) (exchange), which satisfies:

\[\hat{\sigma}^2 \neq e \quad \text{(unlike the permutation group, where } \hat{\sigma}^2 = e\text{)}\]

Instead, only repeated application to a high power gives the identity.

Non-abelian structure: For \(N \geq 3\), the braid group is non-abelian: \(\hat{\sigma}_i \hat{\sigma}_j \neq \hat{\sigma}_j \hat{\sigma}_i\) for \(i \not\in \{j-1, j, j+1\}\).

Yang-Baxter Equation#

The consistency condition for braiding is the Yang-Baxter equation:

\[R_{12} R_{23} R_{12} = R_{23} R_{12} R_{23}\]

where \(R_{ij}\) is the braiding operation on particles \(i\) and \(j\). This ensures that different braiding sequences are compatible—no contradictions arise from different orderings.

Exchange Statistics in 2D: Anyons#

The Adiabatic Phase#

When two identical particles exchange adiabatically in 2D, the wavefunction can acquire an arbitrary phase factor:

(123)#\[\psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1, \boldsymbol{r}_2) \to e^{i\phi} \psi(\boldsymbol{r}_2, \boldsymbol{r}_1)\]

where \(\phi\) can be any real number (or often, quantized in units of \(2\pi\)).

The Statistical Angle#

The exchange phase is often written as:

\[e^{i\pi\theta}\]

where \(\theta\) is the statistical angle or anyon angle, expressed in units of \(2\pi\) (i.e., \(\theta \in [0, 2)\)).

(124)#\[\boxed{\psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1, \boldsymbol{r}_2) = e^{i\pi\theta} \psi(\boldsymbol{r}_2, \boldsymbol{r}_1)}\]

Recovering Bosons and Fermions#

Bosons: \(\theta = 0 \Rightarrow e^{i\pi \cdot 0} = +1\) (symmetric).

Fermions: \(\theta = 1 \Rightarrow e^{i\pi \cdot 1} = -1\) (antisymmetric).

Fractional anyons: \(\theta = 1/3 \Rightarrow e^{i\pi/3}\) (phase of \(60°\)).

Any value between 0 and 2 is allowed (in principle), though physical systems often have specific quantized values.

Braiding Operations and Quantum Dimension#

Braiding as a Unitary Operator#

In a system with \(N\) anyons, the braiding operation that exchanges particles \(i\) and \(j\) is a unitary operator \(R_{ij}\) in Hilbert space. When applied to a many-anyon state:

\[R_{ij} |\psi\rangle = e^{i\pi\theta} |\psi'\rangle\]

For non-abelian anyons, braiding can mix different states—it’s not just a phase factor.

Quantum Dimension#

One of the most striking features of anyons is the concept of quantum dimension. For a system of \(N\) identical anyons:

\[\dim(\mathcal{H}_N) \sim d^N\]

where \(d\) is the quantum dimension of the anyon type.

  • For bosons and fermions in 3D: \(d = 1\) (all \(N\)-particle states reduce to a single Fock sector).

  • For non-abelian Fibonacci anyons (found in certain quantum Hall states): \(d = \phi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618\) (the golden ratio!).

Consequence: The Hilbert space dimension grows exponentially with \(N\), but the growth rate is \(\phi^N\) instead of \(2^N\).

\[d^N = \phi^N \approx 1.618^N\]

This provides exponential advantage for quantum computation if anyons can be manipulated and measured.

One and Two Dimensions: Comparison#

One Dimension#

In 1D, particles on a line cannot pass through each other. “Exchange” is not well-defined—if two particles try to swap positions, they must collide.

Consequence: Identical particles in 1D don’t fall into universal boson/fermion classes. Instead, they are described by Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid theory, which shows power-law correlations rather than simple permutation statistics.

Two Dimensions#

The configuration space has non-trivial topology \(\Rightarrow\) anyonic statistics possible.

Three Dimensions and Higher#

The configuration space is simply connected \(\Rightarrow\) only bosons and fermions.

Theorem: In dimensions \(d \geq 3\), only two types of statistics (boson and fermion) are allowed.

Example: Examples of 2D Systems

Quantum Hall Systems

A 2D electron gas in a strong perpendicular magnetic field forms a quantum Hall state. Quasiparticles (excitations) in these states are anyons!

Cold Atoms in Optical Lattices

Atoms trapped in a 2D optical lattice (a periodic potential created by laser beams) can be engineered to have anyonic statistics via synthetic gauge fields.

Molecular Systems

Some proposals involve engineered molecules or superconductors to realize 2D anyonic systems.

Topological Insulators

The edge states of 2D topological insulators can host anyonic quasiparticles.

The Fundamental Reason: Homotopy of Configuration Space#

Fundamental Group#

The rigorous statement involves the fundamental group \(\pi_1(Q_N)\) of the configuration space.

  • 3D: \(\pi_1(Q_N^{(3D)}) = S_N\) (permutation group) for \(N \geq 2\).

  • 2D: \(\pi_1(Q_N^{(2D)}) = B_N\) (braid group) for \(N \geq 2\).

  • 1D: Configuration space is not even well-defined (particles can’t pass); no ordinary exchange.

Representations of Braid Groups#

Since the wavefunction is a representation of the fundamental group, and \(B_N\) has vastly more representations than \(S_N\), we get vastly more statistics in 2D.

  • Each representation of \(B_N\) corresponds to a possible set of anyonic statistics.

  • Abelian representations (1D) give abelian anyons (fractional statistics).

  • Non-abelian representations give non-abelian anyons (richer structure).

Summary#

  • Configuration space topology: In 3D, it’s simply connected → only bosons/fermions. In 2D, it’s not → anyons possible.

  • Braid group \(B_N\) (2D) replaces permutation group \(S_N\) (3D); braiding order matters.

  • Anyonic statistics: Exchange phase \(e^{i\pi\theta}\) with arbitrary \(\theta \in [0, 2)\).

  • Bosons (\(\theta = 0\)) and fermions (\(\theta = 1\)) are special cases.

  • Quantum dimension \(d > 1\) for non-abelian anyons → exponential growth of Hilbert space.

  • Physical realizations: Quantum Hall systems, cold atoms, topological materials.

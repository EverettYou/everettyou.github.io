6.1.3 Entropy#

Prompts

  • What is von Neumann entropy? How do you compute it from eigenvalues of \(\rho\)?

  • What is the physical meaning of von Neumann entropy? How does it relate to classical Shannon entropy and to uncertainty in quantum states?

  • What are the key properties of entropy: bounds, concavity, subadditivity? Why are these properties physically important?

  • What is the maximum entropy principle? Under what conditions does a system tend toward maximum entropy?

  • For a bipartite pure state, why is entanglement entropy of subsystem A equal to von Neumann entropy of the reduced density matrix \(\rho_A\)?

Lecture Notes#

Classical information theory measures uncertainty through Shannon entropy; quantum mechanics demands its own generalization. Von Neumann entropy quantifies the mixedness of a quantum state, ranging from zero for perfect knowledge to a maximum for complete ignorance. This single number encodes how much pure quantum coherence remains, serving as the quantum analog of ‘how much information we have lost.’

Von Neumann Entropy: Definition#

Discussion

For a pure state, S(ρ) = 0. For the Bell state |Φ+⟩, compute the entanglement entropy of each subsystem. Why is S(ρ_AB) < S(ρ_A) + S(ρ_B)?

Definition: Von Neumann Entropy

For a density matrix \(\rho\), the von Neumann entropy quantifies the quantum information (or uncertainty) in the state:

(270)#\[ S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \ln \rho)\]

In the eigenbasis of \(\rho\) with eigenvalues \(\{\lambda_i\}\), this simplifies to:

(271)#\[ S(\rho) = -\sum_i \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\]

where we use the convention \(0 \ln 0 = 0\) (taking the limit as \(x \to 0^+\): \(x \ln x \to 0\)).

Physical Interpretation

  • Pure state (\(\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\)): \(S(\rho) = 0\) (complete certainty, no entropy)

  • Maximally mixed (\(\rho = I/d\)): \(S(\rho) = \ln d\) (maximum entropy for dimension \(d\))

  • General mixed state: \(0 \leq S(\rho) \leq \ln d\)

Units: We use natural logarithm, giving entropy in nats. Base-2 logarithm gives bits (1 nat \(\approx 1.44\) bits).

Derivation: Von Neumann Entropy

Construction of the formula:

Start with the von Neumann operator:

\[ S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \ln \rho)\]

To evaluate this, work in the eigenbasis where \(\rho = \sum_i \lambda_i |u_i\rangle\langle u_i|\).

Then \(\ln \rho = \sum_i (\ln \lambda_i) |u_i\rangle\langle u_i|\), so:

\[ \rho \ln \rho = \sum_i \lambda_i (\ln \lambda_i) |u_i\rangle\langle u_i|\]

Taking the trace:

\[ \text{Tr}(\rho \ln \rho) = \sum_i \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\]

Therefore:

\[ S(\rho) = -\sum_i \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\]

This is the spectral formula and is independent of basis choice (eigenvalues are invariant).

Limit convention: For eigenvalues \(\lambda_i = 0\), the term \(\lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\) is defined to vanish:

\[ \lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x = 0\]

This makes physical sense: a zero eigenvalue contributes no entropy (it’s not in the support of the state).

Classical vs. Quantum Entropy

Classical Shannon entropy for a probability distribution \(\{p_i\}\):

\[ H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i\]

Quantum von Neumann entropy generalizes this: the eigenvalues of \(\rho\) play the role of probabilities, but \(\rho\) encodes more than classical probabilities (coherences, entanglement).

Key difference: Quantum entropy can increase or decrease under local operations (unlike classical entropy, which cannot decrease under any operation).

Fundamental Examples

Pure State

For \(\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\) (eigenvalues: 1 and 0’s):

(272)#\[ S(\rho) = -1 \cdot \ln 1 - 0 \cdot \ln 0 = 0\]

No uncertainty about the state; entropy is zero.

Maximally Mixed State (d-dimensional)

For \(\rho = I/d\) (eigenvalues: all \(1/d\)):

(273)#\[ S(\rho) = -d \cdot \frac{1}{d} \ln\frac{1}{d} = \ln d\]

For a qubit (\(d=2\)): \(S(I/2) = \ln 2 \approx 0.693\) bits.

Maximum uncertainty for a \(d\)-dimensional system.

Mixed Qubit (Example)

Eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 0.7, \lambda_2 = 0.3\):

(274)#\[ S(\rho) = -0.7 \ln(0.7) - 0.3 \ln(0.3) \approx 0.610 + 1.204 \approx 1.204\text{ nats} \approx 0.610\text{ bits}\]

Intermediate between pure (0) and maximally mixed (\(\ln 2 \approx 0.693\) bits).

Properties of Von Neumann Entropy

1. Boundedness

Entropy Bounds

For a density matrix on \(d\)-dimensional Hilbert space:

(275)#\[ 0 \leq S(\rho) \leq \ln d\]

  • Lower bound: \(S(\rho) = 0\) ⟺ \(\rho\) is pure (rank 1).

  • Upper bound: \(S(\rho) = \ln d\) ⟺ \(\rho = I/d\) (maximally mixed, full rank).

2. Concavity#

For any \(0 \leq p \leq 1\) and density matrices \(\rho_1, \rho_2\):

(276)#\[ S(p\rho_1 + (1-p)\rho_2) \geq p S(\rho_1) + (1-p)S(\rho_2)\]

Interpretation: Entropy of a mixture is at least the average entropy of the components. Mixing increases entropy (or keeps it same).

Consequence: If you don’t know which state you have (ensemble), entropy is higher than if you knew which one it was.

3. Additivity (For Independent Systems)#

If \(\rho_{AB} = \rho_A \otimes \rho_B\) (product state, no entanglement):

(277)#\[ S(\rho_A \otimes \rho_B) = S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\]

Entropies add when systems are uncorrelated.

4. Subadditivity (Araki–Lieb Inequality)#

Subadditivity of Entropy

For any bipartite state \(\rho_{AB}\):

(278)#\[ S(\rho_{AB}) \leq S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\]

Interpretation: Joint entropy is at most the sum of marginal entropies. Entanglement reduces the total entropy.

Equality ⟺ no entanglement: \(\rho_{AB} = \rho_A \otimes \rho_B\).

For pure states: \(S(\rho_{AB}) = 0\), so \(S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B) \geq 0\) (always true, but becomes \(S(\rho_A) = S(\rho_B) = E_{\text{ent}} > 0\) if entangled).

5. Strong Subadditivity#

For a tripartite system \(ABC\):

(279)#\[ S(\rho_{AB}) + S(\rho_{BC}) \geq S(\rho_B) + S(\rho_{ABC})\]

This is the cornerstone of the second law of thermodynamics in quantum information.

Maximum Entropy Principle#

Principle: Among all density matrices consistent with certain constraints (e.g., given expectation values), the one with maximum entropy represents maximum ignorance and is the most natural.

Thermal State (Example)#

At thermal equilibrium, a system reaches the state that maximizes entropy subject to fixed average energy \(\langle H \rangle = E\).

Thermal State and Maximum Entropy

Using Lagrange multipliers (with energy constraint and normalization):

(280)#\[ \rho_\text{thermal} = \frac{\mathrm{e}^{-\beta H}}{\text{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta H})} = \frac{\mathrm{e}^{-\beta H}}{Z}\]

where \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\) (inverse temperature) and \(Z = \text{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta H})\) (partition function).

This maximizes von Neumann entropy for fixed average energy: \(S(\rho_\text{thermal})\) is maximum among all \(\rho\) with \(\langle H \rangle = E\).

Maximally Mixed State#

Unconstrained maximum entropy: \(\rho = I/d\), with \(S_\text{max} = \ln d\).

Von Neumann Entropy and Thermodynamic Entropy#

Boltzmann–Gibbs relation (fundamental in statistical mechanics):

(281)#\[ S_\text{thermo} = k_B \cdot S_\text{von Neumann}(\rho)\]

where:

  • \(S_\text{thermo}\) = thermodynamic entropy (extensive, increases with system size).

  • \(S_\text{von Neumann}(\rho)\) = quantum information entropy.

  • \(k_B\) = Boltzmann constant ≈ \(1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J/K.

Interpretation: Quantum information entropy is the microscopic origin of thermodynamic entropy. Uncertainty in microscopic state manifests as macroscopic irreversibility.

Rényi Entropy#

Definition: Rényi Entropy Family

A family of entropies parameterized by \(\alpha \in (0, \infty) \setminus \{1\}\):

(282)#\[ S_\alpha(\rho) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \ln \text{Tr}(\rho^\alpha)\]

Limit: \(\lim_{\alpha \to 1} S_\alpha(\rho) = S(\rho)\) (von Neumann entropy).

Common Cases#

\(\alpha = 0\) (Hartley entropy):

\[ S_0(\rho) = \ln(\text{rank}(\rho))\]

Counts number of nonzero eigenvalues.

\(\alpha = 1/2\) (Collision entropy variant):

\[ S_{1/2}(\rho) = -2\ln \text{Tr}(\rho^{1/2})\]

\(\alpha = 2\) (Rényi-2, collision entropy):

(283)#\[ S_2(\rho) = -\ln \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = -\ln(\text{Purity})\]

Related to purity: \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = \mathrm{e}^{-S_2(\rho)}\).

\(\alpha \to \infty\) (Min-entropy):

\[ S_\infty(\rho) = -\ln(\lambda_\text{max})\]

where \(\lambda_\text{max}\) is the largest eigenvalue.

Properties#

  1. Monotonicity: \(S_\alpha(\rho) \geq S_{\alpha'}(\rho)\) for \(\alpha \leq \alpha'\) (entropy decreases with order).

  2. Bounds von Neumann: \(S_\infty(\rho) \leq S_1(\rho) \leq S_0(\rho)\).

  3. Operational meaning: Different \(\alpha\) values capture different aspects of information:

    • \(S_0\): Worst-case (max number of distinguishable states).

    • \(S_1\): Average-case (von Neumann).

    • \(S_2\): Accessible without full tomography (measure \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2)\) via correlations).

    • \(S_\infty\): Best-case (min-entropy; security-relevant).

Rényi-2 Experimental Accessibility#

For identical independent measurements, the probability of getting the same outcome twice is:

\[ P(\text{same}) = \text{Tr}(\rho^2)\]

Thus \(S_2(\rho) = -\ln P(\text{same})\) is directly measurable without reconstructing full \(\rho\) (no need for full state tomography).

Conditional Entropy#

For a bipartite state \(\rho_{AB}\), the conditional entropy of A given B is:

(284)#\[ S(A|B) = S(\rho_{AB}) - S(\rho_B)\]

Interpretation: Average entropy of A after measuring B and learning the outcome (on average).

Properties:

  • \(S(A|B) \leq S(A)\) (knowing B cannot increase A’s entropy).

  • \(S(A|B) \geq S(A) - S(B)\) (Araki–Lieb inequality).

  • For entangled pure states: \(S(A|B) = -S(A) = -S(B)\) (negative, indicating strong correlation).

Mutual Information and Entanglement Entropy#

Mutual information (total correlations):

\[ I(A : B) = S(A) + S(B) - S(AB)\]

For a pure bipartite state where \(S(AB) = 0\):

(285)#\[ I(A : B) = S(A) + S(B) = 2 S(A) = 2 E_{\text{ent}}\]

Thus entanglement entropy is half the mutual information for pure bipartite states.

Entropy of subsystem can exceed total

For an entangled pure bipartite state, \(S(\rho_{AB}) = 0\) but \(S(\rho_A) > 0\). The subsystem has more entropy than the whole—a uniquely quantum phenomenon with no classical analogue. This is possible because quantum correlations (entanglement) are not captured by the marginals.

Summary#

  • Von Neumann entropy \(S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \ln \rho) = -\sum_i \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\): Quantifies ignorance/mixedness; zero for pure, \(\ln d\) for maximally mixed.

  • Boundedness: \(0 \leq S(\rho) \leq \ln d\).

  • Concavity: Averaging increases entropy (or keeps same).

  • Additivity: For independent systems, \(S(\rho_A \otimes \rho_B) = S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\).

  • Subadditivity: \(S(\rho_{AB}) \leq S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\); entanglement reduces total entropy.

  • Maximum entropy principle: Thermal state \(\rho = \mathrm{e}^{-\beta H}/Z\) maximizes entropy at fixed average energy.

  • Thermodynamic entropy: \(S_\text{thermo} = k_B S_\text{von Neumann}\); quantum uncertainty is origin of irreversibility.

  • Rényi entropy \(S_\alpha(\rho) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\ln\text{Tr}(\rho^\alpha)\): Family generalizing von Neumann; \(S_2\) experimentally accessible.

  • Conditional entropy: \(S(A|B) = S(AB) - S(B)\); measures A’s entropy after learning B.

  • Mutual information: \(I(A:B) = S(A) + S(B) - S(AB)\); for pure bipartite, \(= 2E_{\text{ent}}\).

1. Von Neumann Entropy from Eigenvalues

A qubit has density matrix with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 0.6\) and \(\lambda_2 = 0.4\). Compute the von Neumann entropy \(S(\rho) = -\sum_i \lambda_i \ln \lambda_i\) in nats. Express your answer to 3 decimal places. What physical interpretation does this value have compared to a pure state (\(S=0\)) and maximally mixed state (\(S = \ln 2\))?

2. Entropy of Product States

Consider a bipartite system \(AB\) where:

  • Subsystem A is in pure state \(\rho_A = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\)

  • Subsystem B is maximally mixed: \(\rho_B = I_2/2\) (qubit)

  • The composite state is a product: \(\rho_{AB} = \rho_A \otimes \rho_B\)

Compute: (a) \(S(\rho_A)\) (b) \(S(\rho_B)\) (c) \(S(\rho_{AB})\)

Verify that additivity holds: \(S(\rho_{AB}) = S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\).

3. Maximally Mixed State Entropy

A \(d\)-dimensional system is in the maximally mixed state \(\rho = I_d/d\).

(a) Show that all eigenvalues equal \(1/d\).

(b) Compute \(S(\rho)\) in terms of \(d\).

(c) For \(d = 2\) (qubit), \(d = 3\) (qutrit), and \(d = 4\), calculate the entropy in nats and bits (use \(1 \text{ nat} = 1/\ln 2 \text{ bits}\)).

(d) Which dimension has entropy = 1 nat?

4. Subadditivity: Entangled Bell State

Consider the Bell state \(|\Psi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) on a two-qubit system.

(a) Write the density matrix \(\rho_{AB} = |\Psi^+\rangle\langle\Psi^+|\).

(b) Compute \(S(\rho_{AB})\) (the composite system is pure).

(c) Compute the reduced density matrices:

  • \(\rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB})\) and its eigenvalues

  • \(\rho_B = \text{Tr}_A(\rho_{AB})\) and its eigenvalues

(d) Compute \(S(\rho_A)\) and \(S(\rho_B)\).

(e) Verify subadditivity: \(S(\rho_{AB}) \leq S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\). Is equality violated? Explain physically.

5. Strong Subadditivity (Conceptual)

Strong subadditivity states: \(S(\rho_{AB}) + S(\rho_{BC}) \geq S(\rho_B) + S(\rho_{ABC})\) for any tripartite state.

(a) Rearrange this inequality to show: \(S(\rho_{AB}) - S(\rho_B) \geq S(\rho_{ABC}) - S(\rho_{BC})\).

(b) The left side is the conditional entropy \(S(A|B) = S(\rho_{AB}) - S(\rho_B)\). Interpret the inequality: What does it say about how learning information about subsystem C affects the conditional entropy of A given B?

(c) Why is this property fundamental to the second law of thermodynamics in quantum information? (Hint: Think about entropy evolution under local operations.)

6. Entanglement Entropy and Mutual Information

A pure bipartite state has subsystem entropies \(S(\rho_A) = 0.5\) nats and \(S(\rho_B) = 0.5\) nats.

(a) What is \(S(\rho_{AB})\)? (Recall: for pure bipartite states, \(S(\rho_{AB}) = 0\).)

(b) Compute the mutual information: \(I(A:B) = S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B) - S(\rho_{AB})\).

(c) Show that \(I(A:B) = 2E_{\text{ent}}\) where \(E_{\text{ent}}\) is the entanglement entropy.

(d) If the state were instead a product (no entanglement), what would \(S(\rho_A)\) and \(S(\rho_B)\) be? Compute the mutual information in that case.

7. Classical Limit: Shannon Entropy Connection

A quantum system’s density matrix in the computational basis is diagonal:

\[\begin{split}\rho = \begin{pmatrix} 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

(a) Is this a pure state or mixed state? What is the physical interpretation?

(b) Compute the von Neumann entropy \(S(\rho)\).

(c) The eigenvalues \(\{0.5, 0.5\}\) can be viewed as a classical probability distribution. Compute the Shannon entropy \(H(p) = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i\) for \(p_1 = 0.5, p_2 = 0.5\).

(d) Show that \(S(\rho) = H(p)\) in this case. Why does this connection hold for diagonal density matrices?

(e) For a general diagonal \(\rho\) with eigenvalues \(\{\lambda_i\}\), argue that von Neumann entropy reduces to classical Shannon entropy. Why is the quantum entropy defined using the spectral decomposition?

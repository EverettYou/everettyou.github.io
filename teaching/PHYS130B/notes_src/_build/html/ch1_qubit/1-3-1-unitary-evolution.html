1.3.1 Unitary Evolution#

Prompts

  • What is a unitary operator, and why must quantum evolution be unitary? (What physical principle demands this?)

  • Starting from the Schrödinger equation, derive the formal solution for the time-evolution operator \(U(t)\).

  • For a spin-1/2 particle with \(\hat{H} = \omega\hat{\sigma}^z\), compute \(U(t)\) explicitly and describe what it does geometrically.

  • Why can we always write a unitary operator as the exponential of a Hermitian operator? What does this tell us about the relationship between symmetries and conservation laws?

  • Does the time-evolution operator depend on what basis you choose to work in? If you compute \(U(t)\) in the \(z\)-basis vs. the \(x\)-basis, do you get the same result? (Hint: think about unitary change of basis.)

Lecture Notes#

Quantum systems do not stand still. They evolve in time according to the Schrödinger equation, a fundamental postulate of quantum mechanics. This evolution is governed by the Hamiltonian operator, which encodes the energy and symmetries of the system. A remarkable feature of quantum evolution is that it must be unitary: it preserves the normalization of quantum states and the probabilities of all outcomes. This section develops the theory of unitary operators, derives the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, and solves it to obtain the time-evolution operator.

Unitary Operators#

In quantum mechanics, time evolution must satisfy a stringent requirement: it must preserve the normalization of states. If \(|\psi(0)\rangle\) is normalized, then \(|\psi(t)\rangle\) must remain normalized for all times \(t\). This is achieved by unitary operators.

#:::{admonition} Unitary Operators :class: important

A linear operator \(\hat{U}\) is unitary if

(37)#\[ \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = \hat{U}\hat{U}^\dagger = I\]

where \(\hat{U}^\dagger\) is the adjoint (conjugate transpose) and \(I\) is the identity.

Key properties of unitary operators:

  1. Inverse: \(\hat{U}^{-1} = \hat{U}^\dagger\). Unitary operators are always invertible.

  2. Preserve inner products: If \(\hat{U}\) is unitary, then

\[ \langle \psi | \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} | \phi \rangle = \langle \psi | \phi \rangle\]

This means \(\hat{U}\) preserves the overlap between any two states.

  1. Preserve normalization: For a normalized state \(|\psi\rangle\) with \(\langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1\),

\[ \langle \psi | \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1\]

So \(|\psi'\rangle = \hat{U}|\psi\rangle\) is also normalized.

Discussion: Unitarity Constraint

The time-evolution operator \(\hat{U}(t)\) must be unitary to preserve probability. But unitarity alone is not the only constraint on time evolution. Consider these additional requirements:

  • Linearity: Suppose \(|\psi_1\rangle \to \hat{U}|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle \to \hat{U}|\psi_2\rangle\). Why must \(\alpha|\psi_1\rangle + \beta|\psi_2\rangle \to \alpha\hat{U}|\psi_1\rangle + \beta\hat{U}|\psi_2\rangle\)? Is this a consequence of unitarity, or an additional postulate? (Hint: think about superposition being fundamental in quantum mechanics.)

  • Composition law: If evolution from \(t=0\) to \(t_1\) is given by \(\hat{U}(t_1)\), and from \(t_1\) to \(t_2\) by \(\hat{U}(t_2-t_1)\), the total must be \(\hat{U}(t_2) = \hat{U}(t_2-t_1)\hat{U}(t_1)\). Show that if we require \(\hat{U}(t)\) to satisfy this composition law, be unitary, and satisfy \(\hat{U}(0) = \boldsymbol{I}\), then \(\hat{U}(t)\) must have the form \(\hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{G}t}\) for some operator \(\hat{G}\). (Hint: differentiate \(\hat{U}(t)\hat{U}(s) = \hat{U}(t+s)\) with respect to \(t\) at \(t=0\).)

  • Hermitian generator: If \(\hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{G}t}\) is unitary for all real \(t\), what must be true about \(\hat{G}\)? Differentiate \(\hat{U}^\dagger(t)\hat{U}(t) = \boldsymbol{I}\) and show that \(\hat{G}\) must be Hermitian.

  1. Preserve probabilities: Measurement probabilities are unchanged. If \(\hat{O}\) is an observable with eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\) and eigenvectors \(|\phi_i\rangle\), then measuring \(\hat{O}\) on the evolved state \(\hat{U}|\psi\rangle\) gives the same probability distribution as measuring \(\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{O} \hat{U}\) on the original state.

Unitarity: Preserves Probability

Consider the probability of outcome \(\lambda_i\) when measuring \(\hat{O}\) on state \(\hat{U}|\psi\rangle\):

\[ P_i(t) = |\langle \phi_i | \hat{U} | \psi \rangle|^2 = \langle \psi | \hat{U}^\dagger | \phi_i \rangle \langle \phi_i | \hat{U} | \psi \rangle\]

By linearity and unitarity (\(\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = I\)), this equals

\[ \langle \psi | \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{O}_i \hat{U} | \psi \rangle\]

where \(\hat{O}_i = |\phi_i\rangle\langle \phi_i|\) is the projector onto the \(i\)-th eigenstate. The probability distribution depends only on the evolved state through the evolved observable \(\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{O} \hat{U}\), preserving all measurement statistics.

Hermitian Generators and Unitary Operators#

A profound connection exists between Hermitian operators and unitary operators. Any unitary operator can be written as the exponential of a Hermitian operator multiplied by an imaginary constant.

The Exponential of an Operator#

For a square matrix \(\hat{A}\), the exponential \(\mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}}\) is defined via the Taylor series:

(38)#\[ \mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}} = I + \hat{A} + \frac{\hat{A}^2}{2!} + \frac{\hat{A}^3}{3!} + \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\hat{A}^n}{n!}\]

#:::{admonition} Unitary: Hermitian Generator :class: important

If \(\hat{G}\) is Hermitian (\(\hat{G}^\dagger = \hat{G}\)) and \(\theta\) is a real number, then

(39)#\[ \hat{U}(\theta) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\hat{G}\theta}\]

is unitary.

Verification

To verify, compute:

\[ \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{G}\theta} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\hat{G}\theta} = \mathrm{e}^0 = I\]

The factor \(i\) in the exponent ensures unitarity: the exponent \(\mathrm{i}\hat{G}\theta\) is anti-Hermitian (its adjoint is the negative of itself), and exponentiating an anti-Hermitian operator yields a unitary matrix.

Example: Pauli Rotations

The Pauli operator \(\hat{\sigma}^z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\) is Hermitian. The unitary operator

\[ \hat{U}(\theta) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^z \theta / 2}\]

represents a rotation about the z-axis by angle \(\theta\) in the Bloch sphere. Expanding via the exponential:

\[\begin{split} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^z \theta / 2} = \cos(\theta/2) I - i\sin(\theta/2) \hat{\sigma}^z = \begin{pmatrix} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\theta/2} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\theta/2} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

This is unitary and applies a phase rotation to the \(|0\rangle\) state and an opposite phase to \(|1\rangle\).

The Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation#

Quantum evolution is governed by a fundamental postulate: the time-dependent Schrödinger equation.

#:::{admonition} Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation :class: important

For a quantum system with Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}\) (the energy operator), the state \(|\psi(t)\rangle\) evolves according to

(40)#\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H} |\psi(t)\rangle\]

where \(\hbar = h/(2\pi)\) is the reduced Planck constant and \(\mathrm{i}\) is the imaginary unit.

The Hamiltonian

The Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}\) is the operator representing the total energy of the system:

\[ \hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V}\]

where \(\hat{T}\) is kinetic energy and \(\hat{V}\) is potential energy. For a single particle in 3D,

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2}{2m} + V(\hat{\boldsymbol{r}})\]

The Hamiltonian is always Hermitian (\(\hat{H}^\dagger = \hat{H}\)) because energy is a real, observable quantity. The eigenvalues of \(\hat{H}\) are the allowed energy levels of the system.

Discussion: Unitary Breakdown

In this section, we have shown that a closed quantum system evolves unitarily. But what happens when a system interacts with an environment?

  • Open system: Suppose a qubit is coupled to a thermal bath (reservoir of other spins). The full Hamiltonian includes qubit, bath, and interaction: \(\hat{H}_\text{total} = \hat{H}_\text{qubit} + \hat{H}_\text{bath} + \hat{H}_\text{int}\). The total system (qubit + bath) evolves unitarily. But we only observe the qubit. Show (conceptually or by exploring an example) that if we trace over the bath (sum over bath states), the reduced density matrix \(\rho_\text{qubit}(t) = \text{Tr}_\text{bath}[\rho_\text{total}(t)]\) does NOT evolve unitarily. What does non-unitary evolution look like? (The general form is called a Lindblad master equation.)

  • Measurement collapse: In a Stern-Gerlach experiment, a spin state is prepared as a superposition. The apparatus separates spin-up from spin-down spatially. After measuring position, the spin state appears to “collapse.” Why is this not a violation of unitarity? (Hint: the apparatus is part of the system; entanglement between spin and apparatus makes the reduced spin density matrix non-unitary.)

  • Decoherence time scale: For a macroscopic object (say, a grain of dust with \(\sim 10^{18}\) atoms), the interaction with the environment is so strong that coherent superpositions become unobservable almost instantly. Is unitary evolution still happening, or has it been replaced? (Look up: “pointer basis” and “decoherence”.)

Time-Independent vs. Time-Dependent#

The time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE) applies when \(\hat{H}\) does not depend on time:

\[ \hat{H} |\psi\rangle = E |\psi\rangle\]

This is an eigenvalue equation for the energy eigenstates and eigenvalues. Any solution to the TISE evolves in time with a simple phase factor:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}Et/\hbar} |\psi\rangle\]

The time-dependent Schrödinger equation (TDSE), equation (40), is the general postulate. When \(\hat{H}\) is time-independent, we can solve it and recover the TISE.

Solution: The Time-Evolution Operator#

For a time-independent Hamiltonian, the general solution to the TDSE can be written using the time-evolution operator.

#:::{admonition} Time-Evolution Operator :class: important

The state at time \(t\) is

(41)#\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{U}(t) |\psi(0)\rangle\]

where the time-evolution operator is

(42)#\[ \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar}\]

Since \(\hat{H}\) is Hermitian, \(\hat{U}(t)\) is unitary (with the Hermitian generator being \(\hat{H}/\hbar\) and the parameter being \(-t\)).

#:::{admonition} Derivation: Time-Evolution :class: dropdown note

Substituting \(|\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{U}(t)|\psi(0)\rangle\) into the TDSE:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \hat{U}(t) |\psi(0)\rangle = \hat{H} \hat{U}(t) |\psi(0)\rangle\]

Taking the time derivative of \(\hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar}\):

\[ \frac{\partial \hat{U}}{\partial t} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar} \hat{H} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar} \hat{H} \hat{U}(t)\]

Thus

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial \hat{U}}{\partial t} = \hat{H} \hat{U}(t) \quad \checkmark\]

Properties of the Time-Evolution Operator#

The time-evolution operator satisfies several important properties:

  1. Composition law: Evolution for time \(t_1\) followed by evolution for time \(t_2\) equals evolution for time \(t_1 + t_2\):

\[ \hat{U}(t_1 + t_2) = \hat{U}(t_1) \hat{U}(t_2)\]

This follows from \(\mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}(t_1+t_2)} = \mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}t_1} \mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}t_2}\) for any operator \(\hat{A}\).

  1. Initial condition: \(\hat{U}(0) = \mathrm{e}^0 = I\) (identity).

  2. Inverse and adjoint: \(\hat{U}^\dagger(t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar} = \hat{U}(-t)\). Evolution backward in time is the adjoint of forward evolution.

Discussion: Hamiltonian Generator

For a spin-1/2 in a magnetic field along \(z\): \(\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar\omega}{2}\hat{\sigma}^z\).

  • Infinitesimal evolution: For small time step \(\delta t\), the time-evolution operator is approximately \(\hat{U}(\delta t) \approx \boldsymbol{I} - \frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\hat{H}\delta t + O(\delta t^2)\). (Derive this by expanding the exponential to first order.) This shows that \(\hat{H}/\hbar\) is the generator of infinitesimal time translations.

  • Action on superposition: Apply \(\hat{U}(\delta t)\) to the state \(|{+x}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{+z}\rangle + |{-z}\rangle)\) to first order in \(\delta t\). What is the new state? Does it stay in the Bloch sphere, or does it move?

  • Rotation interpretation: The time-evolution operator for this system is

\[\hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega t \hat{\sigma}^z/2}\]

Show that this is equivalent to a rotation by angle \(\theta = \omega t\) around the \(z\)-axis of the Bloch sphere. What is the geometric meaning: as time evolves, the state rotates around the Hamiltonian direction.

  • Ehrenfest theorem connection: In classical mechanics with Hamiltonian \(H\), the equation of motion is \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{q}}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial \boldsymbol{p}}\). In quantum mechanics, \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle \hat{O} \rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\langle [\hat{H}, \hat{O}]\rangle\). Do these look related? (Hint: in quantum mechanics, the commutator plays the role of the Poisson bracket.)

  1. Unitarity: \(\hat{U}^\dagger(t) \hat{U}(t) = I\) for all \(t\).

Computing the Time-Evolution Operator for 2×2 Systems#

For a qubit or spin-1/2 system, the Hamiltonian is a 2×2 matrix. The time-evolution operator can be computed explicitly using the spectral decomposition or by exploiting the structure of 2×2 matrices.

#:::{admonition} Spectral: Time Evolution :class: important

If \(\hat{H}\) has eigenvalues \(E_1, E_2\) and orthonormal eigenstates \(|E_1\rangle, |E_2\rangle\), then

\[ \hat{H} = E_1 |E_1\rangle\langle E_1| + E_2 |E_2\rangle\langle E_2|\]

It follows that

(43)#\[ \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar} = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_1 t/\hbar} |E_1\rangle\langle E_1| + \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_2 t/\hbar} |E_2\rangle\langle E_2|\]

The eigenstates do not evolve in direction—they only accumulate phases proportional to their energies.

Derivation: Spectral Exponential

For any operator \(\hat{A}\) with spectral decomposition \(\hat{A} = \sum_i \lambda_i |a_i\rangle\langle a_i|\), we have

\[ \mathrm{e}^{\hat{A}} = \sum_i \mathrm{e}^{\lambda_i} |a_i\rangle\langle a_i|\]

This is because powers of \(\hat{A}\) in the exponential series respect the projection structure. Applying this to \(\hat{H}\):

\[ \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar} = \sum_i \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_i t/\hbar} |E_i\rangle\langle E_i|\]

Computing \(\hat{U}(t)\) for \(\hat{H} = \omega \hat{\sigma}^z\)#

A common case is a spin in a magnetic field along the z-direction: \(\hat{H} = \omega \hat{\sigma}^z = \omega \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\), where \(\omega\) is a frequency.

The eigenvalues are \(E_\pm = \pm \omega\) with eigenstates \(|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).

\[\begin{split} \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t} |0\rangle\langle 0| + \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega t} |1\rangle\langle 1| = \begin{pmatrix} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega t} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Note

A general state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle\) evolves to

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \alpha \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t} |0\rangle + \beta \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega t} |1\rangle\]

Each energy eigenstate accumulates its own phase factor; the relative phase between the two evolves with the energy difference \(2\omega\).

Basis Transformations and Unitary Changes of Basis#

In quantum mechanics, we often switch between different bases (e.g., from the computational basis \(\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}\) to the eigenbasis of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\)). These basis changes are performed by unitary transformations.

#:::{admonition} Change-of-Basis Operator :class: important

Suppose we have an orthonormal basis \(\{|e_i\rangle\}\) and another basis \(\{|f_j\rangle\}\). The change-of-basis operator is

(44)#\[ \hat{U} = \sum_i |f_i\rangle\langle e_i|\]

This unitary operator maps the first basis to the second: \(\hat{U} |e_i\rangle = |f_i\rangle\). It is unitary because

\[ \hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = \sum_i |e_i\rangle\langle f_i| \sum_j |f_j\rangle\langle e_j| = \sum_i |e_i\rangle\langle e_i| = I\]

A state vector transforms as \(|\psi'\rangle = \hat{U} |\psi\rangle\), and its components in the new basis are related to the old by unitary transformations of the coefficient vectors.

Summary

  • Unitary operators preserve inner products, normalization, and measurement probabilities. They form the foundation of reversible quantum evolution.

  • Hermitian operators generate unitary transformations: \(\hat{U} = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\hat{G}\theta}\) where \(\hat{G}^\dagger = \hat{G}\).

  • The time-dependent Schrödinger equation, \(\mathrm{i}\hbar \partial_t|\psi\rangle = \hat{H}|\psi\rangle\), is the fundamental postulate governing quantum evolution. The Hamiltonian is the energy operator.

  • The time-evolution operator \(\hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar}\) solves the Schrödinger equation. It is unitary and satisfies the composition law.

  • For time-independent Hamiltonians, eigenstates evolve with phase factors \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_n t/\hbar}\) determined by their energies. This is the basis of energy-level diagrams and selection rules.

  • Basis transformations are unitary: any two orthonormal bases are related by a unitary operator.

  • Energy eigenstates do not change in time—they only accumulate phases. Superpositions evolve, with relative phases oscillating at frequencies determined by energy differences.

7. Write \(\hat{V}\) as an explicit \(2\times 2\) matrix (recall \(|+\rangle = (|0\rangle + |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) and \(|-\rangle = (|0\rangle - |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\)).

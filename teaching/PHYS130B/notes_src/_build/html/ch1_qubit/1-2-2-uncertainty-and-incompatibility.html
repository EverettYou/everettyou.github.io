1.2.2 Uncertainty and Incompatibility#

Prompts

  • Explain the commutator \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}\) in words. When is the commutator zero, and what does that physically mean?

  • Compute \([\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^z]\) and \([\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y]\) by matrix multiplication. Do your results match the formula \([\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^j] = 2\mathrm{i}\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{\sigma}^k\)?

  • Suppose \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{B}\) commute. Prove that there exists a set of states that are simultaneous eigenstates of both operators. What goes wrong if they don’t commute?

  • State the uncertainty principle in mathematical form. Does it measure the precision of our measurements or something more fundamental about the quantum system itself?

  • If \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{B}\) don’t commute, can we design an apparatus that simultaneously measures both with arbitrary precision? Or is there a fundamental limit? Describe an experiment (real or thought) that demonstrates this incompatibility.

Lecture Notes#

The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is not about the limitations of our instruments; it is a deep feature of quantum reality stemming from the non-commutativity of observables. When two observables don’t commute, we cannot know both their values simultaneously—gaining knowledge of one necessarily destroys knowledge of the other.

The Commutator#

For two operators \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{B}\), the commutator is defined as

(28)#\[ [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}\]

Commutator Definition

The commutator \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A}\) measures whether two operators commute:

  • If \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0\), the operators commute: they can be applied in any order.

  • If \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \neq 0\), the operators don’t commute: the order matters.

This is the fundamental object that determines whether two observables can be measured simultaneously.

Interpretation:

  • If \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0\) (“they commute”), then the order of applying the operators doesn’t matter: \(\hat{A}\hat{B} = \hat{B}\hat{A}\).

  • If \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \neq 0\) (“they don’t commute”), the order matters: \(\hat{A}\hat{B} \neq \hat{B}\hat{A}\).

Properties of Commutators#

  1. Antisymmetry: \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = -[\hat{B}, \hat{A}]\)

  2. Bilinearity: \([c_1\hat{A}_1 + c_2\hat{A}_2, \hat{B}] = c_1[\hat{A}_1, \hat{B}] + c_2[\hat{A}_2, \hat{B}]\)

  3. Jacobi identity: \([\hat{A}, [\hat{B}, \hat{C}]] + [\hat{B}, [\hat{C}, \hat{A}]] + [\hat{C}, [\hat{A}, \hat{B}]] = 0\)

  4. Self-commutation: \([\hat{A}, \hat{A}] = 0\)

Commutators of Pauli Matrices#

The commutation relations among Pauli matrices are fundamental:

(29)#\[ [\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^j] = 2\mathrm{i}\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{\sigma}^k\]

where \(\epsilon_{ijk}\) is the Levi-Civita symbol (totally antisymmetric in all indices).

Examples:

\[ [\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y] = 2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^z\]

Verification: Commutation Relation

\[\begin{split} \hat{\sigma}^x \hat{\sigma}^y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & -i \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\hat{\sigma}^y \hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}[\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y] = \begin{pmatrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & -i \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2i & 0 \\ 0 & -2i \end{pmatrix} = 2i \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = 2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^z \quad \checkmark\end{split}\]

All commutation relations:

\[ [\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y] = 2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^z, \quad [\hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z] = 2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^x, \quad [\hat{\sigma}^z, \hat{\sigma}^x] = 2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^y\]

By antisymmetry, \([\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^i] = 0\) for any \(i\).

Anti-commutators#

The anti-commutator is

\[ \{\hat{A}, \hat{B}\} = \hat{A}\hat{B} + \hat{B}\hat{A}\]

For Pauli matrices:

\[ \{\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^j\} = 2\delta^{ij} I\]

where \(\delta^{ij}\) is the Kronecker delta. This shows that Pauli matrices anti-commute when \(i \neq j\).

Simultaneous Eigenstates and Commutativity#

Theorem: Commuting Operators Share

Two Hermitian operators \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{B}\) share the same set of eigenvectors (a common eigenbasis) if and only if \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0\).

Consequence: Non-commuting observables cannot have simultaneous eigenstates—no quantum state can be a simultaneous eigenstate of both.

Proof: Common Eigenbasis Implies

Suppose \(\hat{A}\) and \(\hat{B}\) have the same eigenbasis \(\{|\phi_i\rangle\}\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\) and \(\mu_i\):

\[ \hat{A} |\phi_i\rangle = \lambda_i |\phi_i\rangle, \quad \hat{B} |\phi_i\rangle = \mu_i |\phi_i\rangle\]

Then:

\[ [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] |\phi_i\rangle = (\hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A})|\phi_i\rangle = \lambda_i \mu_i |\phi_i\rangle - \mu_i \lambda_i |\phi_i\rangle = 0\]

Since this holds for all basis vectors, \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0\).

Corollary: Two non-commuting operators cannot share a complete set of eigenvectors. Therefore, a quantum state cannot be a simultaneous eigenstate of both operators.

Example: \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^z\)

The eigenbases of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) are different:

  • \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) eigenbasis: \(\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}\)

  • \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) eigenbasis: \(\{|+\rangle, |-\rangle\}\)

Since \([\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^z] = -2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^y \neq 0\), they don’t commute. No state is a simultaneous eigenstate of both.

For example, \(|0\rangle\) is an eigenstate of \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) (eigenvalue +1) but not of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\):

\[\begin{split} \hat{\sigma}^x |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = |1\rangle \neq \lambda |0\rangle\end{split}\]

The state is a superposition in the \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) basis:

\[ |0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|+\rangle + |-\rangle)\]

Discussion

Can a quantum state be a simultaneous eigenstate of two non-commuting operators in some special limiting case or under hidden conditions? We proved that two non-commuting Hermitian operators cannot share an eigenbasis. But what if we relax the problem: suppose we allow approximate eigenstates or mixed states. Does the fundamental barrier persist? Or is “no simultaneous eigenstates” merely a feature of pure states?

The Uncertainty Principle#

The Heisenberg uncertainty principle relates the uncertainties (standard deviations) in measurements of two non-commuting observables.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Theorem (Uncertainty Relation):

\[ \Delta \hat{A} \cdot \Delta \hat{B} \geq \frac{1}{2} |\langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle |\]

This fundamental inequality states: if two observables don’t commute, they cannot be simultaneously measured to arbitrary precision. The uncertainty is ontological—a property of nature itself, not epistemic—a limitation of measurement apparatus.

(30)#\[\]

where:

  • \(\Delta \hat{A} = \sqrt{\langle \hat{A}^2 \rangle - \langle \hat{A} \rangle^2}\) is the standard deviation of \(\hat{A}\)

  • \(\Delta \hat{B} = \sqrt{\langle \hat{B}^2 \rangle - \langle \hat{B} \rangle^2}\) is the standard deviation of \(\hat{B}\)

  • \(\langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle = \langle \psi | [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] | \psi \rangle\) is the expectation value of the commutator

Physical Interpretation: If two observables don’t commute (i.e., \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \neq 0\)), then we cannot simultaneously have small uncertainties in both measurements. This is not a limitation of our experimental technique—it is a fundamental property of quantum systems.

For \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^z\):

\[ [\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^z] = -2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^y\]

The uncertainty relation becomes:

(31)#\[ \Delta \hat{\sigma}^x \cdot \Delta \hat{\sigma}^z \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle -2\mathrm{i}\hat{\sigma}^y \rangle| = |\langle \hat{\sigma}^y \rangle|\]

Discussion

The uncertainty principle makes a quantitative prediction — test it on a specific state.

The Robertson uncertainty relation states \(\Delta \hat{A} \cdot \Delta \hat{B} \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle|\) for any state.

Consider the state \(|\psi\rangle = \cos(\theta/2)|{+z}\rangle + \sin(\theta/2)|{-z}\rangle\).

  • Compute \(\Delta \hat{\sigma}^x\) and \(\Delta \hat{\sigma}^z\) for this state as functions of \(\theta\).

  • Compute the right-hand side \(\frac{1}{2}|\langle [\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^z] \rangle| = |\langle \hat{\sigma}^y \rangle|\) for this state.

  • Show that the Robertson inequality is saturated (becomes an equality) at \(\theta = \pi/2\). What is special about this state?

  • Is there ANY state \(|\psi\rangle\) for which \(\Delta \hat{\sigma}^x = \Delta \hat{\sigma}^z = 0\) simultaneously? What does your calculation reveal?

Complementarity and Sequential Measurements#

The non-commutativity of observables has a striking experimental consequence.

Gedankenexperiment: Consider a qubit in state \(|0\rangle\) (spin-up along z).

Scenario A (measure only \(\hat{\sigma}^z\)):

  • Measure \(\hat{\sigma}^z\): get outcome +1 with probability 1.

  • Result: 100% of atoms are spin-up along z.

Scenario B (measure \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) first, then \(\hat{\sigma}^z\)):

  • Measure \(\hat{\sigma}^x\): get outcome \(+1\) with probability 1/2, outcome \(-1\) with probability 1/2.

    • If outcome is +1, state collapses to \(|+\rangle\).

    • If outcome is -1, state collapses to \(|-\rangle\).

  • Now measure \(\hat{\sigma}^z\):

    • Starting from \(|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\): outcomes \(\pm 1\) with equal probability (50-50).

    • Starting from \(|-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle)\): outcomes \(\pm 1\) with equal probability (50-50).

  • Result: Overall, 25% of atoms are spin-up along z, 75% spin-down (or by symmetry, 50-50).

Conclusion: The intermediate \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) measurement destroys the z-information. We cannot simultaneously be prepared with definite z-spin and then measure it with certainty after measuring x.

Discussion

Sequential measurement and the no-cloning theorem.

An ideal projective measurement of \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) on \(|{+x}\rangle = (|{+z}\rangle + |{-z}\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) gives \(+1\) or \(-1\) with equal probability. After the measurement, the state collapses.

  • If we wanted to “copy” the pre-measurement state \(|{+x}\rangle\) before measuring, we could measure it and prepare a fresh \(|{+x}\rangle\). Why does this NOT work for an unknown state? What is the no-cloning theorem?

  • Suppose Alice has a particle in the state \(\alpha|{+z}\rangle + \beta|{-z}\rangle\) and wants to tell Bob what \(|\alpha|^2\) is (the probability of measuring \(+z\)). Why must she measure MANY identically prepared copies of the state to determine \(|\alpha|^2\)? What is the minimum number of measurements needed to determine the state to precision \(\epsilon\)?

  • In the sequential measurement experiment: prepare \(|{+x}\rangle\), measure \(\hat{\sigma}^z\), obtain \(|{+z}\rangle\), then measure \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) again. What probability do you get for \(\hat{\sigma}^x = +1\)? Compare to measuring \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) twice without intermediate \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) measurement.

Observable Algebras and Quantum Complementarity#

Two observables are complementary if their eigenbases are “maximally different.” For a qubit:

  • \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) are complementary: if a state is an eigenstate of one, measurements of the other give completely random results (50-50).

  • This is because the eigenvectors form a mutually unbiased basis (MUB).

Measurement of complementary observables:

  • Measuring \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) gives a definite outcome: \(\langle \psi | \hat{\sigma}^z | \psi \rangle = \pm 1\).

  • Measuring \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) on the same state gives random 50-50 outcomes.

This reflects the fundamental indeterminacy of quantum mechanics: not all properties can have simultaneous definite values.

Summary#

  • Commutators quantify non-commutativity: \([\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0\) means the operators can be applied in any order.

  • Pauli matrices have non-zero commutation relations \([\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^j] = 2\mathrm{i}\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{\sigma}^k\), reflecting the incompatibility of spin components.

  • Non-commuting observables cannot share an eigenbasis; no state is a simultaneous eigenstate of both.

  • Uncertainty principle: \(\Delta \hat{A} \cdot \Delta \hat{B} \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle|\) expresses fundamental incompatibility, not measurement imprecision.

  • Complementarity: Measuring one observable destroys information about a complementary one. Sequential measurements of non-commuting observables yield different results than measuring only the first one.

1.3.2 Spin Dynamics#

Prompts

  • Why is the magnetic moment proportional to the spin? Derive the Hamiltonian for a spin-1/2 in a magnetic field.

  • A spin starts in \(|0\rangle\) (spin-up along z) in a static field \(B\) along z. Will it stay in \(|0\rangle\), or does it evolve? Explain in terms of energy eigenstates.

  • What is the Larmor frequency, and how does it relate to the precession of a classical magnetic dipole?

  • In the rotating wave approximation, why is resonance (drive frequency = Larmor frequency) special? What happens if you drive off-resonance?

  • Suppose you drive a qubit along the \(x\)-direction with Rabi frequency \(\Omega\) for time \(t = \pi/(2\Omega)\). Starting from \(|0\rangle\), what state do you end up in? (This is called a \(\pi/2\) pulse—why?)

  • Challenge: If you apply two pulses in sequence—first a \(\pi/2\) pulse along \(x\), then a \(\pi/2\) pulse along \(y\)—what overall rotation does the qubit undergo? Does order matter?

Lecture Notes#

Knowledge of how a quantum state evolves with time is the key to controlling it. The Schrödinger equation governs this evolution, and for spin systems in magnetic fields, it reveals a remarkably rich physics: spins precess at a natural frequency determined by the field strength, and by applying precisely timed pulses, we can rotate quantum states with exquisite precision. This interplay between natural evolution and engineered control is the foundation of quantum engineering, from atomic clocks to quantum computers.

Hamiltonian of a Spin in a Magnetic Field#

A spin-1/2 particle with magnetic moment \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) experiences an energy shift in an external magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B}\).

Classical Picture#

Classically, the energy of a magnetic dipole in a magnetic field is

\[ E = -\boldsymbol{\mu} \cdot \boldsymbol{B}\]

For a spin-1/2 particle, the magnetic moment is proportional to the spin:

\[ \boldsymbol{\mu} = \gamma \boldsymbol{S}\]

where \(\gamma\) is the gyromagnetic ratio. For an electron, \(\gamma = -g_e \mu_B / \hbar\), where \(g_e \approx 2\) is the g-factor and \(\mu_B = e\hbar/(2m_e) \approx 9.28 \times 10^{-24}\) J/T is the Bohr magneton.

#:::{admonition} Hamiltonian: Spin Field :class: important

The quantum Hamiltonian is

(45)#\[ \hat{H} = -\boldsymbol{\mu} \cdot \boldsymbol{B} = -\gamma \hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{B}\]

where we use the operator form \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} = (\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z)\) for the Pauli matrices.

For a magnetic field along the z-axis, \(\boldsymbol{B} = B_0 \hat{z}\):

\[ \hat{H} = -\gamma B_0 \hat{\sigma}^z\]

Hamiltonian: Matrix Form

(46)#\[\begin{split} \hat{H} = -\gamma B_0 \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -\gamma B_0 & 0 \\ 0 & \gamma B_0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Eigenvalues: \(E_\uparrow = -\gamma B_0\) (spin-up) and \(E_\downarrow = +\gamma B_0\) (spin-down).

Energy difference: \(\Delta E = E_\downarrow - E_\uparrow = 2\gamma B_0\)

Physical Interpretation

A spin aligned with the field (spin-up along z, in the direction of \(\boldsymbol{B}\)) has lower energy. A spin anti-aligned (spin-down, opposite to \(\boldsymbol{B}\)) has higher energy. The energy difference increases linearly with magnetic field strength.

Larmor Frequency

Define the Larmor frequency as

(47)#\[ \omega_0 = \gamma B_0\]

Then the Hamiltonian becomes

(48)#\[ \hat{H} = \frac{\hbar \omega_0}{2} \hat{\sigma}^z\]

(absorbing a factor of \(\hbar/2\) by convention, as is standard for qubits).

The Larmor frequency is the natural precession frequency of the spin around the magnetic field direction. It is fundamental in NMR, ESR, and quantum computing.

Example: Magnetic Field Strength

For a typical NMR field of \(B_0 = 1\) Tesla and a proton (\(\gamma \approx 2.67 \times 10^8\) rad/(s·T)):

\[ \omega_0 = 2.67 \times 10^8 \times 1 = 2.67 \times 10^8 \text{ rad/s} \approx 43 \text{ MHz}\]

This is why NMR operates at radiofrequency (RF) wavelengths.

Time Evolution in a Static Field#

For a Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \frac{\hbar\omega_0}{2}\hat{\sigma}^z\), the time-evolution operator is

(49)#\[ \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hat{H}t/\hbar} = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 \hat{\sigma}^z t/2}\]

Spectral Evaluation

Since \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) is diagonal with eigenvalues \(\pm 1\), we can evaluate this easily:

(50)#\[\begin{split} \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} |0\rangle\langle 0| + \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} |1\rangle\langle 1| = \begin{pmatrix} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Initial State: Spin-Up#

If we start with \(|\psi(0)\rangle = |0\rangle\) (spin-up), then:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{U}(t)|0\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|0\rangle\]

Note

The state acquires only a global phase and doesn’t evolve otherwise. It remains spin-up forever (with unit probability). Global phases are physically unobservable and do not affect measurement outcomes.

Initial State: Equal Superposition#

If we start with \(|\psi(0)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\), then:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|0\rangle + \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|1\rangle\right)\]

The relative phase between the two components is \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t}\). The state precesses around the z-axis at frequency \(\omega_0\) on the Bloch sphere.

Calculation: Larmor Precession

For the equal superposition state, the expectation values are:

Starting from \(|\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|0\rangle + \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|1\rangle)\):

\[ \langle \hat{\sigma}^x \rangle(t) = \frac{1}{2}\langle 0|\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} + \langle 1|\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}^x \cdot (\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|0\rangle + \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}|1\rangle)\]

After simplification:

\[ \langle \hat{\sigma}^x \rangle(t) = \cos(\omega_0 t)\]

Similarly, \(\langle \hat{\sigma}^y \rangle(t) = \sin(\omega_0 t)\) and \(\langle \hat{\sigma}^z \rangle(t) = 0\) (unchanged).

Note

The Bloch vector rotates around the z-axis at angular velocity \(\omega_0\). This is Larmor precession. The superposition state precesses but never decays to a definite eigenstate (in the absence of dissipation).

Discussion: Larmor Precession

Is Larmor precession truly “rotation”? When we say a spin precesses on the Bloch sphere, we imagine the spin vector rotating around the magnetic field direction. However, spin is fundamentally a quantum property, not a classical vector. The Bloch sphere representation is a visualization tool; the actual quantum state is a superposition in Hilbert space. In what sense does the spin “actually” precess? If we measure the spin repeatedly, does it stay in the same state or gradually rotate? What does Larmor precession tell us about the nature of quantum angular momentum versus classical angular momentum?

Driven Evolution: Rabi Oscillations#

In practice, we don’t just let the spin precess in a static field. We apply a driving field to manipulate the state. This is the basis of quantum control.

The Rabi Hamiltonian (Rotating Wave Approximation)#

Consider a qubit subject to a resonant microwave drive at frequency \(\omega_0\) (matching the Larmor frequency). In the rotating frame and under the rotating wave approximation (RWA), the Hamiltonian simplifies to:

Rabi Hamiltonian

(51)#\[ \hat{H}_{\text{RWA}} = \frac{\hbar\Omega}{2}\hat{\sigma}^x\]

where \(\Omega\) is the Rabi frequency, proportional to the drive field amplitude.

The rotating frame eliminates the fast oscillations at \(\omega_0\), leaving only the effect of the drive on the qubit.

Time Evolution in the RWA#

The time-evolution operator is

\[ \hat{U}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\Omega\sigma^x t/2}\]

Evaluation: Rabi Rotation

(52)#\[ \hat{U}(t) = \cos(\Omega t/2) I - i\sin(\Omega t/2)\hat{\sigma}^x\]

Note

This is a rotation around the x-axis on the Bloch sphere by angle \(\Omega t\).

Rabi Oscillations: Explicit Example#

Key Result: Starting from \(|\psi(0)\rangle = |0\rangle\), the system oscillates between \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) with probability:

\[ P_1(t) = \sin^2(\Omega t/2)\]

This shows complete population inversion at \(t = \pi/\Omega\), and returns to the initial state at \(t = 2\pi/\Omega\).

Derivation: Rabi Oscillations

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{U}(t)|0\rangle = \left(\cos(\Omega t/2) I - i\sin(\Omega t/2)\hat{\sigma}^x\right)|0\rangle\]

Since \(\hat{\sigma}^x |0\rangle = |1\rangle\):

(53)#\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \cos(\Omega t/2)|0\rangle - i\sin(\Omega t/2)|1\rangle\]

Probability in state \(|1\rangle\):

(54)#\[ P_1(t) = |\langle 1 | \psi(t) \rangle|^2 = \sin^2(\Omega t/2)\]

Physical Interpretation#

Key timescales for quantum gates:

  • \(\pi/2\)-pulse (\(t = \pi/(2\Omega)\)): \(P_1 = 1\) → creates equal superposition

  • \(\pi\)-pulse (\(t = \pi/\Omega\)): \(P_1 = 0\) → bit-flip (X gate, equivalent to \(\hat{\sigma}^x\))

  • \(2\pi\)-pulse (\(t = 2\pi/\Omega\)): \(P_1 = 0\) → one full oscillation period

The probability oscillates sinusoidally at frequency \(\Omega\) (the Rabi frequency).

Example: Quantum Gates

X-90 gate (\(t = \pi/(2\Omega)\)): Creates equal superposition

\[ |\psi\rangle = \cos(\pi/4)|0\rangle - i\sin(\pi/4)|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - i|1\rangle)\]

This is a 90° rotation around x-axis.

X gate (\(t = \pi/\Omega)\): Flips the state completely

\[ |\psi\rangle = \cos(\pi/2)|0\rangle - i\sin(\pi/2)|1\rangle = -i|1\rangle \approx |1\rangle\]

(global phase is ignored).

Discussion: Rabi Oscillations

What happens when you measure during Rabi oscillations? The equation \(P_1(t) = \sin^2(\Omega t/2)\) describes how the probability oscillates continuously without any measurement. But quantum mechanics postulates that measurement yields a definite outcome. If you perform a measurement at some intermediate time (say, at \(t = \pi/(4\Omega)\), where \(P_1 \approx 0.15\)), the measurement randomly projects the state onto either \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\). Successive measurements disturb this evolution. Does the interference pattern that drives the Rabi oscillations depend on the system remaining in a quantum superposition? Is measurement “back-action” destroying coherence, or is it the mechanism by which we observe the oscillations at all?

Generalization: Arbitrary Drive Directions#

If the drive is applied along the x-axis with Rabi frequency \(\Omega\), we can rotate around any direction \(\hat{n} = (n_x, n_y, n_z)\) by applying the appropriate combination of drive fields:

Arbitrary: Bloch Rotations

(55)#\[ \hat{U}(\theta, \hat{n}) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\theta \hat{n} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}/2} = \cos(\theta/2) I - i\sin(\theta/2) \hat{n} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\]

This is a rotation by angle \(\theta\) around the axis \(\hat{n}\) on the Bloch sphere. Any point on the Bloch sphere can be reached from any other by a suitable unitary rotation.

Discussion: Resonance

How do classical resonance and quantum Rabi oscillations differ fundamentally? In classical mechanics, a driven harmonic oscillator exhibits resonance when the driving frequency matches the natural frequency. The amplitude of oscillation grows linearly with time (in the absence of damping). In quantum mechanics, Rabi oscillations show sinusoidal oscillations between \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), with complete population inversion at resonance. Both involve a resonance condition (matching frequencies), yet the physics is qualitatively different. What is fundamentally quantum about Rabi oscillations that has no classical analog? Can a classical system exhibit complete population inversion, or is this a strictly quantum phenomenon?

Damping and Decoherence (Real Systems)#

In real devices, spins don’t oscillate forever. Two timescales characterize decoherence:

Decoherence Timescales

  1. \(T_1\) (spin-lattice relaxation): Energy decays to the environment. Excited state \(|1\rangle\) decays toward ground state \(|0\rangle\) on timescale \(T_1\).

  2. \(T_2\) (spin-spin dephasing): Coherence decays. The relative phase between \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) is lost on timescale \(T_2 \leq 2T_1\).

For Rabi oscillations to be observable, the pulse time must satisfy \(\Omega T_2 \gg 1\) (many oscillations within the coherence time).

Summary#

  • Hamiltonian for spin in a field: \(\hat{H} = \frac{\hbar\omega_0}{2}\hat{\sigma}^z\), where \(\omega_0 = \gamma B_0\) is the Larmor frequency.

  • Larmor precession: The spin’s Bloch vector precesses around the field direction at the Larmor frequency.

  • Rabi oscillations: Under resonant driving (RWA), the state oscillates between \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) at the Rabi frequency \(\Omega\).

  • Key pulse durations:

    • \(\pi/2\)-pulse (\(t = \pi/(2\Omega)\)): Creates equal superposition

    • \(\pi\)-pulse (\(t = \pi/\Omega)\): Flips the state (bit-flip gate)

  • Arbitrary rotations: Combining drives along different directions enables arbitrary unitary rotations of the Bloch sphere.

  • Decoherence limits: Real systems decay on timescales \(T_1\) and \(T_2\), setting ultimate limits on quantum gate fidelity.

Compute \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^x\rangle(t)\), \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^y\rangle(t)\), and \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^z\rangle(t)\) explicitly. Describe the motion of the Bloch vector.

Demonstrate that the probability of measuring spin-up or spin-down is independent of time. Why is the phase \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_0 t/2}\) called a “global phase” and why is it unobservable?

4. 6. The effective Rabi frequency becomes \(\tilde{\Omega} = \sqrt{\Delta^2 + \Omega^2}\). (a) What is the maximum population transfer \(P_1^\text{max}\) starting from \(|0\rangle\)? (b) Show that \(P_1^\text{max} = 1\) only on resonance (\(\Delta = 0\)).

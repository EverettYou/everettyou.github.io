3.2.2 Free Particle Propagator#

Prompts

  • How do we discretize the path integral via time-slicing and what Gaussian integrals appear?

  • What is the exact free-particle propagator and what does its \((T)^{-d/2}\) factor tell you physically?

  • Why does the propagator oscillate in phase while spreading in width—what quantum principle drives this?

  • How does the path integral result for the propagator connect to the Schrödinger equation?

  • Can you describe a physical process (diffraction pattern, wave packet evolution) where you’d actually measure this propagator?

Lecture Notes#

Definition

The propagator \(K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\) is the quantum amplitude for a particle to travel from position \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) at time \(t_i\) to position \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\) at time \(t_f\). For a free particle, the path integral can be evaluated exactly by summing over all trajectories, producing a Gaussian result that contains the full quantum information: spreading, interference, and the de Broglie wavelength.

Discussion: Wave Packet Spreading

Free-particle propagator evolves an initial delta function \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, 0) = \delta(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_i)\) into a spreading Gaussian. The width grows as \(\Delta x(t) \sim \sqrt{\hbar t / m}\). This spreading is fundamental:

  • If you precisely localize a particle to within \(\Delta x_0\) using position measurement, its momentum uncertainty grows to \(\Delta p \sim \hbar/\Delta x_0\) (uncertainty principle). After time \(t\), the wave packet width should be \(\Delta x(t) \sim \sqrt{\Delta p^2 t^2 / m^2 + \Delta x_0^2}\). Can you reconcile this with the free-particle propagator formula?

  • Quantum spreading (\(\Delta x \propto \sqrt{t}\)) is slower than classical diffusion (\(\Delta x \propto \sqrt{t}\), same scaling!) yet faster than ballistic expansion. Why does the diffusion-like \(\sqrt{t}\) behavior emerge quantum mechanically without any dissipation?

  • Consider a macroscopic baseball: \(m = 0.1\) kg, initially localized to \(\Delta x_0 = 1\) mm. How long does it take for quantum spreading to double the size? Does this explain why we don’t observe quantum superposition in macroscopic objects?

  • If you measure the particle’s momentum after initial position measurement, you prepare a momentum eigenstate with sharp \(p\). Its position uncertainty then grows as \(\Delta x(t) = \Delta x_0 + p t / m\) (no spreading factor!). Is this consistent with the propagator?

Time-Slicing: Discretizing the Path Integral#

The path integral is a functional integral—an infinite-dimensional sum over all paths \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\). To evaluate it, we discretize by dividing time into small intervals.

Setup#

Divide the time interval \([t_i, t_f]\) into \(N\) equal slices:

\[t_i = t_0 < t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_N = t_f, \quad \varepsilon = \frac{t_f - t_i}{N}\]

The path \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\) is represented by positions \(\boldsymbol{x}_n = \boldsymbol{x}(t_n)\) for \(n = 0, 1, \ldots, N\).

Action in a Time Slice#

For a path segment from \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1}\) (duration \(\varepsilon\)), approximate the action as if the particle moves at constant velocity:

\[S_n = \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} L(\boldsymbol{x}, \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}) \, \mathrm{d}t \approx L\left(\boldsymbol{x}_n, \frac{\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n}{\varepsilon}\right) \varepsilon\]

For a free particle with \(L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2\):

\[S_n = \frac{m}{2} \frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2}{\varepsilon}\]

The total action is

\[S = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} S_n = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{m}{2\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2\]

Sliced Path Integral#

The propagator becomes

\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_N, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_0, t_i) = \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_1 \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_2 \cdots \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_{N-1} \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2\right)\]

This is a product of exponentials of quadratic forms in the positions—perfect for Gaussian integrals.

Important Result

The fundamental Gaussian integral is

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{-ax^2} dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}, \quad \text{Re}(a) > 0\]

For complex exponents (relevant in quantum mechanics):

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}ax^2} dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{ia}} = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{|a|}}\]

where \(\sqrt{i} = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4} = (1+i)/\sqrt{2}\).

Shifted Gaussian: If the exponent is shifted:

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}a(x - x_0)^2} dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{ia}}\]

The shift doesn’t change the result—a key property that makes the calculation work.

Multi-Dimensional Gaussian: For a vector \(\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and a symmetric matrix \(A\):

\[\int d^d\boldsymbol{x} \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{x}^T A \boldsymbol{x}} = \sqrt{\frac{\pi^d i^d}{\det A}} = \left(\frac{\pi}{\mathrm{i}}\right)^{d/2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det A}}\]

Computing the Free-Particle Propagator#

We evaluate the sliced path integral by integrating out positions one by one.

Single-Slice Propagator#

The propagator from \(t_n\) to \(t_{n+1}\) is

\[K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}', \boldsymbol{x}; \varepsilon) = \int \mathrm{e}^{\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2} = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{d/2} \mathrm{e}^{\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}' - \boldsymbol{x})^2}\]

The normalization factor \((m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon)^{d/2}\) comes from the Gaussian integral formula.

Two-Slice Propagator#

Integrate over the intermediate position \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\):

\[K_{2\varepsilon} = \int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \, K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}_2, \boldsymbol{x}_1; \varepsilon) K_\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{x}_1, \boldsymbol{x}_0; \varepsilon)\]
\[= \int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^d \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}[(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_1)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2]\right)\]

Derivation: Two-Slice Gaussian Integration

Expand the exponent:

\[( \boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_1)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2 = 2\boldsymbol{x}_1^2 - 2(\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0) \cdot \boldsymbol{x}_1 + (\boldsymbol{x}_2^2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0^2)\]

Complete the square:

\[= 2\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \frac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2}\right)^2 + (\boldsymbol{x}_2^2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0^2) - \frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2}{2}\]
\[= 2\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \frac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2}\right)^2 + \frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2}{2}\]

The integral over \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\) (a shifted Gaussian) gives:

\[\int d^d\boldsymbol{x}_1 \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{\hbar\varepsilon}\left(\boldsymbol{x}_1 - \frac{\boldsymbol{x}_2 + \boldsymbol{x}_0}{2}\right)^2\right) = \left(\frac{\pi\hbar\varepsilon}{im}\right)^{d/2}\]

Combining with the remaining exponent and normalizations:

\[K_{2\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_2, \boldsymbol{x}_0; 2\varepsilon) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar(2\varepsilon)}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar(2\varepsilon)}(\boldsymbol{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{x}_0)^2\right)\]

Key observation: The time increases additively (\(\varepsilon + \varepsilon = 2\varepsilon\)), and the distance appears in the exponent with the total time. This pattern generalizes to \(N\) slices.

The Exact Free-Particle Propagator#

By induction (or by taking the continuum limit \(N \to \infty\)), the exact propagator for a free particle is:

The Free-Particle Propagator

\[K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar T}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\right)\]

where \(T = t_f - t_i\) is the total time interval and \(d\) is the spatial dimension.

Physical Interpretation#

Prefactor \((m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar T)^{d/2}\):

  • Scales as \(T^{-d/2}\) as time increases

  • For \(d=1\): \(K \propto 1/\sqrt{T}\) (wavepacket spreads as \(\sqrt{T}\))

  • For \(d=3\): \(K \propto 1/T^{3/2}\) (faster spreading in higher dimensions)

Exponent \(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\):

  • Equals the classical action for a particle traveling at constant velocity from \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) to \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\):

\[S_\text{cl} = \frac{m v^2}{2} T = \frac{m}{2T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\]

  • Contains the phase that oscillates according to the classical action

Probability density \(|K|^2\):

\[|K|^2 = \frac{m^d}{(2\pi\hbar T)^d} \propto T^{-d}\]

  • For \(d=1\): \(|K|^2 \propto 1/T\) (spreading like diffusion)

  • For \(d=3\): \(|K|^2 \propto 1/T^3\) (very rapid dilution)

The Propagator Satisfies the Schrödinger Equation

\[\mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial K}{\partial t_f} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}_f}^2 K\]

with initial condition

\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_i; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \delta^{(d)}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)\]

This connection shows that the path integral is equivalent to solving the Schrödinger equation. Verification by direct differentiation of \(K_\text{free}\) is straightforward but algebraically involved.

Wavepacket Spreading#

Initial Gaussian Wavepacket#

Consider an initial wavefunction that is a Gaussian wavepacket (minimum uncertainty state):

\[\psi_0(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left(\frac{1}{\pi \sigma_0^2}\right)^{d/4} \exp\left(-\frac{\boldsymbol{x}^2}{2\sigma_0^2} + \mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{k}_0 \cdot \boldsymbol{x}\right)\]

where \(\sigma_0\) is the initial width and \(\boldsymbol{k}_0 = \boldsymbol{p}_0/\hbar\) is the initial momentum.

Time-Evolved Wavepacket#

Using the free-particle propagator:

\[\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \int K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}, t; \boldsymbol{x}_0, 0) \, \psi_0(\boldsymbol{x}_0) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}_0\]

The integral of two Gaussians yields another Gaussian (up to a phase). The result is:

Time Evolution of a Gaussian Wavepacket

\[\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \text{normalization} \times \exp\left(-\frac{(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_\text{cm}(t))^2}{2\sigma(t)^2} + i\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\right)\]

where:

  • Center of mass: \(\boldsymbol{x}_\text{cm}(t) = \boldsymbol{x}_0 + (\boldsymbol{p}_0/m)t\) (classical trajectory)

  • Width: \(\sigma(t) = \sigma_0\sqrt{1 + (\hbar t/(2m\sigma_0^2))^2}\) (spreading)

  • Spreading time scale: \(\tau = 2m\sigma_0^2/\hbar\) (time for width to double)

Note: Quantum Spreading vs. Classical

A classical wavepacket has fixed width—the center moves but the shape doesn’t change. A quantum wavepacket spreads because different momentum components travel at different velocities.

For a macroscopic object (\(m \sim 1\) kg, \(\sigma_0 \sim 1\) mm), the spreading time is \(\tau \sim 10^{40}\) seconds—unobservable. For an electron (\(m \sim 10^{-30}\) kg, \(\sigma_0 \sim 10^{-10}\) m), \(\tau \sim 10^{-16}\) s—very observable. This is why microscopic systems show quantum behavior.

Note: Classical-Quantum Correspondence

The transition from quantum to classical mechanics is smooth. When the action \(S\) is much larger than \(\hbar\), the phase varies rapidly and only the stationary-action path survives quantum interference. This is the essence of the correspondence principle.

In quantum mechanics, the action \(S\) plays the role that phase \(\phi = S/\hbar\) plays in waves. The Planck constant \(\hbar\) sets the scale: when \(S \sim \hbar\), quantum effects are significant; when \(S \gg \hbar\), classical behavior dominates.

Summary#

  • Time-slicing: Discretize time into intervals; paths are represented by positions at each slice

  • Gaussian integrals: Evaluate the quadratic exponents using standard formulas; this is the computational workhorse

  • Free-particle propagator: Exact result is \((m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar T)^{d/2} \exp(\mathrm{i}m(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2/2\hbar T)\)

  • Wavepacket spreading: The amplitude decays as \(T^{-d/2}\); the width grows as \(\sqrt{1 + (\hbar t/2m\sigma_0^2)^2}\)

  • Schrödinger equivalence: The propagator satisfies the Schrödinger equation with a delta-function initial condition

  • Physical picture: Quantum mechanics governs the evolution of probability amplitudes; classical action determines the phase

Homework#

1. Time-Slicing Discretization

Consider dividing the time interval \([0, T]\) into \(N = 100\) equal slices. What is the time step \(\varepsilon\) if \(T = 1\) second? In the limit \(N \to \infty\), how does the discretized action \(S = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{m}{2\varepsilon}(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2\) approximate the continuous action \(S = \int_0^T \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 dt\)?

2. Gaussian Integral with Shifted Argument

Evaluate the integral:

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} a (x - x_0)^2} dx\]

where \(a\) is a real positive constant. Show that the result does not depend on \(x_0\). Why is this property crucial for evaluating the propagator?

3. Single-Slice Propagator in 1D

Using the formula \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} a x^2} dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{ia}}\), derive the single-slice propagator in one dimension:

\[K_\varepsilon(x', x; \varepsilon) = \int \mathrm{e}^{\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(x' - x)^2} dx = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{1/2} \mathrm{e}^{\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar\varepsilon}(x' - x)^2}\]

4. Dimensional Analysis of the Prefactor

The free-particle propagator in \(d\) dimensions has prefactor \((m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar T)^{d/2}\). Show using dimensional analysis that this factor must scale as \(T^{-d/2}\). What does this scaling tell you about how the wavepacket amplitude spreads in space?

5. Relation to Schrödinger Equation

The free-particle propagator satisfies the Schrödinger equation \(\mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial K}{\partial t_f} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}_f}^2 K\). Take the time derivative of the exact result:

\[K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, 0) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar t_f}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m}{2\hbar t_f}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\right)\]

and verify that it satisfies the Schrödinger equation (in the 1D case).

6. Probability Density and Spreading

For a free particle in one dimension, the probability density is \(|K|^2 \propto t^{-1}\). Use this to explain why a quantum wavepacket spreads faster than the amplitude decays. Compare to the case \(d=3\) where \(|K|^2 \propto t^{-3}\).

7. Initial Condition (Delta Function)

Show that the free-particle propagator approaches a delta function as \(t_f \to 0^+\):

\[\lim_{t \to 0^+} K_\text{free}(\boldsymbol{x}, t; \boldsymbol{x}_0, 0) = \delta^{(d)}(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_0)\]

Hint: Use the fact that \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{k} \cdot \boldsymbol{r}}\) integrates to a delta function.

8. Wavepacket Spreading Time

A Gaussian wavepacket has initial width \(\sigma_0 = 1\) nanometer. For an electron (\(m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}\) kg), what is the spreading time \(\tau = 2m\sigma_0^2/\hbar\) in seconds? Compare to the same quantity for a grain of dust with mass \(m = 10^{-12}\) kg and \(\sigma_0 = 1\) micrometer. What does this tell you about observing quantum spreading in macroscopic systems?

9. Classical Action and Phase

For a particle traveling from \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) to \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\) in time \(T\), the classical action is \(S_\text{cl} = \frac{m}{2T}(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2\) (constant-velocity motion). Show that the exponent in the free-particle propagator can be written as \(i S_\text{cl}/\hbar\). Why is the factor \(i\) (rather than \(-i\)) important for the physics?

10. Two-Particle Interference

The propagator to position \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\) includes contributions from all paths through intermediate position \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\). Show that integrating over \(\boldsymbol{x}_1\) using two applications of the single-slice propagator yields the correct two-step result. Explain in physical terms why different intermediate positions lead to quantum interference.

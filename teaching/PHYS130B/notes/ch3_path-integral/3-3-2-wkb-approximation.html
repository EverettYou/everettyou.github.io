3.3.2 WKB Approximation#

Prompts

  • What is the WKB ansatz and why does it work for slowly-varying potentials?

  • How does the WKB method derive the Hamilton-Jacobi equation and connection formulas?

  • What are turning points and why do they require special treatment (connection formulas)?

  • How does the WKB approximation describe tunneling through a potential barrier?

  • What is the validity condition for WKB and when does it break down?

Lecture Notes#

The WKB approximation treats the wavefunction’s phase and amplitude separately, with phase evolving like the classical action and amplitude diffusing quantum-mechanically. This semi-classical picture is extraordinarily powerful for understanding tunneling, scattering, and bound states when quantum effects are small corrections to classical behavior.

The WKB Ansatz: From Schrödinger to Hamilton-JacobiThe WKB (Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin) approximation is a systematic method for solving the Schrödinger equation when the potential varies slowly on the scale of the de Broglie wavelength.#

WKB AnsatzAssume the wavefunction has the form#

(221)#\[\psi(x, t) = A(x, t) \, e^{iS(x, t)/\hbar}\]

where:- \(S(x, t)\) is the action function (related to the classical action).- \(A(x, t)\) is the amplitude (slowly-varying).This is the WKB ansatz—a split between rapidly oscillating phase and slowly-varying envelope.

Deriving the Equations for \(S\) and \(A\):::{admonition} Derivation: WKB Equations from Schrödinger#

:class: dropdown note

Substitute into the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

(222)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2} + V(x)\psi\\$$Compute derivatives:\\$$\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} + i\frac{A}{\hbar}\frac{\partial S}{\partial t}\right) e^{iS/\hbar}\\$$$$\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2} = \left(\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial x^2} + 2i\frac{1}{\hbar}\frac{\partial A}{\partial x}\frac{\partial S}{\partial x} + i\frac{A}{\hbar}\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial x^2} - \frac{A}{\hbar^2}\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial x}\right)^2\right) e^{iS/\hbar}\\$$Substituting and canceling $e^{iS/\hbar}$:\\$$i\hbar\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} - A\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial x^2} - i\frac{\hbar}{m}\frac{\partial A}{\partial x}\frac{\partial S}{\partial x} - i\frac{A}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial x^2} + \frac{A}{2m}\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial x}\right)^2 + VA\\$$Separate into **imaginary** and **real** parts:**Imaginary part** (leading order, dominates for large $S/\hbar$):\\$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2m}\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial x}\right)^2 + V(x) = 0\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

This is the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, the classical equation for the action!Real part (higher order, corrects the amplitude):

(223)#\[\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{m}\frac{\partial A}{\partial x}\frac{\partial S}{\partial x} + \frac{A}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2im}\frac{\partial^2 A}{\partial x^2}\]

In the limit \(\hbar \to 0\), the right side vanishes and we get a continuity equation for the amplitude.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation in WKBFor a stationary state with energy \(E\), write \(\psi(x, t) = \phi(x) e^{-iEt/\hbar}\) and \(S(x, t) = S_0(x) - Et\). The Hamilton-Jacobi equation becomes:

(224)#\[E = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\frac{dS_0}{dx}\right)^2 + V(x)\]

Rearranging:

(225)#\[\frac{dS_0}{dx} = \pm \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))} = \pm p(x)\]

where \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))}\) is the classical momentum.

Solution in Classically Allowed RegionIn a region where \(E > V(x)\) (classically allowed), the momentum is real:

(226)#\[\phi(x) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{p(x)}} \exp\left(\pm\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_0^x p(x') dx'\right)\]

The \(\pm\) corresponds to waves traveling right (\(+\)) or left (\(-\)). The amplitude \(1/\sqrt{p}\) reflects probability conservation: where the momentum is small (slow particle), the wavefunction is large (must stay in the region longer).

Solution in Classically Forbidden RegionIn a region where \(E < V(x)\) (classically forbidden), the momentum is imaginary: \(p \to i\kappa\) where \(\kappa(x) = \sqrt{2m(V(x) - E)}\). Then:

(227)#\[\phi(x) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\kappa(x)}} \exp\left(\mp\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^x \kappa(x') dx'\right)\]

The wavefunction decays exponentially into the classically forbidden region. The characteristic penetration depth is

(228)#\[\lambda_\text{decay} = \frac{\hbar}{\kappa} = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2m(V - E)}}\]

Connection Formulas: Matching at Turning PointsA classical turning point occurs where \(E = V(x)\), so \(p = 0\). At such points, the WKB solutions diverge (\(\sim 1/\sqrt{p}\)) and must be matched carefully.

Simple Turning PointNear a simple turning point \(x_0\) where \(V(x) \approx V(x_0) + V'(x_0)(x - x_0)\):

(229)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}p(x) \approx \sqrt{2mV'(x_0)} \sqrt{x - x_0}\\$$The WKB solution oscillates in the allowed region ($x < x_0$) and decays in the forbidden region ($x > x_0$). The **connection formulas** relate the oscillating solution on one side to the decaying solution on the other:**Left of turning point** ($x < x_0$, allowed region):\\$$\psi(x) \approx \frac{C}{\sqrt{p(x)}} \sin\left(\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_x^{x_0} p(x') dx' + \frac{\pi}{4}\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Right of turning point (\(x > x_0\), forbidden region):

(230)#\[\psi(x) \approx \frac{C}{\sqrt{\kappa(x)}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_{x_0}^x \kappa(x') dx'\right)\]

The phase \(\pi/4\) is crucial—it accounts for the Airy function behavior near the turning point.

Tunneling Through a BarrierConsider a particle encountering a potential barrier of width \(w\) and height \(V_0 > E\). The WKB approximation gives the tunneling probability:

(231)#\[T \approx e^{-2\gamma}, \quad \gamma = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_a^b \kappa(x) dx\]

where the integral is from the left turning point \(a\) to the right turning point \(b\).For a square barrier (\(V(x) = V_0\) for \(0 < x < w\), \(V = 0\) elsewhere):

(232)#\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\gamma = \frac{w}{\hbar}\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}\\$$$$T \approx \exp\left(-\frac{2w}{\hbar}\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Tunneling rate:class: exampleFor an electron tunneling through a 1 nm barrier with height 1 eV above the ground state energy:

$$\gamma \approx (10^{-9} \text{ m})\sqrt{2(10^{-30} \text{ kg})(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J})} / (10^{-34} \text{ J·s}) \sim 5

$\(So \)T \sim e^{-10} \sim 10^{-5}\(, meaning about 1 in \)10^5$ attempts tunnel through. This is why quantum tunneling is rare but non-zero for macroscopic barriers, yet essential for atomic and nuclear processes.

Validity of WKBThe WKB approximation is valid when the potential varies slowly on the scale of the de Broglie wavelength:#

(233)#\[\left|\frac{dV}{dx}\right| \ll \frac{p^2}{m} = \frac{2m(E - V)}{m}\]

Or equivalently:

(234)#\[\left|\frac{d\ln p}{dx}\right| = \left|\frac{1}{p}\frac{dV}{dx}\right| \ll 1\]

This breaks down near:

  • Turning points where \(p \to 0\) (addressed by connection formulas).

  • Potential discontinuities (sharp steps).

  • Rapidly oscillating potentials (wavelength comparable to particle wavelength).For most smooth potentials, WKB accuracy is a few percent or better.

Action and phase:class: noteIn quantum mechanics, the action \(S\) plays the role that phase \(\phi = S/\hbar\) plays in waves. The Planck constant \(\hbar\) sets the scale: when \(S \sim \hbar\), quantum effects are significant; when \(S \gg \hbar\), classical behavior dominates.

Derivation: WKB Quantization Condition from Connection Formulas

For a particle bound in a potential well with classical turning points at \(x = a\) and \(x = b\) (where \(E = V(a) = V(b)\)), we must apply connection formulas at both turning points and require that the wavefunction remains single-valued (periodic) around a closed loop.

In the allowed region (\(a < x < b\)), the WKB wavefunction oscillates:

\[\phi(x) = \frac{C}{\sqrt{p(x)}} \sin\left(\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_a^x p(x') dx' + \frac{\pi}{4}\right)\]

At the right turning point (\(x = b\)), the connection formula relates this to the decaying solution in the forbidden region (\(x > b\)).

At the left turning point (\(x = a\)), the connection formula relates the oscillating solution to a decaying solution in the forbidden region (\(x < a\)).

For a closed orbit (particle bound between \(a\) and \(b\)), the phase accumulated going from \(a\) to \(b\) and back to \(a\) must be a multiple of \(2\pi\):

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}2 \int_a^b p(x) dx = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\\$$where $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ is the quantum number. The factor of $1/2$ comes from the phase shifts at each turning point (each contributes $\pi/4$ at entry and exit).\\Simplifying:\\$$\int_a^b p(x) dx = \pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

With \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))}\), this becomes the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition:

\[\int_a^b \sqrt{2m(E_n - V(x))} \, dx = \pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]

This condition determines the allowed energy levels \(E_n\) for a bound state. For large \(n\) (highly excited states), \(E_n \gg V_\text{min}\), and the formula reduces to the classical action integral.

Example: Harmonic Oscillator

For \(V(x) = \frac{m\omega^2}{2}x^2\), the integral yields

$$E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)

$$which is exactly the quantum result—WKB gives the exact answer for the harmonic oscillator despite being an approximation in general.

Summary

  • WKB ansatz: \(\psi = A e^{iS/\hbar}\) separates phase from amplitude.

  • Phase equation: \(S\) obeys the Hamilton-Jacobi equation—classical mechanics emerges.

  • Momentum and wavelength: \(p = \partial S/\partial x\); WKB wavelength is local (\(\lambda = 2\pi\hbar/p(x)\)).

  • Allowed region: Wavefunction oscillates as \(1/\sqrt{p} \sin(\int p dx/\hbar + \text{phase})\).

  • Forbidden region: Wavefunction decays exponentially as \(1/\sqrt{\kappa} \exp(-\int \kappa dx/\hbar)\).

  • Connection formulas: Match oscillating and decaying solutions across turning points with proper phase shifts.

  • Tunneling: Probability \(T \approx \exp(-2\int\kappa dx/\hbar)\) is exponentially suppressed by barrier width and height.

  • Validity: Excellent for slowly-varying potentials; breaks down near turning points (remedied by connection formulas) and sharp features.

3.1.3 Quantum Mechanics as Optics#

Prompts

  • What is the de Broglie relation and how does it make matter waves consistent with particle properties?

  • How are the Hamilton-Jacobi equation (classical) and the eikonal equation (wave) related by replacing action with phase?

  • What is the probability amplitude and how does it connect to classical action via \(\psi \propto \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\)?

  • Why is the Schrödinger equation the analog of the wave equation for matter, and how does it emerge from action?

  • How does quantization by interference generalize Bohr’s orbital model to arbitrary potentials?

Lecture Notes#

De Broglie’s insight unifies matter and light by assigning waves to particles, with wavelength inversely proportional to momentum. This transforms the Schrödinger equation into a wave equation for matter, where quantization arises naturally from the requirement that the wavefunction be single-valued around closed orbits—the Bohr-Sommerfeld condition.

Discussion: Wave-Geometric Duality in Quantum and Optics

Schrödinger equation has the same form as the wave equation (with \(\hbar\omega\) instead of \(c^2 k^2\)), and action enters the phase just like optical phase. This suggests deep structural kinship:

  • In geometric optics, rays follow paths determined by Fermat’s principle (stationary optical path). In classical mechanics, particles follow Euler-Lagrange equations (stationary action). Are these two instances of the same principle?

  • When light passes through a potential (varying refractive index), the ray bends via Snell’s law. When a quantum particle encounters a potential, its wavelength changes via \(p = h/\lambda\). Can these be unified in a single framework?

  • Consider a wave packet of light entering a dispersive medium (refractive index depends on frequency). The wave packet spreads and disperses. Does a quantum wavepacket in a potential behave similarly? Can you draw parallels between optical and quantum dispersion?

  • The eikonal approximation (geometric limit) applies when wavelength is small compared to obstacle size. For quantum mechanics, \(\lambda = h/p\) becomes small when momentum is large. Does this explain why macroscopic objects follow classical trajectories?

The Matter Wave: De Broglie Relation#

At the heart of quantum mechanics lies a simple but profound observation:

De Broglie Hypothesis (1924)

Particles possess wave properties with wavelength

(143)#\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{2\pi\hbar}{p}\]

and frequency

(144)#\[ f = \frac{E}{h}, \quad \text{or} \quad \omega = \frac{E}{\hbar}\]

where \(p\) is the particle’s momentum, \(E\) is its energy, and \(h = 2\pi\hbar\) is Planck’s constant.

This is the inverse of Planck-Einstein relations. Whereas light (a wave) carries momentum \(p = \hbar k\) and energy \(E = \hbar\omega\), matter (a particle) exhibits waves with those same relations.

Experimental confirmation: Davisson and Germer (1927) passed electrons through a crystal and observed diffraction patterns—proof that electrons behave as waves. The de Broglie wavelength was confirmed to atomic precision.

Wave Vector and Momentum#

For a plane wave \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(\boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{r} - \omega t)}\):

(145)#\[ \text{Spatial oscillation: } \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} \quad \Rightarrow \quad p = \hbar k\]
(146)#\[ \text{Temporal oscillation: } T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \quad \Rightarrow \quad E = \hbar\omega\]

The wave vector \(\boldsymbol{k}\) is related to momentum as \(\boldsymbol{p} = \hbar\boldsymbol{k}\). A particle with definite momentum \(p\) has definite wavelength \(\lambda = 2\pi\hbar/p\).

Hamilton-Jacobi Meets Eikonal: From Action to Wave#

Classical mechanics is based on the principle of stationary action:

(147)#\[ \delta S[\boldsymbol{x}] = 0, \quad S = \int L(\boldsymbol{x}, \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}) \, \mathrm{d}t\]

For a non-relativistic particle with Lagrangian \(L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 - V(\boldsymbol{x})\), the Hamiltonian is \(H = \frac{\boldsymbol{p}^2}{2m} + V(\boldsymbol{x})\), and the Hamilton-Jacobi equation for the action \(S(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\) is

(148)#\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2m}(\nabla S)^2 + V(\boldsymbol{x}) = 0\]

where \(\boldsymbol{p} = \nabla S\) (by the definition of conjugate momentum).

The Quantum Replacement: \(S \to \hbar \Phi\)#

In quantum mechanics, we promote the action to a phase function:

(149)#\[ S(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \longrightarrow \hbar \, \Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\]

And the wavefunction is written as

(150)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = A(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)}\]

where \(A\) is the amplitude and \(\Phi\) is the phase (in radians, dimensionless). The physical phase carried by the wavefunction is \(\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\).

Connecting to the Schrödinger Equation#

Substitute \(\psi = A \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\) into the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

(151)#\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} abla^2 \psi + V(\boldsymbol{x}) \psi\]

Computing derivatives:

\[ \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} + iA\frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial t}\right) \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\]
\[ abla \psi = ( abla A + iA abla\Phi) \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\]
\[ abla^2 \psi = ( abla^2 A + 2i( abla A)( abla\Phi) + iA abla^2\Phi - A( abla\Phi)^2) \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\]

Derivation: Phase and Amplitude Equations

Substituting into the Schrödinger equation and factoring out \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\):

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} - \hbar A\frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}( abla^2 A + 2i( abla A)( abla\Phi) + iA abla^2\Phi - A( abla\Phi)^2) + VA\]

Separating real and imaginary parts:

Imaginary part (leading order for slowly-varying amplitude):

(152)#\[ \frac{\partial\Phi}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2m}( abla\Phi)^2 + V = 0\]

This is exactly the Hamilton-Jacobi equation with \(\Phi = S/\hbar\)! The phase evolution follows the classical action principle.

Real part (amplitude evolution):

(153)#\[ \frac{\partial A^2}{\partial t} + abla \cdot (A^2 abla\Phi/m) = 0\]

This is a continuity equation for probability density \(\rho = |\psi|^2 = A^2\), with velocity \(\boldsymbol{v} = (\hbar abla\Phi)/m\) (the classical momentum divided by mass).

Quantum Mechanical Picture

The decomposition \(\psi = A \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\) reveals the quantum mechanical content:

  • Phase \(\Phi\): Obeys the Hamilton-Jacobi equation—evolves classically.

  • Amplitude \(A\): Determines probability density \(|\psi|^2 = A^2\)—spreads and disperses due to quantum effects.

In the classical limit where quantum spreading is negligible, both amplitude and phase become sharply localized to the classical trajectory.

The Wavefunction as Probability Amplitude#

The Born rule postulates that the probability density for finding a particle at position \(\boldsymbol{x}\) at time \(t\) is

(154)#\[ P(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = |\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)|^2\]

The wavefunction is a probability amplitude—its modulus squared gives probability density.

Plane Wave: Free Particle#

For a free particle with momentum \(p\) and energy \(E = p^2/(2m)\), the wavefunction is

(155)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = N \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(\boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{x} - \omega t)}\]

where \(k = p/\hbar\) and \(\omega = E/\hbar\). The phase is \(\Phi = \boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{x} - \omega t\).

Probability density: \(|\psi|^2 = |N|^2\) is uniform in space—a definite momentum state is completely delocalized! This is the uncertainty principle: definite momentum means completely uncertain position.

Wavepacket: Superposition of Momentum Eigenstates#

To localize a particle, we superpose plane waves with nearby momenta:

(156)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \int d^3\boldsymbol{k} \, \phi(\boldsymbol{k}) \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(\boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{x} - \omega_k t)}\]

where \(\phi(\boldsymbol{k})\) is the momentum distribution and \(\omega_k = \hbar k^2/(2m)\) for a free particle. The width in position space is inversely related to the width in momentum space: \(\Delta x \Delta p \gtrsim \hbar/2\) (Heisenberg uncertainty).

Quantization by Interference: Bohr’s Model Generalized#

In the Bohr model of hydrogen, the electron orbits the nucleus with quantized angular momenta. The condition is that an integer number of de Broglie wavelengths fit around the orbit:

(157)#\[ 2\pi r = n \lambda = n \frac{h}{p}\]

This leads to quantized orbits and quantized energy levels.

General Quantization Rule#

For any bound state in a potential \(V(\boldsymbol{x})\), quantization arises from the requirement that the wavefunction be single-valued and well-behaved. The wavefunction must satisfy:

  1. Wave equation: It obeys the Schrödinger equation.

  2. Boundary conditions: It vanishes at infinity (or is periodic for periodic potentials).

  3. Single-valuedness: As we traverse a closed path in configuration space, the phase must increase by a multiple of \(2\pi\).

In the semiclassical limit, this translates to the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule:

(158)#\[ \oint \boldsymbol{p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{x} = 2\pi\hbar(n + \delta), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

where the integral is around a closed classical orbit and \(\delta\) is a phase correction (typically \(1/2\)).

Why Quantization Works

Quantization emerges because the phase accumulated around a closed orbit must be a multiple of \(2\pi\) for the wavefunction to be single-valued. Since the phase is \(\int \boldsymbol{p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{x} / \hbar\), we get

\[ \frac{1}{\hbar} \oint \boldsymbol{p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{x} = 2\pi(n + \delta)\]

This condition selects discrete energies—the bound-state energies—without assuming any additional quantum postulates beyond the Schrödinger equation and single-valuedness of the wavefunction.

The Schrödinger Equation as the Wave Equation for Matter#

Just as Maxwell’s equations govern light waves, the Schrödinger equation governs matter waves:

(159)#\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi + V(\boldsymbol{x}) \psi\]

Structure:

  • Left side: time derivative of \(\psi\) (wave propagation in time).

  • First term on right: kinetic energy (from the Laplacian—spatial curvature of the wavefunction).

  • Second term on right: potential energy.

This is the quantum mechanical equation of motion. It has the same role as Newton’s \(F = ma\) in classical mechanics or Maxwell’s equations in electrodynamics.

Energy Eigenstates#

For stationary states with energy \(E\), we separate variables: \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}Et/\hbar}\). Substituting into the Schrödinger equation gives the time-independent Schrödinger equation:

(160)#\[ E \phi(\boldsymbol{x}) = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \phi + V(\boldsymbol{x}) \phi\]

This is a Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue problem. Only discrete energies \(E_n\) (eigenvalues) produce normalizable solutions (eigenfunctions). These are the energy levels.

The Path Integral as Quantum Optics#

Huygens’ principle for light says: the amplitude at a point is the superposition of wavelets from all intermediate points on the wavefront, each weighted by its phase.

For matter, the path integral is the analogous principle: the quantum amplitude from one spacetime point to another is the superposition of amplitudes from all possible paths connecting them, each weighted by the phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\).

(161)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}_i \to \boldsymbol{x}_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \, \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\]

where \(S\) is the classical action and the integral is a functional integral over all paths.

Connection to optics:

  • Optical path length \(L = \int n \, \mathrm{d}s\) → Classical action \(S = \int L \, \mathrm{d}t\)

  • Refractive index \(n\) → Lagrangian \(L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 - V\)

  • Light wavelength \(\lambda\) → de Broglie wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\)

  • Huygens’ principle → Path integral

Summary#

  • De Broglie relation: Particles have wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\) and frequency \(f = E/h\)—unifying particle and wave pictures.

  • Hamilton-Jacobi to Schrödinger: The phase of the wavefunction obeys the classical Hamilton-Jacobi equation; the amplitude evolves via a continuity equation.

  • Probability amplitude: The wavefunction \(\psi\) is a complex amplitude; \(|\psi|^2\) is probability density.

  • Quantization by interference: Single-valuedness of \(\psi\) around closed orbits enforces the Bohr-Sommerfeld rule.

  • Schrödinger equation: The quantum equation of motion, analogous to Maxwell’s equations for light or Newton’s laws for particles.

  • Path integral principle: Quantum amplitude is a sum over all paths weighted by \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\)—Huygens’ principle for matter waves.

(Concept: phase obeys classical dynamics)

3. A wavefunction is written as \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = A(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(\boldsymbol{x}, t)}\). If the probability density is uniform in space at all times, what can you conclude about \(A(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\)? What momentum state does this correspond to? (Concept: amplitude-phase decomposition and definite momentum states)

4. For the wavefunction \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = N \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(\boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{x} - \omega t)}\) with \(\omega = \hbar k^2/(2m)\), verify that \(\mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 \psi\) (i.e., the plane wave solves the free-particle Schrödinger equation). (Concept: plane waves are energy eigenstates)

5. A particle is confined to move around a closed circular orbit of radius \(r\). Write down the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition \(\oint \boldsymbol{p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{x} = 2\pi\hbar(n + \delta)\) for this orbit and show that for a particle with constant speed \(p\), it reduces to \(2\pi r p = 2\pi\hbar(n + \delta)\). Interpret this as a condition on the de Broglie wavelength. (Concept: single-valuedness of wavefunction selects discrete energies)

6. Explain why a superposition of two plane waves with different momenta, \(\psi \propto \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{k}_1\cdot\boldsymbol{x}} + \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{k}_2\cdot\boldsymbol{x}}\), produces an interference pattern with spatial oscillations. 7. Show that this equation follows from the decomposition \(\psi = A \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}\) and the Schrödinger equation. What is the physical interpretation of \(\boldsymbol{v}\)? (Concept: probability flows with the phase-defined velocity)

Draw a rough analogy between this principle and the path integral expression \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f) \propto \int_{\boldsymbol{x}_i \to \boldsymbol{x}_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\). What role does the action \(S\) play in the quantum path integral? (Concept: path integral as quantum Huygens’ principle)

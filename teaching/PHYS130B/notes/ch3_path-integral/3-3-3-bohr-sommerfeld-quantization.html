3.3.3 Bohr-Sommerfeld Quantization#

Prompts

  • What is the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule and how does it emerge from single-valuedness of the wavefunction?

  • How does the WKB approximation naturally lead to quantization without assuming discrete energy levels?

  • Why does the Bohr-Sommerfeld rule give exact results for the harmonic oscillator and why does it fail for other potentials?

  • What is the EBK (Einstein-Brillouin-Keller) quantization and how does it generalize Bohr-Sommerfeld?

  • How accurate is Bohr-Sommerfeld for realistic potentials like hydrogen and anharmonic oscillators?

Lecture Notes#

Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization reveals that discrete energy levels arise from a simple geometric condition: the phase accumulated around a closed classical orbit must equal an integer multiple of \(2\pi\). This bridges classical mechanics and quantum mechanics, explaining quantization without solving the Schrödinger equation exactly.

Quantization from Single-Valuedness#

In quantum mechanics, the wavefunction must be single-valued in configuration space. For a bound state (particle oscillating in a potential well), the wavefunction returns to itself after a complete cycle. This imposes a quantization condition on the allowed energies.

The Closed Orbit Condition#

For a bound state in a 1D potential, the particle oscillates between classical turning points \(x_a\) and \(x_b\) (where \(E = V(x)\)). Classically, the particle goes back and forth: \(x_a \to x_b \to x_a \to \cdots\).

In the WKB approximation, the phase accumulated on the left side of the barrier (from \(x_a\) to \(x_b\)) is

\[\Phi_R = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx\]

By symmetry, the phase on the return (from \(x_b\) to \(x_a\)) is the same. The total phase for a complete cycle is

\[\Phi_\text{total} = 2\Phi_R + \text{(phase shifts at turning points)}\]

At a simple turning point, the WKB connection formula introduces a phase shift of \(\pi/4\) (or \(\pi/2\) total, counting both turning points). Thus:

(235)#\[\Phi_\text{total} = \frac{2}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx + \frac{\pi}{2}\]

Single-Valuedness Requirement#

For the wavefunction to be single-valued after a complete oscillation, the total phase must be a multiple of \(2\pi\):

(236)#\[\Phi_\text{total} = 2\pi n, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

Therefore:

\[\frac{2}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx + \frac{\pi}{2} = 2\pi n\]

Simplifying (using a different convention for the quantum number):

(237)#\[\frac{1}{\hbar}\oint p(x) dx = 2\pi\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

This is the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule. The contour integral \(\oint\) is around the closed orbit in phase space.

Bohr-Sommerfeld Rule

For a 1D bound state, the allowed energies \(E_n\) satisfy

(238)#\[\oint p(x) dx = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]

where \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))}\) and the integral is around the complete classical orbit. The factor of \(1/2\) is the zero-point energy correction—even the ground state (\(n=0\)) has non-zero energy.

Example: Harmonic Oscillator

For the harmonic oscillator \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\):

Turning points: At \(E = V(x)\), we have \(\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2 = E\), so \(x = \pm\sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\). Let \(a = \sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\).

Momentum: \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2)} = m\omega\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}\).

Integral:

\[\oint p dx = 2\int_{-a}^{a} m\omega\sqrt{a^2 - x^2} dx = 2m\omega \cdot \frac{\pi a^2}{2} = \pi m\omega a^2 = \pi m\omega \cdot \frac{2E}{m\omega^2} = \frac{2\pi E}{\omega}\]

Quantization condition:

\[\frac{2\pi E_n}{\omega} = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]
(239)#\[E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

This is exactly the quantum mechanical result! For the harmonic oscillator, Bohr-Sommerfeld is exact.

admonition

The harmonic oscillator is special because its potential is quadratic. In the WKB approximation, the momentum \(p(x) \propto \sqrt{E - V}\) is piecewise linear in the integral bounds, making the integral calculable exactly. Moreover, the WKB phase shifts are exact for quadratic potentials. For higher-order potentials, WKB is an approximation.

Example: Hydrogen Atom (Bohr Model Revisited)

For the Coulomb potential \(V(r) = -e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r) = -k/r\) (in Gaussian units, \(V = -k/r\)), the circular orbit has quantized angular momentum \(L = mv_\text{cl}r = n_\phi\hbar\).

For a circular orbit, the WKB quantization in the radial direction gives an additional condition. The result, incorporating both the azimuthal and radial quantization, is

(240)#\[E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]

This agrees with the quantum mechanical result! However, Bohr-Sommerfeld requires careful treatment of the radial turning point and angular momentum quantization separately.

EBK Quantization: Integrable Systems#

The Einstein-Brillouin-Keller (EBK) quantization generalizes Bohr-Sommerfeld to systems with multiple degrees of freedom that are integrable (i.e., have as many constants of motion as degrees of freedom).

Multiple Degrees of Freedom#

For a system with \(N\) degrees of freedom and \(N\) action variables \(J_i\), the quantization condition is

(241)#\[J_i = \oint p_i dq_i = 2\pi\hbar(n_i + \alpha_i), \quad n_i = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

where \(\alpha_i\) is the Maslov index (phase correction), typically \(1/2\) for each degree of freedom with turning points.

Example: 2D Isotropic Oscillator

For the 2D harmonic oscillator \(V(x, y) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2)\), the energy in terms of action variables is

\[E = \frac{1}{2m}(p_x^2 + p_y^2) + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2)\]

Introducing action variables \(J_x = \oint p_x dx\) and \(J_y = \oint p_y dy\), and using them to eliminate the momenta:

\[E = \omega(J_x + J_y)/(2\pi)\]

With the EBK quantization \(J_i = 2\pi\hbar(n_i + 1/2)\):

(242)#\[E_{n_x, n_y} = \hbar\omega(n_x + n_y + 1), \quad n_x, n_y = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

Again, exact agreement with quantum mechanics.

Accuracy and Limitations#

When Bohr-Sommerfeld Works Well#

  1. Quadratic or near-quadratic potentials: Harmonic oscillators and slowly-varying deformations.

  2. Deep potential wells: Large quantum numbers \(n \gg 1\) (large \(S/\hbar\)).

  3. Separable systems: Multiple uncoupled degrees of freedom (use EBK).

  4. WKB-valid potentials: Smooth, slowly-varying (potential derivatives small on wavelength scale).

When Bohr-Sommerfeld Fails#

  1. Steep or discontinuous potentials: Violates WKB assumptions.

  2. Degeneracies: Degenerate states require careful treatment.

  3. Resonances: Coupled oscillators with irrational frequency ratios.

  4. Chaotic systems: Non-integrable dynamics; no action variables.

Accuracy for Hydrogen#

For hydrogen, Bohr-Sommerfeld gives exact energy levels but incorrect radial wave functions. The reason is subtle: the exact cancellation of errors in the action integral, combined with the spherical symmetry. For slightly different potentials (e.g., helium atom), Bohr-Sommerfeld accuracy drops to \(\sim 5-10\%\).

Improving Bohr-Sommerfeld: Higher-Order Corrections#

Beyond the leading WKB order, systematic corrections can be computed. These go as powers of \(1/n\) (for large quantum number \(n\)) and involve higher derivatives of the potential. For high-\(n\) states, WKB corrections typically improve accuracy to \(\sim 0.1\%\).

Connection to Path Integral#

The Bohr-Sommerfeld rule is a direct consequence of the path integral and stationary phase approximation. In the path integral for the propagator between turning points, only paths with stationary action survive. For a bound state, the action integral around the closed orbit encodes the quantization condition.

\[\text{Bohr-Sommerfeld} = \text{Stationary phase applied to periodic orbits}\]

This is why the path integral naturally bridges classical mechanics (action, orbits) and quantum mechanics (phase, quantization).

Summary#

  • Single-valuedness requirement: Wavefunction must return to itself after a complete orbit, quantizing the phase.

  • Bohr-Sommerfeld rule: \(\oint p dx = 2\pi\hbar(n + 1/2)\) determines allowed energies without solving the Schrödinger equation.

  • Zero-point energy: The \(1/2\) factor ensures non-zero ground-state energy, a quintessentially quantum effect.

  • Harmonic oscillator: Bohr-Sommerfeld is exact, giving \(E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)\).

  • Hydrogen atom: Bohr-Sommerfeld gives correct energy levels when applied carefully (azimuthal + radial quantization).

  • EBK quantization: Generalizes to integrable systems with multiple degrees of freedom.

  • Validity and accuracy: Excellent for high-\(n\) states and smooth potentials; fails for chaos and discontinuities.

  • Path integral origin: Bohr-Sommerfeld is the stationary-phase result for periodic orbits in the path integral.

3.3.3 Bohr-Sommerfeld Quantization#

Prompts

  • How does the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule emerge from requiring single-valuedness of the wavefunction?

  • Why does WKB naturally produce quantization without ever assuming discrete energy levels?

  • For what potentials is Bohr-Sommerfeld exact, and what do these potentials have in common?

  • How does the EBK (Einstein-Brillouin-Keller) rule generalize Bohr-Sommerfeld to multidimensional and non-integrable systems?

  • If Bohr-Sommerfeld gives exact results for harmonic oscillators, does that mean it captures all quantum effects for that potential?

Lecture Notes#

Definition

Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization reveals that discrete energy levels arise from a simple geometric condition: the phase accumulated around a closed classical orbit must equal an integer multiple of \(2\pi\). This bridges classical mechanics and quantum mechanics, explaining quantization without solving the Schrödinger equation exactly.

Quantization from Single-Valuedness#

In quantum mechanics, the wavefunction must be single-valued in configuration space. For a bound state (particle oscillating in a potential well), the wavefunction returns to itself after a complete cycle. This imposes a quantization condition on the allowed energies.

The Closed Orbit Condition#

For a bound state in a 1D potential, the particle oscillates between classical turning points \(x_a\) and \(x_b\) (where \(E = V(x)\)). The phase accumulated on the left side of the barrier (from \(x_a\) to \(x_b\)) is

\[\Phi_R = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx\]

By symmetry, the phase on the return (from \(x_b\) to \(x_a\)) is the same. The total phase for a complete cycle is

\[\Phi_\text{total} = \frac{2}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx + \frac{\pi}{2}\]

At a simple turning point, the WKB connection formula introduces a phase shift of \(\pi/4\) (or \(\pi/2\) total, counting both turning points).

Discussion: Semi-Classical Quantization

Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition \(\oint p \, \mathrm{d}x = 2\pi\hbar(n + 1/2)\) emerges from WKB by requiring single-valued wavefunction around a closed orbit. Yet it looks almost classical (an integral around a closed path). This raises foundational questions:

  • Bohr-Sommerfeld correctly predicts energy levels for hydrogen (like the quantum result for \(n\) levels). For more complex atoms, it fails. Why does it work for hydrogen but not for helium?

  • The factor \(1/2\) (zero-point energy) comes from phase shifts at turning points. Is this factor purely quantum, or does it have a semi-classical interpretation?

  • For a chaotic (non-integrable) system, the Bohr-Sommerfeld condition doesn’t apply (no longer valid action variables). Yet quantum mechanics still works. How does quantum mechanics handle chaos when semi-classical intuition fails?

  • Can you design a potential where Bohr-Sommerfeld gives an exact result (matching the full quantum spectrum exactly)? What special property must the potential have?

Single-Valuedness Requirement#

For the wavefunction to be single-valued after a complete oscillation, the total phase must be a multiple of \(2\pi\):

\[\Phi_\text{total} = 2\pi n, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

Therefore:

\[\frac{2}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx + \frac{\pi}{2} = 2\pi n\]

Simplifying:

\[\oint p(x) dx = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

The Bohr-Sommerfeld Rule

For a 1D bound state, the allowed energies \(E_n\) satisfy

\[\oint p(x) dx = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]

where \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))}\) and the integral is around the complete classical orbit. The factor of \(1/2\) is the zero-point energy correction—even the ground state (\(n=0\)) has non-zero energy.

Example: Harmonic Oscillator

For the harmonic oscillator \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\):

Turning points: At \(E = V(x)\), we have \(x = \pm a\) where \(a = \sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\).

Momentum: \(p(x) = m\omega\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}\).

Integral:

\[\oint p dx = 2\int_{-a}^{a} m\omega\sqrt{a^2 - x^2} dx = \pi m\omega a^2 = \frac{2\pi E}{\omega}\]

Quantization condition:

\[\frac{2\pi E_n}{\omega} = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]
\[E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

This is exactly the quantum mechanical result! For the harmonic oscillator, Bohr-Sommerfeld is exact.

Note: Why is the Harmonic Oscillator Special?

The harmonic oscillator is special because its potential is quadratic. In the WKB approximation, the momentum \(p(x) \propto \sqrt{E - V}\) leads to an integral that is tractable exactly. Moreover, the WKB phase shifts are exact for quadratic potentials. For higher-order potentials, WKB and Bohr-Sommerfeld are excellent approximations but not exact.

Example: Hydrogen Atom (Bohr Model)

For the Coulomb potential \(V(r) = -k/r\) with circular orbits, angular momentum quantization \(L = n_\phi\hbar\) combined with radial quantization yields

\[E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]

This agrees with the quantum mechanical result! However, Bohr-Sommerfeld requires careful treatment of the radial turning point and angular momentum quantization separately. The result shows that Bohr-Sommerfeld works even for non-separable or complicated potentials when applied systematically.

EBK Quantization: Integrable Systems#

The Einstein-Brillouin-Keller (EBK) quantization generalizes Bohr-Sommerfeld to systems with multiple degrees of freedom that are integrable (i.e., have as many constants of motion as degrees of freedom).

Multiple Degrees of Freedom#

For a system with \(N\) degrees of freedom and \(N\) action variables \(J_i\), the quantization condition is

\[J_i = \oint p_i dq_i = 2\pi\hbar(n_i + \alpha_i), \quad n_i = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

where \(\alpha_i\) is the Maslov index (phase correction), typically \(1/2\) for each degree of freedom with turning points.

Example: 2D Isotropic Oscillator

For the 2D harmonic oscillator \(V(x, y) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2)\), with action variables \(J_x = \oint p_x dx\) and \(J_y = \oint p_y dy\) and using EBK quantization \(J_i = 2\pi\hbar(n_i + 1/2)\):

\[E_{n_x, n_y} = \hbar\omega(n_x + n_y + 1), \quad n_x, n_y = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\]

Again, exact agreement with quantum mechanics. EBK correctly captures the degeneracies and energy level structure.

Accuracy and Limitations#

When Bohr-Sommerfeld Works Well#

  1. Quadratic or near-quadratic potentials: Harmonic oscillators and slowly-varying deformations

  2. Deep potential wells: Large quantum numbers \(n \gg 1\) (large \(S/\hbar\))

  3. Separable systems: Multiple uncoupled degrees of freedom (use EBK)

  4. WKB-valid potentials: Smooth, slowly-varying (potential derivatives small on wavelength scale)

When Bohr-Sommerfeld Fails#

  1. Steep or discontinuous potentials: Violates WKB assumptions

  2. Degeneracies: Degenerate states require careful treatment

  3. Resonances: Coupled oscillators with irrational frequency ratios

  4. Chaotic systems: Non-integrable dynamics; no action variables

Accuracy for Hydrogen#

For hydrogen, Bohr-Sommerfeld gives exact energy levels but incorrect radial wave functions. The reason is subtle: exact cancellation of errors in the action integral, combined with spherical symmetry. For slightly different potentials (e.g., helium atom), Bohr-Sommerfeld accuracy drops to \(\sim 5\text{-}10\%\).

Improving Bohr-Sommerfeld: Higher-Order Corrections#

Beyond the leading WKB order, systematic corrections can be computed. These go as powers of \(1/n\) (for large quantum number \(n\)) and involve higher derivatives of the potential. For high-\(n\) states, WKB corrections typically improve accuracy to \(\sim 0.1\%\).

Connection to Path Integral#

The Bohr-Sommerfeld rule is a direct consequence of the path integral and stationary phase approximation. In the path integral for the propagator between turning points, only paths with stationary action survive. For a bound state, the action integral around the closed orbit encodes the quantization condition.

\[\text{Bohr-Sommerfeld} = \text{Stationary phase applied to periodic orbits}\]

This is why the path integral naturally bridges classical mechanics (action, orbits) and quantum mechanics (phase, quantization).

Note: Classical Limit and Correspondence Principle

In the classical limit (\(n \gg 1\)), the energy spacing \(\Delta E = E_{n+1} - E_n = \hbar\omega\) should match the classical frequency via the correspondence principle. For the harmonic oscillator, this is exact. For general potentials with Bohr-Sommerfeld, the energy level spacing approaches the classical oscillation frequency, confirming the smooth transition to classical mechanics.

Summary#

  • Single-valuedness requirement: Wavefunction must return to itself after a complete orbit, quantizing the phase

  • Bohr-Sommerfeld rule: \(\oint p dx = 2\pi\hbar(n + 1/2)\) determines allowed energies without solving the Schrödinger equation

  • Zero-point energy: The \(1/2\) factor ensures non-zero ground-state energy, a quintessentially quantum effect

  • Harmonic oscillator: Bohr-Sommerfeld is exact, giving \(E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)\)

  • Hydrogen atom: Bohr-Sommerfeld gives correct energy levels when applied carefully (azimuthal + radial quantization)

  • EBK quantization: Generalizes to integrable systems with multiple degrees of freedom

  • Validity and accuracy: Excellent for high-\(n\) states and smooth potentials; fails for chaos and discontinuities

  • Path integral origin: Bohr-Sommerfeld is the stationary-phase result for periodic orbits in the path integral

  • Classical correspondence: Energy level spacing matches classical frequency, confirming the semiclassical limit

Homework#

1. (Bohr-Sommerfeld for Particle in a Box) A particle of mass \(m\) is confined in a 1D box of width \(a\) with infinite potential walls at \(x=0\) and \(x=a\).

(a) Inside the box, the potential is \(V(x) = 0\) and \(p(x) = \sqrt{2mE}\). Show that the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition

\[\oint p(x) dx = 2\pi\hbar\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]

gives \(E_n = \frac{\pi^2\hbar^2 n^2}{2ma^2}\) (note the factor of \(n\), not \(n+1/2\)). Why does this differ from the exact quantum result by a factor in \(n\)?

(b) Explain why the WKB phase shifts at the hard walls are different from those at soft turning points. What is the correct Maslov index for this system?

2. (Linear Potential) Consider a particle in a linear potential \(V(x) = F x\) for \(x \geq 0\) and \(V(x) = \infty\) for \(x < 0\) (Airy potential).

(a) At energy \(E\), the turning point is at \(x_a = E/F\). Show that

\[\int_0^{x_a} p(x) dx = \int_0^{E/F} \sqrt{2m(E - Fx)} dx = \frac{2}{3F}(2mE)^{3/2}\]

(b) Apply the Bohr-Sommerfeld rule to find the energy levels \(E_n\). How do the spacings \(\Delta E_n = E_{n+1} - E_n\) scale with \(n\)?

(c) Sketch the potential and the first two energy levels. Is the ground state energy zero or non-zero? Discuss.

3. (Beyond Bohr-Sommerfeld — WKB Corrections) The next-order correction to Bohr-Sommerfeld arises from higher-order WKB terms. For a potential with two turning points \(x_a\) and \(x_b\), the leading correction is approximately

\[\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_{x_a}^{x_b} p(x) dx \approx \pi\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{24}\left(\frac{1}{p(x_a)} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{dx}\bigg|_{x_a} - \frac{1}{p(x_b)} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{dx}\bigg|_{x_b}\right)\]

For the harmonic oscillator \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\):

(a) Compute \(\frac{\mathrm{d}V}{dx}\) at the turning points \(x = \pm a = \pm\sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\) and \(p(\pm a) = 0\). What goes wrong with the correction formula at the turning points?

(b) Explain why Bohr-Sommerfeld is exact for the harmonic oscillator and doesn’t require WKB corrections. (Hint: think about which WKB orders contribute.)

4. (Anharmonic Oscillator — Qualitative Analysis) Consider a potential \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2 + \lambda x^4\) with \(\lambda > 0\) (quartic anharmonicity).

(a) For small \(\lambda\), the turning points are approximately at \(x_{\pm} \approx \pm\sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)}\) (same as harmonic oscillator). Explain why the Bohr-Sommerfeld integral will differ from the harmonic result.

(b) Will the energy levels of the anharmonic oscillator be more or less spaced than the harmonic oscillator? Use a physical argument (does the fourth-order term make the well steeper or shallower?).

(c) For which energy range (low \(n\), high \(n\), or both) will Bohr-Sommerfeld for the anharmonic oscillator be less accurate than for the harmonic oscillator? Why?

5. (EBK Quantization — 2D Anisotropic Oscillator) A particle moves in a 2D anisotropic harmonic potential

\[V(x, y) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega_x^2 x^2 + \frac{1}{2}m\omega_y^2 y^2\]

with \(\omega_x \neq \omega_y\). The system is integrable with action variables \(J_x\) and \(J_y\).

(a) For the \(x\)-direction alone, \(\oint p_x dx = 2\pi\hbar(n_x + 1/2)\). Similarly for \(y\). Find the total energy \(E_{n_x, n_y}\) in terms of quantum numbers \(n_x, n_y\) and the frequencies.

(b) Suppose \(\omega_x = 2\omega_y = 2\omega\). Find the first five energy levels (in units of \(\hbar\omega\)) and identify any degeneracies.

(c) Now suppose \(\omega_x/\omega_y = \sqrt{2}\) (irrational). Explain why EBK quantization can still be applied but the system may exhibit complicated dynamics if perturbed. Does irrationality of frequency ratio imply chaos?

6. (Maslov Index and Hard Boundaries) The Maslov index \(\mu\) corrects the phase shift at turning points. For a soft turning point (where \(V(x) = E\)), \(\mu = 1/2\). For a hard wall (infinite potential), \(\mu = 1\).

Consider a particle in potential \(V(x) = V_0\) for \(0 < x < a\) and \(V(x) = \infty\) elsewhere, with total energy \(E > V_0\).

(a) Inside the potential well, \(p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V_0)}\). Write down the Bohr-Sommerfeld condition with correct Maslov indices for both hard walls.

(b) Show that the quantization condition simplifies to

\[k a = n\pi, \quad k = \sqrt{2m(E - V_0)}/\hbar\]

and thus \(E_n = V_0 + \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}\). Compare to Problem 1.

(c) How would the quantization condition change if one boundary were soft (e.g., exponential wall decay) and one hard?

7. (Hydrogen via Bohr-Sommerfeld (Circular Orbits)) In the Bohr model, a circular orbit with radius \(r\) and angular momentum \(L = m v r = n_\phi\hbar\) is stable. For the Coulomb potential \(V(r) = -e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)\), the centripetal force condition is

\[\frac{m v^2}{r} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2}\]

(a) Show that the circular orbit energy is \(E = -\frac{e^2}{8\pi\epsilon_0 r}\) (half the potential energy, by the virial theorem).

(b) For an azimuthal quantization with \(n_\phi\hbar\), use the centripetal condition to eliminate \(v\) and show that

\[r_{n_\phi} = \frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 n_\phi^2 \hbar^2}{m e^2} = n_\phi^2 a_0\]

where \(a_0\) is the Bohr radius.

(c) Find the energy \(E_{n_\phi}\) for each azimuthal quantum number. Why is the hydrogen atom result often written as \(E_n = -13.6 \text{ eV}/n^2\) with a principal quantum number \(n\) that sums all degrees of freedom?

8. (Classical Limit and Correspondence Principle) In the classical limit (\(n \gg 1\)), the energy spacing \(\Delta E = E_{n+1} - E_n\) should match the classical frequency \(\omega_\text{cl}\) via \(\Delta E = \hbar\omega_\text{cl}\) (Bohr correspondence).

(a) For the harmonic oscillator, \(E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)\), so \(\Delta E = \hbar\omega\). Show that this agrees with \(\hbar\omega_\text{cl}\) where \(\omega_\text{cl}\) is the classical frequency of oscillation.

(b) For a power-law potential \(V(x) = C|x|^\alpha\) (e.g., \(\alpha = 2\) for harmonic, \(\alpha = 4\) for quartic), the Bohr-Sommerfeld result is \(E_n \propto n^{2\alpha/(\alpha+2)}\). Show that \(\Delta E / E_n \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\), implying the energy levels become quasi-continuous—the classical limit.

(c) Estimate the quantum number \(n^*\) above which Bohr-Sommerfeld should be accurate (say, within \(10\%\)) for a typical atomic potential. Use the condition \(\hbar\omega / E \lesssim 0.1\) where \(\omega\) is a characteristic frequency scale.

3.4.1 Wick Rotation#

Prompts

  • What is the Wick rotation and why does transforming \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) turn oscillatory integrals into exponentially damped ones?

  • How does the real-time action transform under Wick rotation, and what is the resulting Euclidean action?

  • Why does the imaginary-time path integral converge when the real-time version oscillates wildly?

  • How is the partition function \(Z(\beta)\) related to the imaginary-time propagator?

  • What does it mean to analytically continue results from imaginary time back to real time, and are there subtleties in this procedure?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The path integral oscillates as \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\), making it difficult to evaluate directly. The Wick rotation \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) replaces oscillating phases with exponentially decaying weights \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\), where \(S_E\) is the Euclidean action. This transform reveals a deep connection: the imaginary-time propagator for interval \(\beta\hbar\) is precisely the thermal density matrix \(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta \hat{H}}\), linking quantum dynamics to statistical mechanics.

Wick Rotation

The Wick rotation from real time \(t\) to imaginary time \(\tau = it/\hbar\) transforms the oscillatory quantum path integral into an exponentially damped sum. This analytic continuation reveals that quantum mechanics and thermal statistical mechanics are intimately related—they are two faces of the same mathematical structure.

The Wick Rotation#

In quantum field theory and statistical mechanics, analytic continuation from real time \(t\) to imaginary time \(\tau = -it/\hbar\) simplifies calculations.

Definition.

The Wick rotation is the transformation

\[t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau, \quad \text{where} \quad \tau \in \mathbb{R}\]

Equivalently, \(\tau = it/\hbar\) is the imaginary time, with dimension of time. If thermal context applies, write \(\tau \in [0, \beta\hbar]\) where \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\) is the inverse temperature.

Derivation: Wick Rotation Transform

The real-time action is

\[S[x] = \int_0^T \left(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 - V(x)\right)\,\mathrm{d}t\]

Under \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\), we have \(\mathrm{d}t \to -\mathrm{i}\,\mathrm{d}\tau\) and \(\dot{x} = \mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t \to \mathrm{i}\,\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}\tau\). Thus:

\[S[x] \to \int_0^{\beta\hbar} \left(\frac{m}{2}(\mathrm{i}\dot{x})^2 - V(x)\right) (-\mathrm{i})\,\mathrm{d}\tau = -\mathrm{i} \int_0^{\beta\hbar} \left(-\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 - V(x)\right)\,\mathrm{d}\tau\]

The minus signs combine to give:

\[S[x] \to -\int_0^{\beta\hbar} \left(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 + V(x)\right)\,\mathrm{d}\tau\]

Defining the Euclidean action as

\[S_E[x] = \int_0^{\beta\hbar} \left(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 + V(x)\right)\,\mathrm{d}\tau\]

we have \(S \to -iS_E\), so

\[\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar} \to \mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\]

The rapidly oscillating phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) becomes a rapidly decaying exponential \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\). This convergence is crucial for numerical simulations and analytical approximations.

Why Wick Rotation Matters

Real-time path integrals with \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) are highly oscillatory—contributions from different paths interfere constructively and destructively, making direct integration intractable.

After Wick rotation, the Euclidean path integral with \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\) is exponentially damped—contributions from paths with large action are exponentially suppressed, enabling:

  • Numerical Monte Carlo simulations

  • Perturbative expansions

  • Semi-classical approximations

Euclidean Path Integral#

The Euclidean path integral is

\[Z(\beta) = \int_{\text{periodic}} \mathrm{e}^{-S_E[x]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[x]\]

where the integral is over paths that are periodic in imaginary time: \(x(\tau + \beta\hbar) = x(\tau)\).

Key properties:

  1. Convergence: The exponential \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\) decays exponentially for large actions. Paths with large Euclidean action are suppressed. This makes numerical simulation feasible.

  2. Positive measure: Unlike the oscillating real-time path integral, the Euclidean integral has a well-defined probability measure (positive definite). This is essential for Monte Carlo simulations.

  3. Periodicity: The periodicity in imaginary time \(\tau\) reflects the cyclic nature of the thermal density matrix in quantum statistical mechanics.

Connection to the Partition Function#

In quantum statistical mechanics, the partition function at temperature \(T\) is

\[Z(T) = \operatorname{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta \hat{H}}), \quad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}\]

where \(\hat{H}\) is the Hamiltonian and the trace sums over all quantum states.

Path Integral Representation of Partition Function

\[Z(\beta) = \int_{\text{periodic}} \mathrm{e}^{-S_E[x]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[x]\]

where the sum is over all paths periodic in imaginary time with period \(\beta\hbar = 1/(k_B T)\).

The partition function \(Z = \operatorname{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta \hat{H}})\) equals the functional integral over periodic paths in Euclidean time. This is a profound connection:

  • Trace: sums over all eigenstates

  • Path integral: sums over all closed paths in imaginary time

Discussion

Is “imaginary time” merely a mathematical convenience—a trick to make calculations simpler—or does it represent something physically real? After all, we performed an analytic continuation of the path integral from real time to imaginary time, but real clocks tick forward in real time, not imaginary time. Yet the Wick-rotated formalism reveals that the thermal partition function \(Z(\beta) = \operatorname{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta \hat{H}})\) equals a path integral over periodic paths in imaginary time. Does this mean temperature and imaginary time are two sides of the same coin in quantum mechanics?

Poll: Wick rotation and convergence

Wick rotation replaces \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) (time to imaginary time) in the path integral. The action \(S = \int (\frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - V(x)) \mathrm{d}t\) becomes \(S_E = \int (\frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^\tau)^2 + V(x)) \mathrm{d}\tau\) (Euclidean action). Why does this rotation improve mathematical convergence of the path integral?

(A) The kinetic term \(\propto (\dot{x}^\tau)^2\) remains positive, while the potential \(+V(x)\) suppresses paths that visit high-potential regions.

(B) Imaginary time \(\tau\) has no physical meaning, so the path integral converges by definition.

(C) The Euclidean action \(S_E\) is always positive, unlike the real-time action which can be negative.

(D) Wick rotation converts the oscillatory Feynman integral into a Gaussian path integral, which is easier to evaluate.

Summary#

  • Wick rotation: \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) converts oscillating phases \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) to decaying exponentials \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\)

  • Euclidean action: \(S_E = \int(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 + V)\,\mathrm{d}\tau\) — same form as Hamiltonian but in imaginary time

  • Partition function: \(Z(\beta) = \int_{\text{periodic}}\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\mathcal{D}[x]\) — path integral over periodic paths of period \(\beta\hbar\)

  • Temperature duality: quantum mechanics in imaginary time \(\beta\hbar\) is thermal statistical mechanics at temperature \(T = 1/(k_B \beta)\)

Homework#

1. Free-particle Wick rotation. For a free particle (\(V = 0\)), the real-time action is \(S[x] = \int_0^T \frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2\,\mathrm{d}t\).

(a) Perform the substitution \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) and show that \(S \to -\mathrm{i}S_E\) with \(S_E = \int_0^{\beta\hbar}\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2\,\mathrm{d}\tau > 0\).

(b) Explain why the Euclidean path integral weight \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E/\hbar}\) suppresses paths with large kinetic energy, in contrast to the oscillatory \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\).

2. Harmonic oscillator Euclidean action. For the harmonic oscillator \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\), the real-time action is \(S = \int_0^T\left(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 - \frac{m\omega^2}{2}x^2\right)\mathrm{d}t\).

(a) Apply the Wick rotation to derive the Euclidean action \(S_E = \int_0^{\beta\hbar}\left(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2 + \frac{m\omega^2}{2}x^2\right)\mathrm{d}\tau\).

(b) Note that both terms in \(S_E\) are positive. Explain why this guarantees convergence of the Euclidean path integral.

(c) For a classical path \(x(\tau) = A\cos(\omega\tau)\) with \(A\) small, compute \(S_E\) and verify that it is positive.

3. Convergence comparison. Consider two paths for a free particle traveling from \(x_0 = 0\) to \(x = L\) in time \(T\): the straight-line path \(x_1(t) = Lt/T\) and a wildly oscillating path \(x_2(t) = Lt/T + a\sin(100\pi t/T)\) with \(a \ll L\).

(a) Compute the action \(S[x_1]\) and \(S[x_2]\) and show that \(S[x_2] > S[x_1]\).

(b) In real time, the integrand weights are \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[x_1]/\hbar}\) and \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[x_2]/\hbar}\). Both have the same magnitude. Explain why summing such contributions is numerically difficult (the sign problem).

(c) After Wick rotation, compare \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E[x_1]/\hbar}\) and \(\mathrm{e}^{-S_E[x_2]/\hbar}\). Which path is exponentially suppressed? Why does this make Monte Carlo sampling practical?

4. Trace and periodic paths. The partition function is \(Z(\beta) = \operatorname{Tr}(\mathrm{e}^{-\beta\hat{H}})\). Insert a position-basis resolution of identity to write

\[ Z(\beta) = \int \mathrm{d}x\,\langle x\vert\mathrm{e}^{-\beta\hat{H}}\vert x\rangle. \]

(a) Explain why the matrix element \(\langle x\vert\mathrm{e}^{-\beta\hat{H}}\vert x\rangle\) is the imaginary-time propagator from \(x\) back to \(x\) in Euclidean time \(\beta\hbar\).

(b) Argue that the path integral representation of \(Z(\beta)\) must therefore sum over paths satisfying \(x(0) = x(\beta\hbar)\) (periodic boundary conditions).

(c) What is the physical meaning of the periodicity period \(\beta\hbar = \hbar/(k_B T)\)? If the temperature increases, does the period grow or shrink?

5. High-temperature limit. In the limit \(\beta \to 0\) (high \(T\)), the imaginary-time interval \(\beta\hbar\) becomes very short.

(a) Argue that for very short \(\beta\hbar\), the kinetic term \(\frac{m}{2}\dot{x}^2\) in \(S_E\) becomes large for any path that deviates from a constant, so the dominant paths have \(x(\tau) \approx \text{const}\).

(b) Show that the partition function reduces to \(Z \approx \int\mathrm{d}x\,\left(\frac{m}{2\pi\hbar^2\beta}\right)^{1/2}\mathrm{e}^{-\beta V(x)}\), recovering the classical partition function (up to the thermal de Broglie wavelength prefactor).

(c) Identify the thermal de Broglie wavelength \(\lambda_\text{th} = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar^2\beta/m}\) in this expression. What does it represent physically?

6. Ground-state dominance. In the opposite limit \(\beta \to \infty\) (low \(T\)), expand \(Z(\beta) = \sum_n \mathrm{e}^{-\beta E_n}\) in energy eigenstates.

(a) Show that \(Z(\beta) \to \mathrm{e}^{-\beta E_0}\) as \(\beta \to \infty\), where \(E_0\) is the ground-state energy.

(b) Explain how this allows extraction of \(E_0\) from the Euclidean path integral: \(E_0 = -\lim_{\beta\to\infty}\frac{1}{\beta}\ln Z(\beta)\).

(c) In lattice simulations, one computes \(Z(\beta)\) numerically via Monte Carlo. Why is this method for finding ground-state energies more practical in Euclidean time than in real time?

7. Euclidean free propagator. The exact real-time free-particle propagator is \(K(x,t;x_0,0) = (m/2\pi\mathrm{i}\hbar t)^{1/2}\exp(\mathrm{i}m(x-x_0)^2/2\hbar t)\).

(a) Perform the Wick rotation \(t \to -\mathrm{i}\tau\) to obtain the Euclidean propagator \(K_E(x,\tau;x_0,0)\). Show that it is a real Gaussian: \(K_E = (m/2\pi\hbar\tau)^{1/2}\exp(-m(x-x_0)^2/2\hbar\tau)\).

(b) Verify that \(K_E\) satisfies the imaginary-time Schrödinger equation (diffusion equation) \(-\hbar\partial_\tau K_E = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\partial_x^2 K_E\).

(c) Compare the form of \(K_E\) with the diffusion kernel \(G(x,\tau) = (4\pi D\tau)^{-1/2}\exp(-x^2/4D\tau)\). What is the diffusion constant \(D\) in terms of \(\hbar\) and \(m\)?

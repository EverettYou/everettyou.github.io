3.1.3 Particle-Wave Unification#

Prompts

  • How is the optical path length an “action” for light? What is the analogous quantity in classical mechanics?

  • State the de Broglie relations. What do they imply about matter?

  • What does the equation \(\psi = A\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} S/\hbar}\) mean physically?

  • How does the path integral \(\sum \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} S/\hbar}\) reduce to classical mechanics when \(S \gg \hbar\)?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

This lesson unifies two classical frameworks: geometric optics (ray picture) and classical mechanics (particle picture). Both obey the same variational principle—Fermat’s principle for light, Hamilton’s principle for particles. The bridge between them is de Broglie’s insight: matter has waves, and the phase of the matter wave is the classical action.

Optical Path as Action#

  • Geometric optics: light follows the path of stationary optical path length: \(L = \int n \, \mathrm{d}s\).

  • Classical mechanics: a particle follows the path of stationary action: \(S = \int L \, \mathrm{d}t\).

These have identical structure:

Geometric Optics

Classical Mechanics

Light ray

Particle trajectory

Fermat’s principle: \(\delta L = 0\)

Hamilton’s principle: \(\delta S = 0\)

Optical path \(L = \int n \, \mathrm{d}s\)

Action \(S = \int L \, \mathrm{d}t\)

Ray optics (short wavelength)

Classical limit (\(\hbar \to 0\))

The physical picture: both light and particles are described by a variational principle. The observable path is the one that makes the optical path length (or action) stationary.

De Broglie: Matter Waves#

In 1924, de Broglie proposed that matter, like light, has wave-particle duality.

De Broglie Relations

\[ \boldsymbol{p} = \hbar \boldsymbol{k}, \quad E = \hbar \omega \]

which associate:

  • particle properties: momentum \(\boldsymbol{p}\) and energy \(E\), with

  • wave properties: wave vector \(\boldsymbol{k}\) and angular frequency \(\omega\).

This unifies particle and wave descriptions. Experimental confirmation (Davisson & Germer, 1927): electrons diffracted by crystals exhibit interference patterns with \(\lambda = h/p\), confirming de Broglie’s hypothesis.

Derivation: De Broglie Relations from the Optics–Mechanics Analogy

The key reasoning is by analogy with light.

For light (photons): Einstein (1905) and Planck (1900) established \(E = \hbar\omega\) and \(p = \hbar k = h/\lambda\) for photons. These relate particle properties (energy, momentum) to wave properties (frequency, wave vector).

De Broglie’s hypothesis: If light (a wave) can behave as particles, then matter (particles) should behave as waves. De Broglie proposed the same relations hold for massive particles:

\[ E = \hbar\omega \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \omega = E/\hbar \]
\[ \boldsymbol{p} = \hbar \boldsymbol{k} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lambda = h/p \]

Consistency check with optics. For light in a medium of refractive index \(n\), the local wave number is \(k_{\text{loc}} = n k = n\omega/c\). The optical momentum is \(p_{\text{opt}} = n \cdot (E/c) = \hbar k_{\text{loc}}\). So the de Broglie relation \(p = \hbar k\) is already implicit in geometric optics — de Broglie simply extended it to massive particles.

For a free non-relativistic particle with \(E = p^2/2m\), the de Broglie wavelength is

\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mE}} \]

For an electron at \(E = 100\,\text{eV}\): \(\lambda \approx 0.12\,\text{nm}\), comparable to atomic spacings — hence crystal diffraction is observable.

Action = Phase: The Punchline#

Here is the crucial insight connecting classical and quantum mechanics.

In optics, the phase accumulated by a wave traveling through a medium is:

\[ \phi_\text{optics} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \int n \, \mathrm{d}s \]

In classical mechanics, the accumulated action along a path is:

\[ S = \int L \, \mathrm{d}t \]

De Broglie’s identification: The phase of the quantum wavefunction is the classical action in units of \(\hbar\):

\[ \Phi = \frac{S}{\hbar} \]

Therefore, the quantum wavefunction is:

\[ \boxed{\psi(\boldsymbol{x}, t) = A(\boldsymbol{x}, t) \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S(\boldsymbol{x}, t)/\hbar}} \]

This is the bridge. Phase is action. Once we know the action for any physical system, we can immediately write down the phase of its quantum wavefunction.

Derivation: Action = Phase from the Optics–Mechanics Dictionary

The proof proceeds by matching the optics and mechanics columns of the correspondence table.

In optics, a monochromatic wave in a medium with \(n(\boldsymbol{r})\) has the form

\[ \psi_{\text{opt}} = A \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi}, \quad \Phi = k \int n \, \mathrm{d}s = \frac{\omega}{c} \int n \, \mathrm{d}s = \frac{\omega}{c} L \]

where \(L\) is the optical path length.

In mechanics, the action along a classical trajectory is

\[ S = \int L_{\text{mech}} \, \mathrm{d}t = \int \left(\frac{1}{2}m\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 - V\right) \mathrm{d}t \]

Matching. The optics–mechanics analogy requires the phase of the matter wave to play the role of \(\Phi\):

\[ \Phi_{\text{matter}} = \frac{S}{\hbar} \]

Verification via de Broglie. For a free particle with momentum \(\boldsymbol{p}\) and energy \(E\), the de Broglie wave is \(\psi = A \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(\boldsymbol{k} \cdot \boldsymbol{r} - \omega t)}\), so the phase is

\[ \Phi = \boldsymbol{k} \cdot \boldsymbol{r} - \omega t = \frac{\boldsymbol{p} \cdot \boldsymbol{r} - E t}{\hbar} \]

The classical action for a free particle moving from \(\boldsymbol{r}_0\) to \(\boldsymbol{r}\) in time \(t\) along a straight-line trajectory is \(S = \boldsymbol{p} \cdot (\boldsymbol{r} - \boldsymbol{r}_0) - E t\) (up to a constant), confirming \(\Phi = S/\hbar\).

General result. For any system, the WKB (semiclassical) wavefunction is \(\psi = A \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} S/\hbar}\), where \(S(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi equation — the exact classical-to-quantum bridge.

What is Action?#

The action takes different forms across physics:

System

Action \(S\)

Light ray

\(\int n \, \mathrm{d}s\)

Non-relativistic particle

\(\int \left(\frac{m\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2}{2} - V\right) \mathrm{d}t\)

Relativistic particle

\(-mc^2 \int \mathrm{d}\tau\)

In each case, nature chooses the path that makes \(S\) stationary: \(\delta S = 0\).

Quantization: From Paths to Probability Amplitudes#

Classical mechanics: the observable trajectory satisfies \(\delta S = 0\) (Hamilton’s principle).

Quantum mechanics: the probability amplitude for a particle to travel from \((\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\) to \((\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f)\) is obtained by summing contributions from all possible paths, each weighted by the phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\):

Path Integral Principle

\[ \mathcal{A}(\boldsymbol{x}_i \to \boldsymbol{x}_f) = \int_{\text{all paths}} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\text{path}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\text{path}] \]

where the integral sums over every conceivable path, weighted by its quantum phase.

Classical limit (\(S \gg \hbar\)): The phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) oscillates rapidly. Neighboring paths have wildly different phases and cancel by destructive interference. Only the stationary-phase path—where \(\delta S = 0\)—survives. This is the classical trajectory.

Quantum regime (\(S \sim \hbar\)): All paths have comparable phases and interfere constructively. Diffraction, tunneling, and superposition emerge naturally.

This is Huygens’ principle for matter: every point is a source of “matter wavelets” that propagate and interfere. Quantization is the principle of superposing all these wavelets with the correct quantum phase.

Derivation: Classical Mechanics from Stationary Phase

Start from the path integral:

\[ \mathcal{A}(\boldsymbol{x}_i \to \boldsymbol{x}_f) = \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} S[\boldsymbol{x}(t)]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \]

Step 1. Expand \(S\) around the classical path \(\bar{\boldsymbol{x}}(t)\) (the path satisfying \(\delta S = 0\)). Write \(\boldsymbol{x}(t) = \bar{\boldsymbol{x}}(t) + \boldsymbol{\eta}(t)\) with \(\boldsymbol{\eta}(t_i) = \boldsymbol{\eta}(t_f) = 0\):

\[ S[\bar{\boldsymbol{x}} + \boldsymbol{\eta}] = S[\bar{\boldsymbol{x}}] + \underbrace{\delta S}_{ = \, 0} + \frac{1}{2}\delta^2 S + \cdots \]

The first-order term \(\delta S = 0\) vanishes by definition of the classical path (Hamilton’s principle). This is the Euler-Lagrange equation:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}} \]

Step 2. The second-order fluctuation is

\[ \delta^2 S = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \left(\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot{x}^2} \dot{\eta}^2 + \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial x^2} \eta^2\right) \mathrm{d}t \]

Step 3. In the classical limit \(S_{\text{cl}} \gg \hbar\), the dominant contribution to the path integral comes from paths near \(\bar{\boldsymbol{x}}\). The Gaussian integral over fluctuations \(\boldsymbol{\eta}\) gives

\[ \mathcal{A} \approx C \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} S_{\text{cl}}/\hbar} \]

where \(C\) is a slowly varying prefactor from the quadratic fluctuations. The probability \(|\mathcal{A}|^2\) is dominated by the classical path, and the particle effectively follows the trajectory determined by \(\delta S = 0\).

Correspondence principle. As \(\hbar \to 0\), the Fresnel zone of constructive interference shrinks to zero width around the classical path. Quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics, just as wave optics reduces to geometric optics when \(\lambda \to 0\).

Derivation: Hamilton-Jacobi Equation from the WKB Ansatz

Inserting the WKB ansatz \(\psi = A\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) into the Schrödinger equation shows precisely how classical mechanics emerges from quantum mechanics at \(O(\hbar^0)\).

Step 1: Insert the ansatz.

The time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a particle in potential \(V\) is

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_t\psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi + V\psi \]

With \(\psi = A\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\), compute each term:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_t\psi = \left(\mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_t A - A\,\partial_t S\right)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar} \]
\[ \nabla\psi = \left(\nabla A + \frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}A\,\nabla S\right)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar} \]
\[ \nabla^2\psi = \left(\nabla^2 A + \frac{2\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\nabla A\cdot\nabla S + \frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}A\,\nabla^2 S - \frac{|\nabla S|^2}{\hbar^2}A\right)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar} \]

Step 2: Collect powers of \(\hbar\).

Substituting and dividing by \(A\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\):

\[ \left(\frac{|\nabla S|^2}{2m} + V + \partial_t S\right) + \mathrm{i}\hbar\left(\frac{\partial_t A}{A} + \frac{\nabla A\cdot\nabla S}{mA} + \frac{\nabla^2 S}{2m}\right) - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\nabla^2 A}{A} = 0 \]

Step 3: \(O(\hbar^0)\) — the Hamilton-Jacobi equation.

The \(\hbar^0\) term (real, survives as \(\hbar\to 0\)) must vanish independently:

\[ \boxed{\partial_t S + \frac{|\nabla S|^2}{2m} + V = 0} \]

This is the Hamilton-Jacobi equation of classical mechanics. With the identification \(\boldsymbol{p} = \nabla S\), it becomes \(\partial_t S + H(\boldsymbol{r},\boldsymbol{p}) = 0\). Classical trajectories are the integral curves of the velocity field \(\boldsymbol{v} = \nabla S/m\) — they are the “rays” of the matter wave.

Step 4: \(O(\hbar^1)\) — probability conservation.

Setting the \(\hbar^1\) imaginary part to zero gives:

\[ \partial_t A + \frac{\nabla A\cdot\nabla S}{m} + \frac{A\,\nabla^2 S}{2m} = 0 \]

Multiplying by \(2A\) and using \(\rho = A^2\):

\[ \partial_t\rho + \nabla\cdot\!\left(\rho\,\frac{\nabla S}{m}\right) = 0, \qquad \boldsymbol{j} = \rho\,\frac{\nabla S}{m} = \rho\,\boldsymbol{v} \]

This is the continuity equation \(\partial_t\rho + \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{j} = 0\): probability flows along the classical velocity field \(\boldsymbol{v} = \boldsymbol{p}/m\).

Conclusion. The WKB ansatz splits the Schrödinger equation into two classical structures:

  • Phase (\(\sim\hbar^0\)): Hamilton-Jacobi equation — determines the classical trajectory via \(\delta S = 0\).

  • Amplitude (\(\sim\hbar^1\)): continuity equation — determines how probability spreads along that trajectory.

Classical mechanics is not an approximation tacked onto quantum mechanics; it is the exact leading-order content of the Schrödinger equation.

Discussion

The path integral says we sum \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) over all paths, including wildly non-classical ones. In what sense do these “crazy” paths contribute to observable physics? If a particle tunnels through a barrier, which paths dominate the sum?

Derivation: Gaussian Wave Packet Spreading and Position-Momentum Uncertainty

A Gaussian wave packet provides the cleanest illustration of how the de Broglie relation \(p = \hbar k\) combines with Fourier uncertainty to give \(\Delta x \Delta p \geq \hbar/2\).

Step 1: Initial Gaussian. Consider a wave packet centered at \(x = 0\) with momentum \(p_0 = \hbar k_0\):

\[ \psi(x, 0) = \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_0^2}\right)^{1/4} \mathrm{e}^{-x^2/4\sigma_0^2}\, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}k_0 x}. \]

The initial position uncertainty is \(\Delta x = \sigma_0\).

Step 2: Momentum space. Taking the Fourier transform (and using \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathrm{e}^{-ax^2+bx}\mathrm{d}x = \sqrt{\pi/a}\,\mathrm{e}^{b^2/4a}\)):

\[ \tilde{\psi}(k, 0) = \left(\frac{2\sigma_0^2}{\pi}\right)^{1/4} \mathrm{e}^{-(k-k_0)^2\sigma_0^2}. \]

This is a Gaussian in \(k\) centered at \(k_0\) with width \(\Delta k = 1/(2\sigma_0)\). Using \(p = \hbar k\):

\[ \Delta p = \hbar \Delta k = \frac{\hbar}{2\sigma_0}. \]

Step 3: Uncertainty product. At \(t = 0\):

\[ \Delta x \cdot \Delta p = \sigma_0 \cdot \frac{\hbar}{2\sigma_0} = \frac{\hbar}{2}. \]

The Gaussian wave packet saturates the Heisenberg uncertainty bound — it is the minimum-uncertainty state.

Step 4: Free-particle spreading. Under free time evolution, each momentum component \(k\) acquires phase \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\hbar k^2 t/2m}\), spreading the packet:

\[ \Delta x(t) = \sigma_0 \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\hbar t}{2m\sigma_0^2}\right)^2}. \]

The spread timescale is \(t^* = 2m\sigma_0^2/\hbar\). For an electron with \(\sigma_0 = 1\,\text{Å}\), \(t^* \approx 3 \times 10^{-16}\,\text{s}\) — the wave packet spreads in an attosecond. The position uncertainty grows as \(\Delta x \to \hbar t/(2m\sigma_0)\) at late times, consistent with \(\Delta p\) remaining constant (\(= \hbar/2\sigma_0\)): the particle moves with uncertain velocity \(\Delta v = \Delta p/m\) and its position grows as \(\Delta v \cdot t\).

Summary#

  • Optics–mechanics duality: Geometric optics and classical mechanics obey identical variational principles. Particles are the “geometric optics” of matter.

  • De Broglie relations: Matter has wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\) and angular frequency \(\omega = E/\hbar\).

  • Action = phase: The quantum wavefunction is \(\psi = A \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\). This unifies classical and quantum descriptions.

  • Path integral quantization: Sum over all paths with phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\). The classical path emerges as the stationary-phase path when \(S \gg \hbar\).

  • Classical limit: Stationary phase selects the classical trajectory. Quantum mechanics automatically reproduces classical mechanics in the appropriate limit.

3.3.1 Stationary Phase Approximation#

Prompts

  • What is the stationary phase approximation? Under what conditions does it apply to oscillatory integrals?

  • Why does the classical path (\(\delta S = 0\)) dominate the path integral as \(\hbar \to 0\)?

  • How do quantum fluctuations around the classical path contribute corrections at finite \(\hbar\)?

  • How does stationary phase make the correspondence principle precise — classical mechanics as a limit of quantum mechanics?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The stationary phase approximation (SPA) is the mathematical mechanism by which classical mechanics emerges from quantum mechanics. For integrals of the form \(I = \int f(x)\, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x)/\hbar}\,\mathrm{d}x\) with small \(\hbar\), the dominant contribution comes from stationary points where \(\Phi'(x_0) = 0\). Applied to the path integral, this selects the classical path — the trajectory where \(\delta S = 0\). This is the correspondence principle made precise.

The Mathematical Result#

Many integrals in quantum mechanics take the form

\[ I = \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x)/\hbar} f(x) \, \mathrm{d}x \]

where \(\Phi(x)\) is a phase function, \(f(x)\) is a slowly-varying amplitude, and \(\hbar\) is small. When \(\hbar\) is tiny, the integrand oscillates wildly — contributions cancel everywhere except near points where \(\Phi\) varies slowly.

Stationary Phase Formula

For an oscillatory integral with a single stationary point \(\Phi'(x_0) = 0\), the leading approximation is

\[ I \approx f(x_0) \sqrt{\frac{2\pi\hbar}{\mathrm{i}\Phi''(x_0)}} \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x_0)/\hbar} \]

Valid when: (1) isolated stationary point with \(|\Phi''(x_0)|\) not too small, (2) \(f(x)\) slowly varying, (3) \(\Phi \gg \hbar\).

Derivation: Stationary Phase Formula

Near the stationary point \(x_0\), expand the phase to second order:

\[ \Phi(x) \approx \Phi(x_0) + \frac{1}{2}\Phi''(x_0)(x - x_0)^2 \]

The integral becomes Gaussian:

\[ I \approx \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x_0)/\hbar} f(x_0) \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2/(2\hbar)} \mathrm{d}x \]

Using the Fresnel integral \(\int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}ax^2} \mathrm{d}x = \sqrt{\pi/(\mathrm{i}a)}\), the result follows.

Derivation: The Fresnel Integral \(\int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}ax^2}\mathrm{d}x = \sqrt{\pi/(\mathrm{i}a)}\)

The key mathematical result underlying the stationary phase approximation is the Fresnel integral. Unlike the real Gaussian \(\int \mathrm{e}^{-ax^2}\mathrm{d}x = \sqrt{\pi/a}\) (\(a>0\)), the oscillatory version requires analytic continuation.

Step 1: Rotate the contour.

For \(a > 0\), consider the integral \(I(\epsilon) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}(a + \mathrm{i}\epsilon)x^2}\,\mathrm{d}x\) with \(\epsilon > 0\). The factor \(\mathrm{e}^{-\epsilon x^2}\) ensures convergence, so \(I(\epsilon)\) is well-defined.

Substitute \(u = \sqrt{a + \mathrm{i}\epsilon}\, x\) (rotating the integration variable into the complex plane):

\[ I(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a + \mathrm{i}\epsilon}}\int_{\mathcal{C}} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\,\mathrm{d}u \]

where \(\mathcal{C}\) is the ray \(\arg(u) = \tfrac{1}{2}\arg(a + \mathrm{i}\epsilon)\), tilted slightly above the real axis.

Step 2: Deform to the real axis.

By Cauchy’s theorem, the contour \(\mathcal{C}\) can be deformed back to the real axis without crossing any singularities (the integrand \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\) is entire). The contributions from the arcs at \(|u|\to\infty\) vanish because \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\) decays in the upper half-plane when \(\arg(u) \in (0, \pi/2)\). Therefore:

\[ \int_{\mathcal{C}} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\,\mathrm{d}u = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\,\mathrm{d}u \]

Step 3: Evaluate via the standard Gaussian.

The Euler-Poisson trick gives \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathrm{e}^{-t^2}\,\mathrm{d}t = \sqrt{\pi}\). Setting \(u = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4}t\) (i.e., rotating 45°) transforms \(u^2 = \mathrm{i}t^2\), so:

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}u^2}\,\mathrm{d}u = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{-t^2}\,\mathrm{d}t = \sqrt{\pi}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4} = \sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}} \]

using \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4} = (1+\mathrm{i})/\sqrt{2}\) so \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/2} = \mathrm{i}\) and \(\sqrt{\pi}\cdot\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/4} = \sqrt{\pi}\cdot\sqrt{\mathrm{i}} = \sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}}\).

Step 4: Take \(\epsilon \to 0^+\).

\[ I(\epsilon) = \frac{\sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}}}{\sqrt{a + \mathrm{i}\epsilon}} \xrightarrow{\epsilon\to 0^+} \frac{\sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}}}{\sqrt{a}} = \sqrt{\frac{\pi\mathrm{i}}{a}} = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\mathrm{i}a}}\cdot\mathrm{i} = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{-\mathrm{i}a}\cdot(-1)} \]

More directly: \(\sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}/a} = \sqrt{\pi/(a/\mathrm{i})} = \sqrt{\pi\mathrm{i}/a}\). Using \(1/\mathrm{i} = -\mathrm{i}\):

\[ \boxed{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}ax^2}\,\mathrm{d}x = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\mathrm{i}a}}, \quad a > 0} \]

The factor \(1/\sqrt{\mathrm{i}a}\) combines a magnitude \(1/\sqrt{a}\) (the “width” of the oscillation region) and a phase \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\pi/4}\) (the Maslov phase shift from the 45° rotation).

Extension to \(a < 0\): Replace \(a \to |a|\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi}\) and rotate the contour by \(-\pi/4\) instead, giving \(\sqrt{\pi/|\mathrm{i}a|} = \sqrt{\pi/(\mathrm{i}a)}\) with \(a < 0\) (now \(1/(\mathrm{i}a) > 0\) since \(a<0\)). The formula \(\sqrt{\pi/(\mathrm{i}a)}\) holds for all real \(a \neq 0\), with the square root branch chosen to have positive real part.

Derivation: Detailed Stationary Phase with Higher-Order Corrections

We give a more careful derivation showing how the error term arises.

Setup. Consider \(I = \int f(x)\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x)/\hbar}\,\mathrm{d}x\) with \(\Phi'(x_0) = 0\) and \(\Phi''(x_0) \neq 0\).

Step 1: Localize. Substitute \(x = x_0 + u\) and expand:

\[ \Phi(x_0 + u) = \Phi(x_0) + \tfrac{1}{2}\Phi''(x_0)\,u^2 + \tfrac{1}{6}\Phi'''(x_0)\,u^3 + \tfrac{1}{24}\Phi^{(4)}(x_0)\,u^4 + \cdots \]
\[ f(x_0 + u) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)\,u + \tfrac{1}{2}f''(x_0)\,u^2 + \cdots \]

Step 2: Gaussian integral. The leading term is

\[ I_0 = f(x_0)\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x_0)/\hbar} \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi''(x_0)\,u^2/(2\hbar)}\,\mathrm{d}u = f(x_0)\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi(x_0)/\hbar}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\hbar}{\mathrm{i}\Phi''(x_0)}} \]

where we used the Fresnel integral \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}au^2}\,\mathrm{d}u = \sqrt{\pi/(\mathrm{i}a)}\) with \(a = \Phi''(x_0)/(2\hbar)\).

Step 3: Corrections. The next corrections come from:

  • The \(u^3\) term in \(\Phi\): contributes at \(O(\hbar^{1/2})\) but vanishes by oddness of \(u^3 \cdot \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}au^2}\).

  • The \(u^4\) term in \(\Phi\) crossed with the leading \(f(x_0)\): contributes at \(O(\hbar)\).

  • The \(f'(x_0)\,u\) term: vanishes by oddness.

  • The \(f''(x_0)\,u^2/2\) term: contributes at \(O(\hbar)\).

The first nonvanishing correction is \(O(\hbar)\) relative to \(I_0\):

\[ I = I_0\left[1 + O\!\left(\frac{\hbar\,\Phi^{(4)}}{(\Phi'')^2}\right) + O\!\left(\frac{\hbar\,f''}{f\,\Phi''}\right)\right] \]

The SPA is accurate when \(\hbar\) is small compared to the scales set by \(\Phi''\), \(\Phi^{(4)}\), and the variation of \(f\).

Step 4: Square root branch. The square root \(\sqrt{1/(\mathrm{i}\Phi'')}\) requires a branch choice. For \(\Phi'' > 0\), \(\sqrt{1/\mathrm{i}} = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\pi/4}\); for \(\Phi'' < 0\), \(\sqrt{1/(-\mathrm{i})} = \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\pi/4}\). This phase is the Maslov index contribution, important in semiclassical quantization (§3.3.3).

The width of the region that contributes is \(\Delta x \sim \sqrt{\hbar/|\Phi''|}\). As \(\hbar \to 0\), this region shrinks to zero — only the stationary point survives.

Why the Classical Path Dominates#

In the path integral

\[ K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \]

the phase function is the classical action \(S[\boldsymbol{x}]\). The stationary point is the classical path \(\boldsymbol{x}_\mathrm{cl}(t)\), defined by \(\delta S/\delta \boldsymbol{x} = 0\) — the Euler-Lagrange (Newton’s) equations.

Constructive interference at \(\boldsymbol{x}_\mathrm{cl}\): Nearby paths have actions differing only at second order, so their phases nearly agree — they add coherently.

Destructive interference away from \(\boldsymbol{x}_\mathrm{cl}\): Paths far from the classical trajectory have first-order action differences \(\Delta S \gg \hbar\), producing rapidly oscillating phases that cancel.

Discussion: Classical Emergence

The stationary phase approximation says that as \(\hbar \to 0\), only classical paths survive. This raises several questions:

  • In the limit \(\hbar \to 0\), does stationary phase become exact or just increasingly accurate? What controls the error?

  • For a potential with multiple classical paths (e.g., reflection and transmission), do they interfere constructively or destructively?

  • Tunneling has no classical path, yet it occurs. How does the path integral handle contributions from classically forbidden trajectories?

Quantum Fluctuations: The Second Variation#

Expanding the action to second order around the classical path:

\[ S[\boldsymbol{x}_\mathrm{cl} + \delta\boldsymbol{x}] \approx S_\mathrm{cl} + \frac{1}{2}\int \mathrm{d}t \, \delta\boldsymbol{x} \cdot M \cdot \delta\boldsymbol{x} \]

where \(M = -m\frac{\mathrm{d}^2}{\mathrm{d}t^2} - V''(\boldsymbol{x}_\mathrm{cl})\) is the second-variation operator. The Gaussian integral over fluctuations gives the semiclassical propagator:

Semiclassical Propagator

\[ K \approx \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_\mathrm{cl}/\hbar} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det M}} \]

The phase comes from the classical action; the prefactor encodes quantum fluctuations.

Derivation: Fluctuation Determinant from Gaussian Functional Integral

We derive the prefactor \(1/\sqrt{\det M}\) in the semiclassical propagator.

Step 1: Discretize. Write \(\delta x(t) = \sum_n c_n \phi_n(t)\) where \(\{\phi_n\}\) are orthonormal eigenfunctions of the second-variation operator \(M\):

\[ M\,\phi_n = \lambda_n\,\phi_n, \qquad \int \phi_n(t)\,\phi_m(t)\,\mathrm{d}t = \delta_{nm} \]

with boundary conditions \(\phi_n(t_i) = \phi_n(t_f) = 0\) (fluctuations vanish at the endpoints).

Step 2: Diagonalize. In these coordinates, the second variation becomes

\[ \frac{1}{2}\int \delta x \cdot M \cdot \delta x\,\mathrm{d}t = \frac{1}{2}\sum_n \lambda_n\,c_n^2 \]

The functional integral factorizes into a product of ordinary Gaussian integrals:

\[ \int \mathcal{D}[\delta x]\,\exp\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}}{2\hbar}\sum_n \lambda_n\,c_n^2\right) = \prod_n \int \mathrm{d}c_n\,\exp\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}\lambda_n}{2\hbar}\,c_n^2\right) \]

Step 3: Evaluate. Each Gaussian integral gives \(\sqrt{2\pi\hbar/(\mathrm{i}\lambda_n)}\), so the product is

\[ \prod_n \sqrt{\frac{2\pi\hbar}{\mathrm{i}\lambda_n}} = \left(\frac{2\pi\hbar}{\mathrm{i}}\right)^{N/2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\prod_n \lambda_n}} = \frac{\text{const}}{\sqrt{\det M}} \]

where \(\det M = \prod_n \lambda_n\) is the functional determinant (regularized by the ratio with a reference operator).

Step 4: Combine. Including the classical phase:

\[ K \approx \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\mathrm{i}\hbar}\right)^{1/2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det M / \det M_{\text{free}}}}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_{\text{cl}}/\hbar} \]

The ratio \(\det M / \det M_{\text{free}}\) ensures proper normalization — the free-particle case (\(V = 0\)) reproduces the known result \(K_{\text{free}} = (m/2\pi\mathrm{i}\hbar t)^{1/2}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_{\text{cl}}/\hbar}\).

This procedure — expand to second order, diagonalize, evaluate Gaussian integrals — is the prototype for all semiclassical and perturbative calculations in quantum field theory.

Example: Electron vs Baseball

Problem. Compare \(S/\hbar\) for an electron moving 1 cm in 1 s versus a baseball (0.1 kg) moving 1 m in 1 s.

Solution. The classical action is \(S \sim md^2/t\).

  • Electron: \(S \sim (10^{-30})(10^{-2})^2/1 = 10^{-34}\) J·s \(\approx \hbar\). Quantum regime — all paths matter.

  • Baseball: \(S \sim (0.1)(1)^2/1 = 0.1\) J·s \(= 10^{33}\hbar\). Classical regime — only the classical path survives.

The Correspondence Principle#

Stationary phase quantifies the correspondence principle: quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics when \(S \gg \hbar\).

Derivation: Multiple Stationary Points and Semiclassical Interference

When a physical amplitude receives contributions from two or more classical paths, the semiclassical approximation is a superposition. The resulting interference encodes the quantum mechanical principle that probability amplitudes — not probabilities — add.

Setup. Suppose the path integral \(K(x_f, x_i; t) = \int \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[x]/\hbar}\mathcal{D}[x]\) has two stationary-phase paths \(x_\alpha(t)\) (\(\alpha=1,2\)), each satisfying \(\delta S = 0\). The semiclassical propagator is

\[ K \approx \sum_{\alpha=1}^2 C_\alpha\, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_{cl}^{(\alpha)}/\hbar - \mathrm{i}\pi\nu_\alpha/2}, \]

where \(S_{cl}^{(\alpha)}\) is the classical action along path \(\alpha\), \(C_\alpha = \sqrt{|{-}\partial^2 S_{cl}^{(\alpha)}/\partial x_f\partial x_i|/(2\pi\mathrm{i}\hbar)}\) is the fluctuation amplitude (Van Vleck factor), and \(\nu_\alpha\) is the Maslov index (number of conjugate points, contributing phase \(-\pi/2\) each).

Step 1: Detection probability.

The probability density at \(x_f\) is \(|K|^2 \approx |C_1|^2 + |C_2|^2 + 2\,\mathrm{Re}\bigl(C_1 C_2^*\, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Delta S/\hbar}\bigr)\), where \(\Delta S = S_{cl}^{(1)} - S_{cl}^{(2)} - \tfrac{\pi\hbar}{2}(\nu_1 - \nu_2)\). The cross term is the interference; it oscillates between constructive and destructive as the phase difference \(\Delta S/\hbar\) varies.

Step 2: Double-slit as the canonical example.

Two slits at positions \(\pm d/2\) (separation \(d\)), screen at distance \(L\). A particle of momentum \(p = mv = h/\lambda_\mathrm{dB}\) travels from source to slit \(\alpha\), then to the screen at position \(y\).

The classical action for each path is (approximately, for \(y \ll L\)):

\[ S_{cl}^{(\alpha)} = p \cdot r_\alpha, \quad r_\alpha = \sqrt{L^2 + (y \mp d/2)^2} \approx L + \frac{(y \mp d/2)^2}{2L}. \]

The action difference is

\[ \Delta S = S_{cl}^{(1)} - S_{cl}^{(2)} \approx p\cdot\frac{-2y\cdot d/2}{L} \cdot \frac{1}{1} = \frac{-pyd}{L} = -\frac{2\pi d\sin\theta}{\lambda_\mathrm{dB}}, \]

where \(\sin\theta \approx y/L\) is the diffraction angle.

Step 3: Interference pattern.

Both paths have equal Maslov index and equal prefactors \(|C_1| = |C_2| \equiv C\) (by symmetry). The intensity is

\[ I(y) \propto |C\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_1/\hbar} + C\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_2/\hbar}|^2 = 2C^2\bigl(1 + \cos(\Delta S/\hbar)\bigr) = 4C^2\cos^2\!\left(\frac{\pi d\sin\theta}{\lambda_\mathrm{dB}}\right). \]

This is the standard double-slit fringe formula. Constructive interference occurs when \(d\sin\theta = n\lambda_\mathrm{dB}\) (integer \(n\)); destructive when \(d\sin\theta = (n+\tfrac{1}{2})\lambda_\mathrm{dB}\).

Step 4: Why classical mechanics does not interfere.

In classical mechanics, probabilities — not amplitudes — add: \(P = P_1 + P_2 = 2C^2\), independent of \(y\). The interference term appears only because we sum complex amplitudes \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) before squaring. The fringe visibility goes to zero as \(\hbar \to 0\) faster than the fringe spacing shrinks — in the classical limit, fringes become unresolvably dense and wash out, leaving the uniform \(2C^2\) classical answer. This is how the semiclassical limit recovers classical probability from quantum interference.

Summary#

  • Stationary phase: oscillatory integrals are dominated by points where \(\Phi'(x_0) = 0\); the contributing region has width \(\sim \sqrt{\hbar}\)

  • Classical path selection: the path integral selects \(\delta S = 0\) through constructive interference; all other paths cancel

  • Semiclassical propagator: \(K \approx \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_\mathrm{cl}/\hbar}/\sqrt{\det \hat{D}}\) includes quantum corrections from the second variation

  • Correspondence principle: classical mechanics emerges when \(S \gg \hbar\); the ratio \(S/\hbar\) controls the quantum-to-classical transition

(c) Should the SPA be trusted for this integral? Explain why or why not.

8. Destructive Interference (Qualitative Reasoning)

In the path integral, most paths give phase factors \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\text{path}]/\hbar}\) that point in different directions in the complex plane.

(a) Why do paths that are far from the classical path tend to have very different phases?

(b) Sketch a phasor diagram for five paths: the classical path and two pairs of neighbors symmetrically displaced from it. Explain how the phasors for non-classical paths tend to cancel.

(c) For a macroscopic object, how many distinct “orientations” of phasors (phase differences) occur among all possible paths? Why does this lead to nearly complete cancellation?

9. Semiclassical Propagator

The SPA gives the propagator as

\[K(x_f, t_f; x_i, t_i) \approx \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_{\text{cl}}/\hbar} \sqrt{\frac{1}{\det(\delta^2 S/\delta x^2)}}\]

(a) The exponent \(S_{\text{cl}}/\hbar\) oscillates rapidly as the final position \(x_f\) changes. What physical phenomenon does this oscillation describe?

(b) The prefactor \(\sqrt{1/\det(\delta^2 S/\delta x^2)}\) is a smooth, slowly-varying function. What does it represent?

(c) Why must both terms be included to correctly describe the quantum propagation near the classical path?

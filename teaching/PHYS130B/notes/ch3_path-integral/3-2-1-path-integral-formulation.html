3.2.1 Path Integral Formulation#

Prompts

  • What does it mean to “sum over all paths”? Is Feynman’s path integral a rigorous mathematical sum or a heuristic principle?

  • Define the action functional \(S[\boldsymbol{x}]\). Why does each path contribute a phase factor \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\) and not just a weight factor?

  • How does the stationary phase approximation emerge from the path integral? Why do classical paths dominate in the \(\hbar \to 0\) limit?

  • Explain the connection between path integral and partition function in statistical mechanics (Wick rotation). Why is imaginary time natural in thermal systems?

  • Compare the path integral and operator formalisms: each seems to encode quantum mechanics completely, but which insights does each approach offer that the other obscures?

Lecture Notes#

Feynman’s path integral reveals that a quantum particle does not follow a single trajectory—it explores every possible path simultaneously, with each path contributing a phase proportional to its classical action. This radical idea unifies quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and quantum field theory into a single mathematical framework.

Discussion: Path Integral Convergence

The path integral formulation must yield identical results to the Schrödinger equation (since both describe the same physics), yet they look completely different: one is a sum over infinite paths, the other is a differential equation.

  • How can a path integral (which naively seems to diverge, since there are uncountably many paths) be properly regularized and made mathematically rigorous?

  • The path integral approach seems to treat the classical path and quantum paths equally (all have weight \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\)). Yet in the classical limit \(\hbar \to 0\), the classical path dominates due to stationary phase. Is this emergence of classicality from the quantum formalism something profound, or just a mathematical consequence of the exponential phase?

  • Why must the path integral be taken in Euclidean (imaginary) time rather than real time for thermal/statistical mechanics applications? Does this reveal something about the deep connection between quantum mechanics and statistical mechanics?

Feynman’s Path Integral Principle#

In 1948, Richard Feynman proposed a radically different formulation of quantum mechanics based on a deceptively simple idea:

Path Integral Principle

The quantum amplitude for a particle to evolve from an initial state at \((\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\) to a final state at \((\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f)\) is the sum over all possible paths \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\) connecting these points:

(155)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}(t_i)=\boldsymbol{x}_i}^{\boldsymbol{x}(t_f)=\boldsymbol{x}_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \, \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\]

where:

  • \(S[\boldsymbol{x}] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} L(\boldsymbol{x}, \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}, t) \, \mathrm{d}t\) is the action functional along path \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\).

  • \(\mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\) denotes a functional integral (sum) over all paths.

  • Each path contributes with magnitude 1 (unit weight) and phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\).

This is a profound reformulation. Rather than solving a differential equation (Schrödinger equation), we sum over all possibilities. The quantum evolution is a sum of probability amplitudes, not a unique deterministic trajectory.

The Propagator: Transition Amplitude#

The propagator (also called Green’s function or transition amplitude) is

(156)#\[ K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}(t_i)=\boldsymbol{x}_i}^{\boldsymbol{x}(t_f)=\boldsymbol{x}_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\]

It represents the amplitude for a particle at \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) at time \(t_i\) to arrive at \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\) at time \(t_f\).

Wavefunction evolution: Given an initial wavefunction \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i)\), the wavefunction at a later time is

(157)#\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f) = \int K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) \, \psi(\boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) \, d^3\boldsymbol{x}_i\]

The propagator is the kernel for time evolution.

Composition Property (Chain Rule for Propagators)#

Suppose we want to go from time \(t_i\) to time \(t_f\), but we know the state at an intermediate time \(t_m\) (with \(t_i < t_m < t_f\)). Then

(158)#\[ K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \int K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_m, t_m) K(\boldsymbol{x}_m, t_m; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) \, d^3\boldsymbol{x}_m\]

Physical interpretation: A path from \(t_i\) to \(t_f\) can be decomposed into a path from \(t_i\) to \(t_m\) and then from \(t_m\) to \(t_f\). Integrating over all possible intermediate positions at \(t_m\) gives the full propagator.

Proof: Consider the path integral from \(t_i\) to \(t_f\):

\[ K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, t_f; \boldsymbol{x}_i, t_i) = \int_{\boldsymbol{x}(t_i)=\boldsymbol{x}_i}^{\boldsymbol{x}(t_f)=\boldsymbol{x}_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\]

Any path from \(t_i\) to \(t_f\) passes through some point \(\boldsymbol{x}_m\) at time \(t_m\). Grouping paths by their value at \(t_m\):

\[ K = \int d^3\boldsymbol{x}_m \left( \int_{t_i \to t_m} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[t_i \to t_m]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\text{paths } t_i \to t_m] \right) \left( \int_{t_m \to t_f} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[t_m \to t_f]/\hbar} \, \mathcal{D}[\text{paths } t_m \to t_f] \right)\]

Since the action is additive, \(S[t_i \to t_f] = S[t_i \to t_m] + S[t_m \to t_f]\), the exponentials factor:

\[ \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[t_i \to t_f]/\hbar} = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[t_i \to t_m]/\hbar} \cdot \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[t_m \to t_f]/\hbar}\]

This gives the composition formula.

The Action: Lagrangian Path Integral#

The action along a path \(\boldsymbol{x}(t)\) is defined as

(159)#\[ S[\boldsymbol{x}] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} L(\boldsymbol{x}(t), \dot{\boldsymbol{x}}(t), t) \, \mathrm{d}t\]

where \(L\) is the Lagrangian. For a non-relativistic particle of mass \(m\) in a potential \(V\):

(160)#\[ L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 - V(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\]

The action has dimensions of [energy] \(\times\) [time] = [angular momentum], as does \(\hbar\).

Why the Lagrangian?#

The Lagrangian is fundamental in classical mechanics because the principle of stationary action states:

(161)#\[ \delta S = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Equations of motion}\]

The path followed by a classical particle is the one for which \(S\) is stationary (extremized). Varying \(S\) with respect to the path gives the Euler-Lagrange equations:

(162)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{dt}}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}^i}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x^i} = 0\]

In quantum mechanics, the path integral retains all paths, but each is weighted by the classical action. This is a beautiful synthesis: quantum mechanics respects the classical action, but explores all possibilities.

Physical Interpretation: Amplitude and Interference#

Equal Magnitude Contribution#

Each path contributes with unit amplitude (magnitude 1): \(|\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}| = 1\). This may seem counterintuitive—shouldn’t “unlikely” paths contribute less? In the path integral framework, all paths contribute equally in amplitude; what matters is their relative phase.

Phase Weighting: Interference#

Two paths \(\boldsymbol{x}_1(t)\) and \(\boldsymbol{x}_2(t)\) have phases \(\Phi_1 = S_1/\hbar\) and \(\Phi_2 = S_2/\hbar\). Their phase difference is

(163)#\[ \Delta\Phi = \frac{S_1 - S_2}{\hbar}\]

Constructive interference: When \(\Delta\Phi = 2\pi n\) (phase difference is a multiple of \(2\pi\)), the two paths interfere constructively—their amplitudes add.

Destructive interference: When \(\Delta\Phi = \pi(2n+1)\) (phase difference is an odd multiple of \(\pi\)), the paths interfere destructively—their amplitudes partially cancel.

Stationary action paths: If two paths have nearly the same action (differ by \(\lesssim \hbar\)), their phases are nearly equal, and they interfere constructively. Paths clustered around a stationary point of \(S\) all have similar phases and add coherently.

Classical Path Dominance#

The classical path is defined by \(\delta S = 0\). Near the classical path:

\[ S[\boldsymbol{x}_\text{cl} + \delta\boldsymbol{x}] \approx S[\boldsymbol{x}_\text{cl}] + \frac{1}{2}(\delta\boldsymbol{x})^T M (\delta\boldsymbol{x}) + \cdots\]

where \(M\) is the second variation (Hessian). For a small deviation \(\delta\boldsymbol{x} \sim \sqrt{\hbar}\), the action difference is \(\Delta S \sim \hbar\), giving a phase difference \(\sim 1\) radian.

In the classical limit (\(\hbar \to 0\)):

  • Paths with \(|S - S_\text{cl}| \gg \hbar\) have rapidly oscillating phases and cancel destructively.

  • Only paths with \(|S - S_\text{cl}| \lesssim \hbar\) (a neighborhood of the classical path) interfere constructively.

  • As \(\hbar \to 0\), the constructive region shrinks to zero, and only the classical path survives.

Quantum-to-classical correspondence

The path integral naturally explains the correspondence principle:

  • Macroscopic systems: \(S \gg \hbar\) for every path in the problem. The classical path’s action dominates. Quantum fluctuations are negligible.

  • Microscopic systems: \(S \sim \hbar\). Many paths contribute significantly. Quantum effects are important.

  • Classical limit: \(\hbar \to 0\) (or equivalently, \(S \to \infty\) relative to \(\hbar\)). The classical path is absolutely selected. Quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics.

Path Integral vs. Schrödinger Equation#

The path integral and Schrödinger equation are equivalent formulations of quantum mechanics, but they provide different insights.

Derivability: The Schrödinger equation can be derived from the path integral using the composition property and a small-time-step approximation. Conversely, the path integral can be constructed as a solution to the Schrödinger equation using the propagator.

Free Particle as Motivation#

For a free particle (no potential, \(V = 0\)), the Lagrangian is \(L = \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2\). The action is

(164)#\[ S[\boldsymbol{x}] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 \, \mathrm{d}t\]

Classically, the extremum is a straight line (constant velocity):

(165)#\[ \boldsymbol{x}_\text{cl}(t) = \boldsymbol{x}_i + \frac{\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i}{t_f - t_i}(t - t_i)\]

with action

(166)#\[ S_\text{cl} = \frac{m}{2}\left(\frac{\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i}{t_f - t_i}\right)^2 (t_f - t_i) = \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2}{2(t_f - t_i)}\]

For a free particle, the path integral can be evaluated exactly (Section 3.2.2), giving a Gaussian result.

Summary#

  • Path integral: Quantum amplitude is a sum over all paths, each weighted by \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S/\hbar}\).

  • Propagator: Transition amplitude between two spacetime points; solves the Schrödinger equation.

  • Composition property: Propagators multiply for sequential time intervals.

  • Action functional: \(S[\boldsymbol{x}] = \int L \, \mathrm{d}t\) is the classical action. Stationary points give classical equations of motion.

  • Interference: Paths with nearly equal action interfere constructively; paths with differing action cancel.

  • Classical limit: \(\hbar \to 0\) suppresses paths away from stationary action; classical mechanics emerges.

  • Equivalence: Path integral and Schrödinger equation are equivalent formulations of quantum mechanics.

Derivation: Single-Slice Gaussian Integral

For a free particle propagating over a time interval \(\varepsilon\), the action is

\[S[\boldsymbol{x}] = \int_0^\varepsilon \frac{m}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}^2 \, \mathrm{d}t\]

For a path from \(\boldsymbol{x}_i\) to \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\) in time \(\varepsilon\), we parameterize the path and evaluate the path integral.

In the time-sliced formulation, the path integral becomes

\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, \boldsymbol{x}_i; \varepsilon) = \int \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \, \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{x}]/\hbar}\]

For small \(\varepsilon\), the path is approximately a straight line, and we can write

\[\boldsymbol{x}(t) = \boldsymbol{x}_i + \frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)\varepsilon}{\varepsilon} t = \boldsymbol{x}_i + (\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)\frac{t}{\varepsilon}\]

This gives

\[S = \frac{m}{2} \left(\frac{\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i}{\varepsilon}\right)^2 \varepsilon = \frac{m(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2}{2\varepsilon}\]

The path integral is a Gaussian integral:

\[K = \int \mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] \, \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}\right)\]

Using the Gaussian integral formula \(\int \mathrm{e}^{-\alpha x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi/\alpha}\) (with \(\alpha \to -\mathrm{i}\alpha\)):

\[K(\boldsymbol{x}_f, \boldsymbol{x}_i; \varepsilon) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m(\boldsymbol{x}_f - \boldsymbol{x}_i)^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}\right)\]

where \(d\) is the spatial dimension. The prefactor \((m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon)^{d/2}\) is the normalization factor for a single time slice.

Derivation: Path Integral Normalization

The path integral measure \(\mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}]\) for \(N\) discrete time slices is defined as

\[\mathcal{D}[\boldsymbol{x}] = \prod_{n=1}^{N-1} d^d\boldsymbol{x}_n \cdot \lambda_N^{-N/2}\]

where \(\lambda_N\) is a normalization constant (dimension [length]\(^d\)) that ensures proper dimensions and convergence.

For a free particle, each time slice contributes a factor

\[\lambda_\varepsilon = \left(\frac{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}{m}\right)\]

For \(N\) slices spanning total time \(T = N\varepsilon\):

\[\lambda_N = \lambda_\varepsilon = \frac{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}{m}\]

The full propagator over \(N\) slices is

\[K_N = \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{d/2} \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}m(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2}{2\hbar\varepsilon}\right)\]
\[= \left(\frac{m}{2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon}\right)^{Nd/2} \int \prod_{n=1}^{N-1} d^d\boldsymbol{x}_n \, \exp\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar} \sum_n \frac{m}{2}\frac{(\boldsymbol{x}_{n+1} - \boldsymbol{x}_n)^2}{\varepsilon}\right)\]

The prefactor \(\left(m/2\pi \mathrm{i}\hbar\varepsilon\right)^{Nd/2}\) is the product of normalization factors for each time slice, ensuring that probability is conserved: \(\int K_N(\boldsymbol{x}_f, \boldsymbol{x}_i) \, d^d\boldsymbol{x}_i = 1\) for fixed \(\boldsymbol{x}_f\).

1.1.3 Hermitian Operators#

Prompts

  • What is an observable in quantum mechanics, and why must observables correspond to Hermitian operators?

  • What are eigenvalues and eigenvectors? Why do Hermitian operators have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors?

  • Write down the Pauli matrices explicitly and verify that they are Hermitian. What are their eigenvalues and eigenvectors?

  • What is the expectation value of an observable, and how is it computed? What does it mean physically?

  • How does measurement change a state? Connect the measurement postulate to eigenvalue equations.

Lecture Notes#

Observation in quantum mechanics is not passive—it is fundamentally tied to the mathematical structure of reality through operators. To measure a physical quantity is to ask which values are possible, which states correspond to those values, and how certain we can be of the result. Hermitian operators encode this structure completely. They are the mathematical embodiment of our ability to know, predict, and control quantum systems, and understanding their properties is essential to understanding the bridge between theory and experiment.

Observables as Hermitian Operators#

In quantum mechanics, a physical observable (measurable quantity) is represented by a Hermitian operator acting on the Hilbert space. This is one of the fundamental postulates of quantum mechanics.

Definition (Hermitian operator): An operator \(\hat{O}\) is Hermitian (or self-adjoint) if

(13)#\[\hat{O}^\dagger = \hat{O}\]

where \(\hat{O}^\dagger\) is the Hermitian conjugate (or adjoint), defined by the property

(14)#\[\langle \psi | \hat{O} \phi \rangle = \langle \hat{O}^\dagger \psi | \phi \rangle\]

for all states \(|\psi\rangle, |\phi\rangle\).

Hermitian Conjugate and Matrix Form#

For a matrix \(M\), the Hermitian conjugate is the conjugate transpose:

(15)#\[M^\dagger = (M^*)^T = (M^T)^*\]
\[\begin{split}M = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix},\end{split}\]

A matrix is Hermitian if \(M = M^\dagger\). To compute the Hermitian conjugate:

Derivation: Hermitian Conjugate

\[\begin{split}M^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} a^* & c^* \\ b^* & d^* \end{pmatrix}.\end{split}\]

For Hermiticity, we need \(M = M^\dagger\), which requires:

  • Diagonal elements are real: \(a, d \in \mathbb{R}\)

  • Off-diagonal elements are conjugate pairs: \(b = c^*\)

Hermitian operators have the crucial property that their eigenvalues are real. Since measurement outcomes are real numbers (we observe real values on our instruments), the operators representing observables must be Hermitian. This is not just convenience—it’s a requirement for quantum mechanics to be physically sensible.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

For a linear operator \(\hat{O}\) acting on a state \(|\psi\rangle\), there may exist special states (eigenstates) that are only rescaled by the operator:

Eigenvalue equation:

(16)#\[\hat{O} |\psi\rangle = \lambda |\psi\rangle\]

where:

  • \(|\psi\rangle\) is an eigenvector (or eigenstate)

  • \(\lambda\) is the eigenvalue, a number (generally complex) that scales the eigenvector

Properties of Hermitian Operators

Theorem: Let \(\hat{O}\) be a Hermitian operator. Then:

  1. Eigenvalues are real: If \(\hat{O}|\psi\rangle = \lambda|\psi\rangle\) with \(\hat{O}^\dagger = \hat{O}\), then \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\).

:class: dropdown note

Take the inner product of the eigenvalue equation with \(\langle\psi|\):

\[\langle\psi|\hat{O}|\psi\rangle = \lambda \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = \lambda.\]

Taking the complex conjugate: $\(\langle\psi|\hat{O}|\psi\rangle^* = \lambda^*.\)$

But also:

\[\langle\psi|\hat{O}|\psi\rangle^* = \langle\psi|\hat{O}^\dagger|\psi\rangle = \langle\psi|\hat{O}|\psi\rangle = \lambda\]

(using Hermiticity and the fact that \(\langle\psi|\hat{O}|\psi\rangle\) is a number). Thus \(\lambda = \lambda^*\), so \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\). ✓

  1. Eigenvectors are orthogonal (for distinct eigenvalues): If \(\hat{O}|\psi_1\rangle = \lambda_1|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(\hat{O}|\psi_2\rangle = \lambda_2|\psi_2\rangle\) with \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\), then \(\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = 0\).

:class: dropdown note

\[\langle\psi_1|\hat{O}|\psi_2\rangle = \lambda_2 \langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle.\]

Also:

\[\langle\psi_1|\hat{O}|\psi_2\rangle = (\hat{O}|\psi_1\rangle)^\dagger|\psi_2\rangle = (\lambda_1|\psi_1\rangle)^\dagger|\psi_2\rangle = \lambda_1^* \langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = \lambda_1 \langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle\]

(since \(\lambda_1\) is real for Hermitian operators). Thus:

\[\lambda_2 \langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = \lambda_1 \langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle \implies (\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = 0.\]

Since \(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\), we have \(\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = 0\). ✓

  1. Completeness: For a 2-dimensional system (like a qubit), there are at most two linearly independent eigenvectors. These span the entire Hilbert space and form a basis (the eigenbasis of the operator).

The Pauli Matrices

The most important Hermitian operators for a qubit are the Pauli matrices, representing spin along three orthogonal directions:

(17)#\[\begin{split}\hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\sigma}^y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{\sigma}^z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

All three are Hermitian (\(\hat{\sigma}^i = (\hat{\sigma}^i)^\dagger\)) with eigenvalues \(\pm 1\).

The Pauli matrices satisfy the algebra:

\[[\hat{\sigma}^i, \hat{\sigma}^j] = 2i\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{\sigma}^k\]

where \(\epsilon_{ijk}\) is the Levi-Civita symbol. A detailed exploration of Pauli algebra, spin representations, and commutation relations is the focus of §2.2 in Chapter 2.

Expectation Values

For a quantum state \(|\psi\rangle\) and an observable \(\hat{O}\), the expectation value (or mean value) is

(18)#\[\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{O} | \psi \rangle\]

Properties:

  • For a Hermitian operator, \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle\) is real (automatically).

  • It represents the average outcome if we measure \(\hat{O}\) on many identically prepared states.

For the state \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\) and observable \(\hat{\sigma}^z\):

Calculation: Expectation Value

\[\begin{split}\langle \hat{\sigma}^z \rangle = \frac{1}{2}(\langle 0| + \langle 1|) \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\end{split}\]
\[= \frac{1}{2}(\langle 0| + \langle 1|)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}(1 - 1) = 0\]

This makes physical sense: the state is an equal superposition of spin-up and spin-down along z, so the average z-spin is zero.

The Measurement Postulate#

Postulate (Measurement): When we measure an observable \(\hat{O}\) on a system in state \(|\psi\rangle\):

  1. Possible outcomes: The measurement result is an eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\) of \(\hat{O}\).

  2. Probability: The probability of obtaining outcome \(\lambda_i\) is $$

    P_i = |\langle \psi_i | \psi \rangle|^2

    (19)#\[\]

    where \(|\psi_i\rangle\) is the normalized eigenvector corresponding to eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\).

  3. State collapse: After the measurement, the state collapses to the eigenstate \(|\psi_i\rangle\) corresponding to the observed outcome.

Average Outcome#

The expectation value is the average of all possible outcomes weighted by their probabilities:

(20)#\[\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \sum_i \lambda_i P_i = \sum_i \lambda_i |\langle \psi_i | \psi \rangle|^2\]

This is consistent with the definition \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{O} | \psi \rangle\).

Spectral Decomposition#

Any Hermitian operator can be decomposed in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenprojectors:

(21)#\[\hat{O} = \sum_i \lambda_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\]

where \(|\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\) is the projector onto the eigenstate \(|\psi_i\rangle\).

For \(\hat{\sigma}^z\):

Verification: Spectral Decomposition

\[\begin{split}\hat{\sigma}^z = (+1)|0\rangle\langle 0| + (-1)|1\rangle\langle 1| = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \quad \checkmark\end{split}\]

Key Result: Measurement and Expectation

The expectation value \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{O} | \psi \rangle\) is:

  • The average result of many measurements on identically prepared states.

  • Computable as a sum of eigenvalues weighted by probabilities.

  • A real number (when \(\hat{O}\) is Hermitian).

  • The one number that characterizes the state’s response to measurement of that observable.

Summary#

  • Observables are represented by Hermitian operators, which have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

  • The Pauli matrices \(\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z\) are the fundamental observables for a qubit, representing spin along the three axes.

  • Eigenvalues are the possible measurement outcomes; eigenvectors are the states with definite outcomes.

  • The measurement postulate connects theory to experiment: measurement outcomes are eigenvalues, with probabilities given by the Born rule.

  • Expectation values are averages of measurement outcomes and are computed as \(\langle \hat{O} \rangle = \langle \psi | \hat{O} | \psi \rangle\).

2. Consider the operator \(\hat{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & i \\ i & 1 \end{pmatrix}\). Is \(\hat{A}\) Hermitian? If not, find \(\hat{A}^\dagger\) and construct a Hermitian operator from \(\hat{A}\) using the combination \(\frac{1}{2}(\hat{A} + \hat{A}^\dagger)\).

3. A qubit is in the state \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}|0\rangle + \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}|1\rangle\). Compute the expectation values \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^x\rangle\), \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^y\rangle\), and \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^z\rangle\). Verify that \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^x\rangle^2 + \langle\hat{\sigma}^y\rangle^2 + \langle\hat{\sigma}^z\rangle^2 = 1\).

4. The spectral decomposition of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) is \(\hat{\sigma}^x = (+1)|+\rangle\langle+| + (-1)|-\rangle\langle-|\), where \(|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\) and \(|-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle)\). Verify this by explicitly computing the right-hand side as a \(2\times 2\) matrix.

5. Show that the eigenvalues of any projector \(\hat{P} = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\) are 0 and 1. What are the corresponding eigenstates? Is a projector Hermitian?

6. A spin-1/2 particle is in the state \(|\psi\rangle = \cos\alpha\,|\uparrow\rangle + \sin\alpha\,|\downarrow\rangle\) (with \(\alpha\) real). You measure \(\hat{\sigma}^z\). What are the possible outcomes and their probabilities? Then compute \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^z\rangle\) directly from the definition \(\langle\psi|\hat{\sigma}^z|\psi\rangle\) and verify it equals \(\sum_i \lambda_i P_i\).

7. The operator \(\hat{n}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} = n_x \hat{\sigma}^x + n_y \hat{\sigma}^y + n_z \hat{\sigma}^z\) represents spin along an arbitrary unit vector \(\hat{n} = (n_x, n_y, n_z)\) with \(|\hat{n}| = 1\). Show that \((\hat{n}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}})^2 = \hat{I}\) (the identity matrix). What does this imply about the eigenvalues of \(\hat{n}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\)?

8. Using the result of Problem 7, show that the eigenvalues of \(\hat{n}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\) are \(\pm 1\) for any unit vector \(\hat{n}\). Find the eigenvector corresponding to eigenvalue \(+1\) for the case \(\hat{n} = (\sin\theta, 0, \cos\theta)\) (a vector in the \(xz\)-plane). Express your answer in terms of \(\theta\).

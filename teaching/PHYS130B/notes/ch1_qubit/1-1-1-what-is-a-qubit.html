1.1.1 What is a Qubit#

Prompts

  • What distinguishes quantum physics from classical physics in the way it describes physical systems? How does quantum mechanics represent states and predict outcomes?

  • What is a qubit, and why is it the simplest quantum system? How does it illustrate the core ideas of quantum mechanics?

  • How do classical bits and qubits differ in terms of what information they can hold and how we learn about that information through measurement?

  • Why can the same quantum system (like an electron’s spin) be realized in many different physical platforms, yet all obey identical mathematical rules?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Quantum mechanics is famously abstract. Unlike gravity or fluid flow, you cannot build intuition from everyday experience—no one has ever “seen” a superposition or “felt” an entangled state. The quantum world operates on scales and principles so far from daily life that we cannot simply model it by analogy. Instead, we must rely on mathematical structures and learn what nature does from observation data.

This is not as alien as it sounds. Modern machine learning has shown us a powerful paradigm: everything is a vector. Images, words, proteins, music—all are embedded as vectors in high-dimensional spaces, and all transformations (classification, generation, translation) are operators acting on those vectors. Quantum mechanics arrived at the same insight a century earlier: every physical state is a vector, and every physical process—measurement, time evolution, symmetry—is an operator.

What is Physics? Description and Prediction#

Two Pillars of Physics

Physics rests on two fundamental objectives:

  1. Description: How do we represent the state of a physical system?

  2. Prediction: Given a state, how do we predict the outcome of an experiment?

For a classical system (a ball rolling down a ramp), we describe the state by specifying position and velocity. We predict the future using Newton’s laws.

For a quantum system, the description and prediction mechanisms are radically different.

Quantum mechanics answers:

The Qubit: Mathematical Definition#

Qubit Definition

A qubit is a quantum system that lives in a two-dimensional Hilbert space over the complex numbers. Any pure state is a unit vector (a “ket”):

(1)#\[|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\]

where \(\vert 0\rangle\) and \(\vert 1\rangle\) are orthonormal basis states, and \(\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}\).

Key properties:

  • Basis states \(\vert 0\rangle\) and \(\vert 1\rangle\) are orthonormal: \(\langle 0\vert 1\rangle = 0\), \(\langle 0\vert 0\rangle = 1\).

  • Superposition: Unlike a classical bit (which is 0 or 1), a qubit can be in a superposition of both. The coefficients \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) describe the quantum state.

  • Normalization: The condition \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\) ensures the state is normalized. We interpret \(|\alpha|^2\) as the probability of measuring the qubit in state \(\vert 0\rangle\).

  • Global phase: States that differ by a global phase (e.g., \(\vert \psi\rangle\) and \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi}\vert \psi\rangle\)) are physically identical. Thus a qubit is truly a two-parameter system (after factoring out global phase and normalization).

Derivation: Global Phase is Unobservable

Let \(\vert\psi'\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\vert\psi\rangle\) for any real \(\gamma\). For any measurement outcome \(\vert m\rangle\), the Born rule gives:

\[ P(m \vert \psi') = \vert\langle m\vert\psi'\rangle\vert^2 = \vert\langle m\vert\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\psi\rangle\vert^2 = \vert\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\vert^2\,\vert\langle m\vert\psi\rangle\vert^2 = \vert\langle m\vert\psi\rangle\vert^2 = P(m \vert \psi) \]

Since \(\vert\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\vert = 1\) for any real \(\gamma\), every measurement probability is unchanged. Because all physical information about a quantum state is encoded in measurement probabilities, no experiment can ever distinguish \(\vert\psi\rangle\) from \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\vert\psi\rangle\).

This freedom lets us fix the global phase arbitrarily — for example, by requiring the coefficient of \(\vert 0\rangle\) to be real and non-negative. Doing so removes one real parameter from the state description, reducing the four real numbers \(\{\mathrm{Re}\,\alpha,\, \mathrm{Im}\,\alpha,\, \mathrm{Re}\,\beta,\, \mathrm{Im}\,\beta\}\) to three, and then normalization removes one more, leaving exactly two free real parameters.

Why the same mathematics everywhere: All two-level quantum systems obey the same Schrödinger equation with a \(2 \times 2\) Hamiltonian. The specific physics differs (magnetic fields, photonic transitions, circuit dynamics), but the abstract qubit rules are universal.

Discussion

Why do physicists care about two-level systems when nature has many more states?

A realistic electron has both orbital angular momentum and spin, giving many possible energy levels. A real atom has infinitely many states (different orbitals). Why restrict attention to qubits?

  • In many experimental conditions (low temperature, weak fields, far from resonance with other transitions), only the two lowest energy levels are significantly populated. The two-level approximation is valid when \(k_B T \ll \Delta E\) and the driving field frequency is resonant with only one transition.

  • The qubit is the minimal quantum system that exhibits superposition, entanglement, measurement, and unitary evolution. All quantum computation can (in principle) be built from qubits.

  • Show that if you restrict a three-level system to only two of its three states, you lose nothing: any unitary transformation on the two-level subspace can be applied without mixing in the third state (under what conditions is this true?).

Proof: No-Cloning Theorem

Claim. There is no unitary operator \(\hat{U}\) on a two-qubit Hilbert space such that

\[ \hat{U}\vert\psi\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert\psi\rangle\vert\psi\rangle \quad \text{for all }\vert\psi\rangle. \]

Proof (by linearity of quantum mechanics).

Suppose such a \(\hat{U}\) existed. Apply it to the two computational basis states:

\[ \hat{U}\vert 0\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert 0\rangle\vert 0\rangle, \qquad \hat{U}\vert 1\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\vert 1\rangle. \]

Now consider the superposition state \(\vert+\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert 0\rangle + \vert 1\rangle)\). By the assumed cloning property:

\[ \hat{U}\vert+\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert+\rangle\vert+\rangle = \frac{1}{2}(\vert 0\rangle + \vert 1\rangle)(\vert 0\rangle + \vert 1\rangle) = \frac{1}{2}(\vert 00\rangle + \vert 01\rangle + \vert 10\rangle + \vert 11\rangle). \]

But quantum mechanics is linear: since \(\vert+\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert 0\rangle\vert 0\rangle + \vert 1\rangle\vert 0\rangle)\), unitarity demands:

\[ \hat{U}\vert+\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\hat{U}\vert 0\rangle\vert 0\rangle + \hat{U}\vert 1\rangle\vert 0\rangle\bigr) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert 00\rangle + \vert 11\rangle). \]

These two outputs are different:

\[ \frac{1}{2}(\vert 00\rangle + \vert 01\rangle + \vert 10\rangle + \vert 11\rangle) \neq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert 00\rangle + \vert 11\rangle). \]

The left side is a product state; the right side is a maximally entangled Bell state. This contradicts the assumption that \(\hat{U}\) clones all states. \(\blacksquare\)

Alternative proof via inner products. Suppose \(\hat{U}\) clones two states \(\vert\psi\rangle\) and \(\vert\phi\rangle\):

\[ \hat{U}\vert\psi\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert\psi\rangle\vert\psi\rangle, \qquad \hat{U}\vert\phi\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \vert\phi\rangle\vert\phi\rangle. \]

Since \(\hat{U}\) is unitary, it preserves inner products:

\[ \langle\phi\vert\psi\rangle = \langle\phi\vert\langle 0\vert \hat{U}^\dagger\hat{U} \vert\psi\rangle\vert 0\rangle = \langle\phi\vert\phi\vert\psi\rangle\vert\psi\rangle = (\langle\phi\vert\psi\rangle)^2. \]

Setting \(c = \langle\phi\vert\psi\rangle\): the equation \(c = c^2\) has only two solutions, \(c = 0\) or \(c = 1\). Therefore a cloning machine can only clone states that are identical (\(c=1\)) or perfectly distinguishable (\(c=0\)). An arbitrary unknown state \(\vert\psi\rangle\) cannot be cloned.

Summary#

  • Physics is about description and prediction: Quantum mechanics describes systems as vectors in Hilbert space and predicts outcomes using operators and the Born rule.

  • A qubit is the simplest quantum system: It lives in a two-dimensional complex vector space and can exist in a superposition of basis states \(\vert 0\rangle\) and \(\vert 1\rangle\).

  • Qubits are physically universal: Electron spins, photon polarizations, trapped atoms, superconducting circuits, and many other platforms all function as qubits—the mathematics is identical even though the physics varies.

  • Measurement is invasive in quantum mechanics: Unlike classical measurement, measuring a qubit collapses its superposition and changes the state for future measurements.

  • Qubits form the foundation of quantum information: All quantum computation and quantum information processing is built from qubits and quantum gates operating on them.

(c) Now consider \(\vert\phi\rangle = \tfrac{3}{5}\vert 0\rangle - \tfrac{4}{5}\vert 1\rangle\). Are \(\vert\psi\rangle\) and \(\vert\phi\rangle\) distinguishable by a measurement in the \(\{\vert 0\rangle, \vert 1\rangle\}\) basis? What physical property do they share?

2. A normalized qubit state has the form \(\vert\psi\rangle = A\vert 0\rangle + \tfrac{1+\mathrm{i}}{2}\vert 1\rangle\), where \(A > 0\) is a real constant. Find \(A\) from the normalization condition \(\langle\psi\vert\psi\rangle = 1\). What is the probability \(P(1)\) of measuring outcome \(1\)?

3. Show that two states differing by a global phase, \(\vert\psi'\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma}\vert\psi\rangle\) (with \(\gamma \in \mathbb{R}\)), give identical measurement probabilities for every outcome \(\vert m\rangle\): show \(\vert\langle m\vert\psi'\rangle\vert^2 = \vert\langle m\vert\psi\rangle\vert^2\). Conclude that global phase carries no observable information.

4. A generic qubit state \(\vert\psi\rangle = \alpha\vert 0\rangle + \beta\vert 1\rangle\) naively requires 4 real parameters (Re\(\,\alpha\), Im\(\,\alpha\), Re\(\,\beta\), Im\(\,\beta\)). Show that after imposing (a) normalization \(\vert\alpha\vert^2 + \vert\beta\vert^2 = 1\) and (b) global phase freedom, only 2 real parameters remain. How does this explain why the space of qubit states maps to a 2-dimensional sphere?

(b) Is the photon qubit susceptible to thermal noise at room temperature? Why or why not? (c) A superconducting transmon qubit at \(5\) GHz has energy gap \(\hbar\omega \approx 0.021\) meV.

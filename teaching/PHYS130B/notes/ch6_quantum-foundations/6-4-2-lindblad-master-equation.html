6.4.2 Lindblad Master Equation#

Prompts

  • What is a master equation, and how does it describe the dynamics of an open quantum system?

  • What is the Lindblad (GKSL) equation, and why is it the most general Markovian master equation?

  • What are Lindblad operators (jump operators), and what physical process does each represent?

  • How do you construct a Lindblad equation from first principles for a specific physical system?

  • What is the quantum jump picture, and how does it relate to the Lindblad master equation?

  • What are common examples of Lindblad operators: decay, dephasing, and depolarizing channels?

  • What is a steady state of the master equation, and how do you find it?

Lecture Notes#

For an isolated system, the Schrödinger equation perfectly captures quantum evolution. But real systems interact with their surroundings—heat baths, photon fields, uncontrolled degrees of freedom. The Lindblad master equation describes how open systems evolve, accounting for both unitary dynamics and the irreversible effects of environmental coupling. It is the most general form of Markovian (memoryless) evolution compatible with quantum mechanics.

Master Equation: General Form#

Discussion: Markovian Approximation

The Lindblad equation assumes the system and environment are uncorrelated at each instant—a Markovian approximation. But in reality, the environment can have memory. How do we know when Markovian is okay?

  • The Markovian approximation breaks down if the environment correlation time is comparable to the system timescale. How do we compute that correlation time from first principles?

  • Some systems show “non-Markovian revivals”—the system state temporarily recovers coherence it lost earlier. Does this invalidate the Lindblad equation, or is it simply outside its regime of applicability?

  • If we keep the Lindblad form but allow time-dependent \(\gamma_i(t)\), have we fixed the approximation, or just hidden the non-Markovianity in the time dependence?

For an open system coupled to environment (bath/reservoir), the master equation describes the reduced density matrix \(\rho_S\) (system only):

(351)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_S}{dt} = \mathcal{L}(\rho_S)\]

where \(\mathcal{L}\) is the Liouvillian superoperator (linear map on density matrices).

We derive the Lindblad equation from first principles:

Derivation: Lindblad Equation

Start with total Hamiltonian:

\[ H_\text{total} = H_S \otimes I_E + I_S \otimes H_E + H_\text{int}\]

The full system+environment evolves unitarily. Tracing out the environment (and assuming Markovian approximation: short environment memory):

(352)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_S}{dt} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}[H_S, \rho_S] + \mathcal{D}(\rho_S)\]

where:

  • First term: unitary evolution under system Hamiltonian.

  • Second term: dissipation superoperator describing decoherence.

Lindblad Master Equation (GKSL Form)

The most general form preserving trace and positivity is the Lindblad equation:

(353)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}[H, \rho] + \sum_k \gamma_k \left( L_k \rho L_k^\dagger - \frac{1}{2}\{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho\} \right)\]

where:

  • \(H\) = system Hamiltonian (Hermitian).

  • \(L_k\) = Lindblad operators (or jump operators); arbitrary operators (generally non-Hermitian).

  • \(\gamma_k \geq 0\) = decay rates (non-negative).

  • \(\{A, B\} = AB + BA\) = anticommutator.

Named: Lindblad (1976), Kossakowski, Sudarshan (GKSL form). This is the most general form of Markovian evolution compatible with complete positivity and trace preservation.

Physical Meaning of Terms#

Unitary Evolution#

\[ -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}[H, \rho]\]

Standard Schrödinger evolution of the isolated system.

Gain Term#

\[ \gamma_k L_k \rho L_k^\dagger\]

Gain of population by jumping via \(L_k\). Represents an inflow of probability as system undergoes transition \(L_k\).

Loss Term#

\[ -\frac{1}{2}\gamma_k \{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho\}\]

Loss of population by jumping out of the current state via \(L_k\). Ensures trace is conserved:

\[ \text{Tr}\left(-\frac{1}{2}\{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho\}\right) = -\text{Tr}(L_k^\dagger L_k \rho) = -\text{Tr}(L_k \rho L_k^\dagger)\]

Gain and loss balance to preserve \(\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\).

Key Properties of Lindblad Evolution

The Lindblad master equation preserves the fundamental properties of quantum states:

1. Trace Preservation: \(\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\text{Tr}(\rho) = 0\)
\(\Rightarrow\) \(\text{Tr}(\rho(t)) = 1\) for all \(t\) (probability conservation)

2. Hermiticity Preservation: If \(\rho(0)\) is Hermitian, then \(\rho(t)\) remains Hermitian

3. Positivity (Positive Semidefiniteness): If \(\rho(0) \geq 0\), then \(\rho(t) \geq 0\) for all \(t\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Evolution generates only valid quantum states

4. CPTP (Completely Positive Trace-Preserving): The time evolution generates a CPTP map (quantum channel); mathematically equivalent to Kraus representation with operators \(K_k = \sqrt{\gamma_k} L_k\).

These properties are necessary and sufficient for evolution to respect quantum mechanics; any master equation violating them is unphysical.

1. Trace Preservation:

(354)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\text{Tr}(\rho) = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Tr}(\rho(t)) = 1\]

2. Hermiticity Preservation: If \(\rho(0)\) is Hermitian, then \(\rho(t)\) remains Hermitian.

3. Positive Semidefiniteness (Positivity-Preserving): If \(\rho(0) \geq 0\), then \(\rho(t) \geq 0\) for all \(t\) (valid quantum state).

4. CPTP (Completely Positive Trace-Preserving): The time evolution generates a CPTP map (quantum channel); formally equivalent to Kraus representation.

Common Lindblad Operators#

1. Amplitude Damping: \(L = \sqrt{\Gamma_1} \hat{\sigma}_- = \sqrt{\Gamma_1} |{1}\rangle\langle{0}|\)#

Physical: Excited state decays to ground state; energy dissipation at rate \(\Gamma_1 = 1/T_1\).

Master equation:

(355)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = \Gamma_1 \left( \hat{\sigma}_- \rho \hat{\sigma}_+ - \frac{1}{2}\{\hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_-, \rho\} \right)\]

Effect on populations:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_{00}}{dt} = -\Gamma_1 \rho_{00}, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_{11}}{dt} = +\Gamma_1 \rho_{00}\]

Population decays from excited (\(|{1}\rangle\)) to ground (\(|{0}\rangle\)) at rate \(\Gamma_1\).

Effect on coherence:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_{01}}{dt} = -\frac{\Gamma_1}{2} \rho_{01}\]

Coherences decay at half the rate (because excited state population decreases).

2. Phase Damping (Dephasing): \(L = \sqrt{\Gamma_\phi} \hat{\sigma}^z\)#

Physical: Random phase fluctuations; coherences decay without energy loss at rate \(\Gamma_\phi = 1/T_2\) (where \(T_2 > T_1/2\)).

Master equation:

(356)#\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = \Gamma_\phi (\hat{\sigma}^z \rho \hat{\sigma}^z - \rho)\]

Effect: Only off-diagonal (coherence) terms decay:

\[ \rho_{01}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_\phi t} \rho_{01}(0)\]

Diagonal (population) terms unchanged. Dephasing creates classical mixtures.

3. Depolarizing: \(L_x = \sqrt{\Gamma_d/3} \hat{\sigma}^x\), \(L_y = \sqrt{\Gamma_d/3} \hat{\sigma}^y\), \(L_z = \sqrt{\Gamma_d/3} \hat{\sigma}^z\)#

Physical: Errors on all three Pauli axes with equal probability; generic noise at rate \(\Gamma_d\).

Master equation: Drives state toward maximally mixed \(I/2\) (after time \(\sim 1/\Gamma_d\)).

Steady States#

A steady state \(\rho_\infty\) satisfies:

(357)#\[ \mathcal{L}(\rho_\infty) = 0\]

i.e., \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_\infty}{dt} = 0\); the state does not evolve.

Example: Amplitude Damping

From \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_{00}}{dt} = -\Gamma_1 \rho_{00}\), steady state has \(\rho_{00}^\infty = 0\).

Thus: \(\rho_\infty = |{1}\rangle\langle{1}|\) (ground state; all population decayed).

Example: Amplitude Damping + Pumping

If system is pumped into excited state at rate \(\Gamma_2\) (with operator \(L_2 = \sqrt{\Gamma_2} \hat{\sigma}_+\)):

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho_{00}}{dt} = -\Gamma_1 \rho_{00} + \Gamma_2 \rho_{11}\]

Steady state: balance between decay and pumping.

\[ \rho_{00}^\infty = \frac{\Gamma_2}{\Gamma_1 + \Gamma_2}, \quad \rho_{11}^\infty = \frac{\Gamma_1}{\Gamma_1 + \Gamma_2}\]

Quantum Jump Picture (Stochastic Interpretation)#

Alternative viewpoint: Lindblad equation is the average over many quantum trajectories.

Trajectory picture:

  1. System evolves under modified Hamiltonian:

    \[ H_\text{eff} = H - \frac{\mathrm{i}\hbar}{2} \sum_k \gamma_k L_k^\dagger L_k\]

    (non-Hermitian: evolution is non-unitary).

  2. At random times (Poisson process), a “jump” occurs: \(\rho \to L_k \rho L_k^\dagger / p_k\) with probability \(p_k \propto \gamma_k\).

  3. Averaging over all trajectories (all possible jump sequences) reproduces the Lindblad master equation.

Insight: Decoherence and dissipation arise from random quantum jumps induced by the environment; averaging erases the randomness, revealing the ensemble behavior.

Quantum Regression Theorem#

For a system governed by Lindblad equation with steady state \(\rho_\infty\), the two-time correlation is:

(358)#\[ \langle A(t) B(0) \rangle = \text{Tr}(A \mathcal{E}^{(t)}(B \rho_\infty))\]

where \(\mathcal{E}^{(t)}\) is the time-evolved Liouvillian (solution of master equation).

Interpretation: Correlations at different times obey the same master equation as populations. One can predict oscillations and decay of correlations from the same Lindblad equation.

Solving the Lindblad Equation#

General case: Lindblad equation is a system of coupled ODEs for matrix elements of \(\rho\).

Methods:

  1. Analytical: For simple systems (single qubit, 2-level system); exact solutions exist.

  2. Numerical integration: RK4, implicit methods; QuTiP library (Python) widely used.

  3. Eigenbasis decomposition: Diagonalize Liouvillian matrix; solutions are exponentials \(\mathrm{e}^{\lambda_i t}\).

Example: Decay from Excited State

Initial condition: \(\rho(0) = |{0}\rangle\langle{0}|\). Under amplitude damping:

\[\begin{split} \rho(t) = \begin{pmatrix} (1-\mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_1 t}) & 0 \\ 0 & \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_1 t} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Wait: Let me recalculate. \(\rho_{00}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_1 t}\), so:

(359)#\[\begin{split} \rho(t) = \begin{pmatrix} \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_1 t} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 - \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_1 t} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Excited state population decays exponentially; ground state population grows. As \(t \to \infty\): \(\rho(\infty) = |{1}\rangle\langle{1}|\) (ground state).

Lindblad is Markovian

The Lindblad master equation assumes Markovian (memoryless) dynamics: the environment correlation time is much shorter than the system timescale. For structured environments or strong coupling, non-Markovian generalizations are needed.

Summary#

  • Master equation: Differential equation for reduced density matrix of open system; describes irreversible evolution.

  • Lindblad form \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}[H,\rho] + \sum_k \gamma_k(L_k\rho L_k^\dagger - \frac{1}{2}\{L_k^\dagger L_k, \rho\})\): Most general Markovian master equation.

  • Lindblad operators \(L_k\): Represent quantum jumps/transitions; rates \(\gamma_k \geq 0\).

  • CPTP: Lindblad evolution is completely positive, trace-preserving; valid quantum dynamics.

  • Common operators: Amplitude damping (\(\hat{\sigma}_-\)), phase damping (\(\hat{\sigma}^z\)), depolarizing (\(\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z\)).

  • Steady states: Fixed points \(\mathcal{L}(\rho_\infty) = 0\); often thermal equilibrium or ground states.

  • Quantum jump picture: Lindblad equation averages over random quantum trajectories; jumps occur at Poisson-distributed times.

  • Quantum regression theorem: Two-time correlations follow same master equation as populations.

Homework#

Problem 1: Lindblad Equation and Trace Preservation#

Consider a single qubit under amplitude damping with Lindblad operator \(L = \sqrt{\Gamma} \hat{\sigma}_-\), where \(\hat{\sigma}_- = |0\rangle\langle 1|\) is the lowering operator. The Lindblad master equation is:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = \Gamma \left( \hat{\sigma}_- \rho \hat{\sigma}_+ - \frac{1}{2}\{\hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_-, \rho\} \right)\]

where \(\hat{\sigma}_+ = |1\rangle\langle 0|\) is the raising operator.

(a) Show that \(\text{Tr}(d\rho/dt) = 0\) (trace is conserved). Hint: Use the cyclic property of trace and the fact that \(\{\hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_-, \rho\} = \hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_- \rho + \rho \hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_-\).

(b) Verify that \(\hat{\sigma}_+ \hat{\sigma}_- = I - |1\rangle\langle 1|\) (the projector onto the ground state).

(c) Show explicitly that if \(\rho(0)\) is Hermitian, then \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt}\) is also Hermitian (evolution preserves Hermiticity).

Problem 2: Amplitude Damping Dynamics#

A single qubit decays via amplitude damping at rate \(\Gamma_1 = 1/T_1\). Start with the excited state: \(\rho(0) = |1\rangle\langle 1|\).

(a) Write the Lindblad master equation explicitly for this system.

(b) Solve for the population in the excited state: \(\rho_{11}(t) = \langle 1 | \rho(t) | 1 \rangle\). Show that \(\rho_{11}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-t/T_1}\) (exponential decay).

(c) The ground state population is \(\rho_{00}(t) = 1 - \rho_{11}(t)\). Show that the steady state is \(\rho_\infty = |0\rangle\langle 0|\) (all population in ground state).

(d) Compute the coherence (off-diagonal term) \(\rho_{01}(t)\). Show that coherences decay faster than populations: \(\rho_{01}(t) = \rho_{01}(0) \mathrm{e}^{-t/(2T_1)}\).

Problem 3: Pure Dephasing (Phase Damping)#

A qubit undergoes pure dephasing (no energy loss) via Lindblad operator \(L = \sqrt{\Gamma_\phi} \hat{\sigma}^z\). The master equation is:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\rho}{dt} = \Gamma_\phi (\hat{\sigma}^z \rho \hat{\sigma}^z - \rho)\]

Start with a superposition state: \(\rho(0) = |+\rangle\langle +|\), where \(|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\).

(a) Write the density matrix explicitly: \(\rho(0) = \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\).

(b) Apply the Lindblad superoperator to each element:

  • Show that diagonal elements (\(\rho_{00}\), \(\rho_{11}\)) are not affected by dephasing (populations unchanged).

  • Show that off-diagonal elements decay: \(\rho_{01}(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma_\phi t} \rho_{01}(0)\).

(c) As \(t \to \infty\), the state becomes \(\rho_\infty = \frac{I}{2}\) (classical mixture). Explain physically: why does dephasing destroy coherence while preserving energy?

Problem 4: Lindblad Operators from Physical Processes#

An atom is coupled to a thermal radiation field at temperature \(T\). At low temperature, the dominant processes are:

  • Spontaneous emission (decay): Excited state \(|e\rangle \to |g\rangle\) at rate \(A\).

  • Absorption (excitation): Ground state \(|g\rangle \to |e\rangle\) at rate \(B n_\text{th}\), where \(n_\text{th} = 1/(\mathrm{e}^{\hbar\omega/k_B T} - 1)\) is the thermal photon number.

  • Dephasing: Collisions cause random phase shifts at rate \(\gamma_\phi\).

(a) Write the three Lindblad operators \(L_1\), \(L_2\), \(L_3\) corresponding to these processes (with rates \(A\), \(B n_\text{th}\), \(\gamma_\phi\)).

(b) At zero temperature (\(T \to 0\)), \(n_\text{th} \to 0\); only spontaneous emission occurs. Write the simplified master equation.

(c) Find the steady state at zero temperature. What is the long-time behavior? Explain.

(d) At high temperature (\(T \to \infty\), \(n_\text{th} \to k_B T / \hbar\omega\)), absorption and emission balance. Show that the steady state approaches the thermal Boltzmann distribution: \(\rho_\infty \propto \mathrm{e}^{-\beta E}\) (diagonal in energy basis), where \(\beta = 1/k_B T\).

Problem 5: Quantum Jump Picture#

In the quantum jump picture, the system evolves under an effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian:

\[ H_\text{eff} = H_0 - \frac{\mathrm{i}\hbar}{2} \sum_k \gamma_k L_k^\dagger L_k\]

At random times (Poisson process with rate \(\sum_k \gamma_k \langle L_k^\dagger L_k \rangle\)), a jump \(L_k\) occurs, collapsing the state.

For a single qubit with amplitude damping (\(L = \sqrt{\Gamma} \hat{\sigma}_-\), \(H_0 = 0\)):

(a) Compute \(H_\text{eff} = -\frac{\mathrm{i}\hbar\Gamma}{2} |1\rangle\langle 1|\). Show that this makes the evolution non-unitary; the norm of \(|\psi\rangle\) decreases over time.

(b) For initial state \(|\psi(0)\rangle = |1\rangle\) (excited), the norm decays as \(||\psi(t)||^2 = \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma t}\). This is the probability that no jump has occurred by time \(t\).

(c) If a jump occurs at time \(t\), the state becomes \(|\psi'\rangle \propto L |\psi(t)\rangle = |0\rangle\) (ground state). After the jump, no further jumps occur (excited state is now empty). Explain the physical interpretation: the jump represents a photon emission event.

(d) Compare: The average over all possible trajectories (no jumps, one jump at various times, multiple jumps, …) reproduces the Lindblad master equation. This is why \(\mathcal{E}[\rho(t)] = \mathcal{L}(\rho)\) on average. Conceptually, how is the deterministic master equation an average over stochastic quantum jumps?

Problem 6: Depolarizing Channel and Steady States#

A qubit undergoes depolarizing noise via three Lindblad operators:

\[ L_x = \sqrt{\Gamma/3} \hat{\sigma}^x, \quad L_y = \sqrt{\Gamma/3} \hat{\sigma}^y, \quad L_z = \sqrt{\Gamma/3} \hat{\sigma}^z\]

The master equation drives the state toward the maximally mixed state \(\rho_\infty = I/2\).

(a) Show that \(\rho_\infty = I/2\) is a steady state: \(\mathcal{L}(I/2) = 0\). Hint: Compute the dissipation term for Hermitian operators (which commute with their adjoints).

(b) Start with a pure state \(\rho(0) = |0\rangle\langle 0|\). Define the purity \(\mathcal{P}(t) = \text{Tr}(\rho(t)^2)\). Show that purity decreases monotonically: \(\mathcal{P}(0) = 1\) and \(\mathcal{P}(\infty) = 1/2\).

(c) Derive the differential equation \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathcal{P}}{dt}\) from the Lindblad equation. Show that \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathcal{P}}{dt} = -2\Gamma(\mathcal{P} - 1/2)\) (exponential approach to maximally mixed).

(d) Solve: \(\mathcal{P}(t) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{e}^{-2\Gamma t}\). At what timescale does the state become “classical-looking” (purity \(\approx 1/2\))?

Problem 7: Lindblad Equation and Channel Capacity#

A communication channel applies a depolarizing Lindblad evolution (Problem 6) to a qubit for time \(t = 1/(2\Gamma)\). The state at the output is:

\[ \rho_\text{out}(t) = \mathcal{E}(\rho_\text{in})\]

where \(\mathcal{E}\) is the quantum channel generated by the Lindblad equation.

(a) For input state \(\rho_\text{in} = |0\rangle\langle 0|\), compute the output \(\rho_\text{out}\) at \(t = 1/(2\Gamma)\). Use the result from Problem 6(d).

(b) The distinguishability of two states is measured by the trace distance:

\[ D(\rho, \sigma) = \frac{1}{2}\text{Tr}(|\rho - \sigma|)\]

where \(|A| = \sqrt{A^\dagger A}\).

For \(\rho_\text{in}^{(0)} = |0\rangle\langle 0|\) and \(\rho_\text{in}^{(1)} = |1\rangle\langle 1|\), compute \(D(\mathcal{E}(\rho_\text{in}^{(0)}), \mathcal{E}(\rho_\text{in}^{(1)}))\) at \(t = 1/(2\Gamma)\). How much has the distinguishability decreased?

(c) The classical capacity of a depolarizing channel is the maximum rate at which classical information can be reliably transmitted. At what noise strength (depolarizing parameter \(p = 1 - \mathrm{e}^{-\Gamma t}\)) does the channel become useless for information transmission (capacity drops to zero)?

(d) Conceptual: Explain why strong decoherence (large \(\Gamma\)) fundamentally limits quantum information processing. How would quantum error correction try to overcome this?

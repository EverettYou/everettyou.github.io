6.3.3 Quantum Channels#

Prompts

  • What is a quantum channel, and what properties must it satisfy (CPTP)?

  • What is the Kraus representation, and how does it describe all quantum channels?

  • How do you construct Kraus operators for a given physical channel?

  • What is the Choi-Jamiołkowski isomorphism, and how does it relate channels to states?

  • What are examples of common quantum channels: depolarizing, dephasing, amplitude damping?

  • How do you distinguish reversible (unitary) channels from irreversible ones?

  • What is partial transpose, and how does it detect non-physical channels?

Lecture Notes#

A quantum channel is a map that transforms one density matrix into another while respecting the rules of quantum mechanics: probabilities must be conserved, and all quantum properties preserved even for subsystems. These linear maps are the quantum generalization of classical communication channels, encoding everything from the evolution of open systems to the effects of quantum noise.

Definition

Quantum Channel

Discussion

The completely positive condition requires the channel to remain positive on extensions. Construct a map that is positive but not completely positive (e.g., the transpose map T: ρ → ρ^T) and show why it’s unphysical.

A quantum channel (or quantum operation) is a linear map:

\[ \mathcal{E}: \text{Density matrices on } \mathcal{H}_\text{in} \to \text{Density matrices on } \mathcal{H}_\text{out}\]

Representing any physical process: unitary evolution, measurement, dissipation, entanglement with environment, etc.

CPTP Properties#

A valid quantum channel must satisfy:

1. Linearity:

(332)#\[ \mathcal{E}(p\rho_1 + q\rho_2) = p\mathcal{E}(\rho_1) + q\mathcal{E}(\rho_2)\]

2. Trace preservation:

(333)#\[ \text{Tr}(\mathcal{E}(\rho)) = \text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\]

Probabilities are conserved; output is a valid density matrix.

3. Positivity:

(334)#\[ \rho \geq 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathcal{E}(\rho) \geq 0\]

Positive semidefinite inputs map to positive semidefinite outputs.

4. Complete positivity:

(335)#\[ \mathcal{E} \otimes \mathcal{I}_n \text{ is positive for all } n\]

Even when the system is entangled with auxiliary systems (e.g., reference qubits), the map preserves positivity.

Physical Motivation for Complete Positivity#

Consider two qubits A and B, entangled. You apply a channel \(\mathcal{E}\) to A only. If \(\mathcal{E}\) is merely positive but not completely positive, the map \(\mathcal{E} \otimes \mathcal{I}_B\) could produce negative eigenvalues in the joint system (negative probability!). This is unphysical.

Therefore: All physical channels must be completely positive.

Kraus Representation Theorem#

Theorem: A linear map \(\mathcal{E}\) on density matrices is CPTP if and only if it can be written in Kraus form:

(336)#\[ \mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_{k=1}^r K_k \rho K_k^\dagger\]

where the Kraus operators \(\{K_k\}\) satisfy:

(337)#\[ \sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k = I\]

(sum of products of Kraus operators equals identity).

The Kraus representation ensures complete positivity:

Proof: Kraus Representation Works

  • Trace-preserving: \(\text{Tr}(\sum_k K_k \rho K_k^\dagger) = \sum_k \text{Tr}(\rho K_k^\dagger K_k) = \text{Tr}(\rho \sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k) = \text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\). ✓

  • Positive semidefinite output: If \(\rho \geq 0\), then \(\mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k K_k \rho K_k^\dagger \geq 0\) (sum of positive operators). ✓

  • Completely positive: Structure ensures positivity even under tensor product with identity. ✓

Non-Uniqueness of Kraus Operators#

Given one set \(\{K_k\}\), other valid sets exist related by unitary rotation:

\[ K_k' = \sum_j U_{kj} K_j\]

where \(\{U_{kj}\}\) is a unitary matrix. Different representations yield the same channel map.

Minimal Kraus Representation#

The minimum number of Kraus operators needed is \(r = \text{rank}(J_\mathcal{E})\), where \(J_\mathcal{E}\) is the Choi matrix (discussed below). This is called the Kraus rank.

Choi-Jamiołkowski Isomorphism#

Choi-Jamiołkowski Isomorphism

The Choi matrix (or Choi state) is a bipartite state that uniquely encodes a channel:

(338)#\[ J_\mathcal{E} = (\mathcal{E} \otimes \mathcal{I})(|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|)\]

where \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}\sum_{i=0}^{d-1}|ii\rangle\) is the maximally entangled state (with \(d = \dim(\mathcal{H}_\text{in})\)).

A channel \(\mathcal{E}\) is CPTP if and only if its Choi matrix is positive semidefinite. This establishes a one-to-one correspondence between CPTP channels and positive semidefinite bipartite states.

Interpretation: The identity channel applied to one half of a maximally entangled pair; the other half undergoes channel \(\mathcal{E}\). The resulting state encodes the full information about \(\mathcal{E}\).

Reconstruction#

From the Choi matrix, reconstruct the channel via:

(339)#\[ \mathcal{E}(\rho) = \text{Tr}_1((I \otimes \rho^T) J_\mathcal{E})\]

where the partial transpose \(\rho^T\) is applied to the input system, and we trace over the reference system.

Complete positivity can be checked via the Choi matrix:

Criterion: CPTP via Choi-Jamiołkowski Matrix

\(\mathcal{E}\) is CPTP if and only if its Choi matrix \(J_\mathcal{E}\) is positive semidefinite (eigenvalues \(\geq 0\)).

Consequence: One-to-one correspondence between CPTP channels and positive semidefinite bipartite states.

Common Quantum Channels#

1. Unitary Channel#

(340)#\[ \mathcal{E}_U(\rho) = U \rho U^\dagger\]

Kraus form: Single operator \(K = U\) (with \(U^\dagger U = I\)).

Properties:

  • Reversible: inverse is \(\mathcal{E}_{U^\dagger}\).

  • No information loss: Entropy conserved: \(S(\mathcal{E}_U(\rho)) = S(\rho)\).

  • Isometry in quantum information sense.

2. Depolarizing Channel#

With probability \(p\), the state becomes completely random; with probability \(1-p\), it’s unchanged:

(341)#\[ \mathcal{E}_\text{depol}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p\frac{I}{d}\]

where \(d = \dim(\mathcal{H})\).

Kraus operators (for qubit, \(d=2\)):

(342)#\[ K_0 = \sqrt{1-p}\, I, \quad K_1 = \sqrt{p/3}\, \hat{\sigma}^x, \quad K_2 = \sqrt{p/3}\, \hat{\sigma}^y, \quad K_3 = \sqrt{p/3}\, \hat{\sigma}^z\]

Physical interpretation: Errors on all three Pauli axes with equal probability; models generic noise.

3. Amplitude Damping (\(T_1\) Process)#

Excited state decays to ground state at rate \(\Gamma_1 = 1/T_1\):

(343)#\[ \mathcal{E}_\text{amp}(\rho) = K_0 \rho K_0^\dagger + K_1 \rho K_1^\dagger\]

with:

(344)#\[\begin{split} K_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{1-\gamma} \end{pmatrix}, \quad K_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \sqrt{\gamma} \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(\gamma = 1 - \mathrm{e}^{-t/T_1}\) is the decay probability.

Physical interpretation: Energy dissipation; ground state is absorbing (fixed point).

4. Phase Damping (\(T_2\) Process, Dephasing)#

Coherences decay without energy loss at rate \(\Gamma_\phi = 1/T_2\):

(345)#\[ \mathcal{E}_\text{phase}(\rho) = K_0 \rho K_0^\dagger + K_1 \rho K_1^\dagger\]

with:

(346)#\[\begin{split} K_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{1-\gamma} \end{pmatrix}, \quad K_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{\gamma} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(\gamma = 1 - \mathrm{e}^{-t/T_2}\).

Physical interpretation: Random phase fluctuations; diagonal density matrix (classical-like).

5. Erasure Channel#

With probability \(p\), the system is lost (replaced by flag state \(|e\rangle\)); with probability \(1-p\), passed unchanged:

(347)#\[ \mathcal{E}_\text{erase}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho \oplus p|e\rangle\langle e|\]

Kraus operators:

\[ K_0 = \sqrt{1-p}\, I, \quad K_1 = \sqrt{p}\, |e\rangle\langle 0| + \ldots\]

Physical interpretation: Photon loss, quantum memory readout failure; allows identifying lost systems.

Reversibility and Irreversibility#

Reversible (Unitary) Channel:

  • Single Kraus operator \(K = U\) (unitary).

  • Entropy conserved: \(S(\mathcal{E}(\rho)) = S(\rho)\).

  • Inverse channel exists: \(\mathcal{E}^{-1}(\sigma) = U^\dagger \sigma U\).

  • No information loss; state can be recovered perfectly.

Irreversible (Non-Unitary) Channel:

  • Multiple Kraus operators (\(r > 1\)) or complex behavior.

  • Entropy generally increases: \(S(\mathcal{E}(\rho)) > S(\rho)\) (output more mixed).

  • No inverse: cannot recover original state from output (information lost).

  • Examples: Depolarizing, amplitude damping, erasure.

Partial Transpose and Physical Channels#

The partial transpose of a bipartite state \(\rho_{AB}\) (w.r.t. system B) is:

\[ \rho_{AB}^{T_B} = (I_A \otimes T_B)(\rho_{AB})\]

where \(T_B\) is the transpose operation on subsystem B: \((|i\rangle\langle j|)^T = |j\rangle\langle i|\).

Criterion for CPTP channels (via Choi matrix):

The channel \(\mathcal{E}\) is CPTP if and only if the partial transpose of its Choi matrix is positive semidefinite:

(348)#\[ J_\mathcal{E}^{T_2} \geq 0\]

Physical interpretation: The partial transpose detects whether a state is separable (no entanglement); similarly, it detects whether a channel is physical.

Channel Composition#

If \(\mathcal{E}_1\) and \(\mathcal{E}_2\) are channels, their composition (apply \(\mathcal{E}_1\) first, then \(\mathcal{E}_2\)) is also a channel:

(349)#\[ (\mathcal{E}_2 \circ \mathcal{E}_1)(\rho) = \mathcal{E}_2(\mathcal{E}_1(\rho))\]

Kraus operators: If \(\mathcal{E}_1(\rho) = \sum_k K_k \rho K_k^\dagger\) and \(\mathcal{E}_2(\rho) = \sum_j L_j \rho L_j^\dagger\), then:

\[ (\mathcal{E}_2 \circ \mathcal{E}_1)(\rho) = \sum_{j,k} (L_j K_k) \rho (L_j K_k)^\dagger\]

Kraus operators of the composition are products \(L_j K_k\).

Channel Capacity (Outlook)#

The classical capacity of a quantum channel is the maximum rate of classical information that can be reliably transmitted:

\[ C(\mathcal{E}) = \max_{\{p_i, \rho_i\}} I(X : Y)\]

where the max is over all input ensembles \(\{p_i, \rho_i\}\), and \(I(X:Y)\) is mutual information.

Computing capacity is generally hard; known for specific channels (depolarizing, erasure, amplitude damping).

CPTP maps are the most general evolution

A quantum channel \(\mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k K_k \rho K_k^\dagger\) (Kraus form) is the most general physical evolution of a density matrix. Unitary evolution is the special case with a single Kraus operator. Every open-system evolution is a quantum channel.

Summary#

  • Quantum channel: CPTP linear map from density matrices to density matrices.

  • CPTP: Completely positive, trace-preserving; ensures valid quantum states and probabilities.

  • Kraus representation \(\mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k K_k \rho K_k^\dagger\) with \(\sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k = I\): universal form for channels; non-unique.

  • Choi-Jamiołkowski isomorphism: Channel ↔ Choi state \(J_\mathcal{E} = (\mathcal{E} \otimes I)(|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|)\); CPTP ⟺ \(J_\mathcal{E} \geq 0\).

  • Common channels: Unitary (reversible), depolarizing (generic noise), amplitude damping (\(T_1\)), phase damping (\(T_2\)), erasure.

  • Reversibility: Unitary channels conserve entropy; non-unitary channels increase entropy (information loss).

  • Partial transpose criterion: Channel CPTP ⟺ \(J_\mathcal{E}^{T_2} \geq 0\) (partial transpose of Choi state positive).

  • Channel composition: Sequential channels are channels; Kraus operators compose as products.

Homework#

Problem 1: Kraus Completeness Relation#

Consider a quantum channel with Kraus operators \(\{K_1, K_2, K_3\}\). Show that the completeness relation \(\sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k = I\) is necessary and sufficient for the channel to be trace-preserving.

Hint: Start with the trace of the output \(\text{Tr}(\mathcal{E}(\rho)) = \sum_k \text{Tr}(K_k \rho K_k^\dagger)\) and use the cyclic property of trace.

Problem 2: Depolarizing Channel on Bloch Sphere#

A qubit depolarizing channel with parameter \(p\) is given by:

\[ \mathcal{E}_\text{depol}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + \frac{p}{2}I\]

Express this in Kraus form using Pauli operators \(\{I, \hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z\}\).

(a) Write down all four Kraus operators explicitly.

(b) Verify the completeness relation \(\sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k = I\).

(c) Interpret the action of the depolarizing channel on the Bloch vector: if \(\rho = \frac{1}{2}(I + \boldsymbol{r} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}})\) with Bloch vector \(\boldsymbol{r}\), show that the output has Bloch vector \(\boldsymbol{r}' = (1-p)\boldsymbol{r}\).

Problem 3: Amplitude Damping and Spontaneous Emission#

A two-level atom decays from excited state \(|1\rangle\) to ground state \(|0\rangle\) with lifetime \(T_1\). After time \(t\), the amplitude damping channel is given by Kraus operators:

\[\begin{split} K_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{1-\gamma} \end{pmatrix}, \quad K_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \sqrt{\gamma} \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(\gamma = 1 - \mathrm{e}^{-t/T_1}\).

(a) Verify the completeness relation \(K_0^\dagger K_0 + K_1^\dagger K_1 = I\).

(b) Apply the channel to a pure state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\). Write the output density matrix \(\mathcal{E}_\text{amp}(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)\) and interpret physically.

(c) Show that the ground state \(|0\rangle\) is a fixed point of the channel: \(\mathcal{E}_\text{amp}(|0\rangle\langle 0|) = |0\rangle\langle 0|\).

Problem 4: Completely Positive Trace-Preserving (CPTP) Maps#

Consider the map \(\mathcal{E}\) defined by:

\[ \mathcal{E}(\rho) = \frac{1}{2}\rho + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^z \rho \sigma^z\]

where \(\sigma^z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\).

(a) Show that this map is linear and trace-preserving.

(b) Express this map in Kraus form with operators \(K_0\) and \(K_1\).

(c) Verify complete positivity by checking that the map remains positive when tensored with identity: test on the maximally entangled state \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) and confirm \(J_\mathcal{E} \geq 0\).

Problem 5: Choi Matrix and Channel-State Duality#

For the amplitude damping channel (Problem 3), compute the Choi matrix:

\[ J_\mathcal{E} = (\mathcal{E} \otimes \mathcal{I})(|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|)\]

where \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) and \(\gamma = 0.5\) for concreteness.

(a) Apply the amplitude damping channel to the first qubit of \(|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|\) and compute the resulting \(4 \times 4\) Choi matrix.

(b) Verify that \(J_\mathcal{E}\) is positive semidefinite (i.e., all eigenvalues \(\geq 0\)).

(c) The Kraus rank of the channel equals the rank of the Choi matrix. Show that this channel has Kraus rank 2 by confirming the rank of \(J_\mathcal{E}\).

Problem 6: Composing Channels#

Consider two channels:

  • Phase flip (bit-flip on \(\sigma^z\)): \(\mathcal{E}_\text{phase}(\rho) = (1-q)\rho + q\sigma^z\rho\sigma^z\)

  • Amplitude damping: \(\mathcal{E}_\text{amp}\) from Problem 3 with \(\gamma = 0.1\)

(a) Express the phase-flip channel in Kraus form.

(b) The composition \(\mathcal{E}_\text{comp} = \mathcal{E}_\text{amp} \circ \mathcal{E}_\text{phase}\) applies phase flip first, then amplitude damping. Write the Kraus operators of the composed channel.

(c) Show that the composed channel is still CPTP. Is the composition commutative? Does \(\mathcal{E}_\text{amp} \circ \mathcal{E}_\text{phase} = \mathcal{E}_\text{phase} \circ \mathcal{E}_\text{amp}\)? Explain physically.

Problem 7: Distinguishing Quantum Noise Channels#

Three noise channels (all with parameter \(p = 0.5\)) act on a qubit:

  • Bit-flip (X noise): \(\mathcal{E}_X(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p\sigma^x\rho\sigma^x\)

  • Phase-flip (Z noise): \(\mathcal{E}_Z(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p\sigma^z\rho\sigma^z\)

  • Depolarizing: \(\mathcal{E}_\text{depol}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + \frac{p}{2}I\) (random Pauli, normalized)

(a) Compute the Choi matrix for each channel and verify all are positive semidefinite.

(b) Compute the purity \(\text{Tr}(J_\mathcal{E}^2)\) of each Choi matrix. Which channel is most/least noisy?

(c) Apply each channel to the input state \(\rho_\text{in} = \frac{1}{2}(I + \hat{\sigma}^x)\) (eigenstate of \(\sigma^x\)). Compare the outputs: which channel preserves the most information about the input?

(d) Conceptual: The depolarizing channel is called “universal noise” because it cannot distinguish the computational basis. Explain why bit-flip and phase-flip channels can be distinguished by an appropriate measurement on the output, but depolarizing cannot.

6.1.1 Mixed States#

Prompts

  • What is the fundamental difference between a pure state and a mixed state? Why can’t you describe a mixed state by a single ket?

  • For a qubit in an equal superposition, construct its density matrix. What would the density matrix look like for a statistical mixture (classical noise)?

  • What mathematical properties must a density matrix satisfy? (Hermitian, trace, positive semidefinite)

  • How does \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2)\) measure purity? What does \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) < 1\) tell you about the mixedness of a state?

  • Can two completely different ensembles give the same density matrix? What does this tell us about the information lost in going from state to density matrix?

Lecture Notes#

Not all quantum states are pure; sometimes we possess only incomplete information about a system, or it has interacted with an inaccessible environment. The density matrix elegantly captures this ignorance, unifying our description: whether we lack information by choice or by necessity, the formalism treats both identically. This single operator becomes the bridge between pure quantum coherence and classical uncertainty.

Pure States via Density Matrix#

Discussion

A density matrix ρ = (1/2)I describes maximum ignorance. But two different ensembles {|+z⟩,|-z⟩} and {|+x⟩,|-x⟩} give the same ρ. Construct an experiment that distinguishes these two ensembles — is it possible?

Discussion

One of the deepest questions in quantum mechanics: does a mixed state \(\rho\) represent our ignorance about which pure state the system “truly” occupies, or is mixedness a fundamental property of the system itself?

  • If I have a 50-50 mixture of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), is there a fact of the matter about which one it is, but I just don’t know? Or is the system genuinely neither one nor a superposition?

  • Can a truly isolated quantum system, with no inaccessible environment, ever be in a genuinely mixed state? Or would isolation force it to be pure?

  • If two different ensembles produce the same \(\rho\), does that mean they are physically equivalent, or could measurements in the future distinguish them?

Definition: Density Matrix for Pure States

For a pure state \(|\psi\rangle\), the density operator is:

(182)#\[ \rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\]

This is an outer product (rank-1 projection operator).

Example: Qubit in Superposition

Let \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha |{0}\rangle + \beta |{1}\rangle\) with \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\).

(183)#\[\begin{split} |\psi\rangle\langle\psi| = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} |\alpha|^2 & \alpha^*\beta \\ \beta^*\alpha & |\beta|^2 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Interpretation:

  • Diagonal elements (populations): \(|\alpha|^2\) = probability of measuring \(|{0}\rangle\); \(|\beta|^2\) = probability of measuring \(|{1}\rangle\).

  • Off-diagonal elements (coherences): \(\alpha^*\beta, \beta^*\alpha\) = quantum interference terms; reflect superposition.

If \(\alpha = 1/\sqrt{2}, \beta = 1/\sqrt{2}\) (superposition of \(|{0}\rangle + |{1}\rangle\)):

(184)#\[\begin{split} \rho = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Off-diagonal terms \(\rho_{01} = 1/2\) indicate coherence between states.

Properties of Pure-State Density Matrices#

For a pure state \(|\psi\rangle\), the density matrix \(\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\) satisfies fundamental properties:

  1. Hermiticity: \(\rho^\dagger = \rho\)

  2. Trace normalization: \(\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\)

  3. Idempotence: \(\rho^2 = \rho\) (equivalently: \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = 1\))

  4. Projection: Eigenvalues are 0 and 1; eigenvector for eigenvalue 1 is \(|\psi\rangle\)

These properties are characteristic of rank-1 projectors and distinguish pure states from mixed states.

Proof: Properties of Pure-State Density Matrices

Detailed derivations:

  1. Hermiticity: \(\rho^\dagger = (|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)^\dagger = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi| = \rho\). ✓

  2. Trace normalization: \(\text{Tr}(\rho) = \text{Tr}(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|) = \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1\). ✓

  3. Idempotence: \(\rho^2 = (|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|) = |\psi\rangle(\langle\psi|\psi\rangle)\langle\psi| = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi| = \rho\). Thus \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = \text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\). ✓

  4. Projection: Eigenvalues of \(\rho\) are 0 and 1. The nonzero eigenvalue is 1 with eigenvector \(|\psi\rangle\).

Mixed States and Ensembles#

Suppose we don’t know which pure state the system is in. Instead:

  • With probability \(p_1\), system is in state \(|\psi_1\rangle\).

  • With probability \(p_2\), system is in state \(|\psi_2\rangle\).

  • …

  • With probability \(p_n\), system is in state \(|\psi_n\rangle\).

where \(\sum_i p_i = 1\) and \(p_i \geq 0\). This is a statistical ensemble.

Definition: Density Matrix for Ensembles

The density matrix of the ensemble is:

(185)#\[ \rho = \sum_i p_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\]

If \(|\psi_i\rangle\) are orthonormal, this is the spectral decomposition of \(\rho\). More generally, the \(|\psi_i\rangle\) need not be orthogonal (ensemble ambiguity, discussed below).

Example: Maximally Mixed Qubit

Ensemble: 50% chance of \(|{0}\rangle\), 50% chance of \(|{1}\rangle\).

(186)#\[\begin{split} \rho = \frac{1}{2}|{0}\rangle\langle{0}| + \frac{1}{2}|{1}\rangle\langle{1}| = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{I}{2}\end{split}\]

No coherences: Off-diagonal elements are zero. The reduced density matrix is diagonal (classical-like).

Example: Thermal State

System at temperature \(T\) in equilibrium with a heat bath. Probability of energy eigenstate \(|n\rangle\) is:

(187)#\[ p_n = \frac{\mathrm{e}^{-E_n / k_B T}}{Z}, \quad Z = \sum_m \mathrm{e}^{-E_m / k_B T} \quad \text{(partition function)}\]

Density matrix (in energy eigenbasis):

(188)#\[ \rho_\text{thermal} = \sum_n \frac{\mathrm{e}^{-E_n / k_B T}}{Z} |n\rangle\langle n| = \frac{\mathrm{e}^{-H/k_B T}}{Z}\]

This is diagonal in the energy basis (no coherence at thermal equilibrium). Diagonal elements decrease with energy.

Definition: Valid Density Matrix Conditions

Any valid quantum state (pure or mixed) is represented by a density matrix \(\rho\) satisfying:

1. Hermiticity:

(189)#\[ \rho^\dagger = \rho \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{eigenvalues} \in \mathbb{R}\]

2. Trace normalization:

(190)#\[ \text{Tr}(\rho) = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sum_i \lambda_i = 1\]

where \(\lambda_i\) are eigenvalues. This ensures probability conservation.

3. Positive semidefiniteness:

(191)#\[ \rho \geq 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_i \geq 0 \text{ for all } i\]

Equivalently: \(\langle\phi|\rho|\phi\rangle \geq 0\) for all \(|\phi\rangle\). This ensures probabilities are non-negative.

Definition: Purity

The purity of a density matrix is defined as:

(192)#\[ \mathcal{P} = \text{Tr}(\rho^2)\]

Physical Interpretation

In the eigenbasis of \(\rho\) (with eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\)):

(193)#\[ \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = \sum_i \lambda_i^2\]

Pure vs. Mixed:

  • Pure state (\(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\)): One eigenvalue = 1, rest = 0. Thus \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = 1\).

  • Maximally mixed (dimension \(d\)): All eigenvalues = \(1/d\). Thus \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = d \cdot (1/d)^2 = 1/d\).

  • General: \(1/d \leq \text{Tr}(\rho^2) \leq 1\).

Interpretation:

  • \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2)\) close to 1: State is nearly pure; little uncertainty.

  • \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2)\) close to \(1/d\): State is nearly maximally mixed; maximum uncertainty.

Alternative: Purity as Reciprocal#

Sometimes defined as:

\[ \gamma = \text{Tr}(\rho^2) \quad \text{or} \quad \text{Purity} = d \cdot \text{Tr}(\rho^2)\]

to normalize to \([0, 1]\) or \([1/d, 1]\) depending on convention.

Ensemble Ambiguity#

Surprising fact: Different ensembles can produce the same density matrix.

Example: #### Example

Ensemble 1:

  • 100% of state \(|{0}\rangle \langle{0}|\) (pure, definitely \(|0\rangle\)).

  • \(\rho_1 = |{0}\rangle\langle{0}| = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).

Ensemble 2:

  • 50% of state \(|\rightarrow\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{0}\rangle + |{1}\rangle)\).

  • 50% of state \(|\leftarrow\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{0}\rangle - |{1}\rangle)\).

Compute \(\rho_2\):

\[ \rho_2 = \frac{1}{2}|\rightarrow\rangle\langle\rightarrow| + \frac{1}{2}|\leftarrow\rangle\langle\leftarrow|\]
\[\begin{split}= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}= \frac{1}{4}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Wait, this gives \(\rho_2 = I/2\) (maximally mixed), not \(\rho_1\). Let me reconsider.

Better example: Ensemble 1 and Ensemble 2 give different physics, so they won’t both yield \(\rho_1\).

Instead, consider:

Ensemble A: 50% \(|0\rangle\) along \(z\); 50% \(|1\rangle\) along \(z\):

\[ \rho_A = \frac{1}{2}|{0}\rangle\langle{0}| + \frac{1}{2}|{1}\rangle\langle{1}| = \frac{I}{2}\]

Ensemble B: 50% spin-right along \(x\); 50% spin-left along \(x\):

\[ \rho_B = \frac{1}{2}|\rightarrow\rangle\langle\rightarrow| + \frac{1}{2}|\leftarrow\rangle\langle\leftarrow| = \frac{I}{2}\]

Both give \(\rho = I/2\) (maximally mixed). But physical ensembles differ: in A, outcomes along \(z\) are 50/50 deterministic (given ensemble); in B, outcomes along \(x\) are 50/50.

Key insight: The density matrix alone cannot distinguish between these ensembles. Physical predictions (for observables in any basis) are identical; only preparation history differs.

Spectral Decomposition#

If \(\rho = \sum_i \lambda_i |u_i\rangle\langle u_i|\) where \(\{|u_i\rangle\}\) are eigenvectors and \(\lambda_i \geq 0\) are eigenvalues, this is the unique spectral decomposition. All other decompositions \(\rho = \sum_j p_j |\psi_j\rangle\langle\psi_j|\) (with non-orthogonal \(|\psi_j\rangle\)) represent different ensembles yielding the same \(\rho\).

The Bloch Ball Representation

A qubit mixed state can be written:

(194)#\[ \rho = \frac{1}{2}(I + \boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})\]

where \(\boldsymbol{r} = (r_x, r_y, r_z)\) is the Bloch vector and \(\boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}} = (\hat{\sigma}^x, \hat{\sigma}^y, \hat{\sigma}^z)\) are Pauli matrices.

Bloch ball constraints:

(195)#\[ |\boldsymbol{r}| \leq 1\]

  • Pure states: \(|\boldsymbol{r}| = 1\) (surface of Bloch sphere).

  • Mixed states: \(|\boldsymbol{r}| < 1\) (interior of Bloch ball).

  • Maximally mixed: \(\boldsymbol{r} = \boldsymbol{0}\) (center of ball); \(\rho = I/2\).

Purity in Terms of Bloch Vector#

The purity of a density matrix in terms of the Bloch vector is:

(196)#\[ \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + |\boldsymbol{r}|^2)\]

This formula connects the purity to the magnitude of the Bloch vector:

  • Pure state: \(|\boldsymbol{r}| = 1 \Rightarrow \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = 1\)

  • Maximally mixed: \(|\boldsymbol{r}| = 0 \Rightarrow \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = 1/2\)

The Bloch vector norm directly measures how “pure” the quantum state is. A state at the surface of the Bloch sphere (\(|\boldsymbol{r}| = 1\)) is pure; deeper in the interior, it becomes increasingly mixed.

Derivation: Purity in Terms of Bloch Vector

Detailed calculation:

Starting from \(\rho = \frac{1}{2}(I + \boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})\):

\[ \rho^2 = \frac{1}{4}(I + \boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})^2 = \frac{1}{4}(I + 2\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}} + (\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})^2)\]

Using \((\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})^2 = |\boldsymbol{r}|^2 I\) (property of Pauli matrices):

\[ \rho^2 = \frac{1}{4}(I + 2\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}} + |\boldsymbol{r}|^2 I) = \frac{1}{4}((1 + |\boldsymbol{r}|^2) I + 2\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\hat{\sigma}})\]

Taking the trace:

\[ \text{Tr}(\rho^2) = \frac{1}{4}(2(1 + |\boldsymbol{r}|^2)) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + |\boldsymbol{r}|^2)\]

Example: Mixed Qubit State

Consider \(\rho = 0.8|{0}\rangle\langle{0}| + 0.2|{1}\rangle\langle{1}|\) (mixture favoring \(|0\rangle\)).

Eigenvalues: \(\lambda_1 = 0.8, \lambda_2 = 0.2\).

\[ \boldsymbol{r} = (0, 0, \lambda_1 - \lambda_2) = (0, 0, 0.6)\]

Thus \(\rho = \frac{1}{2}(I + 0.6 \hat{\sigma}^z) = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.2 \end{pmatrix}\). ✓

Bloch vector magnitude: \(|\boldsymbol{r}| = 0.6 < 1\) (inside Bloch ball; mixed state). ✓

Ensemble ambiguity

Different statistical ensembles can produce the same density matrix. Physical predictions depend only on \(\rho\), not on which ensemble generated it. This means the density matrix is the fundamental object—not the ensemble decomposition.

Ensemble ambiguity

Different statistical ensembles can produce the same density matrix. Physical predictions depend only on \(\rho\), not on which ensemble generated it. This means the density matrix is the fundamental object—not the ensemble decomposition.

Summary

  • Pure state: \(\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\); \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) = 1\); single rank-1 projection.

  • Mixed state: \(\rho = \sum_i p_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\) (ensemble); \(\text{Tr}(\rho^2) < 1\); rank \(> 1\) generally.

  • Density operator properties: Hermitian, \(\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1\), positive semidefinite.

  • Purity \(\mathcal{P} = \text{Tr}(\rho^2)\): Measures mixedness; \(\in [1/d, 1]\) (1 = pure, \(1/d\) = maximally mixed).

  • Ensemble ambiguity: Multiple ensembles yield same \(\rho\); density matrix is all that matters for predictions.

Homework#

1. Density Matrix Properties. Show that if \(\hat{\rho} = \sum_i p_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\) with \(p_i \geq 0\) and \(\sum_i p_i = 1\), then:

(a) \(\hat{\rho}\) is Hermitian: \(\hat{\rho}^\dagger = \hat{\rho}\).

(b) \(\hat{\rho}\) has non-negative eigenvalues.

(c) \(\mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}) = 1\).

2. Pure vs. Mixed States. (a) Show that a pure state \(\hat{\rho} = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\) satisfies \(\hat{\rho}^2 = \hat{\rho}\) and \(\mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}^2) = 1\).

(b) Show that for a mixed state, \(\mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}^2) < 1\). (Hint: write \(\hat{\rho}\) in its eigenbasis and use the constraint \(\sum p_i = 1\) with at least two \(p_i > 0\).)

(c) What is the minimum value of \(\mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}^2)\) for a \(d\)-dimensional system?

3. Expectation Values from Density Matrix. For an observable \(\hat{A}\), show that \(\langle\hat{A}\rangle = \mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}\hat{A})\) reproduces the quantum mechanical expectation value for a pure state \(\hat{\rho} = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\).

4. Bloch Vector. A qubit density matrix can be written as

\[ \hat{\rho} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{I} + \boldsymbol{r}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}})\]

where \(\boldsymbol{r} = (r_x, r_y, r_z)\) is the Bloch vector.

(a) Show that \(r^i = \langle\hat{\sigma}^i\rangle = \mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}\hat{\sigma}^i)\).

(b) Show that \(|\boldsymbol{r}| \leq 1\) with equality iff the state is pure.

(c) What Bloch vector corresponds to the maximally mixed state \(\hat{\rho} = \hat{I}/2\)?

5. Von Neumann Equation. The time evolution of the density matrix is governed by

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat{\rho}}{\mathrm{d}t} = [\hat{H}, \hat{\rho}]\]

(a) Derive this equation from \(\hat{\rho}(t) = \hat{U}(t)\hat{\rho}(0)\hat{U}^\dagger(t)\) by differentiating.

(b) Show that \(\mathrm{Tr}(\hat{\rho}^2)\) is conserved under unitary evolution. What does this imply — can unitary evolution turn a pure state into a mixed state?

6. Coherences and Classical Mixing. Consider two states: (i) the superposition \(|+\rangle = (|0\rangle + |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\), and (ii) the 50-50 mixture \(\hat{\rho}_{\rm mix} = (|0\rangle\langle 0| + |1\rangle\langle 1|)/2\).

(a) Write the density matrices for both.

(b) Compute \(\langle\hat{\sigma}^x\rangle\) for both. Explain the physical difference.

(c) Why can’t any sequence of local measurements on state (ii) distinguish it from “I prepared either \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\) with equal probability but forgot which”?

7. Energy Basis Representation. In the energy eigenbasis \(\{|n\rangle\}\), write out the matrix elements of \(\hat{\rho}(t)\) under evolution with time-independent \(\hat{H}\). Show that the diagonal elements (populations) are time-independent, while the off-diagonal elements (coherences) oscillate at frequency \((E_n - E_m)/\hbar\). What happens to coherences when averaged over a long time?

8. Preparation and Equivalence. Two experimenters prepare qubits differently: Alice prepares \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\) with probability 1/2 each; Bob prepares \(|+\rangle = (|0\rangle+|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) or \(|-\rangle = (|0\rangle-|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) with probability 1/2 each. Show that both ensembles give the same density matrix \(\hat{\rho} = \hat{I}/2\), and therefore no experiment can distinguish the two preparation procedures.

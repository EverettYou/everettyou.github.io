6.4.1 Decoherence#

Prompts

  • What is decoherence, and how does interaction with the environment destroy quantum superpositions?

  • What is the system-environment interaction, and how does tracing out the environment lead to decoherence?

  • What are pointer states, and why are they special under decoherence?

  • What is einselection (environment-induced decoherence selection)? How does it select preferred basis?

  • How fast does decoherence occur, and what is the decoherence timescale?

  • What is the quantum-to-classical transition, and how does decoherence enable it?

  • Can you recover lost coherence by controlling the environment? How (Dynamical Decoupling)?

Lecture Notes#

Why do we never see macroscopic superpositions—a cat that is both alive and dead? When a quantum system couples to its environment, entanglement with countless environmental degrees of freedom causes the delicate quantum coherences to decay away, leaving only a classical mixture of states. Decoherence is the bridge between quantum and classical: the process by which the quantum world becomes invisible to us.

Decoherence Process#

Consider a system S (e.g., qubit) entangled with environment E (bath of many modes). The total state is initially:

(452)#\[|\Psi\rangle_{SE} = (\alpha|{\uparrow}\rangle_S + \beta|{\downarrow}\rangle_S) \otimes |E_0\rangle_E\]

After interaction for time \(t\), the system entangles with different environmental states:

(453)#\[|\Psi(t)\rangle_{SE} = \alpha|{\uparrow}\rangle_S |E_\uparrow(t)\rangle_E + \beta|{\downarrow}\rangle_S |E_\downarrow(t)\rangle_E\]

where \(|E_\uparrow(t)\rangle\) and \(|E_\downarrow(t)\rangle\) are orthogonal (or nearly orthogonal) environmental states.

Key: The environment has become entangled with the system, encoding which state the system is in (“which-path” information).

Reduced Density Matrix and Decoherence#

The reduced density matrix of the system (after tracing out environment) is:

(454)#\[\rho_S(t) = \text{Tr}_E(|\Psi(t)\rangle\langle\Psi(t)|) = \rho_\text{diag} + \rho_\text{coherence}\]

Computing the partial trace:

(455)#\[\rho_S(t) = |\alpha|^2 |{\uparrow}\rangle\langle{\uparrow}| + |\beta|^2 |{\downarrow}\rangle\langle{\downarrow}| + \alpha^*\beta e^{-t/T_2} |{\uparrow}\rangle\langle{\downarrow}| + \beta^*\alpha e^{-t/T_2} |{\downarrow}\rangle\langle{\uparrow}|\]

where the coherence factor is:

(456)#\[e^{-t/T_2} = \langle E_\uparrow(t) | E_\downarrow(t) \rangle\]

Interpretation: The overlap \(\langle E_\uparrow(t) | E_\downarrow(t) \rangle\) measures how distinguishable the environmental states are. As they diverge (become orthogonal), the coherence decays exponentially.

Exponential Decay: Timescale#

In the weak coupling limit (system weakly interacts with large environment):

(457)#\[\langle E_\uparrow(t) | E_\downarrow(t) \rangle \approx e^{-\gamma t}\]

where \(\gamma\) is the decoherence rate. The decoherence timescale (dephasing time) is:

(458)#\[T_2 = 1/\gamma\]

Off-diagonal elements decay as:

(459)#\[\rho_{\uparrow\downarrow}(t) = \alpha^*\beta e^{-t/T_2}\]

A microscopic model shows how decoherence emerges:

Derivation: Decoherence via System-Environment Coupling

Typically, the interaction Hamiltonian has the form:

(460)#\[H_\text{int} = \sum_k A_S \otimes B_{E,k}\]

where:

  • \(A_S\) = system operator (e.g., \(\hat{\sigma}_z\)).

  • \(B_{E,k}\) = environmental operators (e.g., bath modes).

Example: Magnetic noise:

\[H_\text{int} = \hat{\sigma}_z \otimes \sum_k g_k (a_k e^{-i\omega_k t} + a_k^\dagger e^{i\omega_k t})\]

coupling spin to radiation field fluctuations.

Pointer States and Einselection#

Pointer states: Special quantum states that are robust against decoherence; they remain pure (or nearly pure) even when entangled with the environment.

Definition: States that diagonalize the system-environment coupling:

\[A_S = \sum_i a_i |i\rangle\langle i| \quad \text{(diagonal)}\]

The pointer basis is the eigenbasis of \(A_S\).

Why Pointer States Are Robust#

If the system is in pointer state \(|i\rangle\), the system-environment interaction gives:

\[H_\text{int} |i\rangle \otimes |E_k\rangle = a_i |i\rangle \otimes B_{E,k} |E_k\rangle\]

The environmental state shifts but doesn’t entangle with different system states (system state remains definite). Only superposition of pointer states decoheres.

Einselection (Environment-Induced Selection)#

Zurek’s concept: The environment selects a preferred basis (pointer basis) by rapidly decohering superpositions in that basis.

Mechanism:

  1. System in arbitrary superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |i\rangle\) (not necessarily pointer basis).

  2. Interaction with environment entangles system with different environmental states for each component.

  3. Environment becomes a which-path detector: stores information about which component the system is in.

  4. Superposition of pointer states collapses effectively to a mixture.

  5. Result: Classical-looking mixture in pointer basis.

Quantum-to-Classical Transition#

Question: How does a quantum superposition become a classical mixture?

Answer: Via decoherence and einselection.

Single Pointer State#

If system is in pointer state (e.g., \(|{\uparrow}\rangle\) or \(|{\downarrow}\rangle\)), it evolves deterministically under environment interaction (no randomness):

\[\rho_S(t) = |{\uparrow}\rangle\langle{\uparrow}| \quad \text{(pure state, no decoherence)}\]

Superposition of Pointer States#

If system is in superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{\uparrow}\rangle + |{\downarrow}\rangle)\):

\[\begin{split}\rho_S(t) \approx \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & e^{-t/T_2}/2 \\ e^{-t/T_2}/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} \to \frac{I}{2}\end{split}\]

As \(t \gg T_2\), the coherence vanishes; the state looks like a classical 50/50 mixture:

\[\rho_S(\infty) = \frac{1}{2}|{\uparrow}\rangle\langle{\uparrow}| + \frac{1}{2}|{\downarrow}\rangle\langle{\downarrow}|\]

But: The full system+environment state is still pure (reversible). The apparent classicality is only from the system’s perspective; the environment has the information.

Macroscopic Objects#

For macroscopic superpositions (Schrödinger cat): \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|\text{alive}\rangle + |\text{dead}\rangle)\), the decoherence rate is enormous:

\[\gamma \sim 10^{23} \text{ s}^{-1} \Rightarrow T_2 \sim 10^{-23}\text{ s}\]

Macroscopic superpositions decohere almost instantaneously; this is why we never observe them.

Decoherence-Free Subspaces (DFS)#

Exception: Some quantum states can be protected from certain decoherence channels via symmetry.

Example: Singlet state \(|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{\uparrow\downarrow}\rangle - |{\downarrow\uparrow}\rangle)\).

If both qubits undergo identical amplitude damping (same coupling \(g_k\)), the singlet state is immune (decoherence-free):

\[\hat{\sigma}_-^{(1)} + \hat{\sigma}_-^{(2)} \text{ acting on } |\Psi^-\rangle = 0\]

Application: Quantum error correction; storing information in entangled subspaces.

Dynamical Decoupling#

Idea: Apply rapidly oscillating pulses to the system to average out environmental coupling and suppress decoherence.

Example (CPMG sequence): Apply \(\pi\)-pulses at regular intervals. If the control field is much faster than environment: the effective interaction averages to zero.

Result: Coherence time extended: \(T_2^\text{eff} >> T_2^\text{bare}\).

Trade-off: Requires rapid, accurate pulses (control overhead).

Timescale Hierarchy#

In typical systems:

\[T_\text{system} >> T_2 >> T_1\]

where:

  • \(T_\text{system}\) ~ inverse Rabi frequency (system evolution timescale).

  • \(T_2\) ~ dephasing time (coherence decay); environment-induced randomness.

  • \(T_1\) ~ relaxation time (energy dissipation); thermal equilibration.

Practical consequence: Decoherence (via dephasing) is often the dominant limitation before energy relaxation becomes significant.

Decoherence is not collapse

Decoherence explains why macroscopic superpositions are never observed—off-diagonal elements of \(\rho\) decay on timescale \(T_2\). But decoherence alone does not select a single outcome; it only suppresses interference between branches. The measurement problem remains.

Quantum Darwinism: Emergence of Classicality via Environmental Redundancy#

Concept (Zurek): Beyond decoherence lies Quantum Darwinism—the proliferation of information about a quantum system throughout the environment. Multiple environmental degrees of freedom independently acquire information about the system’s state, enabling multiple observers to determine which state the system is in without directly accessing it. This redundant encoding explains why we see a classical world emerging from quantum mechanics.

Einselection Revisited: Environment-Induced Superselection#

Recall that einselection picks out the pointer states as special: the environment selects a preferred basis because superpositions in that basis rapidly decohere. More fundamentally, einselection is a superselection rule imposed by the environment.

Definition: A superselection rule forbids certain coherences (off-diagonal elements of the density matrix) from being created or detected by the environment.

Mechanism:

  1. System initially in arbitrary superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |i\rangle\) (arbitrary basis).

  2. System couples to environment; each component \(|i\rangle\) entangles with distinct environmental state \(|E_i\rangle\).

  3. The system-environment state becomes:

\[| \Psi \rangle_{SE} = \sum_i c_i |i\rangle \otimes |E_i\rangle\]

  1. Environmental states are orthogonal (or nearly so): \(\langle E_i | E_j \rangle \approx \delta_{ij}\).

  2. The environment has become a which-path detector: by measuring the environment, any external agent can determine which component the system is in.

  3. Coherences between different pointer states are unobservable from the system’s perspective (traced-out environment contains the “which-path” information).

  4. Result: The pointer basis is the only basis in which the system appears as a classical mixture; superposition in that basis effectively disappears.

Redundant Information: Multiple Copies in the Environment#

Key insight: The environment is large—many degrees of freedom. Information about which pointer state the system is in doesn’t get stored in just one environmental mode; it spreads across countless modes.

Redundancy mechanism:

  1. System in pointer state \(|i\rangle\) interacts with environment.

  2. A small environmental subsystem \(E_1\) (single photon, atom, phonon mode) becomes entangled: can carry “fingerprint” of state \(|i\rangle\).

  3. Another subsystem \(E_2\) subsequently interacts; it also becomes entangled and acquires the same information.

  4. This repeats: \(E_3, E_4, \ldots\) all gradually learn which state the system is in.

  5. Result: \(N\) independent environmental fragments, each carrying (partial) information about the system state.

Classical signature: In classical physics, we don’t worry about copies. Many independent observers can measure the same property (e.g., the temperature of a room) simultaneously; the environment stores this information redundantly. Quantum Darwinism shows that quantum information also becomes redundant for pointer states, mimicking classicality.

Quantifying Redundancy: Quantum Mutual Information#

Mutual information \(I(S : E_k)\) between system \(S\) and environmental fragment \(E_k\) quantifies how much information the fragment carries about the system:

\[I(S : E_k) = S(\rho_S) + S(\rho_{E_k}) - S(\rho_{S E_k})\]

where \(S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \log_2 \rho)\) is the von Neumann entropy.

Interpretation:

  • \(I(S : E_k) = 0\): Fragment \(E_k\) carries no information about \(S\).

  • \(I(S : E_k) > 0\): Fragment \(E_k\) carries some information about \(S\).

  • \(I(S : E_k) \approx S(\rho_S)\): Fragment \(E_k\) nearly determines the system state (maximum redundancy).

Darwinian redundancy: Total information in \(N\) fragments:

\[\mathcal{R} = \sum_{k=1}^N I(S : E_k)\]

Classical case (e.g., room temperature): If \(N\) observers independently measure the temperature, each acquires the full information (entropy \(\sim \log 2\) states). Total \(\mathcal{R} \sim N\)—massive redundancy.

Quantum pointer states: As the system and environment interact, \(\mathcal{R}\) grows. Multiple environmental fragments each learn the pointer state; redundancy increases exponentially with environment size. This is the hallmark of Darwinism.

Objectivity: Why the Classical World Emerges#

Definition: A property is objective if multiple independent observers measure it and obtain the same result.

Classical objectivity: A thermometer reads 20 °C. Another observer, measuring at the same location, also reads 20 °C. Property is objective because it’s independent of who measures.

Quantum Darwinism explanation:

  1. System in pointer state \(|i\rangle\) (e.g., spin up).

  2. Observer Alice measures a small environmental fragment; she learns the system is in state \(|i\rangle\).

  3. Observer Bob, independently measuring a different environmental fragment, also learns the system is in state \(|i\rangle\).

  4. They agree on the result; the property (system state) is objective.

  5. This works because both fragments carry the same information (redundancy).

Non-objectivity in superposition: If the system were in superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\):

  • Alice measures fragment \(E_1\), learns state 0.

  • Bob measures fragment \(E_2\), learns state 1.

  • They disagree; there is no objective state.

  • However, with decoherence and Darwinism, the superposition rapidly evolves into a mixture where state \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\) is definite (though we don’t know which without measuring). Redundancy ensures any observer learns the same state.

Classical world emerges: The pointer states become objective observables. Superpositions are not objective (not accessible to multiple independent observers), so they effectively don’t exist in the realm we observe.

Connection to Decoherence: Darwinism as the Next Step#

Decoherence (local view):

  • System’s density matrix: coherences decay away (\(\rho_{ij} \to 0\) for \(i \ne j\)).

  • Time scale: \(T_2 \sim 1/\gamma\) (depends on coupling strength and environment size).

  • Question unanswered: Why do we observe definite states, not a statistical ensemble of unknown states?

Quantum Darwinism (global view):

  • Explains WHY pointer states are special: information about them spreads redundantly.

  • Explains OBJECTIVITY: multiple observers independently learn the same pointer state from different environmental fragments.

  • Explains CLASSICALITY: redundancy prevents quantum coherences from being recovered; pointer states appear classical.

  • Connects decoherence to observation: decoherence of superpositions is equivalent to proliferation of information about pointer states throughout the environment.

Quantitative relation:

As decoherence progresses (off-diagonal \(\rho_{ij}\) decay), mutual information \(I(S : E_k)\) grows:

\[\frac{dI(S : E_k)}{dt} \propto \gamma \quad \text{(Darwinian growth rate)}\]

The faster decoherence (larger \(\gamma\)), the faster Darwinian redundancy builds up. Decoherence erases quantum coherence while simultaneously encoding information redundantly throughout the environment.

Example: Quantum Darwinism in a Simple Model#

Example: Two-Level System Interacting with Photon Environment

A two-level system (qubit, spin) is in superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\).

Pointer states: \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) (energy eigenstates).

Environment: Blackbody radiation at temperature \(T\); infinite modes.

Interaction:

  • Qubit in state \(|1\rangle\) emits a photon (decay); transitions to \(|0\rangle\).

  • Each photon carries information: “qubit was in \(|1\rangle\)”.

  • Different photons encode the same information (redundancy).

Darwinism:

  1. First photon emitted; fragment \(E_1\) learns state is \(|1\rangle\).

  2. Second photon emitted; fragment \(E_2\) learns state is \(|1\rangle\).

  3. \(N\)-th photon: fragment \(E_N\) also learns state is \(|1\rangle\).

  4. Any observer measuring a photon learns “qubit is in \(|1\rangle\)”.

  5. Objectivity: all observers agree.

Decoherence side: As more photons are emitted, the qubit’s state becomes increasingly mixed (decay). Superposition decoheres. This is the local manifestation.

Darwinism side: Simultaneously, each emitted photon carries the same information; massive redundancy. The environment “has learned” which state the qubit is in; classicality emerges.

Limits of Darwinism: Pure Einselection#

Not all states are pointer states: States that don’t decohere (decoherence-free subspaces, quantum error correcting codes) don’t undergo Darwinism.

Information in non-pointer basis: If the system is in a superposition of non-pointer states (e.g., entangled state \(|\Psi^-\rangle\)), the environment does not redundantly encode information in the usual sense. To learn the state requires global measurements (on full environment); single environmental fragments carry minimal information.

Quantum advantage: This is the basis of quantum error correction and quantum information processing—information can be hidden from local environmental access via entanglement.

Summary#

  • Decoherence: Interaction with environment entangles system with environmental degrees of freedom; reduced density matrix becomes mixed; coherences decay exponentially.

  • Mechanism: System-environment entanglement encodes which-path information; environment becomes a detector; quantum interference is erased.

  • Timescale: Decoherence time \(T_2 = 1/\gamma\); depends on system size, environment temperature, coupling strength.

  • Pointer states: Eigenstates of system operator in coupling Hamiltonian; robust against decoherence.

  • Einselection: Environment selects preferred basis (pointer basis); superposition in that basis rapidly decoheres to mixture.

  • Quantum-to-classical: Coherent superposition → classical mixture; pointer states classically distinguishable; non-pointer basis requires quantum control to maintain.

  • Macroscopic superpositions: Decohere almost instantaneously due to many environmental couplings.

  • Decoherence-free subspaces: Symmetry-protected states immune to certain channels; used in quantum error correction.

  • Dynamical decoupling: Rapid control pulses suppress decoherence via refocusing; extends coherence time.

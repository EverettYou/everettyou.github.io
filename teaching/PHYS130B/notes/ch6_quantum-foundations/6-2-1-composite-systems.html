6.2.1 Composite Systems#

Prompts

  • What is the tensor product? How does it formalize the concept of combining two quantum systems into a larger Hilbert space?

  • What is a product state? How can you identify one just by looking at the mathematical form?

  • What is the difference between a separable state and an entangled state? Can you determine entanglement by visual inspection?

  • What is the Schmidt decomposition? Why is the Schmidt rank an important entanglement measure?

  • For a bipartite pure state, how does Schmidt decomposition reveal the minimal structure needed to represent entanglement?

Lecture Notes#

When two or more quantum systems are brought together, their combined state lives in a vastly larger space—a tensor product Hilbert space. Yet something remarkable can happen: the total system cannot be decomposed into independent pieces, even if we try. This phenomenon of entanglement reveals that quantum correlations are fundamentally different from classical ones: measuring one part instantly affects the statistics for the other, no matter how far apart they are.

Tensor Product Hilbert Space#

Discussion

The Schmidt decomposition is unique when Schmidt coefficients are non-degenerate. What happens when two Schmidt coefficients are equal? Does this affect entanglement measures?

Definition: Tensor Product Hilbert Space

For two quantum systems \(A\) and \(B\) with Hilbert spaces \(\mathcal{H}_A\) (dimension \(d_A\)) and \(\mathcal{H}_B\) (dimension \(d_B\)), the composite system \(AB\) lives in the tensor product space:

(276)#\[\mathcal{H}_{AB} = \mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\]

with dimension \(\dim(\mathcal{H}_{AB}) = d_A \cdot d_B\).

Basis for Composite Space#

If \(\{|i\rangle_A : i=1,\ldots,d_A\}\) is a basis for \(\mathcal{H}_A\) and \(\{|j\rangle_B : j=1,\ldots,d_B\}\) is a basis for \(\mathcal{H}_B\), then a basis for \(\mathcal{H}_{AB}\) is:

(277)#\[\{|i\rangle_A \otimes |j\rangle_B\}_{i,j}\]

We often abbreviate \(|i\rangle_A \otimes |j\rangle_B\) as \(|ij\rangle\) or \(|i\rangle|j\rangle\).

Tensor Product of Vectors#

For state vectors \(|\psi\rangle_A \in \mathcal{H}_A\) and \(|\phi\rangle_B \in \mathcal{H}_B\):

(278)#\[\begin{split}|\psi\rangle_A \otimes |\phi\rangle_B = \begin{pmatrix} \psi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \psi_{d_A} \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} \phi_1 \\ \vdots \\ \phi_{d_B} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \psi_1 \phi_1 \\ \psi_1 \phi_2 \\ \vdots \\ \psi_{d_A} \phi_{d_B} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Dimension: \(d_A \cdot d_B\) components.

Tensor Product of Operators#

For operators \(A \in \text{End}(\mathcal{H}_A)\) and \(B \in \text{End}(\mathcal{H}_B)\), the tensor product is:

(279)#\[\begin{split}A \otimes B = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} B & a_{12} B & \cdots \\ a_{21} B & a_{22} B & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(a_{ij}\) are matrix elements of \(A\). Results:- \((A \otimes B)(A' \otimes B') = (AA') \otimes (BB')\) (composition)- \((A \otimes B)^\dagger = A^\dagger \otimes B^\dagger\) (adjoint)- \(\text{Tr}(A \otimes B) = \text{Tr}(A) \cdot \text{Tr}(B)\) (trace factorizes)

Bipartite Systems and Product States#

For two qubits (A and B), a general state is:

(280)#\[|\Psi\rangle_{AB} = \sum_{i,j=0}^{1} c_{ij} |i\rangle_A |j\rangle_B = c_{00}|{00}\rangle + c_{01}|{01}\rangle + c_{10}|{10}\rangle + c_{11}|{11}\rangle\]

with normalization \(\sum_{i,j}|c_{ij}|^2 = 1\) (4 parameters, minus 1 phase = 7 real degrees of freedom).

#:::{admonition} Definition: Separable (Product) States :class: important

A state is separable (or factorizable) if it can be written as:

(281)#\[|\Psi\rangle_{AB} = |\psi\rangle_A \otimes |\phi\rangle_B\]

Expanding with \(|\psi\rangle_A = \alpha |{0}\rangle + \beta |{1}\rangle\) and \(|\phi\rangle_B = \gamma |{0}\rangle + \delta |{1}\rangle\):

(282)#\[c_{00} = \alpha\gamma, \quad c_{01} = \alpha\delta, \quad c_{10} = \beta\gamma, \quad c_{11} = \beta\delta\]

Key constraint: \(c_{00} c_{11} = c_{01} c_{10}\) (the determinant of the \(2 \times 2\) matrix \([c_{ij}]\) vanishes).

Recognizing Entanglement by the Determinant Test

For a two-qubit state \(|\Psi\rangle = \sum_{i,j}c_{ij}|ij\rangle\), form the matrix:

(283)#\[\begin{split}C = \begin{pmatrix} c_{00} & c_{01} \\ c_{10} & c_{11} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

  • \(\det(C) = 0\): State is separable (product state).

  • \(\det(C) \neq 0\): State is entangled (cannot factor).

    Proof: For \(|\Psi\rangle = (\alpha_0|0\rangle + \alpha_1|1\rangle)_A \otimes (\beta_0|0\rangle + \beta_1|1\rangle)_B\), the coefficient matrix is \(C = \boldsymbol{\alpha} \boldsymbol{\beta}^T\), which has rank 1, hence \(\det(C) = 0\). Conversely, a rank-1 matrix factors as an outer product.

Partial Trace and Reduced Density Matrix

Given a bipartite state \(\rho_{AB}\), the reduced density matrix for subsystem A is \(\rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB})\), obtained by the partial trace operation. This extracts all information about subsystem A alone and is essential for understanding entanglement. See §6.1.2 for the full mathematical treatment.

Theorem: Schmidt Decomposition

Any bipartite pure state \(|\Psi\rangle_{AB}\) can be written in the form:

(284)#\[|\Psi\rangle_{AB} = \sum_{k=1}^r \lambda_k |u_k\rangle_A \otimes |v_k\rangle_B\]

where:

  • \(r = \text{rank}(C)\) is the Schmidt rank (number of nonzero terms).

  • \(\lambda_k > 0\) are the Schmidt coefficients (normalized: \(\sum_k \lambda_k^2 = 1\)).

  • \(\{|u_k\rangle_A\}\) and \(\{|v_k\rangle_B\}\) are orthonormal sets in their respective spaces.

To decompose a bipartite state, we use the singular value decomposition (SVD):

Proof: Schmidt Decomposition

Expand \(|\Psi\rangle = \sum_{i,j} c_{ij} |i\rangle_A |j\rangle_B\). Form the matrix \(C\) with elements \(c_{ij}\). By singular value decomposition:

(285)#\[C = U \Lambda V^\dagger\]

where \(U\) (\(d_A \times d_A\)) and \(V\) (\(d_B \times d_B\)) are unitary, and \(\Lambda\) is diagonal with non-negative entries \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq 0\). Then:

(286)#\[|\Psi\rangle = \sum_{k} \lambda_k |u_k\rangle_A |v_k\rangle_B\]

where \(|u_k\rangle_A\) and \(|v_k\rangle_B\) are columns of \(U\) and \(V\).

Example: Bell State

Consider \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{00}\rangle + |{11}\rangle)\). Coefficient matrix: \(C = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\). SVD: \(\Lambda = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}I\), so \(\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\). Thus \(|\Phi^+\rangle\) has Schmidt rank 2 (maximally entangled for two qubits). Schmidt decomposition: \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|{0}\rangle_A|{0}\rangle_B + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|{1}\rangle_A|{1}\rangle_B\).

Reduced Density Matrix and Schmidt Coefficients#

From the Schmidt decomposition, the reduced density matrix of subsystem A is:

(287)#\[\rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(|\Psi\rangle\langle\Psi|) = \sum_k \lambda_k^2 |u_k\rangle_A\langle u_k|\]

Key insight: The eigenvalues of \(\rho_A\) are \(\lambda_k^2\) (squares of Schmidt coefficients), and the eigenstates are the Schmidt basis states \(|u_k\rangle_A\).

Purity and Schmidt Rank#

(288)#\[\text{Tr}(\rho_A^2) = \sum_k \lambda_k^4\]

  • Pure state (\(|\Psi\rangle_{AB}\)): \(\text{Tr}(\rho_A^2) = 1\) iff \(\rho_A\) is pure, which occurs iff Schmidt rank = 1 (product state).

  • Maximally entangled (equal Schmidt coefficients): \(\text{Tr}(\rho_A^2) = 1/r\) (minimum purity).

Schmidt Rank and Entanglement

The Schmidt rank (number of nonzero Schmidt coefficients) quantifies entanglement:

(289)#\[r = \text{rank}(\rho_A) = \dim(\text{supp}(\rho_A))\]

  • \(r = 1\): Separable (product state); \(\lambda_1 = 1\), all others zero.

  • \(r = \min(d_A, d_B)\): Maximally entangled; all nonzero Schmidt coefficients equal: \(\lambda_k = 1/\sqrt{r}\) for \(k=1,\ldots,r\).

  • \(1 < r < \min(d_A, d_B)\): Partially entangled.

Schmidt Number as a Discrete Entanglement Witness#

If \(r \geq 2\), the state is entangled (cannot be written as a product).Computational check: Compute the rank of the coefficient matrix \(C = [c_{ij}]\). If \(\text{rank}(C) \geq 2\), state is entangled.

Multipartite Systems#

For \(N\) systems, the Hilbert space is:

(290)#\[\mathcal{H}_{1\cdots N} = \mathcal{H}_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathcal{H}_N\]

with dimension \(\prod_i d_i\).Entanglement types:

  • Fully separable: \(|\Psi\rangle = |\psi_1\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\psi_N\rangle\).

  • Bipartite entanglement: Entangled between groups of systems (e.g., \(|\psi_{1,2}\rangle \otimes |\psi_3\rangle\)).

  • Multipartite entanglement: All systems entangled together; cannot be split into separable blocks.

Example: GHZ state (3 qubits):

\[|\text{GHZ}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{000}\rangle + |{111}\rangle)\]

This is tripartitely entangled; removing any one qubit leaves the other two in a product state.

Entanglement is the rule, not the exception

Most states in a composite Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\) are entangled. Product (separable) states form a measure-zero subset. Any interaction between subsystems generically creates entanglement.

Summary#

  • Tensor product \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\): Hilbert space of composite system; dimension \(d_A \cdot d_B\).

  • Product state: Separable as \(|\psi\rangle_A \otimes |\phi\rangle_B\); characterized by \(\det(C) = 0\) (determinant of coefficient matrix).

  • Partial trace: \(\rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB})\) extracts subsystem A information; eigenvalues = \(\lambda_k^2\) (Schmidt coefficients squared); see §6.1.2.

  • Schmidt decomposition: \(|\Psi\rangle = \sum_k \lambda_k |u_k\rangle_A |v_k\rangle_B\); obtained via SVD of coefficient matrix.

  • Schmidt rank \(r = \text{rank}(C)\): - \(r=1\) ⟹ separable (product state). - \(r \geq 2\) ⟹ entangled. - \(r = \min(d_A, d_B)\) ⟹ maximally entangled.

  • Purity: \(\text{Tr}(\rho_A^2) = \sum_k \lambda_k^4 \leq 1\); equals 1 iff \(r=1\) (product state).

  • Multipartite entanglement: More complex; requires analysis of entanglement structure for each bipartition.

Homework#

1. Show that the state \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle_A + |1\rangle_A) \otimes |0\rangle_B\) is a product state. Write it in the standard two-qubit basis \(\{|00\rangle, |01\rangle, |10\rangle, |11\rangle\}\) and verify directly that it is separable.

2. The Schmidt decomposition of a bipartite pure state \(|\psi\rangle_{AB}\) is \(|\psi\rangle = \sum_i \sqrt{\lambda_i} |i\rangle_A |i'\rangle_B\) where \(\lambda_i \geq 0\) and \(\sum_i \lambda_i = 1\). Show that \(|\psi\rangle\) is a product state if and only if it has Schmidt rank 1 (only one nonzero \(\lambda_i\)).

3. Find the Schmidt decomposition of \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{2}(|00\rangle + |01\rangle + |10\rangle + |11\rangle)\). What is the Schmidt rank? Is this state entangled?

4. Find the Schmidt decomposition of the state \(|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(|00\rangle + |01\rangle + |11\rangle)\). (Hint: write the coefficient matrix and find its singular value decomposition.)

5. A density matrix \(\rho_{AB}\) is called separable if it can be written as \(\rho_{AB} = \sum_i p_i \rho_A^{(i)} \otimes \rho_B^{(i)}\) with \(p_i \geq 0\), \(\sum_i p_i = 1\). Show that the maximally mixed state \(\rho = \mathbf{1}/4\) on two qubits is separable by explicitly constructing such a decomposition.

6. A bipartite state \(\rho_{AB}\) satisfies the PPT (positive partial transpose) criterion if \((\mathrm{id}_A \otimes T_B)(\rho_{AB}) \geq 0\), where \(T_B\) is the transpose in the \(B\) subsystem. Show that all separable states satisfy PPT. (Hint: for a product state \(\rho_A \otimes \rho_B\), what is the partial transpose?)

7. Apply the PPT criterion to the Bell state \(\rho = |\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|\) where \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\). Show that the partial transpose has a negative eigenvalue, concluding that \(\rho\) is entangled.

8. For a pure state \(|\psi\rangle_{AB}\) with Schmidt decomposition \(|\psi\rangle = \sum_i \sqrt{\lambda_i}|i\rangle_A|i'\rangle_B\), show that the reduced density matrices are \(\rho_A = \sum_i \lambda_i |i\rangle\langle i|\) and \(\rho_B = \sum_i \lambda_i |i'\rangle\langle i'|\). What do the Schmidt coefficients tell us about the entanglement?

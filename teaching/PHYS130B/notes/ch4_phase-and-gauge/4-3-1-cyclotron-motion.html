4.3.1 Cyclotron Motion#

Prompts

  • Derive the cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c = qB/m\) from the Lorentz force. Why is it independent of particle energy?

  • Explain the classical Hall effect: how does the Hall voltage arise in a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field?

  • What is the Hall resistance \(R_H = B/(nq)\)? How would you measure it experimentally?

  • The integer quantum Hall effect shows that conductance becomes quantized: \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu e^2/h\). Why is this quantization so precise, and what quantum phenomenon must explain it?

  • What is the magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(qB)}\)? How does it compare to typical atomic sizes?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The classical motion of a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field leads to cyclotron orbits—circular trajectories with a characteristic frequency independent of energy. This classical picture provides crucial intuition but faces a profound puzzle: under strong magnetic fields at low temperatures, the Hall conductance “locks in” to discrete quantized values with parts-per-billion precision. This quantization cannot be explained classically and demands quantum mechanics. Understanding cyclotron motion and the magnetic length scale \(\ell_B\) provides the foundation for Landau quantization and the quantum Hall effects—among the most important phenomena in condensed-matter physics.

Classical Cyclotron Motion#

Equations of Motion.

A charged particle with charge \(q\) and mass \(m\) in a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) experiences the Lorentz force:

\[ m\boldsymbol{a} = q\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B} \]

In Cartesian components:

\[ m\dot{v}_x = qv_yB, \quad m\dot{v}_y = -qv_xB, \quad m\dot{v}_z = 0 \]

Motion parallel to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) is unaffected. For the perpendicular components:

\[ \ddot{v}_x + \omega_c^2 v_x = 0, \quad \ddot{v}_y + \omega_c^2 v_y = 0 \]

where:

Cyclotron Frequency

\[ \omega_c = \frac{qB}{m} \]

Key properties:

  • Independent of particle velocity or kinetic energy

  • Depends only on charge-to-mass ratio and field strength

  • Orbital period: \(T_c = 2\pi/\omega_c = 2\pi m/(qB)\) is also energy-independent

  • For electrons: \(\omega_c = eB/m_e\) (note: \(e < 0\) in SI; the sign is built into \(q\))

The general solution describes circular motion in the \(xy\)-plane:

\[ v_x(t) = v_\perp \cos(\omega_c t + \phi_0), \quad v_y(t) = v_\perp \sin(\omega_c t + \phi_0) \]

where \(v_\perp = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}\) is the speed perpendicular to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) and \(\phi_0\) is an arbitrary phase. Integrating to find position:

\[ x(t) = x_0 + \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c}\sin(\omega_c t + \phi_0), \quad y(t) = y_0 - \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c}\cos(\omega_c t + \phi_0) \]

This is a circular orbit centered at the guiding center \((x_0, y_0)\).

Cyclotron Radius and Orbital Period.

Cyclotron Radius (Larmor Radius)

The radius of the circular orbit is:

\[ r_c = \frac{v_\perp}{\omega_c} = \frac{mv_\perp}{qB} \]

Stronger fields confine orbits more tightly; radius scales with perpendicular momentum.

Orbital Period: Energy-Independent Timescale

The time for one complete orbit is:

\[ T_c = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_c} = \frac{2\pi m}{qB} \]

Remarkably, \(T_c\) is completely independent of the particle’s kinetic energy. A slow particle and a fast particle orbit in circles of different radii, but both take the same time to complete one loop. This is why cyclotron accelerators work: by applying a radiofrequency voltage synchronized to \(f_c = \omega_c/(2\pi)\), particles are accelerated in phase regardless of their instantaneous energy.

Classical Hall Effect#

When charge carriers flow through a conductor in a perpendicular magnetic field, they are deflected by the Lorentz force and accumulate on one edge until an electric field balances the magnetic force. This produces a Hall voltage across the conductor.

For a conductor with \(n\) charge carriers per unit volume and carrier charge \(q\):

Classical Hall Conductance

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \frac{nq}{B} \]

Equivalently, the Hall coefficient is \(R_H = 1/(nq)\), and the Hall resistivity is \(\rho_H = B/(nq)\). Classically, Hall conductance varies continuously with \(B\).

Integer Quantum Hall Effect: A Window to Quantization#

At low temperature (typically below 4 K) and strong magnetic field (several Tesla), experiments reveal something startling that classical physics cannot explain:

Integer Quantum Hall Effect

The Hall conductance becomes quantized in integer units of \(e^2/h\):

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \nu \frac{e^2}{h} \]

where \(\nu = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) is the filling factor (number of occupied Landau levels).

Key features:

  • Quantization is exact to parts per billion—better than any classical physical parameter

  • Conductance is independent of sample details (geometry, disorder, purity)

  • Hall resistance shows plateaus at \(R_{xy} = h/(\nu e^2)\)

  • Simultaneously, longitudinal resistance \(R_{xx}\) drops to nearly zero on the plateaus

  • This precision (von Klitzing, 1980) is now used to define electrical resistance standards

The Quantum Puzzle

Classical Hall effect predicts \(\sigma_{xy} = nq/B\), which is continuous in \(B\). But experiments show discrete plateaus. Explanation requires:

  1. The magnetic field quantizes electron motion into Landau levels (discrete energy states)

  2. Each Landau level can hold a fixed number of electrons (set by its degeneracy)

  3. When the Fermi level lies between Landau levels, scattering is suppressed

  4. This explains both the quantization and extraordinary precision

These ideas are developed in §4.3.2 Landau Quantization.

Magnetic Length: The Quantum Scale#

In a strong magnetic field, the quantum mechanical extent of a particle’s wavefunction is set by the magnetic length:

Magnetic Length

\[ \ell_B = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{qB}} \]

Physical interpretation:

  • Characteristic size of a quantum wavefunction in a magnetic field

  • Plays the role of lattice constant in condensed-matter systems

  • Independent of particle mass; depends only on charge and field strength

  • For electrons in a 1 T field: \(\ell_B \approx 26\) nm

  • For electrons in a 10 T field: \(\ell_B \approx 8\) nm

The magnetic length represents the minimum uncertainty in position allowed by the uncertainty principle in a magnetic field. In the lowest Landau level, commutation relations between canonical momentum \(\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) and position lead to \(\Delta x \, \Delta y \sim \hbar / (qB)\). With equal uncertainty in both perpendicular directions, this yields \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(qB)}\). A detailed quantum mechanical analysis is presented in §4.3.2.

Discussion

Consider a quantum particle in a very strong magnetic field in two limits:

(a) For an electron in a 10 T magnetic field, compute \(\ell_B\) in nanometers. Compare this to the Bohr radius \(a_0 \approx 0.53\) Å. In what sense is the quantum wavefunction “squeezed”?

(b) In the quantum Hall regime, the magnetic length becomes the relevant length scale. Explain why the degeneracy of each Landau level is of order \(A/\ell_B^2\), where \(A\) is the sample area.

(c) The classical cyclotron radius is \(r_c = mv_\perp/(qB)\). For an electron in the lowest Landau level, the “typical” momentum is \(p \sim \hbar/\ell_B\). Show that this relates the orbital size to quantum mechanics.

Poll: Cyclotron frequency and magnetic field

A charged particle in a uniform magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}\) undergoes circular (or helical) motion at the cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c = qB / m\). Which statement correctly explains why the cyclotron frequency depends only on the magnetic field strength and the charge-to-mass ratio, not on the particle’s velocity?

(A) The magnetic force \(\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}\) is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work and only changes direction, not speed.

(B) The radius of the circular orbit scales linearly with velocity, so the period (and frequency) is independent of velocity.

(C) The Lorentz force provides the centripetal acceleration, and equating \(qvB = mv^2 / r\) gives a period \(T = 2\pi m / (qB)\) that is independent of \(v\).

(D) All of the above.

Summary#

  • Cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c = qB/m\) and cyclotron radius \(r_c = mv_\perp/(qB)\) characterize classical orbits; the period \(T_c = 2\pi/\omega_c\) is constant independent of energy.

  • Classical Hall effect arises from Lorentz-force balance: Hall conductance \(\sigma_{xy} = nq/B\) is continuous classically.

  • Integer Quantum Hall Effect quantizes \(\sigma_{xy}\) to exact multiples of \(e^2/h\)—impossible classically, revealing discrete Landau levels.

  • Magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(qB)}\) sets the quantum wavefunction size scale, emerging from the uncertainty principle.

Homework#

1. Circular orbit from Lorentz. A classical particle with charge \(q\) and mass \(m\) moves in a uniform field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) with initial velocity \(\boldsymbol{v}_0 = v_\perp\hat{x}\).

(a) Write the equations of motion \(m\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} = q\boldsymbol{v}\times\boldsymbol{B}\) in component form. Show that \(v_z\) is constant while \(v_x\) and \(v_y\) satisfy coupled oscillator equations.

(b) Solve for the trajectory \(\boldsymbol{r}(t)\) and identify the cyclotron radius \(r_c\) and cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c\). Which depends on the particle’s energy, and which does not?

(c) Two electrons in a \(B = 1\,\text{T}\) field have kinetic energies \(E_1 = 1\,\text{eV}\) and \(E_2 = 100\,\text{eV}\). Compare their cyclotron radii and periods. Why is the energy-independence of \(\omega_c\) crucial for cyclotron particle accelerators?

2. Helical motion. A proton enters a \(B = 2\,\text{T}\) field with velocity \(\boldsymbol{v} = (10^6\hat{x} + 5\times 10^5\hat{z})\,\text{m/s}\).

(a) Decompose the motion into circular (perpendicular) and uniform (parallel) components. Find the cyclotron radius and the pitch of the helix.

(b) If this proton were in the Earth’s magnetic field (\(B \sim 50\,\mu\text{T}\)), estimate the cyclotron radius. Is it larger or smaller than the Earth’s radius?

(c) Charged particles from the Sun spiral along Earth’s magnetic field lines and converge at the poles, producing aurora. Explain qualitatively why the field-line convergence reflects the particles, creating “magnetic bottles.”

3. Classical Hall effect. A current-carrying conductor of width \(w\) and thickness \(d\) is placed in a field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) with current \(I\) in the \(x\)-direction.

(a) The Lorentz force deflects charge carriers, building up a transverse electric field \(E_H\). Derive \(E_H\) from force balance in steady state.

(b) The Hall coefficient \(R_H = E_H/(jB)\) where \(j = I/(wd)\) is the current density. Show that \(R_H = 1/(nq)\) and explain how the sign of \(R_H\) reveals the carrier type.

(c) In a semiconductor where both electrons (density \(n_e\), mobility \(\mu_e\)) and holes (\(n_h\), \(\mu_h\)) contribute, the effective Hall coefficient is \(R_H = (n_h\mu_h^2 - n_e\mu_e^2)/[e(n_h\mu_h + n_e\mu_e)^2]\). Under what condition is \(R_H = 0\)? What physical situation does this represent?

4. Magnetic length. The magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\) is the characteristic quantum length scale in a magnetic field.

(a) Compute \(\ell_B\) for \(B = 1\,\text{T}\) and \(B = 10\,\text{T}\). Compare to the Bohr radius \(a_0 = 0.053\,\text{nm}\).

(b) Show that \(\ell_B\) can be written as \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\Phi_0/(2\pi B)}\) where \(\Phi_0 = h/e\). Interpret: \(\ell_B^2\) is the area per flux quantum.

(c) The ratio \(r_c/\ell_B = p_\perp/\sqrt{e\hbar B}\) compares the classical orbit to the quantum scale. For what kinetic energy \(E_\perp\) does \(r_c = \ell_B\)? This marks the crossover to the quantum regime.

5. Energy scales. The Landau level spacing is \(\hbar\omega_c = \hbar eB/m^*\).

(a) For an electron in vacuum (\(m^* = m_e\)) at \(B = 10\,\text{T}\), compute \(\hbar\omega_c\) in meV and the corresponding temperature \(T^* = \hbar\omega_c/k_B\) in kelvin.

(b) Repeat for a GaAs electron (\(m^* = 0.067m_e\)). Why does the smaller effective mass make the QHE easier to observe?

(c) At room temperature (\(T = 300\,\text{K}\)), what minimum field \(B_\text{min}\) is needed so that \(\hbar\omega_c > k_BT\) for GaAs? Is this achievable in a laboratory?

6. Quantization preview. The Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule \(\oint \boldsymbol{p}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r} = (n + \tfrac{1}{2})h\) applied to cyclotron orbits gives \(E_n = (n + \tfrac{1}{2})\hbar\omega_c\).

(a) For a circular orbit of radius \(r\) at the cyclotron frequency, compute \(\oint \boldsymbol{p}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r}\) using \(\vert\boldsymbol{p}\vert = m\omega_c r\) and derive the quantized energies.

(b) The quantized orbit areas are \(A_n = 2\pi\ell_B^2(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\). How many flux quanta \(\Phi_0\) thread each orbit? Why is this an integer (up to the \(1/2\) offset)?

(c) The Bohr-Sommerfeld approach misses the degeneracy of each Landau level. Explain qualitatively what degree of freedom is responsible for the degeneracy. (Hint: what determines the center of the orbit?)

7. Quantum Hall phenomenology. The integer QHE shows Hall resistance quantized at \(R_H = h/(\nu e^2)\) for integer \(\nu\).

(a) The classical Hall resistance \(R_H^\text{cl} = B/(ne)\) varies continuously with \(B\). Explain qualitatively why discrete Landau levels with fixed degeneracy per level produce plateaus instead of a continuous curve.

(b) At the center of a plateau, bulk states are localized by disorder. Where does the current actually flow? What prevents backscattering?

(c) The resistance standard \(R_K = h/e^2 \approx 25.8\,\text{k}\Omega\) is material-independent. Explain why this universality is a consequence of topology rather than material-specific parameters.

8. Dimensional crossover. The magnetic length \(\ell_B\) and the 2D electron system thickness \(d\) define different regimes.

(a) When \(\ell_B \gg d\), the system is effectively 2D and Landau levels are well-defined. When \(\ell_B \ll d\), the magnetic field is a small perturbation. Estimate the crossover field \(B^*\) for a quantum well of width \(d = 10\,\text{nm}\).

(b) In a 3D bulk metal, the density of states oscillates with \(1/B\) (de Haas-van Alphen effect) but does not show plateaus. Explain the difference from the 2D case.

(c) Graphene has a linear dispersion \(E = \hbar v_F k\) instead of \(E = \hbar^2 k^2/(2m)\). Its Landau levels are \(E_n = \text{sgn}(n)\hbar v_F\sqrt{2e\vert n\vert B}\) (including \(n = 0\)). How does the unequal spacing affect the QHE compared to the parabolic case?

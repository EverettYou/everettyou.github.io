4.1.2 Electromagnetic Coupling#

Prompts

  • What is kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\), and how does it differ from canonical momentum? Use Heisenberg equations to show how both relate to observable motion.

  • How does the kinetic momentum evolve under the Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \frac{(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2}{2m} + q\Phi\)? Show that the equation of motion reproduces the Lorentz force.

  • How do \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\) arise from the gauge potentials \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\Phi\)? Write the explicit expressions.

  • We derived electromagnetism from the requirement of local U(1) phase invariance. What assumptions went in, and what came out? Why does gauge theory demand the existence of electromagnetism?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The gauge-invariant Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \frac{(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2}{2m} + q\Phi\), derived in §4.1.1 purely from local U(1) phase symmetry, contains a profound discovery: it automatically describes charged particles interacting with electromagnetic fields. By computing equations of motion, we extract the Lorentz force and identify the electric and magnetic fields. The gauge principle transforms a mathematical symmetry requirement into the fundamental interaction that shapes the universe.

Kinetic Momentum#

The Heisenberg equation for position gives:

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\boldsymbol{x}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}\langle[\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}, \hat{H}]\rangle = \frac{\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\rangle}{m} \]

using \([\hat{x}_i, \hat{p}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\delta_{ij}\) and \([\hat{x}_i, \boldsymbol{A}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}})] = 0\).

Kinetic Momentum

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}, \qquad \boldsymbol{v} = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}}{m} \]

The kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\) determines the particle’s velocity. The canonical momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\) is gauge-dependent; only \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\) is directly observable.

Caution: Kinetic Momentum Does Not Commute

Unlike canonical momentum (\([\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j] = 0\)), kinetic momentum components satisfy \([\hat{\pi}_i, \hat{\pi}_j] = \mathrm{i}q\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}B_k\) in a magnetic field. This noncommutativity is central to Landau quantization (§4.3).

The Lorentz Force Law#

Computing \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t}\) requires the commutator \([\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}, \hat{H}]\). Using \([\hat{p}_i, A_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\partial_i A_j\) and the product rule on \((\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2\), one extracts gradient and curl terms from the gauge potentials.

Lorentz Force from Gauge Theory

\[ m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = q\left\langle\boldsymbol{E} + \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})\right\rangle \]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}/m\), \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{A}}{\partial t}\), and \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\).

This is the quantum Lorentz force law, derived entirely from the gauge-invariant Hamiltonian. The magnetic force involves the symmetrized product \(\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})\) because \(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}})\) do not commute.

Derivation: Equation of Motion for Kinetic Momentum

Step 1 — Split into canonical momentum and vector potential. We want \(m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle \hat{v}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t}\) where \(\hat{v}_i = \hat{\pi}_i/m = (\hat{p}_i - qA_i)/m\). Since \(A_i(\boldsymbol{x},t)\) depends on both position and time,

\[ \begin{split} m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle \hat{v}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{p}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} - q\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle A_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} \end{split} \]

We evaluate each term separately.

Step 2 — Heisenberg equation for canonical momentum. \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{p}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}\langle[\hat{p}_i, \hat{H}]\rangle\) with \(\hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}\hat{\pi}_j\hat{\pi}_j + q\Phi\). Since \([\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j^2]=0\), the only contributions come from terms involving \(A_j\) and \(\Phi\).

Step 3 — Evaluate each commutator using \([\hat{p}_i, f(\boldsymbol{x})] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_i f\) and the product rule \([A, BC] = [A,B]C + B[A,C]\):

\[ [\hat{p}_i, A_j\hat{p}_j] = [\hat{p}_i, A_j]\hat{p}_j = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(\partial_i A_j)\hat{p}_j \]
\[ [\hat{p}_i, \hat{p}_j A_j] = \hat{p}_j[\hat{p}_i, A_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\,\hat{p}_j(\partial_i A_j) \]
\[ [\hat{p}_i, q^2 A_j^2] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\,q^2\,\partial_i(A_j^2) = -2\mathrm{i}\hbar\,q^2 A_j(\partial_i A_j) \]
\[ [\hat{p}_i, q\Phi] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\,q\,\partial_i\Phi \]

Step 4 — Collect and factor out \(\hat{\pi}_j\). Assembling \([\hat{p}_i, \hat{H}]\) from the four commutators above (the cross term in \(\hat{\pi}^2 = \hat{p}^2 - q\hat{p}\cdot A - qA\cdot\hat{p} + q^2 A^2\) carries a factor \(-q\) on the first two and \(+q^2\) on the last):

\[ \begin{split} \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}[\hat{p}_i, \hat{H}] &= \frac{q}{2m}\bigl[(\partial_i A_j)\hat{p}_j + \hat{p}_j(\partial_i A_j) - 2q A_j(\partial_i A_j)\bigr] - q\,\partial_i\Phi \end{split} \]

Recognizing \(\hat{p}_j - qA_j = \hat{\pi}_j\) in the bracket:

\[ \begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{p}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \frac{q}{2m}\bigl\langle(\partial_i A_j)\hat{\pi}_j + \hat{\pi}_j(\partial_i A_j)\bigr\rangle - q\langle\partial_i\Phi\rangle \end{split} \]

The symmetrized product \((\partial_i A_j)\hat{\pi}_j + \hat{\pi}_j(\partial_i A_j)\) appears because \(\hat{\pi}_j\) and \(\partial_i A_j\) do not commute.

Step 5 — Total time derivative of \(A_i\). By the Heisenberg equation for \(A_i(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}, t)\):

\[ \begin{split} \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle A_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} &= \left\langle\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial t}\right\rangle + \frac{1}{2m}\bigl\langle(\partial_j A_i)\hat{\pi}_j + \hat{\pi}_j(\partial_j A_i)\bigr\rangle \end{split} \]

The second term is the symmetrized convective derivative \(\hat{v}_j\,\partial_j A_i\).

Step 6 — Combine and identify \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\). Substituting Steps 4 and 5 into Step 1:

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle \hat{v}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} &= -q\left\langle\partial_i\Phi + \frac{\partial A_i}{\partial t}\right\rangle \\ &\quad + \frac{q}{2m}\bigl\langle\hat{\pi}_j(\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i) + (\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i)\hat{\pi}_j\bigr\rangle \end{split} \end{split}\]

The first line gives \(q\langle E_i\rangle\) since \(E_i = -\partial_i\Phi - \partial_t A_i\). In the second line, recognize \(\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i = \epsilon_{ijk}B_k\), so

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle \hat{v}_i\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} &= q\langle E_i\rangle + \frac{q}{2m}\,\epsilon_{ijk}\bigl\langle\hat{\pi}_j B_k + B_k\hat{\pi}_j\bigr\rangle \\ &= q\left\langle E_i + \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})_i\right\rangle \end{split} \end{split}\]

The magnetic term is not a simple cross product: because \(\hat{v}_j\) and \(B_k\) do not commute, the symmetrized combination \(\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})\) is the correct quantum-mechanical operator ordering.

Discussion

In classical mechanics, the Lorentz force \(m\boldsymbol{a} = q(\boldsymbol{E} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B})\) is an empirical law. In quantum mechanics, we derived it from the gauge principle alone. Does this mean quantum mechanics is “deeper” than classical mechanics? Could a different gauge group produce a different force law?

Electric and Magnetic Fields from Potentials#

The gauge potentials \((\Phi, \boldsymbol{A})\) are gauge-dependent and not directly observable. The physical fields are:

\[ \boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{A}}{\partial t}, \qquad \boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A} \]

In spacetime notation with \(A_\mu = (\Phi, \boldsymbol{A})\), both fields are encoded in the field strength tensor:

Field Strength Tensor

\[ F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu \]

\(F_{\mu\nu}\) is gauge-invariant: under \(A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu\chi\), the antisymmetry gives \(F'_{\mu\nu} = F_{\mu\nu}\) because \(\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\chi = \partial_\nu\partial_\mu\chi\).

Spacetime Notation: A Gentle Introduction

The key idea is simple: space and time are two aspects of the same thing, and the index \(\mu = 0, 1, 2, 3\) lets us treat them on equal footing.

Four-vectors. Each physical quantity has a time component (\(\mu = 0\)) and three spatial components (\(\mu = 1, 2, 3\)):

Four-vector

\(\mu = 0\) (time)

\(\mu = 1,2,3\) (space)

Coordinates \(x^\mu\)

\(x^0 = t\)

\(\boldsymbol{x} = (x^1, x^2, x^3)\)

Derivatives \(\partial_\mu\)

\(\partial_0 = \partial_t\)

\(\nabla = (\partial_1, \partial_2, \partial_3)\)

Energy-momentum \(p^\mu\)

\(p^0 = E\)

\(\boldsymbol{p} = (p^1, p^2, p^3)\)

Gauge field \(A_\mu\)

\(A_0 = \Phi\)

\(\boldsymbol{A} = (A_1, A_2, A_3)\)

Covariant derivative \(D_\mu\)

\(D_0 = \partial_t + \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)\Phi\)

\(\boldsymbol{D} = \nabla - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)\boldsymbol{A}\)

Notice the pattern: every familiar pair (energy and momentum, scalar and vector potential, time and space derivatives) is unified into a single four-vector.

Index conventions. Upper and lower indices are related by the Lorentz metric \(g_{\mu\nu} = \mathrm{diag}(-1, +1, +1, +1)\):

\[ a_\mu = g_{\mu\nu}\, a^\nu \quad \Longrightarrow \quad a_0 = -a^0, \quad a_i = a^i \; (i = 1,2,3) \]

Repeated indices (one upper, one lower) are summed over: \(a^\mu b_\mu = -a^0 b^0 + a^1 b^1 + a^2 b^2 + a^3 b^3\). This is the Einstein summation convention.

The field strength tensor. With this notation, \(F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu\) is a \(4 \times 4\) antisymmetric matrix whose components are exactly \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\):

\[\begin{split} F_{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & E_x & E_y & E_z \\ -E_x & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ -E_y & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ -E_z & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

For example, \(F_{01} = \partial_0 A_1 - \partial_1 A_0 = \partial_t A_x + \partial_x \Phi = -(-\partial_x \Phi - \partial_t A_x) = E_x\), and \(F_{12} = \partial_1 A_2 - \partial_2 A_1 = -(\nabla \times \boldsymbol{A})_z = -B_z\).

The beauty of \(F_{\mu\nu}\) is that it packages six quantities (\(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\)) into one geometric object that transforms simply under changes of reference frame.

The Gauge Principle Requires Electromagnetism#

The Gauge Principle

Starting from only two inputs:

  1. Local U(1) phase invariance: \(\psi(\boldsymbol{x},t) \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi(\boldsymbol{x},t)}\psi(\boldsymbol{x},t)\)

  2. The Schrödinger equation retains its form

we were forced to introduce a gauge field \(\boldsymbol{A}\), replace \(\nabla \to \nabla - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)\boldsymbol{A}\) (covariant derivative), and adopt \(\hat{H} = \frac{(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2}{2m} + q\Phi\). From this, the Lorentz force and the electric/magnetic fields emerged automatically.

Symmetry \(\to\) Interaction: The gauge field is not freely chosen—it is required by quantum consistency. Every charged particle couples to the same universal gauge field.

Summary#

  • Kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) determines velocity; its components do not commute in a magnetic field.

  • The Lorentz force \(m\boldsymbol{a} = q(\boldsymbol{E} + \boldsymbol{v}\times\boldsymbol{B})\) is derived from the gauge-invariant Hamiltonian, not assumed.

  • Electric and magnetic fields arise from gauge potentials: \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \partial_t\boldsymbol{A}\), \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla\times\boldsymbol{A}\).

  • The field strength tensor \(F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu\) is gauge-invariant.

  • The gauge principle transforms symmetry into interaction: local U(1) invariance requires electromagnetism.

1. Velocity from kinetic momentum. Compute the commutator \([\hat{x}_i, (\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2]\) using \([\hat{x}_i, \hat{p}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\delta_{ij}\) and \([\hat{x}_i, \boldsymbol{A}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}})] = 0\). Show that

\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\boldsymbol{x}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\rangle}{m} = \frac{\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\rangle}{m} \]

Explain why this result identifies the kinetic momentum with the momentum that determines velocity.

2. Kinetic momentum commutator. Show that the kinetic momentum components \(\hat{\pi}_i = \hat{p}_i - qA_i\) do not commute:

\[ [\hat{\pi}_i, \hat{\pi}_j] = \mathrm{i}q\hbar\,\epsilon_{ijk}B_k \]

(a) Start from \([\hat{\pi}_i, \hat{\pi}_j] = [\hat{p}_i - qA_i,\, \hat{p}_j - qA_j]\) and use \([\hat{p}_i, A_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_i A_j\).

(b) Show that the result simplifies to \(\mathrm{i}q\hbar\,(\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i) = \mathrm{i}q\hbar\,\epsilon_{ijk}B_k\).

(c) Evaluate explicitly for a uniform field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) and verify \([\hat{\pi}_x, \hat{\pi}_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar qB\).

3. Lorentz force from Heisenberg equation. Derive the Lorentz force law from the Heisenberg equation of motion. Starting with \(\hat{H} = \frac{(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2}{2m} + q\Phi\):

(a) Compute \(\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}, \hat{H}]\) by evaluating the commutators step by step.

(b) Combine terms using \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \partial_t\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) to recover

\[ m\frac{\mathrm{d}\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\rangle}{\mathrm{d}t} = q\left\langle\boldsymbol{E} + \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})\right\rangle \]

(c) Explain why the symmetrized cross product \(\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B} - \boldsymbol{B}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{v}})\) is needed instead of a simple \(\hat{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\boldsymbol{B}\).

4. Symmetric gauge verification. In a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\), use the symmetric gauge \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{B}{2}(-y, x, 0)\).

(a) Verify that \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) gives \(B\hat{z}\).

(b) Compute \([\hat{\pi}_x, \hat{\pi}_y]\) explicitly using this gauge and confirm \([\hat{\pi}_x, \hat{\pi}_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar qB\).

(c) Interpret this non-zero commutator physically: what does it say about simultaneous measurability of velocity components?

5. Gauge invariance of fields. Under the gauge transformation \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\chi\) and \(\Phi \to \Phi - \partial_t\chi\), show that \(\boldsymbol{E}' = \boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}' = \boldsymbol{B}\). Explain why this guarantees that all observable physics is independent of gauge choice.

6. Comparing gauge choices. Compute \(\boldsymbol{B}\) from \(\nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) for each of the following vector potentials, all describing a uniform field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\):

(a) \(\boldsymbol{A} = (0, Bx, 0)\)

(b) \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{B}{2}(-y, x, 0)\)

(c) \(\boldsymbol{A} = (-By, 0, 0)\)

Verify that all three produce the same \(\boldsymbol{B}\). Write down the gauge transformation \(\chi\) that takes (a) to (b).

7. Field strength tensor components. Write out all components of \(F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu\) as a \(4\times 4\) matrix (rows and columns labeled \(0, x, y, z\)). Verify that \(F_{0i} = E_i\) and \(F_{ij} = -\epsilon_{ijk}B_k\) by computing a few entries explicitly from the definitions \(\boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \partial_t\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla\times\boldsymbol{A}\).

8. Gauge principle and beyond. Explain why the gauge principle (local U(1) invariance) forces the existence of a gauge field that couples to charged particles.

(a) What would change if one demanded only global phase invariance instead?

(b) What additional structure arises if one replaces U(1) with a non-Abelian group such as SU(2)?

(c) Discuss briefly how this principle generalizes beyond electromagnetism to the strong and weak nuclear forces.

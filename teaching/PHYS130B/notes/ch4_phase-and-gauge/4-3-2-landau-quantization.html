4.3.2 Landau Quantization#

Prompts

  • Derive the quantum Hamiltonian for a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field using the symmetric gauge.

  • Construct the ladder operators \(\hat{a}\) and \(\hat{a}^\dagger\) from the kinetic momentum components. What are their commutation relations?

  • Show that the Hamiltonian is a harmonic oscillator in these operators. What are the Landau level energies?

  • What is the magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\)? Why is it the fundamental length scale in the problem?

  • Calculate the degeneracy of a single Landau level. How many states fit in a given level?

Lecture Notes#

Landau quantization describes the energy spectrum of a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field quantum mechanically. This is a cornerstone result: it shows that the continuous kinetic energy of a free particle becomes quantized into discrete Landau levels, each with enormous degeneracy. This degeneracy is the key to understanding the quantum Hall effect.

Quantum Hamiltonian in a Magnetic Field#

Minimal Coupling and Gauge Choice#

The quantum Hamiltonian for a charged particle in an electromagnetic field is:

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}\right)^2 + q\Phi\]

(In SI units, drop the \(1/c\) factor.) For a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) with no electric potential, \(\Phi = 0\).

The energy depends on the choice of gauge for \(\boldsymbol{A}\). Two convenient choices are:

Gauge Choices

  1. Symmetric (Landau) gauge: \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{B}{2}(-y, x, 0)\). This gauge respects rotational symmetry.

  2. Landau gauge: \(\boldsymbol{A} = (0, Bx, 0)\). This breaks translational symmetry in \(x\) but simplifies algebra.

Both give the same physical spectrum; choice is a matter of convenience.

Symmetric Gauge Approach#

In the symmetric gauge, the kinetic momentum components are:

\[ \hat{\pi}_x = \hat{p}_x + \frac{qB}{2c}y, \quad \hat{\pi}_y = \hat{p}_y - \frac{qB}{2c}x\]

The Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\hat{\pi}_x^2 + \hat{\pi}_y^2\right) + \frac{\hat{p}_z^2}{2m}\]

Motion along \(z\) is unaffected. For motion in the \(xy\)-plane, define the cyclotron frequency:

\[ \omega_c = \frac{qB}{mc}\]

The kinetic momentum components satisfy:

\[ [\hat{\pi}_x, \hat{\pi}_y] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{qB}{c} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar m\omega_c\]

Ladder Operator Construction#

The perpendicular kinetic momentum components look like position and momentum in harmonic oscillator form. Define ladder operators:

\[ \hat{a} = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega_c}{2\hbar}}\hat{\pi}_x + \mathrm{i}\sqrt{\frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega_c}}\hat{\pi}_y\]
\[\hat{a}^\dagger = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega_c}{2\hbar}}\hat{\pi}_x - \mathrm{i}\sqrt{\frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega_c}}\hat{\pi}_y\]

These satisfy the standard commutation relation:

\[ [\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1\]

Hamiltonian in Ladder Operator Form

\[ \hat{H}_{\text{2D}} = \hbar\omega_c\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\]

This is exactly the Hamiltonian of a 2D harmonic oscillator, with each Landau level separated by energy \(\hbar\omega_c\).

Landau Levels and Energy Spectrum#

The eigenvalue problem is:

\[ \hat{H}|n\rangle = E_n|n\rangle\]

with \(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a}|n\rangle = n|n\rangle\) for \(n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\)

Landau Level Energies

\[ E_n = \left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_c\]

All states with the same \(n\) belong to the \(n\)-th Landau level (LL). There is no energy separation between states with different guiding center positions—only the quantum number \(n\) matters.

The ground state \(n = 0\) has energy \(E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_c\) (the zero-point energy). Higher Landau levels are spaced by \(\hbar\omega_c\) intervals.

Magnetic Length and Degeneracy#

The characteristic length scale in this problem is the magnetic length:

Magnetic Length

\[ \ell_B = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{qB}} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar c}{qBc}}\]

(In Gaussian units: \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/eB}\).)

The magnetic length sets the size of the quantum wavefunction in the lowest Landau level. Two distinct guiding center positions separated by \(\gtrsim \ell_B\) correspond to orthogonal wavefunctions.

For a sample of area \(A\), the degeneracy of each Landau level is:

Landau Level Degeneracy

\[ N_{\phi} = \frac{\Phi_{\text{total}}}{\Phi_0} = \frac{BA}{\Phi_0}\]

where \(\Phi_0 = \frac{hc}{q} = \frac{h}\mathrm{e}\) is the flux quantum (using \(q = e\) for electrons).

Equivalently:

\[ N_{\phi} = \frac{A}{\pi \ell_B^2}\]

Each Landau level can accommodate \(N_{\phi}\) quantum states—a macroscopic degeneracy.

Derivation: Degeneracy from Uncertainty Principle

In the lowest Landau level (\(n = 0\)), the guiding center position uncertainty is \(\Delta X \sim \ell_B\). The number of distinct guiding center positions that fit in a region of area \(A\) is approximately \(N \sim A/(\pi\ell_B^2)\).

More rigorously, each state can be labeled by its guiding center position \((X, Y)\), and the separation between orthogonal states is \(\ell_B\). Thus the degeneracy is the number of lattice points in an \(\ell_B\)-spacing lattice covering area \(A\).

Discussion

The degeneracy of each Landau level, \(N_\phi = BA/\Phi_0\), grows linearly with the magnetic field strength \(B\). This is counterintuitive: stronger fields should confine particles more tightly, not create more states. What is really happening?

(a) The magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(qB)}\) decreases as \(B\) increases. How does the number of lattice sites (separated by \(\ell_B\)) that fit in a fixed area \(A\) scale with decreasing \(\ell_B\)?

(b) A sample with area \(A = 1 \text{ mm}^2 = 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2\) at \(B = 1 \text{ T}\) has \(N_\phi \approx 10^{10}\) degenerate states in the lowest Landau level. If you increase \(B\) to \(10 \text{ T}\) while keeping the sample size fixed, what happens to \(N_\phi\)? Estimate the new value.

(c) If all \(N_\phi\) states have the same energy (to leading order), what prevents the ground state energy from being completely determined by the number of particles \(N_e\)? Why is the filling factor \(\nu = N_e/N_\phi\) the crucial quantity?

Physical Consequences#

Macroscopic Degeneracy and Its Significance

  • The degeneracy \(N_{\phi} \sim 10^{10}\) for a typical Hall bar (area \(\sim 1 \text{ mm}^2\) in a \(B \sim 1 \text{ T}\) field).

  • All \(N_{\phi}\) states in a Landau level have the same energy (to leading order).

  • This massive degeneracy is broken by disorder, interactions, and finite size effects.

  • The degeneracy is the origin of the integer quantum Hall effect: it allows distinct edge states to carry independent current.

Quantum vs Classical#

Landau quantization is fundamentally quantum:

  • Classically, all energies up to the cyclotron energy are allowed.

  • Quantum mechanically, only discrete Landau levels appear, spaced by \(\hbar\omega_c\).

  • The gap between Landau levels grows with \(B\); at very strong fields, only the lowest level is populated.

Summary#

Key Results: Landau Quantization

  • Perpendicular motion in a magnetic field is equivalent to a 2D harmonic oscillator.

  • Energy levels: \(E_n = (n + \tfrac{1}{2})\hbar\omega_c\) with \(n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\)

  • Each level is macroscopically degenerate: \(N_{\phi} = BA/\Phi_0\) states per level.

  • Magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(qB)}\) characterizes wavefunction size.

  • Landau quantization is the basis for quantum Hall physics and topological quantum phenomena.

Problem 1: Cyclotron Frequency and Magnetic Length

A charged particle in a magnetic field has cyclotron frequency \(\omega_c = eB/m\). Show that the magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\) can be written as:

\[\ell_B = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{m\omega_c}}\]

Interpret this result physically: what does this tell you about the relationship between the magnetic length and the quantum zero-point motion at the cyclotron frequency?

Problem 2: Commutation Relations of Kinetic Momentum

In the symmetric gauge with \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) and \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{B}{2}(-y, x, 0)\), the kinetic momentum components are:

\[\pi_x = p_x + \frac{eBy}{2}, \quad \pi_y = p_y - \frac{eBx}{2}\]

(a) Verify that \([\pi_x, \pi_y] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\omega_c\) using the canonical commutation relations \([p_i, x_j] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\delta_{ij}\).

(b) Why does the non-commuting structure of kinetic momentum imply that the particle’s motion must have quantized energy levels?

Problem 3: Ladder Operators and Harmonic Oscillator Structure

Show that the ladder operators:

\[\hat{a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar\omega_c}}(\pi_x + i\pi_y), \quad \hat{a}^\dagger = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar\omega_c}}(\pi_x - i\pi_y)\]

satisfy the canonical commutation relation \([\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1\).

Hint: Use the result \([\pi_x, \pi_y] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\omega_c\) and recall that \([\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = \frac{1}{2\hbar\omega_c}[(\pi_x + i\pi_y), (\pi_x - i\pi_y)]\).

Problem 4: Energy Eigenvalues from Ladder Operators

The kinetic energy of the particle can be written as:

\[\hat{H} = \hbar\omega_c\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \frac{1}{2}\right)\]

(a) Use the ladder operator algebra to find the energy eigenvalues \(E_n\) and show they are equally spaced with gap \(\hbar\omega_c\).

(b) What is the zero-point energy \(E_0\)? Explain why this non-zero value is purely quantum mechanical and has no classical analog.

(c) If the magnetic field is doubled (\(B \to 2B\)), how do the Landau level spacings change?

Problem 5: Magnetic Length and Localization

The magnetic length \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\) is the characteristic size of all Landau level wavefunctions, independent of which Landau level \(n\) they belong to.

(a) For an electron in a magnetic field \(B = 1\) Tesla, calculate \(\ell_B\) numerically (give result in nanometers).

(b) Explain physically why the wavefunction size should depend on \(B\) but not on \(n\). (Hint: Consider what happens when you increase \(B\); how does the “confinement” of the particle change?)

(c) In the quantum Hall regime, typical magnetic fields are \(B \sim 10\) Tesla. How much smaller is \(\ell_B\) in this case?

Problem 6: Guiding Center Non-Commutativity

The guiding center coordinates are:

\[\hat{X} = \hat{x} + \frac{\hat{p}_y}{eB}, \quad \hat{Y} = \hat{y} - \frac{\hat{p}_x}{eB}\]

Show that \([\hat{X}, \hat{Y}] = -\mathrm{i}\ell_B^2\).

Why is this non-zero commutator profound? What does it imply about the minimum area that a quantum state can occupy in guiding center space?

Problem 7: Wavefunction Structure in the Lowest Landau Level

The lowest Landau level wavefunction is:

\[\psi_0(x, y) = \frac{1}{(\pi\ell_B^2)^{1/4}}\exp\left(-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\ell_B^2}\right)\]

(a) Verify that this wavefunction is normalized: \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi_0(x,y)|^2\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}y = 1\).

(b) What is the probability of finding the particle at a distance greater than \(2\ell_B\) from the origin? (You may leave your answer in terms of the error function.)

(c) All states within the lowest Landau level are translations of this Gaussian. What quantum number labels different states within the same Landau level?

Problem 8: Holomorphic Polynomial Structure

In complex coordinates \(z = x + iy\), the \(n\)-th Landau level wavefunction has the form:

\[\psi_n(z, \bar{z}) = P_n(z)\exp\left(-\frac{|z|^2}{2\ell_B^2}\right)\]

where \(P_n(z)\) is a holomorphic polynomial of degree \(n\).

(a) Write down the explicit form of \(P_n(z)\) for \(n = 0, 1, 2\).

(b) Why must \(P_n(z)\) be holomorphic (depend only on \(z\), not \(\bar{z}\))? (Hint: Think about the ground state condition \(\hat{a}|\psi_0\rangle = 0\) and what it implies for \(\bar{z}\)-derivatives.)

(c) How many independent states are there in the \(n\)-th Landau level for a system confined to a region of area \(A\)? Explain your reasoning.

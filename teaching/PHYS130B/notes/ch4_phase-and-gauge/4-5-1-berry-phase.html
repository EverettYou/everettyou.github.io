4.5.1 Berry Phase#

Prompts

  • What is the difference between adiabatic and diabatic evolution? How do we decide which regime applies?

  • Define Berry phase geometrically. Why does it depend only on the shape of the path in parameter space, not the speed of traversal?

  • What are Berry connection and Berry curvature, and how do they relate to familiar concepts like vector potential and magnetic field?

  • Explain the difference between the geometric (Berry) phase and the familiar dynamical phase. Can these two phases interfere?

  • Prove that Berry curvature is gauge-invariant. Why is this invariance crucial for physical observables?

Lecture Notes#

The Adiabatic Theorem and Phase Evolution#

When a quantum system’s Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\) changes slowly compared to the gap \(\Delta E\) between eigenlevels, the system remains in its instantaneous eigenstate:

\[\hat{H}(\boldsymbol{R})|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle = E_n(\boldsymbol{R})|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\]

This is the adiabatic theorem. If the system starts in eigenstate \(|n(\boldsymbol{R}_0)\rangle\), it will stay in \(|n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle\) throughout evolution.

However, the quantum state acquired during this process is not merely:

\[|\psi(t)\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_n(t)/\hbar} |n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle\]

This expression misses a geometric phase — Berry’s phase — that depends on the path traced through parameter space, not just the initial and final states.

The Phase Evolution

The full adiabatic evolution of the \(n\)-th eigenstate is:

\[|\psi(t)\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\gamma_n(t)} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\theta_n(t)} |n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle\]

where:

  • Dynamical phase: \(\theta_n(t) = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^t E_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t'))\,\mathrm{d}t'\)

  • Geometric phase (Berry phase): \(\gamma_n(t) = \mathrm{i}\int_0^t \langle n(\boldsymbol{R})|{\boldsymbol{\nabla}_{\boldsymbol{R}}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle} \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{R}}\,\mathrm{d}t'\)

Berry Connection and Berry Curvature#

Define the Berry connection:

\[\mathcal{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\]

It is a vector field in parameter space. Then:

\[\gamma_n(t) = \int_C \mathcal{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\]

where \(C\) is the path in parameter space from initial to final \(\boldsymbol{R}(t)\).

Berry Connection as Geometric Vector Potential

The Berry connection \(\mathcal{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) plays the role of a vector potential in parameter space, analogous to the electromagnetic vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\).

Key properties:

  • Gauge-dependent: Transforms as \(\mathcal{A}_n \to \mathcal{A}_n + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\xi\) under phase rotation \(|n\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\xi}|n\rangle\)

  • Line integral gives Berry phase: \(\gamma_n = \int_C \mathcal{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\) (for open paths, gauge-dependent)

  • Real-valued: \(\mathcal{A}_n\) is always real because \(\langle n|n\rangle = 1 \Rightarrow \langle n|\nabla|n\rangle + \text{c.c.} = 0\)

The Berry curvature is:

\[\mathcal{F}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}} \times \mathcal{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R})\]

By Stokes’ theorem, for a closed path enclosing a surface \(S\) in parameter space:

\[\gamma_n = \oint_C \mathcal{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = \int_S \mathcal{F}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}\]

Berry Curvature and Chern Number

The Berry curvature \(\mathcal{F}_{n,\mu\nu}(\boldsymbol{R}) = \partial_{R_\mu}\mathcal{A}_{n,\nu} - \partial_{R_\nu}\mathcal{A}_{n,\mu}\) is a rank-2 tensor (the “field strength” of the Berry connection).

Arises from off-diagonal couplings:

\[\mathcal{F}_{n,\mu\nu} = -2\,\mathrm{Im}\sum_{m\neq n} \frac{\langle n|\partial_\mu \hat{H}|m\rangle\langle m|\partial_\nu \hat{H}|n\rangle}{(E_m - E_n)^2}\]

For a 2D parameter space (e.g., crystal momentum \(\boldsymbol{k}\)), the Chern number is the topological integral:

\[C_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int \mathcal{F}_n \, \mathrm{d}A \in \mathbb{Z}\]

This is always an integer—a topological invariant that cannot change without closing the band gap.

Derivation: Berry Curvature Formula

The Berry curvature can be expressed in terms of the Hamiltonian. Acting on eigenstate \(|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\):

\[\partial_\mu \mathcal{A}_{n,\nu} = \mathrm{i}\partial_\mu\langle n|\partial_\nu n\rangle = \mathrm{i}[\partial_\mu\langle n|]\partial_\nu|n\rangle + \mathrm{i}\langle n|\partial_{\mu}\partial_\nu|n\rangle\]

The off-diagonal component comes from:

\[\mathrm{i}\partial_\mu\langle n| = \mathrm{i}\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{\langle m|\partial_\mu\hat{H}|n\rangle}{(E_n - E_m)}\langle m|\]

Combining and subtracting to form the curl gives the result above.

Gauge Invariance and Physical Observables#

The geometric phase is physically well-defined only for closed paths. This is because we can perform a gauge transformation:

\[|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi(\boldsymbol{R})}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\]

Under this redefinition, the Berry connection transforms as:

\[\mathcal{A}_n \to \mathcal{A}_n + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\phi\]

For an open path, the Berry phase changes. But for a closed path:

\[\oint_C (\mathcal{A}_n + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\phi) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = \oint_C \mathcal{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} + \oint_C \nabla\phi \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = \oint_C \mathcal{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\]

The gradient term vanishes around a closed loop, so \(\gamma_n\) is gauge-invariant for closed paths (modulo \(2\pi\)).

Gauge Invariance: Connection vs. Curvature

  • Berry connection \(\mathcal{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R})\): Gauge-dependent (transforms like vector potential)

  • Berry curvature \(\mathcal{F}_n(\boldsymbol{R})\): Gauge-invariant (transforms like magnetic field)

  • Berry phase on closed loops: Gauge-invariant (depends only on geometry, not phase choice)

  • Berry phase on open paths: Gauge-dependent (depends on choice of phase convention)

This mirrors electromagnetism: \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is gauge-dependent, but \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\oint \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\ell}\) around closed loops are gauge-invariant.

Discussion: Gauge Invariance and Geometric Phase

Berry phase is gauge-invariant but the Berry connection is not.

Explain this apparent contradiction. Then compute the Berry phase for a spin-1/2 in a magnetic field as its direction is adiabatically traced around a cone of half-angle θ.

Why is the solid angle traced out the fundamental quantity that determines the geometric phase?

Spin-1/2 in a Rotating Magnetic Field#

Example: A spin-1/2 particle in a slowly rotating magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B}(t)\) with fixed magnitude \(B_0\) but rotating direction.

For adiabatic evolution, the spin stays aligned with the instantaneous field. As \(\boldsymbol{B}\) traces a closed path on the unit sphere, sweeping solid angle \(\Omega\), the Berry phase is:

\[\gamma = -\frac{\Omega}{2}\]

The negative sign reflects the spinor’s intrinsic properties. For a path that encloses a full hemisphere (\(\Omega = 2\pi\)), \(\gamma = -\pi\).

This is intimately connected to the fact that a spin-1/2 wave function has \(4\pi\) periodicity (a \(2\pi\) rotation about any axis gives \(-1\), not \(+1\)). The Berry phase partially compensates this geometric structure.

Solid Angle Formula for Spin-1/2

For a spin-1/2 in a rotating magnetic field, as the field direction traces a closed path enclosing solid angle \(\Omega\) on the unit sphere:

\[\gamma_{\text{Berry}} = -\frac{\Omega}{2}\]

This is called the Pancharatnam-Berry phase or geometric phase. The factor \(-1/2\) is characteristic of spin-1/2; higher spins have different coefficients.

The solid angle \(\Omega\) can be computed from the path using spherical geometry. For a cone of half-angle \(\theta_0\) traversed once: \(\Omega = 2\pi(1 - \cos\theta_0)\).

Derivation: Solid Angle and Berry Phase

For a spin-1/2 in field \(\boldsymbol{B}(t) = B_0(\sin\theta(t)\cos\phi(t), \sin\theta(t)\sin\phi(t), \cos\theta(t))\), the instantaneous ground state is:

\[\begin{split}|+(\theta,\phi)\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta/2) \\ \sin(\theta/2)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi} \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

The Berry connection is:

\[\mathcal{A} = \mathrm{i}\langle +|\nabla|+\rangle\]

Computing \(\langle +|\partial_\theta |+\rangle\) and \(\langle +|\partial_\phi|+\rangle\):

\[\langle +|\partial_\theta|+\rangle = 0, \quad \langle +|\partial_\phi|+\rangle = -\frac{1}{2}\]

Thus \(\mathcal{A} = -\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{d}\phi\), and:

\[\gamma = \oint \mathcal{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = -\frac{1}{2}\oint \mathrm{d}\phi = -\frac{\Omega}{2}\]

where \(\Omega = \int \mathrm{d}\Omega\) is the solid angle.

Physical Interpretation: Holonomy#

Berry phase is a holonomy — a phase acquired by parallel transport of a quantum state around a closed path in an abstract geometric space (the space of Hamiltonian parameters).

In general relativity, parallel transport around a closed loop in curved spacetime results in a rotation of the vector. Similarly, the quantum state’s phase is “rotated” by geometric curvature in the parameter space landscape.

This perspective unifies many quantum phenomena:

  • Molecular spectra (Berry phase in electronic coordinates)

  • Transport in solids (Berry phase in crystal momentum space)

  • Photon polarization (Berry phase in direction of propagation)

Homework#

1. Adiabatic Condition. A quantum system has energy gap \(\Delta E\) between its ground and first excited state. The Hamiltonian varies at rate \(\dot{H}\). State the adiabatic condition in terms of these quantities and explain physically why rapid changes cause non-adiabatic transitions.

2. Berry Phase as a Line Integral. The Berry phase is defined as

\[ \gamma_n[C] = \oint_C \boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) is the Berry connection. Show that \(\boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n\) is real-valued by differentiating the normalization condition \(\langle n|n\rangle = 1\).

3. Gauge Transformation. Under a local gauge transformation \(|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\xi(\boldsymbol{R})}|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\), show that the Berry connection transforms as

\[ \boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n \to \boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n - \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\xi(\boldsymbol{R})\]

and explain why the Berry phase \(\gamma_n[C]\) around a closed loop \(C\) is gauge invariant.

4. Berry Curvature from Stokes’ Theorem. The Berry curvature is \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}} \times \boldsymbol{\mathcal{A}}_n\). Using Stokes’ theorem, express the Berry phase as a surface integral of the Berry curvature over a surface \(S\) bounded by \(C\). What is the electromagnetic analogy?

5. Spin-1/2 in a Magnetic Field: Solid Angle Formula. For a spin-1/2 particle in a magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B(\sin\theta\cos\phi,\, \sin\theta\sin\phi,\, \cos\theta)\), the ground state is the spin-up eigenstate along \(\hat{\boldsymbol{B}}\). When \(\boldsymbol{B}\) traces a closed loop on a cone of half-angle \(\theta_0\) at fixed \(|\boldsymbol{B}|\):

(a) Show that the Berry phase equals \(-\Omega/2\) where \(\Omega\) is the solid angle enclosed by the loop on the unit sphere.

(b) Calculate the Berry phase for a complete circle at fixed \(\theta_0 = \pi/2\).

6. Degeneracy Points as Magnetic Monopoles. The Berry curvature \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n\) is large near degeneracy points where \(E_n = E_m\). For a two-level system with Hamiltonian \(H = \boldsymbol{d}(\boldsymbol{R})\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\), the degeneracy occurs at \(\boldsymbol{d} = 0\). Show that the Berry curvature acts like a magnetic monopole of charge \(\pm 1/2\) at this point in parameter space, and explain the physical significance.

7. Non-Abelian Berry Phase in Degenerate Subspace. When the instantaneous energy level \(n\) is degenerate (degeneracy \(N > 1\)), the Berry “phase” becomes a unitary matrix \(U \in U(N)\) in the degenerate subspace, rather than a scalar. Explain qualitatively why path-ordering matters for the non-Abelian case (i.e., why \(U[C_1 \to C_2] \neq U[C_2 \to C_1]\)), and give one physical system where this arises.

8. Breakdown of Adiabatic Approximation. A two-level system has instantaneous eigenstates \(|+\rangle\) and \(|-\rangle\) with energy splitting \(\Delta(t) = \Delta_0 + vt\) (linearly increasing). The system starts in \(|+\rangle\) at \(t=0\).

(a) At what time \(t^*\) does the gap become zero? Estimate the characteristic timescale for adiabatic-to-diabatic transition near this point using the Landau-Zener criterion.

(b) For what range of velocities \(v\) is the adiabatic approximation valid throughout the evolution?

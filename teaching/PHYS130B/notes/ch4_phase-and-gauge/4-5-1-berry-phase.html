4.5.1 Berry Phase#

Prompts

  • State the adiabatic theorem. What is the difference between adiabatic and diabatic evolution?

  • Derive the Berry phase formula \(\gamma_n(C) = i\oint_C \langle n | \nabla_R n \rangle \cdot dR\). Why does it depend only on the path?

  • Define Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n(R)\) and Berry curvature \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n(R)\). What do they measure physically?

  • Explain the difference between geometric and dynamical phases. Can they interfere?

  • Prove gauge-invariance of Berry curvature. Why is the curvature observable while the connection is not?

Lecture Notes#

As a quantum system evolves adiabatically through parameter space along a closed path, it acquires not just the dynamical phase from energy, but an additional geometric phase—the Berry phase—that depends only on the path’s geometry, not on evolution speed. This phase arises from the curvature of the eigenstate space and reveals that quantum mechanics has a hidden geometric structure. Berry phase connects gauge theory, topology, and observable interference phenomena, and appears throughout quantum systems: atoms in slowly-rotating fields, electrons in slowly-varying potentials, and topological condensed matter.

Berry Phase Survives Adiabatic Approximation

The adiabatic approximation ensures no transitions between states. Yet the Berry phase accumulates even when the system never leaves its instantaneous eigenstate. This is the magic of the geometric phase.

The Adiabatic Theorem#

The adiabatic theorem is a fundamental principle in quantum mechanics that applies when parameters in the Hamiltonian change slowly.

Statement: If a quantum system starts in an eigenstate of \(\hat{H}(\boldsymbol{R}(0))\) and the parameters \(\boldsymbol{R}(t)\) change slowly (adiabatically), then the system remains in the instantaneous eigenstate throughout the evolution:

\[|\psi(t)\rangle = e^{i\gamma_n(t)}e^{-i\phi_d(t)}|n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle\]

The adiabatic condition requires:

\[\left|\frac{d\boldsymbol{R}/dt}{E_{m} - E_n}\right| \ll 1 \quad (m \neq n)\]

That is, parameters must change much more slowly than the intrinsic timescale set by energy level spacings.

Rigorous derivation: The adiabatic theorem can be derived rigorously from time-dependent perturbation theory. See §5.2.3 for the full derivation showing how adiabatic evolution naturally emerges when the perturbation timescale is much longer than energy gaps.

Dynamical Phase#

The dynamical phase is the phase accumulated from the time evolution of an energy eigenstate:

\[\phi_d(t) = \frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^t E_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t'))\, dt'\]

This is exactly what we expect from the Schrödinger equation \(i\hbar\partial_t|\psi\rangle = \hat{H}|\psi\rangle\). For a state with fixed energy \(E_n\), the phase factor is simply \(e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}\).

The dynamical phase:

  • Depends on the evolution time (explicitly through the integral)

  • Depends on the actual path taken through parameter space (via \(E_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\))

  • Is easily understood in terms of energy considerations

Berry Phase: The Geometric Contribution#

Beyond the dynamical phase, an adiabatically-evolving eigenstate acquires an additional geometric phase, discovered by Michael Berry in 1984:

\[\gamma_n(C) = i\oint_C \langle n(\boldsymbol{R}) | \nabla_\boldsymbol{R} n(\boldsymbol{R}) \rangle \cdot d\boldsymbol{R}\]

where the integral is over a closed loop \(C\) in parameter space.

Remarkable properties:

  1. Path-dependent, not time-dependent: The Berry phase depends only on the geometric shape of the path \(C\), NOT on how long the evolution takes. Fast or slow adiabatic evolution gives the same Berry phase!

  2. Geometric origin: It depends on the geometry of the eigenstate in Hilbert space as a function of parameters.

  3. Closed paths only: For open paths, the phase can be gauged away by redefining the phase of the eigenstate. Only for closed loops is the phase well-defined and unambiguous.

  4. Gauge ambiguity: The Berry phase depends on the phase convention chosen for \(|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\). Different phase choices shift the Berry phase by multiples of \(2\pi\).

Berry Connection#

Define the Berry connection (a gauge field in parameter space):

\[\boldsymbol{A}_{n}(\boldsymbol{R}) = i\langle n(\boldsymbol{R}) | \nabla_\boldsymbol{R} n(\boldsymbol{R}) \rangle\]

This is a vector field in parameter space with dimensions of inverse length. It encodes how the phase of the eigenstate changes as we move through parameter space.

The Berry phase can then be written compactly as:

\[\gamma_n(C) = \oint_C \boldsymbol{A}_{n}(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot d\boldsymbol{R}\]

This is the line integral of the Berry connection, analogous to the phase acquired by a charged particle traveling through an electromagnetic vector potential.

Berry Curvature#

Just as the electromagnetic field is the curl of the vector potential, we define the Berry curvature as a 2-form in parameter space:

\[\Omega_{n,ij}(\boldsymbol{R}) = \partial_{R_i}A_{n,j} - \partial_{R_j}A_{n,i}\]

By Stokes’ theorem, the Berry phase can be expressed as a surface integral of the curvature:

\[\gamma_n(C) = \oint_C \boldsymbol{A}_n \cdot d\boldsymbol{R} = \int_S \boldsymbol{\Omega}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot d\boldsymbol{S}\]

Key property: Unlike the Berry connection (which is gauge-dependent), the Berry curvature is gauge-invariant. This makes the curvature a fundamental observable quantity that cannot be transformed away by phase redefinitions of the eigenstate.

Derivation

Just as the electromagnetic field is the curl of the vector potential, we define the Berry curvature:

\[\Omega_{n,ij}(\boldsymbol{R}) = \partial_{R_i}A_{n,j} - \partial_{R_j}A_{n,i}\]

This is an antisymmetric tensor (2-form) in parameter space.

By Stokes’ theorem, the line integral of the connection equals the surface integral of the curvature:

\[\gamma_n(C) = \oint_C \boldsymbol{A}_n \cdot d\boldsymbol{R} = \int_S \boldsymbol{\Omega}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot d\boldsymbol{S}\]

The Berry curvature:

  • Is gauge-invariant: Unlike the connection, which changes under phase redefinition of \(|n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\), the curvature is independent of gauge choice

  • Measures the geometric twist in the eigenstate Hilbert space

  • Encodes observable topological properties

Explicit Formula for Curvature#

The Berry curvature can be expressed in terms of matrix elements of the Hamiltonian derivatives:

\[\Omega_{n,ij} = -2\text{Im}\sum_{m \neq n}\frac{\langle n | \partial_i \hat{H} | m \rangle \langle m | \partial_j \hat{H} | n \rangle}{(E_n - E_m)^2}\]

This formula reveals that:

  • The curvature involves off-diagonal matrix elements of Hamiltonian derivatives

  • States with large energy differences contribute weakly (denominator suppression)

  • The sum is over all excited states \(m eq n\)

  • The formula is intrinsically gauge-invariant (imaginary part of a fraction)

This provides a practical way to calculate Berry curvature from first principles given the Hamiltonian structure.

Derivation and Example

The Berry curvature can be expressed in terms of other eigenstates:

\[\Omega_{n,ij} = -2\text{Im}\sum_{m \neq n}\frac{\langle n | \partial_i \hat{H} | m \rangle \langle m | \partial_j \hat{H} | n \rangle}{(E_n - E_m)^2}\]

This formula shows that the curvature involves matrix elements of the Hamiltonian derivatives weighted by energy differences. States that are far in energy contribute weakly.

:class: example

A paradigmatic example: a spin-1/2 particle in a slowly rotating magnetic field.

Let \(\boldsymbol{B}(t)\) rotate around the \(z\)-axis with angular velocity \(\Omega \ll \omega_L\) (much slower than Larmor frequency \(\omega_L = g B/(2m_e c)\)).

The spin adiabatically follows the magnetic field direction (following the instantaneous eigenstate). As the field completes one full rotation around the \(z\)-axis, tracing a cone of half-angle \(\theta\), the spin acquires Berry phase:

\[\gamma = \frac{1}{2}\Omega_\text{solid angle} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2\pi(1 - \cos\theta) = \pi(1 - \cos\theta)\]

For a field rotating around the equator (\(\theta = \pi/2\)):

\[\gamma = \pi\]

This is the famous geometric phase of a spinning particle.

Summary#

  • Adiabatic theorem: Slow parameter change preserves eigenstate (up to phases).

  • Dynamical phase: Standard energy-dependent phase \(\phi_d = -\int E_n dt/\hbar\).

  • Berry phase: Additional geometric phase \(\gamma = i\oint \langle n|\nabla_R n\rangle \cdot dR\) from path geometry.

  • Path-dependent: Berry phase depends only on path shape, not evolution speed.

  • Berry connection: Gauge field \(\boldsymbol{A}_n = i\langle n|\nabla_R n\rangle\) in parameter space.

  • Berry curvature: \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}_n\)—gauge-invariant field strength.

  • Stokes’ theorem: \(\gamma = \oint \boldsymbol{A} \cdot dR = \int \boldsymbol{\Omega} \cdot dS\) connects connection and curvature.

  • Geometric interpretation: Curvature measures geometric twist of eigenstate in Hilbert space.

  • Closed loops only: Non-zero Berry phase requires topologically non-trivial loops.

  • Rigorous foundation: The adiabatic theorem and Berry phase emerge naturally from time-dependent perturbation theory (see §5.2.3).

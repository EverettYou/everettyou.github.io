4.2.3 Gauge Invariance#

Prompts

  • How do different gauge choices (Coulomb gauge, Landau gauge, Lorenz gauge) simplify specific problems? Give examples.

  • What is a Wilson loop? How does it encode gauge-invariant information about electromagnetic fields?

  • Derive the Peierls substitution for tight-binding models. How does it incorporate magnetic fields into lattice quantum mechanics?

  • Prove that expectation values of physical observables are gauge-invariant. Which quantities depend on gauge choice?

  • How do you measure gauge-invariant quantities in experiments? (E.g., flux quantization, Hall conductance, interference patterns.)

Lecture Notes#

Gauge invariance is not merely a mathematical symmetry—it is the principle that guarantees all physical predictions are independent of unobservable choices. In this section, we explore how gauge freedom is exploited to simplify calculations, and which quantities are truly gauge-invariant (observable) versus gauge-dependent (unobservable).

Gauge Choices and Computational Simplification#

The freedom to choose a gauge is a powerful tool. Different problems are often easiest in different gauges.

Common Gauge Choices#

Coulomb Gauge: \(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\)

Advantages:

  • The scalar potential \(\Phi\) is determined instantaneously by the charge density: \(\nabla^2\Phi = -\rho/\epsilon_0\).

  • Natural for non-relativistic QM with static charge distributions.

  • Separates the longitudinal electric field (from charges) from the transverse field (from currents).

Example: Coulomb Gauge in Atomic Physics

In an atom, the Coulomb gauge makes the Coulomb potential appear explicitly and separately from the radiation field effects. This is ideal for calculating atomic structure with radiation as a perturbation.

Landau Gauge: \(A_x = 0, A_y = Bx\) (for a uniform field \(B = B\hat{z}\))

Advantages:

  • Explicitly breaks translational symmetry in one direction, simplifying separation of variables.

  • Natural for calculating Landau levels (quantum Hall effect).

  • Eigenfunctions have simpler form (plane waves in one direction).

Symmetric (Landau) Gauge: \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{B} \times \boldsymbol{r}\)

Advantages:

  • Manifests rotational symmetry about the magnetic field axis.

  • Natural for problems with cylindrical symmetry.

Gauge Transformations Between Choices#

Transforming from one gauge to another involves choosing a scalar function \(\chi(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) such that:

\[ \boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi, \quad \Phi' = \Phi + \frac{1}{q}\partial_t\chi\]

The wavefunction transforms as \(\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi/(\hbar c)}\psi\) to keep the physics unchanged.

Gauge Transformations and Simplification

Although different gauges simplify different calculations, the energy eigenvalues, transition rates, and all observable predictions must be identical. If they are not, an error has been made—gauge invariance provides a consistency check.

Gauge-Invariant and Gauge-Dependent Quantities#

Not all quantum mechanical quantities are observable. Some depend on the gauge choice.

Classification of Observables

Gauge-Invariant (Observable):

  • Probability density: \(|\psi|^2\)

  • Probability current: \(\boldsymbol{j} = \text{Im}(\psi^*\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\psi)/m\)

  • Energy eigenvalues: \(E_n\)

  • Transition probabilities: \(|\langle n|O|m \rangle|^2\)

  • Magnetic flux through a loop: \(\oint \boldsymbol{B} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}\)

  • Wilson loop: \(W(C) = \exp(\mathrm{i}q\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}/(\hbar c))\) (for certain geometric configurations)

Gauge-Dependent (Unobservable in isolation):

  • Vector potential: \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\)

  • Scalar potential: \(\Phi(\boldsymbol{r})\)

  • Phase of the wavefunction: \(\arg[\psi(\boldsymbol{r})]\) at a single point

  • Canonical momentum: \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\)

  • Individual Aharonov-Bohm phase along a single path

The key insight: quantities that are intrinsically gauge-dependent can still combine in gauge-invariant ways. For example, the relative phase difference between two paths is gauge-invariant even though individual path phases are gauge-dependent.

Discussion

Gauge transformations leave measurable quantities invariant, yet different gauges give different vector potentials and scalar potentials. Suppose you solve the Landau level problem (cyclotron motion in a magnetic field) in two different gauges: the symmetric gauge \(\boldsymbol{A} = \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{B} \times \boldsymbol{r}\) and the Landau gauge \(\boldsymbol{A} = (0, Bx, 0)\).

(a) Would you obtain the same energy spectrum \(E_n = (n+\frac{1}{2})\hbar\omega_c\) in both gauges? Explain.

(b) Would the wavefunctions \(\psi_{n}(\boldsymbol{r})\) have the same functional form in both gauges, or would they look completely different? If different, why must physical predictions (transition rates, probability density integrals) still agree?

(c) How would you verify explicitly that a gauge transformation from one to the other does not change an observable like the probability current \(\boldsymbol{j} = \text{Im}(\psi^*\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\psi)/m\)?

Wilson Loops and Non-Abelian Gauge Theory#

A Wilson loop is a path-ordered integral of the gauge field around a closed contour:

\[ W(C) = \mathcal{P}\exp\left[\frac{\mathrm{i}q}{\hbar c}\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\right]\]

For an abelian gauge (electromagnetism), this simplifies to:

\[ W(C) = \exp\left[\frac{\mathrm{i}q}{\hbar c}\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\right] = \exp\left[\frac{\mathrm{i}q\Phi_B}{\hbar c}\right]\]

where \(\Phi_B\) is the flux enclosed by the loop.

Wilson Loop as Gauge-Invariant Observable

The Wilson loop \(W(C)\) is gauge-invariant: it depends only on the flux enclosed by \(C\), not on the detailed form of \(\boldsymbol{A}\).

In non-abelian gauge theories (like QCD), Wilson loops play a central role in understanding confinement and the quark-gluon dynamics of the strong nuclear force.

Peierls Substitution: Gauge Fields on a Lattice#

For a tight-binding model on a lattice, the Peierls substitution incorporates a gauge field by modifying the hopping amplitudes:

\[ c_j^\dagger c_{j+1} \to \exp\left[\frac{\mathrm{i}q}{\hbar c}\int_j^{j+1}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\right] c_j^\dagger c_{j+1}\]

This gauge-invariant coupling naturally incorporates magnetic effects into condensed matter models.

Example: Lattice Electrons in a Magnetic Field

In a 2D square lattice with nearest-neighbor hopping \(t\), applying a perpendicular magnetic field creates a spatial modulation of phases. The Peierls substitution correctly captures this, leading to fractal energy spectra (Hofstadter butterfly) and eventually to integer and fractional quantum Hall effects at strong fields.

Universal Properties: Independence of Microscopic Details#

Some quantities are so fundamental that they depend only on topological (global) properties, not microscopic details. This topological robustness is a hallmark of gauge-invariant physics.

Topological Protection of Observables

Consider the integer quantum Hall conductance \(\sigma = ne^2/h\) for \(n\) filled Landau levels. This is:

  • Independent of disorder (within each Landau level; disorder causes broadening but not reduction of plateaus)

  • Independent of microscopic details (material composition, electron density variations, sample shape)

  • Quantized to integer multiples of \(e^2/h\)

Why? Because the Hall conductance is fundamentally a topological invariant—the integrated Berry curvature over the Brillouin zone. Gauge invariance ensures that only topological properties affect universal quantities.

This is why quantum Hall plateaus are so robust and have become standards for electrical resistance calibration worldwide.

Gauge Invariance of the Schrödinger Equation Revisited#

Finally, let’s verify explicitly that the Schrödinger equation itself is gauge-covariant.

Derivation: Covariance of the Schrödinger Equation

Suppose \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) satisfies:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \left[\frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 + q\Phi\right]\psi\]

Under gauge transformation \(\psi \to \psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi/(\hbar c)}\psi\) with \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi\) and \(\Phi \to \Phi' = \Phi + \partial_t\chi/q\):

The time derivative becomes:

\[ \frac{\partial\psi'}{\partial t} = \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar c}\frac{\partial\chi}{\partial t}\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi/(\hbar c)}\psi + \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi/(\hbar c)}\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t}\]

The spatial derivative involves:

\[ (\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}')\psi' = \left[\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q(\boldsymbol{A} - \nabla\chi)\right]\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi/(\hbar c)}\psi\]

After careful calculation (collecting terms from the product rule), the \(\chi\) terms cancel perfectly, and \(\psi'\) satisfies the equation with the primed potentials.

The Schrödinger equation has the same form in all gauges—it is manifestly gauge-covariant.

Summary#

  • Different gauge choices simplify different problems: Coulomb gauge for atoms, Landau gauge for Landau levels, symmetric gauge for cylindrical symmetry.

  • Gauge-invariant quantities (energy, transition rates, flux) are observable; gauge-dependent quantities (\(\boldsymbol{A}\), \(\Phi\), individual phases) are not.

  • Wilson loops and topological invariants encode gauge-invariant information about enclosed flux and field configurations.

  • Peierls substitution incorporates gauge fields into lattice models in a gauge-invariant way.

  • Topological robustness explains why integer quantum Hall plateaus and other universal features are independent of microscopic disorder and details.

  • The Schrödinger equation is gauge-covariant: it has identical form in all gauges, confirming that physics is gauge-invariant.

Homework#

1. Suppose an electron (charge \(-e\)) propagates in a region with uniform vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A} = (0, Bx, 0)\) (Landau gauge, \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\)). Use a gauge transformation \(\chi(\boldsymbol{r}) = -Bxy\) to transform to the symmetric gauge \(\boldsymbol{A}' = \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{B} \times \boldsymbol{r}\). Show explicitly that:

  • The magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) is unchanged.

  • The scalar potential transforms correctly: \(\Phi' = \Phi - \partial_t \chi\).

  • If \(\Phi = 0\) (no electric field), does the transformation affect the physics described by the time-dependent Schrödinger equation?

2. A wavefunction \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) transforms under a gauge transformation as:

\[\psi'(\boldsymbol{r}, t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi(\boldsymbol{r}, t)/\hbar c}\psi(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\]

Show that the probability density \(|\psi'(\boldsymbol{r}, t)|^2\) is gauge-invariant (i.e., unchanged under the transformation). Explain physically why this must be true.

3. Gauge-Invariant vs Gauge-Dependent Observables: Classify each of the following as either gauge-invariant (observable) or gauge-dependent (unobservable), and briefly explain why:

  • (a) The vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\) at a point.

  • (b) The probability current \(\boldsymbol{j}(\boldsymbol{r}) = \text{Re}[\psi^*\nabla\psi] - \frac{q}{mc}|\psi|^2\boldsymbol{A}\).

  • (c) The energy expectation value \(\langle H \rangle\).

  • (d) The circulation \(\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\) around a closed loop \(C\).

  • (e) The relative phase of the wavefunction at two points: \(\arg[\psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1)] - \arg[\psi(\boldsymbol{r}_2)]\).

4. Wilson Loop and Enclosed Flux: Consider a rectangular loop of size \(a \times b\) in the \(xy\)-plane, with corners at \((0,0)\), \((a, 0)\), \((a, b)\), \((0, b)\). The system is in a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\). Use the Landau gauge \(\boldsymbol{A} = (0, Bx, 0)\) to compute the Wilson loop:

\[W(C) = \exp\left[\frac{\mathrm{i} q}{\hbar c}\oint_C \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\right]\]

Express your result in terms of the magnetic flux \(\Phi_B = ab \cdot B\) through the loop. Does the result depend on the gauge choice?

5. Peierls Phase on a Lattice: Consider a tight-binding model on a 1D chain with hopping amplitude \(-t\). A uniform vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is turned on, so each hopping acquires a Peierls phase:

\[\text{Hopping term: } c_j^\dagger c_{j+1} \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi_{j,j+1}} c_j^\dagger c_{j+1}\]

where \(\phi_{j,j+1} = \frac{q}{\hbar c} \int_j^{j+1} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\). If all hoppings are to nearest neighbors with spacing \(a\), show that the total kinetic energy:

\[E = -t \sum_j \left( \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi_j} c_j^\dagger c_{j+1} + \text{h.c.} \right)\]

is gauge-invariant under a uniform gauge transformation \(\chi(j) = \chi_0\) (constant shift of \(\chi\) at each site). Is it invariant under a position-dependent gauge transformation?

6. Coulomb vs Lorenz Gauge: In the Coulomb gauge (\(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\)), the scalar potential \(\Phi\) satisfies Poisson’s equation \(\nabla^2\Phi = -\rho/\epsilon_0\) and gives the instantaneous Coulomb potential. In the Lorenz gauge (\(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \frac{1}{c^2}\partial_t\Phi = 0\)), both potentials obey wave equations.

  • (a) Why is the Coulomb gauge more convenient for non-relativistic quantum mechanics with static charge distributions?

  • (b) For a time-dependent charge density (e.g., an oscillating dipole), explain why the Lorenz gauge is more natural from a relativistic perspective.

  • (c) Despite these differences, argue that any physical prediction (energy spectrum, transition rates, etc.) must be independent of gauge choice.

7. Gauge Invariance of Landau Levels: In 2D with a uniform magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\), the single-particle Hamiltonian in the Symmetric gauge is:

\[H = \frac{1}{2m}\left[\left(\hat{p}_x - \frac{qBy}{2c}\right)^2 + \left(\hat{p}_y + \frac{qBx}{2c}\right)^2\right]\]

Show that this can be rewritten in terms of ladder operators (harmonic oscillator), yielding Landau levels \(E_n = \hbar\omega_c(n + 1/2)\) with \(\omega_c = qB/(mc)\). Then argue that the existence and spacing of these energy levels are gauge-invariant properties, even though the intermediate expressions for ladder operators differ in other gauges.

8. Aharonov-Bohm Effect and Gauge Invariance: An electron with charge \(q\) follows two paths around a solenoid (containing flux \(\Phi_B\) but no field outside). The two paths have lengths \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) and encircle the solenoid in opposite directions. Both paths have vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) pointing along the solenoid axis (outside the solenoid, \(\boldsymbol{B} = 0\) but \(\boldsymbol{A} \neq 0\)).

  • (a) Calculate the path-dependent phase acquired by each arm: \(\phi_1 = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\int_{L_1}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\) and \(\phi_2 = \frac{q}{\hbar c}\int_{L_2}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{l}\).

  • (b) Show that the phase difference \(\phi_1 - \phi_2\) equals \(\frac{q\Phi_B}{\hbar c}\) and is gauge-invariant.

  • (c) Explain why the interference pattern depends on the enclosed flux (a gauge-invariant quantity) rather than the detailed form of \(\boldsymbol{A}\) (which is gauge-dependent).

  • (d) Is the observable fringe shift \(\Delta\theta \propto \Phi_B\) affected if you choose a different gauge to describe the same physical flux?

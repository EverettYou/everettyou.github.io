4.3.3 Quantum Hall Effect#

Prompts

  • What is the filling factor \(\nu\)? How does it relate the number of electrons to the number of flux quanta?

  • Derive the integer quantum Hall conductance \(\sigma_H = \nu e^2/h\). Why is it quantized in integer units?

  • Explain why the Hall conductance is insensitive to disorder and impurities. What is the role of edge states?

  • How does current flow in the quantum Hall regime? Why are bulk states localized while edge states are extended?

  • What is the topological reason for quantization? How does the Chern number enter?

Lecture Notes#

The quantum Hall effect (QHE) is one of the most celebrated discoveries in condensed matter physics. It demonstrates that in a strong perpendicular magnetic field, the Hall conductance becomes quantized in units of \(e^2/h\), achieving values independent of microscopic details like sample purity and geometry. This unexpected robustness points to deep topological structure underlying the effect.

Integer Quantum Hall Effect (IQHE)#

Experimental Setup and Observation#

A 2D electron gas (a thin layer of free electrons confined to move in a plane) is placed in a perpendicular magnetic field \(B\). Current \(I\) flows in one direction (the \(x\)-direction), and the transverse voltage \(V_y\) (perpendicular to current) is measured.

In a classical conductor, Ohm’s law gives \(V_y = R_H I\) where \(R_H\) is the Hall resistance. The classical Hall coefficient is:

\[ R_H^{\text{cl}} = \frac{B}{ne}\]

Crucially, \(R_H^{\text{cl}}\) is continuous in \(B\) and depends on the carrier density \(n\).

The quantum surprise: In a strong magnetic field at low temperature, \(R_H\) jumps to quantized values:

Integer Quantum Hall Conductance

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \nu \frac{e^2}{h}, \quad R_H = \frac{h}{\nu e^2}\]

where \(\nu = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) is the filling factor, an integer.

The conductance is exactly quantized—independent of disorder, sample geometry, and other microscopic details.

Filling Factor and Magnetic Length#

The filling factor is the ratio of electrons to flux quanta:

Filling Factor

\[ \nu = \frac{N_e}{N_\phi}\]

where \(N_e\) is the number of electrons and \(N_\phi = BA/\Phi_0 = BA\cdot e/h\) is the number of flux quanta piercing the sample.

For integer \(\nu\), exactly \(\nu\) Landau levels are filled.

When \(\nu\) is an integer, the Fermi level lies in a gap between filled and empty Landau levels. This gap (the cyclotron gap) has size \(\hbar\omega_c\) and is crucial for the robustness of the effect.

Hall Conductance Derivation#

Consider a filled Landau level with \(N_{\phi}\) electrons. Each electron carries charge \(-e\). A current \(I\) flowing through the sample means charges move with drift velocity. The Hall field arises from the Lorentz force:

\[ \boldsymbol{F} = -e\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}\]

At steady state, the electric field \(E_y\) balances this force to prevent further acceleration. The relation between Hall field and current gives:

Derivation: Hall Conductance

For a filled Landau level in a Hall bar of width \(W\) and length \(L\):

  • Current \(I\) means \(I/e\) electrons per unit time cross a transverse section.

  • These electrons are deflected by \(\boldsymbol{B}\), creating a charge accumulation on one edge.

  • The resulting electric field \(E_y\) creates a force that exactly cancels the Lorentz force for the drifting electrons.

The Hall voltage is \(V_y = E_y \cdot W\). From the balance of forces and the number of filled levels:

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \frac{I}{V_y/L} = \frac{e^2 h}{h} \cdot \nu = \nu \frac{e^2}{h}\]

The result depends only on \(\nu\) (the number of filled Landau levels) and fundamental constants—not on \(B\), \(n\), or sample properties.

Robustness and Topological Origin#

Why Disorder Doesn’t Destroy Quantization#

Intuitively, disorder should spoil any delicate quantum effect. Yet the QHE conductance is observed to be quantized to parts per billion precision, even in very dirty samples.

The key is the Landau level gap: Disorder cannot mix states within a single Landau level if the energy cost is \(\hbar\omega_c\). Electrons in a filled level cannot scatter into empty states above the gap. Thus:

Disorder Robustness

  • The quantized Hall conductance is topologically protected: it depends only on the topology of the filled band structure, not microscopic details.

  • Disorder can localize states deep in the Landau level bulk, but edge states remain delocalized.

  • Edge states carry the current; their number is fixed by the topology, independent of disorder.

Edge States#

A real sample has boundaries. Near the boundary, the potential energy is not uniform—it rises toward the edge (to confine electrons). This confining potential shifts Landau level energies, causing them to fan upward toward the boundary. The result: some states near the edge have energy within the bulk gap.

These edge states are topological: they are guaranteed to exist at the boundary of a filled Landau level, and they remain intact even with disorder.

Edge State Properties

  • Edge states are chiral: they propagate in one direction only (set by \(\boldsymbol{E} \times \boldsymbol{B}\) drift).

  • Each edge state carries exactly one quantum \(e^2/h\) of conductance.

  • The total Hall conductance equals the number of chiral edge states times \(e^2/h\).

  • Disorder cannot backscatter an electron along the edge without energy cost \(\sim \hbar\omega_c\).

Discussion

The statement “disorder cannot mix states within a single Landau level if the energy cost is \(\hbar\omega_c\)” is powerful, but requires careful thinking. Consider an electron in a filled Landau level (all states occupied) with a small random potential \(V(\boldsymbol{r})\) due to impurities.

(a) If the electron scatters off an impurity, can it scatter into an empty state above the current Landau level (i.e., into the next Landau level)? What is the minimum energy it must gain to do so?

(b) Within the current filled Landau level, are there empty states available into which the electron could scatter? If all states are filled, what does the Pauli exclusion principle forbid?

(c) Explain carefully why “the quantized Hall conductance is topologically protected”: How does the absence of scattering channels (due to the Landau level gap and Pauli blocking) guarantee that edge states remain delocalized even with strong disorder?

(d) What happens if the disorder potential has regions where \(V(\boldsymbol{r}) > \hbar\omega_c\)? Can such strong disorder create a scattering channel into the next Landau level?

Laughlin’s Wavefunction and Fractionality#

For the integer case (\(\nu\) integer), all electrons fit neatly into \(\nu\) filled Landau levels. But what happens at fractional fillings, like \(\nu = 1/3\)?

At fractional fillings, electron-electron interactions become essential. The ground state is a highly entangled many-body state with long-range order. Laughlin proposed an elegant wavefunction for \(\nu = 1/m\) (where \(m\) is odd):

\[ \Psi_{\text{Laughlin}} = \prod_{i<j} (z_i - z_j)^m \exp\left(-\frac{1}{4\ell_B^2}\sum_i |z_i|^2\right)\]

This wavefunction describes electrons with filling \(\nu = 1/m\). Its key features are:

Laughlin Wavefunction Insights

  • The factor \((z_i - z_j)^m\) enforces that electrons repel: the closer two electrons, the more suppressed the wavefunction.

  • The state is a quantum fluid—incompressible, with a gap to excitations.

  • Excitations are fractional quasiparticles carrying charge \(e/m\) (and fractional statistics).

  • Laughlin’s picture extends to the fractional quantum Hall effect (FQHE), where \(\nu\) is a rational fraction.

Experimental Significance and Modern Applications#

The quantum Hall effect had enormous impact:

Historical and Practical Importance

  • Discovery (1980): Von Klitzing discovered the IQHE and won the Nobel Prize in 1985.

  • Precision metrology: QHE provides a standard for electrical resistance, independent of microscopic details. It defines the ohm in the SI system.

  • Topological quantum matter: QHE is the prototypical topological phase—a state of matter with no classical analog, protected by global constraints rather than symmetry.

  • Modern research: QHE connections to topological insulators, topological superconductors, and anyons continue to drive fundamental physics and quantum information science.

Key Results: Quantum Hall Effect

  • Integer QHE: Hall conductance \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu e^2/h\) is exactly quantized at integer filling \(\nu\).

  • Filling factor: \(\nu = N_e/N_\phi\); integer \(\nu\) means \(\nu\) Landau levels are completely filled.

  • Robustness: Quantization is independent of disorder, sample geometry, and microscopic details.

  • Origin: Landau level gap and topological edge states ensure current is carried by delocalized chiral edge modes.

  • Fractional QHE: At fractional fillings, interactions create incompressible quantum fluids with fractional charge excitations.

  • Significance: Canonical example of topological phases; defines the ohm in SI metrology.

Homework#

Problem 1: Filling Factor and Flux Quanta

A 2D electron gas contains \(N_e = 2.4 \times 10^{11}\) electrons. The sample is square with side \(L = 1\,\text{mm}\). A perpendicular magnetic field \(B = 5\,\text{T}\) is applied.

(a) Calculate the number of flux quanta \(N_\phi = \Phi_{\text{total}}/\Phi_0\) through the sample, where \(\Phi_0 = h/e\) is the flux quantum.

(b) Find the filling factor \(\nu = N_e/N_\phi\).

(c) How many Landau levels are completely filled? Is the system in an integer quantum Hall regime?

Problem 2: Hall Conductance and Conductance Quantum

At filling factor \(\nu = 3\) (three completely filled Landau levels), the sample exhibits the quantum Hall effect.

(a) Calculate the Hall conductance \(\sigma_H\) in SI units (S = siemens). Use \(e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}\) and \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{J·s}\).

(b) Express your answer as a multiple of the conductance quantum \(e^2/h \approx 77.5\,\mu\text{S}\).

(c) What is the Hall resistance \(\rho_H = 1/\sigma_H\)? (This is often called the von Klitzing resistance for \(\nu = 1\).)

Problem 3: Topological Protection and Disorder

Consider two quantum Hall samples at \(\nu = 1\): one ultra-pure (mean free path \(\ell \gg \ell_B\), where \(\ell_B = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\) is the magnetic length) and one with strong disorder (mean free path \(\ell \sim \ell_B\)).

(a) Explain qualitatively why both samples exhibit the same quantized Hall conductance \(\sigma_H = e^2/h\), despite vastly different disorder levels.

(b) In the pure sample, do bulk electrons contribute to Hall transport? Why or why not?

(c) In the disordered sample, are bulk Landau level states extended or localized? Explain how this is consistent with the edge state picture.

Problem 4: Edge States and Conductance

When \(\nu = 2\) Landau levels are completely filled, the sample has two edge state branches propagating along its boundary.

(a) Each edge branch acts like a 1D quantum wire carrying current. What is the conductance of a single edge branch in units of \(e^2/h\)?

(b) If there are two independent edge branches, what is the total Hall conductance? Write it in the form \(\sigma_H = \nu \times (e^2/h)\).

(c) Explain why disorder along the edge does not destroy the edge state conductance (even if edge roughness or impurities are present).

Problem 5: Chern Number and Topological Invariant

The Hall conductance is related to a topological property of the electron band: the Chern number

\[ C = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}}\mathrm{d}^2k\, \Omega(\boldsymbol{k}),\]

where \(\Omega(\boldsymbol{k})\) is the Berry curvature. The relation is \(\sigma_H = C \times (e^2/h)\).

(a) For a filled Landau level, the Chern number is \(C = \pm 1\). What is the Chern number for \(\nu = 5\) (five filled Landau levels)? Assume each Landau level contributes equally.

(b) Why is the Chern number called a “topological” invariant? (Hint: Can it change if you continuously deform the band structure without closing the gap?)

(c) A Chern number of \(C = 5\) means there must be exactly 5 chiral edge modes—edge states that propagate in only one direction around the sample boundary. Use this to re-derive the Hall conductance formula \(\sigma_H = \nu e^2/h\) in terms of edge modes.

Problem 6: Charge Pumping

The quantum Hall effect can be understood as a charge pumping effect. As the magnetic field increases by \(\Delta\Phi = \Phi_0\) (one flux quantum), exactly \(\nu\) electrons are “pumped” from the bulk to the edge (or equivalently, exit the sample).

(a) If the sample has area \(A\) and the magnetic field increases from \(B\) to \(B + \Delta B\), the flux through the sample changes by \(\Delta\Phi = A \cdot \Delta B\). How many flux quanta correspond to this change?

(b) At \(\nu = 1\), how many electrons are pumped out when \(\Delta\Phi = \Phi_0\)?

(c) This pumping process is adiabatic—the system evolves slowly enough that the Landau level gap is always maintained. Why is the Landau level gap important for ensuring quantized pumping?

Problem 7: Longitudinal vs. Hall Conductivity

In the quantum Hall regime, the conductivity tensor takes the form:

\[\begin{split} \hat{\sigma} = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \sigma_{xy} \\ -\sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{yy} \end{pmatrix},\end{split}\]

where \(\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy}\) (longitudinal) and \(\sigma_{xy}\) (Hall).

(a) In the quantum Hall regime at \(\nu = 1\), which component (\(\sigma_{xx}\) or \(\sigma_{xy}\)) is quantized? Which is approximately zero?

(b) A classical conductor in a magnetic field should have \(\sigma_{xx} > 0\) and \(\sigma_{xy} \neq 0\). Explain physically why the quantum Hall system behaves so differently (perfect insulator in the \(x\)-direction, but perfect quantized conductance in the \(y\)-direction).

(c) An applied electric field \(E_x\) drives a current. Sketch the current direction (\(J_x\) and \(J_y\)) and explain what is carrying it.

Problem 8: Robustness and Universal Measurement

The integer quantum Hall effect is measured to a precision of better than 1 part in \(10^9\). This precision is not limited by material quality, impurities, or sample geometry—only by fundamental physics. This universality is why the IQHE is used to define the standard of electrical resistance.

(a) Identify the three key properties of the quantum Hall state that make it so robust to perturbations: (i) one about disorder, (ii) one about the bulk vs. edge distinction, and (iii) one about topological protection.

(b) Suppose the Landau level spacing \(\hbar\omega_c\) is very small (weak magnetic field). Can we still observe a clean integer quantum Hall plateau at \(\nu = 1\)? Why or why not?

(c) Why must the sample be genuinely 2D (not 3D) for the quantum Hall effect to occur? (Hint: How does the number of extended edge states change if disorder or finite thickness allows 3D localization?)

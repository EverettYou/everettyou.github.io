4.3.3 Quantum Hall Effect#

Prompts

  • What is the filling factor \(\nu\)? How does it relate the number of electrons to flux quanta?

  • Explain Laughlin’s charge pumping argument: how does threading a flux quantum through a Corbino disk quantize the Hall conductance?

  • At fractional filling, why does the Hall conductance become \(\sigma_{xy} = (p/q) e^2/h\)? What is an anyonic quasiparticle, and how do anyons and fractional statistics emerge at fractional filling?

  • Why is the quantum Hall conductance robust to disorder and sample impurities, despite the apparent delicacy of quantum effects?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The quantum Hall effect reveals one of the most robust and universal phenomena in condensed matter physics. When a 2D electron gas is placed in a strong perpendicular magnetic field, the Hall conductance becomes quantized in units of \(e^2/h\). Integer quantum Hall effect (IQHE) occurs when Landau levels are completely filled; fractional quantum Hall effect (FQHE) occurs at rational filling factors, where interactions dominate. The charge pumping argument elegantly shows why quantization emerges and connects gauge physics to topological properties.

Filling Landau Levels#

In a perpendicular magnetic field \(B\), electron energy levels organize into Landau levels, each with degeneracy \(N_\phi = BA\Phi_0^{-1} = BAe/h\), where \(A\) is the sample area.

Filling Factor

The filling factor is

\[ \nu = \frac{N_e}{N_\phi} \]

where \(N_e\) is the number of electrons and \(N_\phi\) is the number of flux quanta. When \(\nu\) is an integer, exactly \(\nu\) Landau levels are completely filled, and the Fermi level lies in a gap. The energy cost to excite an electron across the gap is \(\hbar\omega_c\) (cyclotron energy), which is crucial for protecting quantization against disorder.

Charge Pumping Argument#

The most elegant derivation uses Laughlin’s charge pumping picture. Consider a Corbino disk: a 2D sample shaped like an annulus, with inner and outer edges. Imagine slowly threading a single flux quantum \(\Phi_0 = h/e\) through the hole, adiabatically, while maintaining filled Landau levels. As the flux increases, \(N_\phi\) increases by exactly one.

Key insight: If \(N_e\) is fixed and \(N_\phi\) increases by 1, the system must adjust. Since one Landau level is completely filled and a new flux quantum appears, one electron must be “pumped” out to make room. This pumping occurs adiabatically at the edge.

Charge Pumping and Quantized Conductance

When one flux quantum \(\Phi_0\) is threaded through the sample at filling \(\nu\), exactly \(\nu\) electrons are pumped out. This pumping is adiabatic: the Landau level gap remains open, preventing level mixing.

For each pumped charge \(\nu e\), the Hall conductance is:

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \nu \frac{e^2}{h} \]

This is exact and depends only on \(\nu\), \(e\), \(h\)—not on disorder, sample geometry, or microscopic details. The quantization emerges from a global constraint (flux conservation and filling factor) combined with a gap that prevents local scattering from destroying the state.

Discussion

(a) Why is adiabaticity essential? What would happen if the flux were threaded quickly instead of slowly?

(b) Can strong disorder nucleate a scattering channel that would violate charge quantization?

(c) In real devices, explain how the pumped charge actually leaves the sample: through the bulk, or along the edge?

(d) For fractional fillings (e.g., \(\nu = 1/3\)), what is the physical meaning of pumping \(\nu = 1/3\) of an electron in one cycle?

Linear Response and Hall Conductance#

Apply a weak oscillatory electric field \(\boldsymbol{E}(t) = \boldsymbol{E}\,\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}(\omega + \mathrm{i}0^+)t}\) adiabatically switched on from the infinite past. First-order time-dependent perturbation theory yields the Kubo formula for the Hall conductivity, which gives the same quantization \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu\, e^2/h\) from a completely different approach.

Kubo Formula for Hall Conductivity

(69)#\[ \sigma_H = \frac{1}{\mathrm{i} N_\phi} \sum_{\substack{n,m \in \text{occ}}} \sum_{n' \neq n} \frac{\langle n,m \vert \hat{\pi}_x \vert n',m \rangle \langle n',m \vert \hat{\pi}_y \vert n,m \rangle - \text{h.c.}}{(E_{n'} - E_n)^2} \]

where \(\vert n,m\rangle\) are Landau level states, \(E_n = \hbar\omega_c(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\), and \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - e\boldsymbol{A}/c\) is the kinetic momentum.

Evaluating this sum gives \(\sigma_H = \nu \frac{e^2}{h}\): each occupied state contributes exactly \(1/N_\phi\) to the Hall conductivity.

Derivation: Kubo Formula Evaluation

In units \(m = e = \hbar = 1\), the Landau level Hamiltonian is \(\hat{H} = \tfrac{1}{2}\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^2\) with energies \(E_n = B(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\).

Setup: An oscillatory electric field \(\boldsymbol{E}(t) = \boldsymbol{E}\,\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}(\omega + \mathrm{i}0^+)t}\) corresponds to a vector potential perturbation

\[ \delta\boldsymbol{A}(t) = -\frac{\boldsymbol{E}}{\mathrm{i}\omega}\,\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}(\omega + \mathrm{i}0^+)t} \]

which perturbs \(\hat{H}\) as \(\delta\hat{H}(t) = -\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} \cdot \delta\boldsymbol{A}(t)\).

First-order perturbation: Time-dependent perturbation theory (Dyson series) gives the current density \(\langle \boldsymbol{j}(t)\rangle = \langle \boldsymbol{j}^{(0)}\rangle + \langle \boldsymbol{j}^{(1)}(t)\rangle\). The zeroth-order term vanishes because \(\langle n,m\vert\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\vert n,m\rangle = 0\) (\(\hat{\pi}_x, \hat{\pi}_y\) are off-diagonal in Landau level index). The first-order term yields:

\[ \sigma_{ji}(\omega) = \frac{\mathrm{i}B}{\omega N_\phi} \sum_{n,m \in \text{occ}} \sum_{n' \neq n} \left(\frac{\langle n,m\vert \hat{\pi}_j\vert n',m\rangle\langle n',m\vert \hat{\pi}_i\vert n,m\rangle}{\omega - E_n + E_{n'}} - \frac{\langle n,m\vert \hat{\pi}_i\vert n',m\rangle\langle n',m\vert \hat{\pi}_j\vert n,m\rangle}{\omega + E_n - E_{n'}}\right) \]

DC limit: Expand each denominator to first order in \(\omega\). The antisymmetric piece (contributing to Hall conductivity) is:

\[ \sigma_H = \frac{B}{\mathrm{i}N_\phi} \sum_{n,m \in \text{occ}} \sum_{n' \neq n} \frac{\langle n,m\vert \hat{\pi}_x\vert n',m\rangle\langle n',m\vert \hat{\pi}_y\vert n,m\rangle - \text{h.c.}}{(E_{n'} - E_n)^2} \]

Ladder operators: Using \(\hat{\pi}_x = \sqrt{B/2}\,(\hat{a}^\dagger + \hat{a})\) and \(\hat{\pi}_y = \mathrm{i}\sqrt{B/2}\,(\hat{a}^\dagger - \hat{a})\):

\[ \langle n,m\vert \hat{\pi}_x\vert n',m\rangle\langle n',m\vert \hat{\pi}_y\vert n,m\rangle = \frac{\mathrm{i}B}{2}\Big((n+1)\,\delta_{n', n+1} - n\,\delta_{n', n-1}\Big) \]

Since \(E_{n'} - E_n = B(n' - n)\), both denominators equal \(B^2\), so:

\[ \sigma_H = \frac{1}{N_\phi} \sum_{n,m \in \text{occ}} \Big((n+1) - n\Big) = \frac{1}{N_\phi} \sum_{n,m \in \text{occ}} 1 \]

For \(\nu\) completely filled Landau levels: \(\sigma_H = \nu\, e^2/h\). \(\checkmark\)

Discussion

Why do the charge pumping argument and the Kubo formula agree, despite using completely different reasoning?

  • Charge pumping is topological: it counts electron transfer when flux is threaded, independent of the microscopic Hamiltonian.

  • Kubo is dynamical: it sums current operator matrix elements between all occupied and unoccupied states.

What property of Landau levels guarantees that each level contributes exactly \(e^2/h\)?

Fractional Quantum Hall Effect#

At fractional fillings \(\nu = p/q\) (coprime integers), many-body electron-electron interactions become essential. The ground state is a highly entangled quantum fluid with no simple mean-field description.

Laughlin Wavefunction

For \(\nu = 1/m\) (odd \(m\)), Laughlin proposed:

\[ \Psi_{\text{Laughlin}} = \prod_{i<j} (z_i - z_j)^m \exp\left(-\frac{1}{4\ell_B^2}\sum_i |z_i|^2\right) \]

The factor \((z_i - z_j)^m\) enforces strong repulsion between electrons, creating an incompressible quantum fluid with a gap to excitations.

The charge pumping argument extends: when one flux quantum is added at \(\nu = p/q\), exactly \(p/q\) of an electron is pumped out. This fractional pumping is possible because the quantum fluid redistributes charge non-locally. The quasiparticles (hole excitations) carry fractional charge \(e/q\) and obey fractional statistics—the anyons from 2.3.2 Fractional Quantum Hall Anyons.

Hall Conductance at Fractional Filling

At fractional filling \(\nu = p/q\), the Hall conductance is:

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \nu \frac{e^2}{h} = \frac{p}{q} \frac{e^2}{h} \]

Quasiparticles carry charge \(\pm e/q\) and obey fractional Bragg statistics: exchanging two quasiparticles introduces a phase \(\exp(2\pi \mathrm{i} p/q)\).

Poll: Hall resistance quantization

In the quantum Hall effect, the Hall resistance is quantized as \(R_H = h / (q^2 \nu)\) where \(\nu\) is the filling factor (ratio of electrons to flux quanta). The remarkable feature is that \(R_H\) depends only on fundamental constants and the filling factor, independent of material details like the sample geometry or the magnetic field strength (over wide ranges). Which statement correctly explains this universal quantization?

(A) At integer filling factors, all Landau levels below a certain energy are completely filled, so the system is insulating and all electrons contribute equally to transport.

(B) The quantized nature of Landau levels means only a fixed number of electrons can carry current, regardless of other parameters.

(C) The Hall resistance is determined by topological properties of the electron states, which are robust against disorder and material variations.

(D) Both (A) and (C).

Summary#

  • Filling factor \(\nu = N_e/N_\phi\) counts filled Landau levels; integer \(\nu\) means a gap protects the state.

  • Charge pumping: Threading a flux quantum through an adiabatic cycle pumps out \(\nu\) electrons, giving exact quantization \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu e^2/h\).

  • Robustness comes from the Landau level gap: disorder cannot mix states within a filled level, and quantization depends only on global filling.

  • Fractional QHE at \(\nu = p/q\) produces incompressible quantum fluids with fractional charge and fractional statistics.

  • The QHE unifies gauge physics, topology, and many-body physics (Laughlin wavefunction and anyons).

Homework#

1. Filling factor. A 2D electron system with density \(n = 2.4\times 10^{15}\,\text{m}^{-2}\) is placed in a perpendicular field \(B = 5\,\text{T}\).

(a) Compute the number of flux quanta per unit area \(n_\phi = eB/h\) and the filling factor \(\nu = n/n_\phi\).

(b) How many Landau levels are completely filled? Is the system in an integer quantum Hall regime?

(c) At what magnetic field \(B^*\) would the filling factor be exactly \(\nu = 1\)? What about \(\nu = 1/3\)?

2. Edge state transport. At filling \(\nu\), there are \(\nu\) chiral edge branches propagating along the sample boundary.

(a) Each edge branch carries conductance \(e^2/h\). Derive the total Hall conductance \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu e^2/h\) for integer \(\nu\).

(b) Chiral means the edge states propagate in one direction only. Explain why backscattering is forbidden and why disorder along the edge does not destroy the quantized conductance.

(c) An edge state at filling \(\nu = 2\) encounters an impurity. Compare its behavior to a 1D wire with the same impurity. Why is the Hall plateau robust while the 1D wire shows Anderson localization?

3. Disorder and plateaus. The classical Hall conductance \(\sigma_{xy}^\text{cl} = ne/B\) is a smooth function of \(B\), but the quantum Hall conductance shows flat plateaus.

(a) Explain qualitatively how Landau level broadening by disorder creates localized states in the tails and extended states at the center of each level.

(b) As \(B\) increases and the filling factor passes through \(\nu = 2\), explain why \(\sigma_{xy}\) remains fixed at \(2e^2/h\) over a range of \(B\) rather than decreasing continuously.

(c) At what filling factors do transitions between plateaus occur? What role do the extended states at the Landau level centers play?

4. Charge pumping argument. Laughlin’s thought experiment threads flux \(\Phi\) through a cylindrical quantum Hall sample.

(a) When one flux quantum \(\Phi_0 = h/e\) is adiabatically inserted, the induced EMF drives a Hall current. Using \(\sigma_{xy} = \nu e^2/h\), show that exactly \(\nu\) electrons are pumped from one edge to the other.

(b) The pumped charge must be an integer (in units of \(e\)) because the system returns to its original state after inserting \(\Phi_0\). Use this to argue that \(\sigma_{xy}\) must be quantized as \(\nu e^2/h\) with integer \(\nu\).

(c) Extend the argument to fractional \(\nu = 1/3\): inserting one flux quantum pumps \(e/3\) of charge. What does this imply about the existence of fractionally charged quasiparticles?

5. Laughlin quasiparticles. At \(\nu = 1/3\), quasihole excitations carry charge \(e^* = e/3\).

(a) Use the charge pumping argument: threading one flux quantum through a \(\nu = 1/3\) droplet creates one quasihole. Show that its charge must be \(e/3\).

(b) For \(\nu = 2/5\), what would the quasiparticle charge be? What is the Hall conductance?

(c) The quasiparticles at \(\nu = 1/3\) obey fractional (anyonic) statistics: exchanging two quasiholes produces a phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\pi/3}\) instead of \(\pm 1\). Explain why this is neither bosonic nor fermionic, and why it is only possible in 2D.

6. Landau level gap. The energy gap between Landau levels is \(\hbar\omega_c = \hbar eB/m^*\).

(a) For GaAs (\(m^* = 0.067m_e\)) at \(B = 10\,\text{T}\), compute \(\hbar\omega_c\) in meV and the corresponding temperature \(T^* = \hbar\omega_c/k_B\).

(b) Explain why the integer QHE requires \(k_BT \ll \hbar\omega_c\) and why GaAs (small \(m^*\)) is preferred over silicon (\(m^* \approx 0.2m_e\)).

(c) Disorder broadens Landau levels by \(\Gamma\). For the QHE to be observable, we need \(\Gamma < \hbar\omega_c\). Estimate the maximum scattering rate \(1/\tau = \Gamma/\hbar\) and the minimum mobility \(\mu = e\tau/m^*\) required.

7. Von Klitzing constant. The quantum of resistance \(R_K = h/e^2 \approx 25.8\,\text{k}\Omega\) is known to extraordinary precision.

(a) The metrological value of the QHE is that \(R_K\) depends only on fundamental constants, not on material properties. Why is the Hall resistance at \(\nu = 1\) exactly \(R_K\), independent of sample geometry, material, or disorder?

(b) Before 2019, the SI ohm was defined using a physical artifact. Explain how the QHE now provides a resistance standard.

(c) The fine structure constant is \(\alpha = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar c)\). Show that \(R_K = h/e^2 = 1/(2\alpha\epsilon_0 c)\). How does a precision measurement of \(R_K\) constrain \(\alpha\)?

8. Composite fermions. The fractional QHE at \(\nu = p/(2ps + 1)\) can be understood by attaching \(2s\) flux quanta to each electron, forming composite fermions (CFs).

(a) At \(\nu = 1/3\), each electron binds two flux quanta. Show that the effective magnetic field seen by CFs is \(B^* = B - 2n\Phi_0\) and that CFs fill exactly one effective Landau level (\(\nu^* = 1\)).

(b) The sequence \(\nu = 1/3, 2/5, 3/7, \ldots\) corresponds to CFs filling \(p = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) effective levels. Derive the general formula \(\nu = p/(2p+1)\).

(c) At \(\nu = 1/2\), the effective field \(B^* = 0\) and CFs form a Fermi sea rather than filling Landau levels. What experimental signature would distinguish this composite fermion Fermi sea from an ordinary electron Fermi sea?

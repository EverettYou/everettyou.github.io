4.1.1 Gauge Principle#

Prompts

  • We know global phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\psi\) is unobservable. What goes wrong when you try to make \(\alpha\) depend on position? Why does the ordinary derivative \(\partial_i\) fail?

  • What is the covariant derivative \(D_i = \partial_i - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\), and why does it fix the problem? Walk me through the cancellation when both \(\psi\) and \(\boldsymbol{A}\) transform.

  • Explain why demanding local phase invariance forces the kinetic energy to take the form \(\frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2\). Is the coupling to \(\boldsymbol{A}\) an assumption or a consequence?

  • Why is \(\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) the kinetic momentum? Show that it equals \(m \boldsymbol{v}\).

  • Expand \((\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2\) and identify the paramagnetic and diamagnetic terms. What is the Coulomb gauge, and why does it simplify things?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Global phase is unobservable (§1.1.1). What happens when we promote the phase to a function of position? The Schrödinger equation breaks—and fixing it forces us to introduce a gauge field and a new kind of derivative.

From Global to Local#

Under a global phase rotation \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\psi\) (\(\alpha\) constant), all observables are unchanged—this is the U(1) global symmetry (§1.1.1). If physics is truly local, the description should not depend on a choice of phase convention at each point. This is the local phase ambiguity.

Promote \(\alpha\) to a function of position:

\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{r}) \to \psi'(\boldsymbol{r}) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})}\psi(\boldsymbol{r}) \]

The probability density is still invariant:

\[ |\psi'(\boldsymbol{r})|^2 = |\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})}\psi(\boldsymbol{r})|^2 = |\psi(\boldsymbol{r})|^2 \]

But check the Schrödinger equation \(\hat{H}\psi = E\psi\) with \(\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\). We say an equation is covariant under a transformation if it keeps the same form. For a global phase, \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\) factors through \(\nabla^2\) and cancels—covariance holds. For a local phase, the derivative picks up an extra term:

\[ \partial_i\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\left(\partial_i + \mathrm{i}\,\partial_i\alpha\right)\psi \]

The gradient of \(\alpha\) does not cancel. The Laplacian is worse:

\[ \nabla^2\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\left[\nabla^2 + 2\mathrm{i}(\nabla\alpha) \cdot \nabla + \mathrm{i}(\nabla^2\alpha) - (\nabla\alpha) \cdot (\nabla\alpha)\right]\psi \]

The Gauge Problem

Under \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})}\psi\), the Schrödinger equation is not covariant:

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi' \neq \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi\right) \]

Global phase invariance does not extend to local phase invariance. If we want local phase to be as unobservable as global phase, the theory must be modified.

The Covariant Derivative#

The extra terms come from \(\partial_i\) acting on \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\). The fix: introduce a vector field \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\)—the gauge field—and define the covariant derivative

\[ D_i = \partial_i - \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}A_i \]

where \(q\) is the particle’s charge. If we require \(\boldsymbol{A}\) to shift as

\[ \boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \frac{\hbar}{q}\nabla\alpha \]

whenever we rotate \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi\), then the shift in \(\boldsymbol{A}\) cancels the unwanted gradient. This compensating shift is called a gauge transformation.

Covariant Derivative

Under \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi\) and \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\alpha\):

\[ D_i\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}(D_i\psi) \]

The covariant derivative transforms like \(\psi\) itself—unlike the ordinary derivative \(\partial_i\).

Derivation: Covariance of \(D_i\)

Under \(\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi\) and \(\boldsymbol{A}' = \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\alpha\):

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} D_i'\psi' &= \left(\partial_i - \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}(A_i + \partial_i\alpha)\right)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi \\ &= \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\left(\mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}\partial_i\alpha\right)\psi + \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\partial_i\psi - \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}A_i\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi - \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}(\partial_i\alpha)\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi \end{split} \end{split}\]

The first and last terms cancel:

\[ D_i'\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\left(\partial_i - \mathrm{i}\frac{q}{\hbar}A_i\right)\psi = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}(D_i\psi) \]

Gauge-Invariant Hamiltonian#

Replace every \(\partial_i\) with \(D_i\)—the minimal coupling prescription:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla \;\longrightarrow\; \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\boldsymbol{D} \]
\[ \hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 + V(\boldsymbol{r}) \]

Since \(D_i\psi\) transforms like \(\psi\), so does \(D_iD_j\psi\), and the Schrödinger equation with this Hamiltonian is covariant under local gauge transformations.

The Gauge Principle

Requiring local phase covariance forces:

  1. A gauge field \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\) with \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\alpha\).

  2. The covariant derivative \(D_i = \partial_i - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\).

  3. The kinetic energy \(\frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2\).

The coupling to \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is a consequence of the symmetry, not an assumption.

Kinetic Momentum#

The combination \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}\) that appears in the Hamiltonian has a direct physical meaning. From Hamilton’s equation \(\dot{\boldsymbol{r}} = \partial H / \partial \boldsymbol{p}\):

\[ m\dot{\boldsymbol{r}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} \]

so \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\) is the kinetic momentum—it equals \(m\boldsymbol{v}\), the mass times velocity. The canonical momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\) includes an additional contribution \(q\boldsymbol{A}\) from the gauge field. The distinction between canonical and kinetic momentum, and which quantities are gauge-invariant, will be discussed in §4.1.3.

Expansion of the Kinetic Energy

Problem. Expand \((\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2\) and identify the couplings.

Solution.

\[ (\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2 - q(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}) + q^2\boldsymbol{A}^2 \]

Using \(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar\nabla\):

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} + 2\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \nabla) \]

In the Coulomb gauge \(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A} = 0\):

\[ \frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2}{2m} - \frac{q}{m}\boldsymbol{A} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} + \frac{q^2}{2m}\boldsymbol{A}^2 \]

The linear term is the paramagnetic coupling; the quadratic term is the diamagnetic coupling.

Poll: Local phase and the ordinary derivative

Under \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}) \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})}\psi(\boldsymbol{r})\), the ordinary derivative transforms as \(\partial_i\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}(\partial_i + \mathrm{i}\partial_i\alpha)\psi\). What is the role of \(A_i\) in the covariant derivative \(D_i = \partial_i - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\)?

(A) \(A_i\) shifts as \(A_i \to A_i + (\hbar/q)\partial_i\alpha\), cancelling the extra \(\mathrm{i}(\partial_i\alpha)\psi\) term so that \(D_i\psi\) transforms like \(\psi\).

(B) \(A_i\) makes the wavefunction real-valued, eliminating the need for complex phases.

(C) \(A_i\) ensures that \(\partial_i\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\partial_i\psi\) without any additional terms.

(D) \(A_i\) is a mathematical convenience with no physical consequence—it can always be set to zero.

Summary#

  • Local phase ambiguity (motivated by local frame independence) breaks the eigenvalue equation because \(\partial_i\) acting on \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha}\) generates extra terms.

  • The covariant derivative \(D_i = \partial_i - \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\), with \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\alpha\), transforms like \(\psi\) and restores covariance.

  • The gauge principle: local phase covariance forces \(\hat{H} = \frac{1}{2m}(\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2 + V\).

  • The kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A} = m\boldsymbol{v}\) is the physical momentum that determines velocity.

Homework#

1. Gauge transform of a wavefunction. A particle has wavefunction \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi a_0^3}}\mathrm{e}^{-r/a_0}\) (hydrogen ground state) in a region with vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\). A gauge transformation with \(\alpha(\boldsymbol{r}) = k x\) is applied.

(a) Write down the transformed wavefunction \(\psi'(\boldsymbol{r})\).

(b) Verify that the probability density is unchanged: \(\vert\psi'(\boldsymbol{r})\vert^2 = \vert\psi(\boldsymbol{r})\vert^2\).

(c) How does the vector potential transform: \(\boldsymbol{A}'= ?\) Write \(\boldsymbol{A}' - \boldsymbol{A}\) explicitly for this choice of \(\alpha\).

2. Covariant derivative check. The covariant derivative is \(D_i = \partial_i + \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\).

(a) For a 1D gauge transformation \(\psi'(x) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha(x)/\hbar}\psi(x)\), \(A' = A + \partial_x\alpha\), show by direct computation that \(D'_x\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}D_x\psi\). This confirms covariance.

(b) Show that the ordinary derivative \(\partial_x\psi'\) does NOT transform covariantly — it picks up an extra term proportional to \(\partial_x\alpha\).

(c) Explain in one sentence why the covariant derivative is needed to make the kinetic energy gauge-invariant.

3. Gauging a plane wave. A free particle has wavefunction \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{r}}\) (a momentum eigenstate with \(\boldsymbol{p} = \hbar\boldsymbol{k}\)). A spatially varying gauge transformation \(\alpha(\boldsymbol{r}) = \boldsymbol{c}\cdot\boldsymbol{r}\) is applied, where \(\boldsymbol{c}\) is a constant vector.

(a) Write the new wavefunction \(\psi'\). Show it is still a plane wave but with a shifted wavevector.

(b) What is the new canonical momentum \(\hbar\boldsymbol{k}'\)? Is it the same as the old one?

(c) Compute the kinetic momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}\psi' = (\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A}')\psi'\) and show it equals the original kinetic momentum applied to \(\psi\) and then gauge-transformed. This confirms kinetic momentum is gauge-invariant.

4. Coulomb gauge expansion. Expand \((\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2/(2m)\) and use the Coulomb gauge condition \(\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{A} = 0\) to show that the Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}^2}{2m} - \frac{q}{m}\boldsymbol{A}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{p}} + \frac{q^2\boldsymbol{A}^2}{2m} + q\Phi \]

For a hydrogen atom in a weak uniform magnetic field, estimate the ratio of the \(\boldsymbol{A}^2\) term to the \(\boldsymbol{A}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\) term. Under what conditions can \(\boldsymbol{A}^2\) be neglected?

5. Gravity as a gauge theory. Consider a particle in a gravitational potential \(V(\boldsymbol{r}) = mgz\). One might claim: “I can remove the gravitational potential by a local phase transformation, just like removing a vector potential by a gauge transformation.”

(a) Try to find \(\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})\) that eliminates \(V\) from the Schrödinger equation. Where does the attempt fail?

(b) What is the key difference between the scalar potential \(V\) and the gauge coupling via \(\boldsymbol{A}\)?

(c) Explain why gravity couples to mass (not charge) and does not arise from a U(1) gauge symmetry.

6. The wrong covariant derivative. Suppose one proposes \(\tilde{D}_i = \partial_i + (q/\hbar)^2 A_i^2\) (a quadratic coupling) instead of the standard \(D_i = \partial_i + \mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\).

(a) Check whether \(\tilde{D}_i\psi'\) transforms covariantly under \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}\psi\), \(A_i \to A_i + \partial_i\alpha\).

(b) Explain why only the linear coupling \(\mathrm{i}(q/\hbar)A_i\) works.

7. Inventing the covariant derivative. Suppose you know only that (i) the ordinary derivative \(\partial_i\psi'\) picks up an unwanted term \(\mathrm{i}(\partial_i\alpha)\psi'\) under a gauge transformation and (ii) \(A_i \to A_i + \partial_i\alpha\).

(a) Construct the unique first-order differential operator \(D_i = \partial_i + c\,A_i\) (with constant \(c\)) such that \(D_i'\psi' = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\alpha/\hbar}D_i\psi\).

(b) Determine \(c\). Why is \(c\) imaginary?

(c) Explain why the gauge principle forces the form of the interaction — the coupling constant and the structure of the Hamiltonian are not free parameters.

8. Classical limit. In classical mechanics, a charged particle in a magnetic field obeys Hamilton’s equations with \(H = (\boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A})^2/(2m)\).

(a) Compute \(\dot{x}_i = \partial H/\partial p_i\) and show \(m\dot{\boldsymbol{x}} = \boldsymbol{p} - q\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{\pi}\).

(b) Compute \(\dot{p}_i = -\partial H/\partial x_i\) and use \(\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}\) from (a) to show \(m\ddot{\boldsymbol{x}} = q\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}\times\boldsymbol{B}\).

(c) Explain how the quantum gauge-invariant Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^2/(2m)\) naturally yields the classical Lorentz force in the correspondence limit.

4.2.1 Berry Phase#

Prompts

  • What is the adiabatic theorem and why does a system stay in an instantaneous eigenstate under slow changes?

  • Explain the difference between dynamical phase (energy integral) and geometric (Berry) phase. How do they arise from the time-dependent Schrödinger equation?

  • Derive the Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R}) \vert \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}} \vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) and explain why it is a gauge field in parameter space.

  • How does Berry phase connect to the Phase = Action framework? How are the phase potentials \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\Phi\) in parameter space related to momentum and energy?

  • Why does the Berry phase of a spin-1/2 traversing a closed path on the Bloch sphere equal half the solid angle, rather than the full solid angle, the area, or the path length?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

The Berry phase is a geometric phase acquired by a quantum system when it undergoes adiabatic evolution along a closed path in parameter space. Unlike the dynamical phase, which accumulates from the Hamiltonian eigenvalues, the Berry phase arises purely from the geometric structure of the quantum state. This fundamental concept reveals that quantum mechanics has a built-in gauge structure in parameter space—a deep connection to classical field theory that underpins topological physics.

The Adiabatic Theorem#

When a quantum system’s Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}(t)\) varies slowly in time, the system remains in its instantaneous eigenstate. We do not need to specify how the time-dependence enters \(\hat{H}\) at this stage — it could be a turn-on protocol, an externally driven matrix element, a slowly rotating magnetic field, or anything else. All that matters is the time profile \(\hat{H}(t)\) itself.

Adiabatic Theorem

Let \(\hat{H}(t)\vert n(t)\rangle = E_n(t)\vert n(t)\rangle\) define the instantaneous eigenstates, with non-degenerate gaps to other levels. If the system starts in \(\vert n(0)\rangle\) and \(\hat{H}(t)\) varies on a timescale \(T \gg \hbar/\Delta E\), then the state remains in the instantaneous \(n\)-th eigenstate:

\[ \vert\psi(t)\rangle \approx \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(t)}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}}(t)}\,\vert n(t)\rangle, \]

picking up

  • a dynamical phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}}\) from the eigenenergy

    \[ \Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}}(t) = -\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^t E_n(t')\,\mathrm{d}t', \]

  • and a Berry phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) from the way the eigenstate basis itself moves.

    \[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(t) = \int_0^t \phi_n(t')\,\mathrm{d}t' \]

    where \(\phi_n(t)\) is the Berry connection in time (analog of the EM scalar potential \(\phi\))

    \[ \phi_n(t) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(t)\vert\partial_t n(t)\rangle. \]

    \(\phi_n(t)\) is real because \(\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle\) is purely imaginary (from \(\langle n\vert n\rangle = 1\)).

  • Under a rephasing \(\vert n(t)\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(t)}\vert n(t)\rangle\), the Berry connection shifts as \(\phi_n \to \phi_n - \dot\alpha\) — exactly the EM gauge form \(\phi \to \phi - \partial_t\alpha\) from §4.1.3. The open-time integral \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) therefore shifts by the boundary terms \(\alpha(0) - \alpha(t)\) and is not invariant on its own; the dynamical phase is invariant.

  • The theorem fails when the gap closes (Landau-Zener transitions). To see when it holds: \(\hat{H}\) varies on timescale \(T\), so its matrix elements change at rate \(\dot{H}_{mn} \sim \Delta E / T\). The interband leakage rate is therefore \(\hbar\dot{H}_{mn}/\Delta E^2 \sim \hbar/(T\Delta E)\), which is \(\ll 1\) exactly when \(T \gg \hbar/\Delta E\) — i.e. the slow-time condition is precisely the statement that this small parameter is small (explicit suppression: Step 3 of the derivation). When the gap closes, \(\Delta E \to 0\), the small parameter blows up — there is no slow-time regime, and Landau-Zener transitions take over.

Derivation: Adiabatic Following and the Berry Phase

Setup. Suppose at anchor time \(t\) the system is in the \(n\)-th instantaneous eigenstate of \(\hat{H}(t)\):

\[ \vert\psi(t)\rangle = \psi_n(t)\,\vert n(t)\rangle, \]

with \(\psi_n(t) = \langle n(t)\vert\psi(t)\rangle\) a single complex coefficient (amplitude and phase). What is \(\vert\psi(t+\mathrm{d}t)\rangle\), and what new coefficient \(\psi_n(t+\mathrm{d}t)\) does it have in the eigenstate basis at the new time \(t+\mathrm{d}t\)?

Step 1: direct Schrödinger evolution. The time-dependent Schrödinger equation \(\mathrm{i}\hbar\,\partial_t\vert\psi\rangle = \hat{H}(t)\vert\psi\rangle\) propagates the state by \(\mathrm{d}t\):

\[ \vert\psi(t+\mathrm{d}t)\rangle = \Bigl(1 - \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\hat{H}(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\Bigr)\vert\psi(t)\rangle = \psi_n(t)\Bigl(1 - \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}E_n(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\Bigr)\,\vert n(t)\rangle. \]

Direct evolution rotates the coefficient by the dynamical-phase factor but leaves the basis ket alone. The state is still expressed in the anchor-time basis \(\vert n(t)\rangle\), which is no longer the instantaneous eigenstate at \(t+\mathrm{d}t\).

Step 2: re-express in the new instantaneous basis. Define

\[ \psi_n(t+\mathrm{d}t) = \langle n(t+\mathrm{d}t)\vert\psi(t+\mathrm{d}t)\rangle. \]

Taylor expand \(\langle n(t+\mathrm{d}t)\vert = \langle n(t)\vert + \mathrm{d}t\,\langle\partial_t n(t)\vert\). Differentiating \(\langle n\vert n\rangle = 1\) gives \(\langle\partial_t n\vert n\rangle = -\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle\), so

\[ \langle n(t+\mathrm{d}t)\vert n(t)\rangle = 1 - \mathrm{d}t\,\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle + O(\mathrm{d}t^2). \]

Combining with Step 1,

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \psi_n(t+\mathrm{d}t) &= \psi_n(t)\Bigl(1 - \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}E_n\,\mathrm{d}t\Bigr)\Bigl(1 - \mathrm{d}t\,\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle\Bigr) \\ &= \psi_n(t)\Bigl(1 - \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}E_n\,\mathrm{d}t - \mathrm{d}t\,\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle\Bigr) + O(\mathrm{d}t^2). \end{split} \end{split}\]

Step 3: adiabatic following. Projecting onto \(\langle m(t+\mathrm{d}t)\vert\) for \(m\ne n\) gives interband amplitudes \(\propto \mathrm{d}t\,\langle m\vert\partial_t n\rangle\). Differentiating the eigenvalue equation \(\hat{H}\vert n\rangle = E_n\vert n\rangle\) rewrites these as \(\langle m\vert\partial_t n\rangle = \langle m\vert\partial_t\hat{H}\vert n\rangle/(E_n - E_m)\), so they are suppressed by the adiabatic small parameter \(\hbar\dot{H}_{mn}/\Delta E^2\). In the adiabatic limit they are dropped: the system remains in the instantaneous eigenstate \(\vert n(t+\mathrm{d}t)\rangle\). Adiabatic following is an output of the time-stepping argument, not an input.

Step 4: ODE and solution. Recognising \(\langle n\vert\partial_t n\rangle = -\mathrm{i}\,\phi_n(t)\), the recursion \(\psi_n(t+\mathrm{d}t) = \psi_n(t)\bigl(1 - \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}E_n\,\mathrm{d}t + \mathrm{i}\,\phi_n(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\bigr)\) gives the ODE

\[ \dot{\psi}_n(t) = -\tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}E_n(t)\,\psi_n(t) + \mathrm{i}\,\phi_n(t)\,\psi_n(t), \]

which integrates to

\[ \psi_n(t) = \psi_n(0)\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}}(t)}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(t)}, \]

reproducing the Adiabatic Theorem box. The dynamical phase comes from direct Schrödinger evolution at fixed Hamiltonian (Step 1); the Berry phase comes from the bookkeeping correction needed when the eigenstate basis itself moves (Step 2).

Berry Phase and Berry Connection#

In realistic settings the time-dependence of \(\hat{H}(t)\) enters through a finite set of parameters \(\boldsymbol{R}(t)\) — the direction of an external magnetic field, the lattice momentum in a Bloch band, the position of a slowly moving classical source, and so on — so that \(\hat{H}(t) = \hat{H}(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\) and the instantaneous eigenstates inherit the same parameter dependence: \(\vert n(t)\rangle = \vert n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle\). The Berry connection in time then reorganizes into a vector field on parameter space.

Berry Connection and Berry Curvature

  • The Berry connection on parameter space is (analogous to a vector potential)

    \[ \boxed{ \boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})\vert\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle.} \]

  • The Berry curvature is the curl of the connection (analogous to a magnetic field in parameter space),

    \[ \boldsymbol{\Omega}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\times\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}). \]

The time trajectory \(t \mapsto \boldsymbol{R}(t)\) traces a geometric path \(\mathcal{C}\) in parameter space. Using \(\phi_n(t)=\dot{\boldsymbol{R}}(t)\cdot\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\), the time-integrated Berry phase becomes

\[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = \int \phi_n(t)\,\mathrm{d}t = \int \dot{\boldsymbol{R}}(t)\cdot\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\,\mathrm{d}t = \int_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}, \]

This shows why the Berry phase is geometric: for a fixed path \(\mathcal{C}\), \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) depends on the curve in parameter space, not on how fast it is traversed (provided the evolution remains adiabatic).

Berry Phase

  • Along a path \(\mathcal{C}\) in parameter space, the Berry phase is the line integral

    \[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(\mathcal{C}) = \int_{\mathcal{C}}\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R})\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}. \]

  • For a closed loop \(\mathcal{C}=\partial\Sigma\), Stokes’ theorem gives the equivalent flux form

    \[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(\mathcal{C}) = \oint_{\mathcal{C}}\boldsymbol{A}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = \int_{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}. \]

Derivation: Time to Parameter-Space Connection

The previous section established the Berry connection in time, \(\phi_n(t) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(t)\vert\partial_t n(t)\rangle\), as a real-valued one-form along the trajectory. When the time-dependence enters through parameters \(\boldsymbol{R}(t)\), the chain rule \(\partial_t\vert n(\boldsymbol{R}(t))\rangle = \dot{\boldsymbol{R}}(t)\cdot\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) factors \(\phi_n\) into a directional derivative along the path:

\[ \phi_n(t) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(t)\vert\partial_t n(t)\rangle = \dot{\boldsymbol{R}}(t)\cdot\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t)), \]

where the velocity-independent vector field \(\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})\vert\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) on parameter space is the Berry connection. Integrating over time accumulates the Berry phase along the path \(\mathcal{C}\) traced by \(\boldsymbol{R}(t)\):

\[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}(\mathcal{C}) = \int_0^t \phi_n(t')\,\mathrm{d}t' = \int_0^t \dot{\boldsymbol{R}}(t')\cdot\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}(t'))\,\mathrm{d}t' = \int_{\mathcal{C}}\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R})\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}. \]

The Berry phase is now a geometric quantity: it depends only on the path \(\mathcal{C}\) in parameter space, not on the speed at which it is traversed (provided the speed stays in the adiabatic regime).

For a closed loop \(\mathcal{C} = \partial\Sigma\), Stokes’ theorem turns the line integral into a surface integral of the curl \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\times\boldsymbol{A}_n\):

\[ \oint_{\mathcal{C}}\boldsymbol{A}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R} = \int_{\Sigma}(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\times\boldsymbol{A}_n)\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \int_{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}. \]

This is the same gauge structure as in §4.1, lifted from real space to parameter space. The local phase ambiguity \(\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{R})}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) here plays the role of \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}) \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{r})}\psi(\boldsymbol{r})\) in §4.1.3, and the Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n\) plays the role of the EM gauge connection \(q\boldsymbol{A}/\hbar\).

Direct computation gives \(\boldsymbol{A}_n \to \boldsymbol{A}_n - \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\alpha\), mirroring the EM gauge transformation \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\alpha\). The open-vs-closed dichotomy carries over verbatim: along an open path, \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) shifts by boundary terms \(\alpha(\boldsymbol{R}_{\text{end}}) - \alpha(\boldsymbol{R}_{\text{start}})\) and is not a physical observable; for a closed loop the boundary terms cancel, so \(\oint_{\mathcal{C}}\boldsymbol{A}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\) is gauge-invariant — the holonomy. See 4.1.3 Gauge Invariance § Open path vs closed loop for the EM analog.

Discussion: gauge dependence of the Berry connection

The Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n\) depends on the phase convention for \(\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\). If we redefine \(\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{R})}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\), how does \(\boldsymbol{A}_n\) change? Is the closed-loop integral (Berry phase) still gauge-independent?

Connection to Phase = Action (Chapter 3)#

Recall from Chapter 3 that the phase of a wavefunction encodes spacetime evolution:

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \boldsymbol{p} &= \hbar\boldsymbol{k} \sim \nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}} \Phi_{\mathrm{path}},\\ E &= \hbar\omega \sim \partial_t \Phi_{\mathrm{path}}, \end{split} \end{split}\]

Berry phase plays the same role in parameter space:

Berry Phase as Generalized Action

A charged particle moving through a gauge field in real space accumulates phase \((q/\hbar)\int \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{l}\). Similarly, a quantum system evolving adiabatically through parameter space accumulates Berry phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = \oint \boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\). Both are examples of how gauge fields—whether electromagnetic or geometric—shift the phase of quantum amplitudes.

There is in fact no fundamental difference between the two. The parameter can be position (giving the electromagnetic gauge phase), the direction of a magnetic field (giving the spin Berry phase), or any other variable that \(\hat{H}\) depends on — in every case the wavefunction lives on a fiber bundle over the parameter manifold, and the gauge field is the connection on that bundle. Real-space electromagnetic phase is the Berry phase whose parameter happens to be position.

Example: Spin-1/2 in a Rotating Magnetic Field#

Consider a spin-1/2 in a slowly rotating magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B}(t) = B\,\boldsymbol{n}(t)\), where \(\boldsymbol{n}(t)\) traces a closed path on the unit sphere. The Hamiltonian is \(\hat{H}(t) = -\mu B\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{n}(t)\), with \(\mu\) the magnetic-moment magnitude associated with \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}\). Parametrize the unit vector by spherical angles \((\theta, \varphi)\),

\[ \boldsymbol{n}(\theta, \varphi) = (\sin\theta\cos\varphi,\,\sin\theta\sin\varphi,\,\cos\theta). \]

The instantaneous “spin-up” eigenstate along \(\boldsymbol{n}\) is

\[\begin{split} \vert\uparrow(\boldsymbol{n})\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}\cos(\theta/2)\\\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2)\end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

Derivation: Berry Connection for Spin-1/2

Use

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \vert\uparrow(\boldsymbol{n})\rangle &= \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta/2)\\ \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) \end{pmatrix},\\ \langle\uparrow(\boldsymbol{n})\vert &= \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta/2) & \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) \end{pmatrix}. \end{split} \end{split}\]

Berry connection components in parameter space are

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} A_\theta &=\mathrm{i}\langle\uparrow\vert\partial_\theta\vert\uparrow\rangle,\\ A_\varphi &=\mathrm{i}\langle\uparrow\vert\partial_\varphi\vert\uparrow\rangle. \end{split} \end{split}\]

First compute derivatives:

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \partial_\theta\vert\uparrow\rangle &= \begin{pmatrix} -\tfrac12\sin(\theta/2)\\ \tfrac12\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\cos(\theta/2) \end{pmatrix},\\ \partial_\varphi\vert\uparrow\rangle &= \begin{pmatrix} 0\\ \mathrm{i}\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) \end{pmatrix}. \end{split} \end{split}\]

Then take inner products:

\[ \langle\uparrow\vert\partial_\theta\vert\uparrow\rangle =\cos(\theta/2)\left(-\tfrac12\sin(\theta/2)\right) +\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2)\left(\tfrac12\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\cos(\theta/2)\right)=0, \]

so \(A_\theta=0\), and

\[ \langle\uparrow\vert\partial_\varphi\vert\uparrow\rangle =\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) \left(\mathrm{i}\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\varphi}\sin(\theta/2)\right) =\mathrm{i}\sin^2(\theta/2), \]

hence

\[ A_\varphi = \mathrm{i}\langle\uparrow\vert\partial_\varphi\vert\uparrow\rangle = -\sin^2(\theta/2)= -\frac{1-\cos\theta}{2}. \]

(With the opposite phase convention for \(\vert\uparrow\rangle\), this flips sign; physical curvature and closed-loop phase are gauge-invariant.)

Now take the curl on the sphere:

\[ \Omega_{\theta\varphi}=\partial_\theta A_\varphi-\partial_\varphi A_\theta = -\frac12\sin\theta. \]

To convert from the coordinate-component curvature \(\Omega_{\theta\varphi}\) to a vector-field form \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\) on the unit sphere (\(R=1\)), match surface-flux integrals. The sphere’s vector area element is \(\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \hat{\boldsymbol{n}}\,\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi\), so for any patch \(S\)

\[ \int_S\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \int_S(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{n}})\sin\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi = \int_S \Omega_{\theta\varphi}\,\mathrm{d}\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi, \]

which forces \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{n}} = \Omega_{\theta\varphi}/\sin\theta = -\tfrac12\). The eigenstate depends only on the field direction \(\hat{\boldsymbol{n}}\), so the curvature is purely radial,

\[ \boldsymbol{\Omega} = -\frac12\,\hat{\boldsymbol{n}}. \]

This is exactly the field of a unit monopole in parameter space (cf. §4.4.3).

When the field direction traces a closed path subtending solid angle \(\Omega\), the Berry phase is:

Spin-1/2 Berry Phase

For a closed loop \(\mathcal{C}\) traced by \(\boldsymbol{n}(t)\) on the Bloch sphere, substituting \(\boldsymbol{\Omega} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\hat{\boldsymbol{n}}\) from the derivation above and using the unit-sphere identification \(\hat{\boldsymbol{n}}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \mathrm{d}\Omega_{\text{solid}}\) gives

\[ \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = -\frac{\Omega_{\text{solid}}}{2}. \]

The Berry phase of a spin-1/2 equals half the solid angle subtended by the field’s path on the Bloch sphere.

Example: Precessing Magnetic Field

Problem. A spin-1/2 starts in state \(\vert\uparrow\rangle\). The magnetic field is adiabatically rotated around a great circle (equator): \(\boldsymbol{B}(t) = B(\cos(2\pi t/T)\hat{x} + \sin(2\pi t/T)\hat{y})\) over one period \(T\). Find the Berry phase.

Solution. The field sweeps the equator, subtending solid angle \(\Omega_{\text{solid}} = 2\pi\). The Berry phase is \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = -\Omega_{\text{solid}}/2 = -\pi\). After one cycle, the state acquires \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\pi} = -1\) — the state returns to itself up to a geometric sign flip.

Physical Manifestations#

The Berry phase appears in measurable quantities across physics: the quantum Hall effect (Berry curvature in momentum space drives anomalous transport), topological insulators (non-trivial Berry curvature gives robust edge states), the spin Hall effect (spin-dependent Berry phases produce spin currents without magnetic fields), and molecular dynamics (geometric phases govern nonadiabatic transitions in photochemistry).

For a spin-1/2 in a slowly rotating magnetic field, the system accumulates both a dynamic phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}} = -\int E(t) \mathrm{d}t / \hbar\) and a geometric (Berry) phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\). (D) Both (B) and (C).

Summary#

  • Adiabatic evolution keeps a system in its instantaneous eigenstate, acquiring both dynamical and geometric phases.

  • Berry phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = \oint \boldsymbol{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\) is a gauge-invariant geometric phase accumulated along a closed path in parameter space.

  • Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n = \mathrm{i}\langle n\vert\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert n\rangle\) is a gauge field; Berry curvature \(\boldsymbol{\Omega}_n = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}_n\) is gauge-invariant.

  • For spin-1/2, the Berry phase equals half the solid angle subtended by the path on the Bloch sphere.

  • Berry phases connect quantum mechanics to topological physics and explain anomalous transport.

2. Berry curvature on Bloch sphere. The Berry curvature in the 2D parameter space \((\theta,\varphi)\) is \(\Omega = \partial_\theta A_\varphi - \partial_\varphi A_\theta\).

(a) Using \(A_\theta = 0\) and \(A_\varphi = -(1-\cos\theta)/2\) from Problem 1, compute \(\Omega(\theta,\varphi)\).

(b) The Chern number is the parameter-space integral \(c_1 = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\mathrm{d}\varphi\int_0^\pi\mathrm{d}\theta\,\Omega\) (note: no \(\sin\theta\) Jacobian when integrating the curvature in the parameter coordinates). Verify \(c_1 = -1\) for the spin-up state, consistent with the negative Berry phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = -\Omega_{\mathrm{solid}}/2\) found in Problem 1.

(c) Use Stokes’ theorem to rederive the Berry phase from Problem 1(b) as a parameter-space integral of \(\Omega\) over the region bounded by the cone.

3. Gauge dependence of connection. The Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{R}) = \mathrm{i}\langle n(\boldsymbol{R})\vert\nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert n(\boldsymbol{R})\rangle\) is gauge-dependent, but the Berry phase for a closed loop is not.

(a) Under \(\vert n\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\alpha(\boldsymbol{R})}\vert n\rangle\), show that \(\boldsymbol{A}_n \to \boldsymbol{A}_n - \nabla_{\boldsymbol{R}}\alpha\).

(b) Show that for a closed loop, \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}} = \oint \boldsymbol{A}_n\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\) is gauge-invariant.

(c) For an open path from \(\boldsymbol{R}_a\) to \(\boldsymbol{R}_b\), explain why the line integral depends on the gauge choice. What gauge-invariant quantity can you define instead?

4. Spin-1 Berry phase. Extend the spin-1/2 result to spin-1.

(a) For a spin-1 system, the Berry phase of eigenstate \(\vert m\rangle\) (\(m = -1, 0, 1\)) in a field rotating around a cone of half-angle \(\theta_0\) is \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry},m} = -m\,\Omega_\text{solid}\). Verify this for \(m = 1\) by computing the solid angle for a full revolution.

(b) For a great circle (\(\theta_0 = \pi/2\)), compute \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry},m}\) for each \(m\). Which eigenstate acquires no geometric phase?

(c) The Chern number of eigenstate \(\vert m\rangle\) is \(c_1 = -2m\) (consistent with the sign convention used in Problem 2). Verify for \(m = 1\) by integrating the parameter-space curvature \(\Omega = -m\sin\theta\) over \(\int\mathrm{d}\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi\).

5. Dynamical versus geometric phase. After one adiabatic cycle of period \(T\), the total phase is \(\Phi_\text{total} = \Phi_\text{dyn} + \Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) where \(\Phi_\text{dyn} = -\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^T E_n(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\).

(a) For spin-1/2 in a field of magnitude \(B\) rotating at angular velocity \(\omega\), compute the dynamical phase \(\Phi_{\mathrm{dyn}}\) after one cycle. Express it in terms of \(\omega_L = \gamma B\) (Larmor frequency) and \(T = 2\pi/\omega\).

(b) Compute the ratio \(\vert\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}/\Phi_\text{dyn}\vert\). Show it equals \(\omega/\omega_L \ll 1\) in the adiabatic regime.

(c) Despite being small, the Berry phase is measurable because it depends on the path geometry, not the speed. Design a measurement that isolates \(\Phi_{\mathrm{Berry}}\) from \(\Phi_\text{dyn}\). (Hint: compare two spins traversing the same cone at different speeds.)

6. Adiabatic breakdown. The adiabatic theorem requires a nonzero energy gap \(\Delta E\) throughout the parameter evolution.

(a) For a two-level system with gap \(\Delta E\), the adiabatic condition is \(\hbar\vert\dot{\boldsymbol{R}}\vert/\Delta E \ll 1\). Explain this criterion in words: why does a small gap require slower evolution?

(b) If two energy levels cross (\(\Delta E = 0\)) at a point in parameter space, the adiabatic approximation breaks down. Explain why the Berry curvature is typically concentrated near such degeneracy points.

(c) A path that encloses a degeneracy point can acquire a nontrivial Berry phase even if it stays far from the degeneracy. Explain why this is possible using the flux interpretation of Berry curvature.

7. Chern number and quantization. The Chern number \(c_1 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{S^2}\Omega\,\mathrm{d}S\) over a closed surface must be an integer.

(a) Verify the integer quantization for spin-\(s\): the Berry curvature of \(\vert m\rangle\) is \(\Omega = -m\sin\theta\), and \(c_1 = -2m\) is always an integer for allowed \(m\) values.

(b) The integer quantization is topological — \(c_1\) cannot change under smooth deformations of the Hamiltonian. Explain why this implies robustness of the quantum Hall conductance \(\sigma_{xy} = (e^2/h)\,c_1\).

(c) Berry curvature acts like a “magnetic field in parameter space.” The Chern number is the total “flux.” How does this analogy connect to the Dirac monopole of §4.4.2?

8. Berry phase in crystals. In a crystalline solid, Bloch electrons have wavevector \(\boldsymbol{k}\) in the Brillouin zone as the parameter.

(a) The Berry connection is \(\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{k}) = \mathrm{i}\langle u_{n\boldsymbol{k}}\vert\nabla_{\boldsymbol{k}}\vert u_{n\boldsymbol{k}}\rangle\). Explain why the Brillouin zone, being a torus, is a closed manifold on which the Chern number is well-defined.

(b) A nonzero Chern number \(c_1 \neq 0\) for a filled band implies quantized Hall conductance. Why can \(c_1\) not change as long as the band gap remains open?

(c) Time-reversal symmetry forces \(c_1 = 0\) for every band. Explain why, and describe what happens in materials that break time-reversal symmetry.

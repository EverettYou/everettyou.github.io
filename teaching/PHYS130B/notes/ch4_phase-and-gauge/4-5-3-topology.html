Lecture Notes#

The Chern Number and Topological Invariants#

The Chern number is an integer topological invariant that classifies quantum states and energy bands. For a 2D band structure, it is defined as:

\[ C_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}} \mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}(\boldsymbol{k})\,\mathrm{d}^2k \in \mathbb{Z}\]

where the integral is over the Brillouin zone and \(\mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}\) is the Berry curvature of band \(n\) in momentum space.

The Chern number is called a topological invariant because:

  1. It is always an integer

  2. It cannot change under smooth deformations of the Hamiltonian (unless the band gap closes)

  3. It is independent of the choice of gauge (the choice of phase for the Bloch states)

These properties make \(C_n\) robust to disorder and perturbations — it characterizes a fundamentally different phase of matter.

Chern Number Definition

The Chern number \(C_n \in \mathbb{Z}\) of a 2D Bloch band \(n\) is a topological invariant:

\[ C_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}} \mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}(\boldsymbol{k})\,\mathrm{d}^2k \in \mathbb{Z}\]

where \(\mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}(\boldsymbol{k}) = \partial_{k_x} \mathcal{A}_y^{(n)} - \partial_{k_y} \mathcal{A}_x^{(n)}\) is the Berry curvature and \(\mathcal{A}^{(n)} = i\langle n(\boldsymbol{k})|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{k}}n(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle\) is the Berry connection.

Key properties:

  • Always an integer (topological quantization)

  • Cannot change under smooth deformations without closing the band gap

  • Gauge-invariant: independent of the choice of phase for Bloch states

  • Characterizes the winding of the electronic state’s phase as momentum traverses the Brillouin zone

Physical Picture: Berry Flux and Winding#

Imagine smoothly deforming a band by continuously changing Hamiltonian parameters. The Berry curvature \(\mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}(\boldsymbol{k})\) encodes the “twist” or “winding” of the electron’s internal quantum state as we move through momentum space.

Integrating this curvature over the entire Brillouin zone gives the total “magnetic flux” of Berry curvature:

\[ \Phi_{\text{Berry}} = \int_{\text{BZ}} \mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}\,\mathrm{d}^2k = 2\pi C_n\]

The quantization condition forces \(C_n\) to be an integer. You cannot continuously deform the band to reduce this flux by less than one “flux quantum” (\(2\pi\)) without closing the gap or changing topological structure.

Discussion: Chern Number and Topological Protection

The Chern number C = (1/2π)∫_BZ F dk² is always an integer.

Show that a band with C=1 cannot be smoothly deformed to a flat band (trivial insulator) without closing a gap.

Why is the integer nature of the Chern number a topological obstruction? How does this protect edge states in topological insulators?

The TKNN Formula and Integer Quantum Hall Effect#

Theorem (TKNN, 1982): The quantized Hall conductance of a 2D electron system with filled energy bands is:

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \frac{e^2}{h}\sum_{n=1}^{N_{\text{filled}}} C_n\]

where the sum runs over all occupied bands and \(C_n\) is the Chern number of band \(n\).

TKNN Formula: Quantized Hall Conductance

Thouless, Kohmoto, Nightingale, and Newnham (1982): The quantized Hall conductance of a 2D electron system with filled energy bands is:

\[ \sigma_{xy} = \frac{e^2}{h} \sum_{n=1}^{N_{\text{filled}}} C_n\]

where the sum runs over all occupied bands and \(C_n\) is the Chern number of band \(n\).

Physical significance:

  • Relates a macroscopic transport coefficient (Hall conductance) to a microscopic topological invariant (Chern number)

  • Hall conductance is quantized in units of \(e^2/h\) (no device-dependent factors)

  • Quantization is exact and robust against disorder, impurities, and perturbations (as long as the band gap remains open)

  • This explains the universal plateaus observed in the integer quantum Hall effect

This remarkable formula connects:

  • A macroscopic observable (Hall conductance, measured in volts and currents)

  • A microscopic quantum topological invariant (Chern number, counting how many times Berry curvature “winds” around momentum space)

The result is that the Hall conductance is quantized in units of \(e^2/h\) and immune to disorder (up to localization effects).

Derivation Sketch: Why Hall Conductance is Topological

When a magnetic field is applied perpendicular to a 2D electron system, the kinetic energy operator becomes noncommuting in different directions. Electrons cannot move freely in the field direction (they form Landau levels), but within each band, Berry curvature encodes an effective “anomalous velocity” perpendicular to the applied electric field.

The transverse conductance arises entirely from this Berry-curvature-induced anomalous velocity. Integrating over all occupied states in all occupied bands, the current is proportional to the total Chern number.

Topological vs. Geometric Invariants#

It is crucial to distinguish:

Geometric (Berry) phases:

  • Path-dependent

  • Change when the path changes

  • Observable in open-path interferometry

  • Example: \(\gamma_n = \oint_C \mathcal{A}_n \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{R}\) depends on the specific path \(C\)

Topological invariants (Chern numbers):

  • Path-independent (for closed loops)

  • Invariant under smooth deformations of the system

  • Characterize global structure of the Hilbert space bundle

  • Example: \(C_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}} \mathcal{F}_{xy}^{(n)}\,\mathrm{d}^2k\) depends only on the band structure, not on how we parametrize it

A Chern number is an integrated geometric property — it sums up the Berry curvature to yield an integer that encodes global topology.

Bulk-Boundary Correspondence (Index Theorem)#

One of the most profound results in topological band theory:

Index Theorem: If a 2D system with bulk Chern number \(C_n \neq 0\) is terminated at a boundary, there must exist chiral edge states at that boundary.

The number of gapless one-way propagating edge modes equals \(|C_n|\):

  • If \(C = +1\): one chiral edge state propagating clockwise

  • If \(C = -1\): one chiral edge state propagating counterclockwise

  • If \(C = 0\): no necessary edge states (generic case)

These edge states are topologically protected — they cannot be removed by disorder or perturbations without closing the bulk band gap.

Physical Explanation

Imagine “cutting” the 2D system along a line. The Berry curvature in the bulk represents a kind of “circulation” of electron momentum. At the boundary, this circulation must be interrupted. The result is a current that flows along the edge, carried by states that live at the boundary.

These edge currents are one-directional and robust because reversing them would require changing the global Chern number — an energetically expensive process involving gap closure.

Time-Reversal Symmetry and Topological Insulators#

If a system has time-reversal symmetry (T-symmetry), then:

\[ \hat{T}\hat{H}\hat{T}^{-1} = \hat{H}\]

For fermionic systems, T-symmetry requires that bands come in pairs with opposite Chern numbers:

\[ C_n(\boldsymbol{k}) = -C_n(-\boldsymbol{k})\]

Therefore, the total Chern number for all occupied bands is forced to be zero, and \(C = 0\) does not predict edge states.

However, TR-symmetric systems can be topologically nontrivial via a different integer invariant: the \(\mathbb{Z}_2\) topological invariant. This predicts an even number of protected edge modes that are resilient to backscattering but not one-directional.

Such systems are called topological insulators.

Winding Numbers in 1D Chains#

In one-dimensional systems (e.g., the SSH model — Su-Schrieffer-Heeger model of polyacetylene), the analog of the Chern number is the winding number:

\[ w = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_0^{2\pi} \arg(\det[\hat{H}(\boldsymbol{k})])\,\mathrm{d}k \in \mathbb{Z}\]

This integer also cannot change without closing the band gap. A non-zero winding number predicts zero-energy edge states (bound states at the ends of the chain).

For the SSH model:

  • Two sublattices (A and B) in a 1D chain

  • Alternating hopping amplitudes \(t_1\) and \(t_2\)

  • Winding number \(w = \mathrm{sign}(t_2 - t_1)\) predicts which sublattice hosts the edge state

This is the 1D version of bulk-boundary correspondence.

Key Physical Consequences of Topological Structure#

  1. Quantized Responses: Hall conductance, thermal Hall effect, etc., are quantized integers determined by Chern numbers.

  2. Robust Edge States: Gapless boundary modes exist and are protected against disorder, defects, and weak perturbations.

  3. Immune to Disorder: As long as the band gap remains open, disorder cannot change the Chern number or destroy the topological protection.

  4. Observable Signatures:

    • Quantized transverse conductance

    • Chiral edge currents

    • Anomalous transport (anomalous Hall effect)

    • Edge magnetization and thermal transport

  5. Applications: Topological insulators and materials promise fault-tolerant electronics, quantum computing platforms, and robust quantum devices.

1. Integer Quantization of the Chern Number

Consider a 2D electron band with Berry curvature \(\Omega(\boldsymbol{k})\). Use Stokes’ theorem to show that the Chern number \(C = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}} \Omega(\boldsymbol{k}) \, \mathrm{d}^2k\) must be an integer. Explain why periodic boundary conditions on the torus-shaped Brillouin zone are crucial for this quantization. Would the Chern number be quantized if the BZ had a different topology (e.g., an open plane)?

2. Berry Phase for a One-Dimensional Circle

A 1D system has a closed Brillouin zone of circumference \(2\pi\). The Berry connection is \(A(k) = i\langle\psi(k)|\partial_k\psi(k)\rangle\). Show that the Berry phase is:

\[ \gamma = \oint_0^{2\pi} A(k) \, \mathrm{d}k = 2\pi w\]

where \(w\) is an integer winding number. Relate the winding number to the argument of \(\langle\psi(2\pi)|\psi(0)\rangle\). Why does the winding number equal the number of times the electronic phase winds around the complex plane as \(k\) traverses the Brillouin zone?

3. Gauge Invariance of the Chern Number

The Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_n(\boldsymbol{k}) = i\langle n(\boldsymbol{k})|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{k}}n(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle\) is not gauge-invariant: under a local U(1) phase rotation \(|n(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\lambda(\boldsymbol{k})}|n(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle\), the connection transforms as \(\boldsymbol{A} \to \boldsymbol{A} + \nabla\lambda\). Show that the Berry curvature \(\Omega = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\) is gauge-invariant, and therefore the Chern number is well-defined despite the choice of phase for the eigenstates.

4. Chern Number for a Simple 2D Model

Consider a toy 2D tight-binding Hamiltonian:

\[ \hat{H}(\boldsymbol{k}) = 2t[\cos(k_x) + \cos(k_y)]\sigma^x + m\sigma^z\]

where \(t > 0\), \(m > 0\) are parameters, and \(\sigma^{x,z}\) are Pauli matrices.

(a) Find the eigenvalues \(E_{\pm}(\boldsymbol{k})\) and eigenstates \(|n_+(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle\), \(|n_-(\boldsymbol{k})\rangle\) in terms of \(\boldsymbol{k}\).

(b) Compute the Berry connection \(\boldsymbol{A}_+(\boldsymbol{k}) = i\langle n_+|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{k}}n_+\rangle\) for the upper band.

(c) Compute the Berry curvature \(\Omega_+(\boldsymbol{k}) = \partial_{k_x} A_{+,y} - \partial_{k_y} A_{+,x}\).

(d) Integrate to find the Chern number \(C_+ = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\text{BZ}} \Omega_+ \, \mathrm{d}^2k\). [Hint: Use symmetry; the integral should be straightforward.]

5. Bulk-Boundary Correspondence: Edge States

A topological insulator with bulk Chern number \(C = 1\) is cut to create a boundary. According to the bulk-boundary correspondence theorem, exactly one chiral edge state should cross the band gap.

(a) Explain physically why edge states must exist at the boundary between a Chern insulator and vacuum.

(b) Why must this edge state be chiral (traveling only in one direction)?

(c) If we add a non-magnetic impurity at the edge, why is the edge state robust to this perturbation? (Hint: Consider how backscattering would require a transition to other edge states.)

6. Quantized Hall Conductance

The integer quantum Hall effect shows \(\sigma_H = \nu \frac{e^2}{h}\) where \(\nu\) is the number of filled Landau levels. Explain why this quantization is exact using topological arguments:

(a) Show that when \(\nu\) Landau levels are filled, the occupied band manifold has total Chern number \(C = \nu\).

(b) Use bulk-boundary correspondence to argue that there are \(\nu\) chiral edge states.

(c) Each edge state is a 1D band carrying current. Why does each edge state contribute exactly \(\frac{e^2}{h}\) to the conductance?

(d) Why is this quantization robust against disorder and microscopic details?

7. Topological Phase Transition

Consider a family of Hamiltonians \(\hat{H}(m)\) parameterized by a mass term \(m\). Suppose that for \(m < 0\) the system has Chern number \(C = 0\), and for \(m > 0\) it has Chern number \(C = 1\).

(a) What must happen to the band gap at \(m = 0\)?

(b) Sketch the band structure near the critical point and explain how states from occupied and unoccupied bands “mix” during the transition.

(c) Explain why a topological phase transition is fundamentally different from a conventional symmetry-breaking phase transition (like ferromagnetism).

(d) If the system is small and \(m\) is changed slowly across \(m = 0\), can the occupied band adiabatically evolve from \(C = 0\) to \(C = 1\) without crossing \(m = 0\)? Why or why not?

4.1.2 Gauge Connection#

Prompts

  • What is the gauge connection (vector potential), and how does it differ conceptually from the field strength?

  • Derive the commutator \([D_i, D_j]\) to obtain the field strength tensor. Why does the field strength measure curvature?

  • What is parallel transport? How does it relate to the gauge connection and Berry phase?

  • For an abelian gauge (like electromagnetism), show that \(F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i\) and relates to \(\boldsymbol{B}\) and \(\boldsymbol{E}\).

  • Why is field strength gauge-invariant while the vector potential is gauge-dependent?

Lecture Notes#

The gauge field \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and the field strength tensor \(F_{ij}\) represent two complementary aspects of gauge theory: the connection (which is gauge-dependent and unobservable in isolation) and the curvature (which is gauge-invariant and represents the physical field). Understanding their relationship is the bridge between abstract differential geometry and concrete physics.

The Gauge Connection (Vector Potential)#

Definition and Interpretation#

The gauge field \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) appears in the covariant derivative:

\[ D_i = \partial_i + \mathrm{i}qA_i\]

This field is called the gauge connection or vector potential. In electromagnetism, \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is the familiar vector potential related to the magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\).

Gauge Connection (Vector Potential)

The gauge connection \(A_i(\boldsymbol{r}, t)\) is the component of a gauge field that couples to charges according to the minimal coupling prescription:

\[ (\partial_i - \mathrm{i}qA_i)\psi = D_i\psi\]

The connection is gauge-dependent: under a gauge transformation \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi}\psi\), the connection transforms as:

\[ A_i \to A_i - \partial_i\chi\]

Because the connection transforms, it is not directly observable. Only gauge-invariant combinations (like the field strength) have direct physical meaning.

Field Strength Tensor: The Commutator of Covariant Derivatives#

The key insight is that the field strength—the true, gauge-invariant physical field—can be extracted from the commutator of two covariant derivatives.

Derivation#

Consider the commutator of covariant derivatives acting on a scalar wavefunction \(\psi\):

\[ [D_i, D_j]\psi = [(\partial_i + \mathrm{i}qA_i), (\partial_j + \mathrm{i}qA_j)]\psi\]

Expand carefully:

\[ [D_i, D_j]\psi = D_i(D_j\psi) - D_j(D_i\psi)\]
\[= (\partial_i + \mathrm{i}qA_i)[(\partial_j + \mathrm{i}qA_j)\psi] - (\partial_j + \mathrm{i}qA_j)[(\partial_i + \mathrm{i}qA_i)\psi]\]
\[= \partial_i\partial_j\psi + \mathrm{i}qA_i\partial_j\psi + \mathrm{i}q(\partial_i A_j)\psi + \mathrm{i}qA_j(\partial_i\psi) - (i \leftrightarrow j)\]

The \(\partial_i\partial_j\psi\) and \(\partial_i\partial_j\psi\) terms cancel (because mixed partials commute for smooth functions). Collecting terms:

\[ [D_i, D_j]\psi = \mathrm{i}q[(\partial_i A_j) - (\partial_j A_i)]\psi + \mathrm{i}q[A_i\partial_j - A_j\partial_i]\psi\]

The second bracket vanishes because \([A_i, \partial_j] = 0\) (the vector potential commutes with spatial derivatives). Therefore:

\[ [D_i, D_j]\psi = \mathrm{i}q(\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i)\psi\]

Field Strength Tensor

Define the field strength tensor as:

\[ F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i\]

Then:

\[ [D_i, D_j] = \mathrm{i}qF_{ij}\]

The field strength tensor is gauge-invariant: under a gauge transformation, \(F_{ij}' = F_{ij}\). This is because:

\[ F_{ij}' = \partial_i A_j' - \partial_j A_i' = \partial_i(A_j - \partial_j\chi) - \partial_j(A_i - \partial_i\chi) = F_{ij}\]

(The derivatives of \(\chi\) cancel.)

Derivation in Dropdown#

Derivation: Gauge Invariance of Field Strength

Under gauge transformation \(\psi \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}q\chi}\psi\) and \(A_i \to A_i - \partial_i\chi\):

\[ F_{ij}' = \partial_i(A_j - \partial_j\chi) - \partial_j(A_i - \partial_i\chi)\]
\[= \partial_i A_j - \partial_i\partial_j\chi - \partial_j A_i + \partial_j\partial_i\chi\]
\[= (\partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i) + (\partial_j\partial_i\chi - \partial_i\partial_j\chi) = F_{ij} + 0\]

since mixed partial derivatives are equal for smooth functions. Thus \(F_{ij}\) is gauge-invariant.

Electromagnetic Field Strength#

In electromagnetism, the field strength tensor is a 4×4 matrix where \(i, j \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}\) (spacetime indices). With \(A_0 = \Phi/c\) (scalar potential in spacetime units), the components are:

\[ F_{0i} = \partial_0 A_i - \partial_i A_0 = -\frac{1}{c}\partial_t A_i - \partial_i\Phi\]

relates to the electric field. The spatial components:

\[ F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i\]

relate to the magnetic field via \(B_k = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_{ijk}F_{ij}\).

For a 3D system with scalar potential \(\Phi\) and vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\):

Example: Electromagnetic Fields from Potentials

The electric field is:

\[ \boldsymbol{E} = -\nabla\Phi - \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{A}}{\partial t}\]

The magnetic field is:

\[ \boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\]

These are obtained from components of the field strength tensor. Both are gauge-invariant: different gauge choices for \((\Phi, \boldsymbol{A})\) produce the same \((\boldsymbol{E}, \boldsymbol{B})\).

Parallel Transport and Holonomy#

One of the deepest insights connecting geometry to physics is the notion of parallel transport along a path.

Concept#

Imagine a quantum state “moving” along a path in space while interacting with a gauge field. The state’s phase is “parallel transported” along the path, meaning its phase evolution is determined by the local connection.

Discussion

In a different gauge, where \(A_i' = A_i - \partial_i\chi\), how does the Wilson line \(U[\mathcal{C}]\) change? If we measure the same phase shift with a different gauge choice, how can we reconcile this with gauge invariance? What physical quantity remains invariant?

For a particle moving along a path \(\mathcal{C}\) parametrized by \(s\), with \(x_i(s)\) its position, the path-ordered exponential (or Wilson line) is:

\[ U[\mathcal{C}] = \mathcal{P}\exp\left(-\mathrm{i}q\int_{\mathcal{C}} A_i(\boldsymbol{r}(s))\,\frac{\mathrm{d}x_i}{\mathrm{d}s}\,\mathrm{d}s\right)\]

This describes how a quantum state is rotated as it travels along the path \(\mathcal{C}\).

Phase Circulation Around a Loop#

Consider a closed loop \(\mathcal{C}\). The phase accumulated when a state is parallel-transported around the loop is:

\[ \phi_{\text{loop}} = q\oint_{\mathcal{C}} A_i(\boldsymbol{r})\,\mathrm{d}x_i\]

By Stokes’ theorem:

\[ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r} = \int_{\text{surface}} (\nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}) \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \int_{\text{surface}} \boldsymbol{B} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S} = \Phi_B\]

where \(\Phi_B\) is the magnetic flux through the surface bounded by the loop. Therefore:

Holonomy and Flux

The phase accumulated by parallel transport around a closed loop equals the flux of the field strength through the enclosed surface:

\[ \phi_{\text{loop}} = q\int_{\text{surface}} F \cdot \mathrm{d}S\]

This is a topological result: it depends only on the integral of the field strength, not on the detailed shape of the loop.

Discussion

The field strength tensor \(F_{ij}\) is defined as a commutator of covariant derivatives. In ordinary calculus, the commutator of \(\partial_i\) and \(\partial_j\) vanishes. Why does adding the gauge field make the covariant derivatives non-commuting? What is the geometric meaning: does this non-commutativity measure something about space itself?

Non-Abelian Gauge Fields (Brief Introduction)#

For more complicated gauge groups (like SU(3) in quantum chromodynamics), the gauge field components themselves are matrices. The field strength becomes:

\[ F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i + \mathrm{i}q[A_i, A_j]\]

The commutator term makes non-abelian gauge theories nonlinear: the field strength depends on the fields themselves, creating self-interactions that are absent in electromagnetism.

Summary#

  • The gauge connection \(A_i\) couples to charges via minimal coupling but is gauge-dependent and unobservable in isolation.

  • The field strength tensor \(F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i\) is obtained from the commutator of covariant derivatives and is gauge-invariant.

  • In electromagnetism, \(F_{ij}\) encodes the electric and magnetic fields.

  • Parallel transport along a path is described by the Wilson line; phase accumulated around a closed loop equals the flux of the field strength through the surface.

  • Non-abelian gauge theories have self-interacting fields due to \([A_i, A_j]\) terms in the field strength.

Homework#

1. Consider a gauge transformation with parameter \(\chi(x) = qx\) (position-dependent phase). If the original vector potential is \(A_i = 0\), what is the transformed potential \(A_i'\) in each spatial direction? Is this transformed potential physically observable?

2. Two particles have the same wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) but are subject to different gauge choices. Particle A experiences potential \(\boldsymbol{A}_A(x)\), particle B experiences \(\boldsymbol{A}_B(x)\). Both particles move along the same path from \(x_1\) to \(x_2\). Explain how the phase difference between their wavefunctions depends on the difference in potentials along the path. (Hint: consider the path-ordered exponential of the connection.)

3. Derive the commutator \([D_i, D_j]\) acting on a scalar wavefunction \(\psi(x)\). Show all steps and verify that you recover \(F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i\) for abelian gauge fields. Why does the commutator not depend on the wavefunction itself?

4. Parallel transport around a small square loop in the \(xy\)-plane. The loop has side length \(\epsilon\) centered at the origin. (a) Calculate the total accumulated phase by integrating the connection around the loop. (b) Show that to leading order in \(\epsilon\), the result is \(q F_{xy} \epsilon^2\). (c) Interpret this result physically: what does it mean if \(F_{xy} \neq 0\)?

5. Consider uniform constant magnetic field \(\boldsymbol{B} = B\hat{z}\) in electromagnetism. (a) Propose a gauge choice for the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) (Cartesian coordinates). (b) Verify that your choice reproduces the correct \(\boldsymbol{B}\) via the relation \(\boldsymbol{B} = \nabla \times \boldsymbol{A}\). (c) Now propose a different gauge choice that also gives the same \(\boldsymbol{B}\). (d) Show explicitly that the field strength tensor \(F_{ij}\) is identical in both gauges.

6. In a certain region of space, \(F_{xy} = B_0\) (constant) and all other components of the field strength tensor vanish. (a) What is the corresponding electromagnetic field configuration? (b) Is there a gauge choice in which \(A_z = 0\)? If so, what are the remaining non-zero components of \(\boldsymbol{A}\)?

7. Suppose a charged particle’s wavefunction is \(\psi(x, y) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi(x,y)}\) where \(\phi\) is some smooth phase function. The covariant derivatives are \(D_x \psi = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi}(\partial_x + iqA_x)\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\phi} \cdot \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi}\) and similarly for \(D_y\). (a) Compute the commutator \([D_x, D_y]\psi\) in terms of \(\partial_x\phi\), \(\partial_y\phi\), and the field strength. (b) Show that the result is independent of the choice of \(\phi\) (i.e., independent of how we write the phase). What does this tell you about gauge invariance?

8. A non-abelian gauge field has connection components \(A_i = A_i^a T_a\) where \(T_a\) are Lie algebra generators (matrices) and \([T_a, T_b] \neq 0\). The field strength is \(F_{ij} = \partial_i A_j - \partial_j A_i + \frac{1}{iq}[A_i, A_j]\). (a) Show that the extra commutator term \([A_i, A_j]\) arises naturally from the commutator of covariant derivatives when the gauge fields do not commute. (b) Explain why this term makes the theory nonlinear (why interactions between field components become important). (c) Does this term affect gauge invariance of \(F_{ij}\)? Justify your answer.

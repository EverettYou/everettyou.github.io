5.1.3 Degenerate Perturbation Theory#

Prompts

  • Why does non-degenerate perturbation theory fail when multiple unperturbed states share the same energy, and what is the minimal fix?

  • In a degenerate subspace, why must we diagonalize the perturbation first, and how does this define the correct zeroth-order basis?

  • What is the physical difference between first-order splitting inside the degenerate manifold and second-order shifts from virtual coupling to outside states?

  • For a concrete model, how can you diagnose whether degeneracy is fully lifted, partially lifted, or unchanged at first order, and what should be done next in each case?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Sec. 5.1.2 assumed isolated levels and produced scalar corrections. This subsection handles the complementary case: when unperturbed levels are degenerate, perturbation theory must be organized as a block problem inside each degenerate manifold.

The practical workflow is: identify the degenerate manifold, diagonalize in-manifold matrix elements at first order, then add virtual-coupling corrections from outside manifolds at second order.

Why non-degenerate formulas fail#

For \(\hat{H}(\lambda)=\hat{H}_0+\lambda\hat{V}\), suppose \(\hat{H}_0\) has a \(d_n\)-fold degenerate eigenspace at energy \(E_n\), spanned by \(|n,\alpha\rangle\) (\(\alpha=1,\dots,d_n\)):

\[ \hat{H}_0|n,\alpha\rangle=E_n|n,\alpha\rangle. \]

A quick wrong attempt is to reuse the non-degenerate structure directly:

\[ |n,\alpha\rangle^{(1)}\sim\sum_{(m,\beta)\neq(n,\alpha)}|m,\beta\rangle\frac{(\cdots)}{E_n-E_m}. \]

Then terms with \(m=n\) but \(\beta\neq\alpha\) give

\[ E_n-E_m=E_n-E_n=0, \]

so the denominator diverges.

Core Idea

The proper strategy is divide and conquer:

  • reorganize the Hilbert space into degenerate-manifold blocks,

  • construct the effective Hamiltonian for each block,

  • diagonalize each block,

  • if residual degeneracy remains, continue to the next order/block level.

The central goal of degenerate perturbation theory is to construct the effective Hamiltonian within each degenerate subspace.

Problem Setup#

Degenerate Perturbation Problem

Consider \(\hat H(\lambda)=\hat H_0+\lambda\hat V\), and work in a basis \(|n,\alpha\rangle\) where \(n\) labels the manifold and \(\alpha=1,\dots,d_n\) labels orthonormal states inside that manifold:

\[ \hat H_0=\sum_n\sum_\alpha E_n\,|n,\alpha\rangle\langle n,\alpha|. \]
\[ \hat V=\sum_{m,\alpha}\sum_{n,\beta}|m,\alpha\rangle V_{m\alpha,n\beta}\langle n,\beta|. \]

Here \(V_{m\alpha,n\beta}=\langle m,\alpha|\hat V|n,\beta\rangle\), and states inside one degenerate manifold can mix.

The corresponding eigenvalue equation is \(\hat H(\lambda)|n,\beta(\lambda)\rangle=\sum_\alpha E_{n,\alpha\beta}(\lambda)|n,\alpha(\lambda)\rangle\), and our objective is to construct \(E_{n,\alpha\beta}(\lambda)\) and \(|n,\alpha(\lambda)\rangle\) order by order in \(\lambda\).

Hellmann-Feynman Identities (Degenerate Form)#

Differentiate the matrix eigenproblem and project onto \(\langle m,\gamma|\) at \(\lambda=0\):

\[ \langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda\hat H|n,\beta\rangle =\partial_\lambda E_{n,\gamma\beta}\,\delta_{mn} +(E_n-E_m)\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle. \]

Hellmann-Feynman identities (degenerate)

  • 1st Hellmann-Feynman Identity (energy matrix derivative, within manifold):

\[ \partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}=V_{n\alpha,n\beta}. \]

  • 2nd Hellmann-Feynman Identity (state-mixing derivative, across manifolds):

\[ \langle m,\alpha|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\frac{V_{m\alpha,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m} \text{ for }m\neq n. \]

Derivation: degenerate Hellmann-Feynman identities

Step 1: Differentiate the matrix eigenvalue equation

Start from

\[ \hat H(\lambda)|n,\beta(\lambda)\rangle =\sum_\alpha E_{n,\alpha\beta}(\lambda)\,|n,\alpha(\lambda)\rangle. \]

Differentiate with respect to \(\lambda\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \partial_\lambda\hat H\,|n,\beta\rangle+\hat H\,|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\sum_\alpha\Big[ (\partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta})|n,\alpha\rangle +E_{n,\alpha\beta}|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle \Big]. \end{aligned} \]

Step 2: Project with \(\langle m,\gamma|\) and evaluate at \(\lambda=0\)

Left-multiply by \(\langle m,\gamma|\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda\hat H|n,\beta\rangle +\langle m,\gamma|\hat H|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\sum_\alpha\Big[ \partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}\,\langle m,\gamma|n,\alpha\rangle +E_{n,\alpha\beta}\,\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle \Big]. \end{aligned} \]

Evaluate at \(\lambda=0\):

\[ V_{m\gamma,n\beta}+E_m\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\delta_{mn}\,\partial_\lambda E_{n,\gamma\beta}+E_n\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle. \]

So

\[ V_{m\gamma,n\beta} =\delta_{mn}\,\partial_\lambda E_{n,\gamma\beta} +(E_n-E_m)\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle. \]

Step 3: Split into \(m=n\) and \(m\neq n\)

If \(m=n\),

\[ \partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}=V_{n\alpha,n\beta}. \]

If \(m\neq n\),

\[ \langle m,\alpha|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\frac{V_{m\alpha,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m}. \]

These are the two degenerate Hellmann-Feynman identities used below.

Energy Corrections#

Use the Taylor expansion of the in-manifold energy matrix:

Energy matrix expansion up to second order

Using degenerate Hellmann-Feynman identities, the energy matrix correction is given by:

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} E_{n,\alpha\beta}(\lambda) &=E_n\delta_{\alpha\beta} +\lambda\,\partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta} +\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\,\partial_\lambda^2 E_{n,\alpha\beta} +O(\lambda^3)\\ &=E_n\delta_{\alpha\beta} +\lambda V_{n\alpha,n\beta} +\lambda^2\sum_{m\neq n}\sum_\gamma \frac{V_{n\alpha,m\gamma}V_{m\gamma,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m} +O(\lambda^3). \end{split} \end{split}\]

Derivation: first-order energy matrix derivative

Set \(m=n\) in the projected derivative equation:

\[ \langle n,\alpha|\partial_\lambda \hat H|n,\beta\rangle =\partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}. \]

With \(\partial_\lambda\hat H=\hat V\), this gives

\[ \partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}=V_{n\alpha,n\beta}. \]

Derivation: second-order energy matrix derivative

Differentiate \(\partial_\lambda E_{n,\alpha\beta}=\langle n,\alpha|\hat V|n,\beta\rangle\) once more:

\[ \partial_\lambda^2 E_{n,\alpha\beta} =\langle\partial_\lambda n,\alpha|\hat V|n,\beta\rangle +\langle n,\alpha|\hat V|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle. \]

Insert a complete basis explicitly:

\[ \partial_\lambda^2 E_{n,\alpha\beta} =\sum_{m,\gamma}\Big(\langle\partial_\lambda n,\alpha|m,\gamma\rangle V_{m\gamma,n\beta} +V_{n\alpha,m\gamma}\langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle\Big). \]

Now split into \(m\neq n\) and \(m=n\) pieces. For \(m\neq n\), use the second Hellmann-Feynman identity:

\[ \langle m,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle =\frac{V_{m\gamma,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m}, \]

and its conjugate relation, giving

\[ 2\sum_{m\neq n}\sum_\gamma \frac{V_{n\alpha,m\gamma}V_{m\gamma,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m}. \]

For \(m=n\), terms involve in-manifold overlaps \(A_{\gamma\beta}=\langle n,\gamma|\partial_\lambda n,\beta\rangle\). These are gauge/basis-choice dependent within the degenerate manifold and can be set to zero by parallel-transport gauge after fixing the first-order in-manifold basis.

Therefore

\[ \partial_\lambda^2 E_{n,\alpha\beta} =2\sum_{m\neq n}\sum_\gamma \frac{V_{n\alpha,m\gamma}V_{m\gamma,n\beta}}{E_n-E_m}. \]

State Corrections#

The corrected basis vectors are also expanded in \(\lambda\).

State expansion and first-order mixing

Using the second Hellmann-Feynman identity, the state correction is given by:

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} |n,\alpha(\lambda)\rangle &=|n,\alpha\rangle +\lambda|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle +O(\lambda^2)\\ &=|n,\alpha\rangle +\lambda\sum_{m\neq n}\sum_\beta |m,\beta\rangle\frac{V_{m\beta,n\alpha}}{E_n-E_m} +O(\lambda^2). \end{split} \end{split}\]

Derivation: first-order state derivative

Expand in the unperturbed basis and separate the two parts:

\[ |\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle =\sum_{\beta}|n,\beta\rangle\langle n,\beta|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle +\sum_{m\neq n,\beta}|m,\beta\rangle\langle m,\beta|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle. \]

For \(m\neq n\), apply

\[ \langle m,\beta|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle =\frac{V_{m\beta,n\alpha}}{E_n-E_m}. \]

For \(m=n\), define in-manifold overlaps \(A_{\beta\alpha}=\langle n,\beta|\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle\). From orthonormality, \(A\) is anti-Hermitian, and by basis/gauge choice inside the degenerate manifold (after first-order diagonalization) one may set \(A=0\).

Under that gauge,

\[ |\partial_\lambda n,\alpha\rangle =\sum_{m\neq n,\beta}|m,\beta\rangle\frac{V_{m\beta,n\alpha}}{E_n-E_m}. \]

Note: Operator Form

The same result can be written with projectors \(\hat{P}_n=\sum_\alpha |n,\alpha\rangle\langle n,\alpha|\) and \(\hat{Q}_n=\hat I-\hat P_n\):

\[ \hat H^{\text{eff}}_n =E_n\hat I_d +\lambda\hat P_n\hat V\hat P_n +\lambda^2\hat P_n\hat V\hat Q_n \frac{1}{E_n-\hat Q_n\hat H_0\hat Q_n} \hat Q_n\hat V\hat P_n +O(\lambda^3). \]

This is exactly the same content as the component formulas above:

  • \(\hat P_n\hat V\hat P_n \leftrightarrow V_{n\alpha,n\beta}\) (first-order block),

  • second-order projector term \(\leftrightarrow \sum_{m\neq n,\gamma}V_{n\alpha,m\gamma}V_{m\gamma,n\beta}/(E_n-E_m)\).

Use component form for calculations; use operator form as a compact summary.

Example: Three-level bright/dark mechanism

Problem. Consider

\[ \hat H_0=\Delta|3\rangle\langle3|, \]
\[ \hat V=\lambda_1|1\rangle\langle3|+\lambda_2|2\rangle\langle3|+\mathrm{h.c.}, \]

with \(|\lambda_{1,2}|\ll\Delta\). The degenerate manifold is \(\{|1\rangle,|2\rangle\}\) at energy \(0\).

Step 1. First-order block check. Inside the manifold,

\[ (H^{\text{eff}})_{ij}^{(1)}=\langle i|\hat V|j\rangle=0,\qquad i,j\in\{1,2\}, \]

so first-order splitting is absent.

Step 2. Build second-order block element-by-element. Because the only outside state is \(|3\rangle\) with energy \(\Delta\),

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} (H^{\text{eff}})_{ij}^{(2)} &=\sum_{m\notin\{1,2\}}\frac{\langle i|\hat V|m\rangle\langle m|\hat V|j\rangle}{0-E_m}\\ &=-\frac{1}{\Delta}\langle i|\hat V|3\rangle\langle3|\hat V|j\rangle. \end{split} \end{split}\]

Now evaluate four entries explicitly:

\[ (H^{\text{eff}})_{11}^{(2)}=-\frac{|\lambda_1|^2}{\Delta}, \qquad (H^{\text{eff}})_{22}^{(2)}=-\frac{|\lambda_2|^2}{\Delta}, \]
\[ (H^{\text{eff}})_{12}^{(2)}=-\frac{\lambda_1\lambda_2^*}{\Delta}, \qquad (H^{\text{eff}})_{21}^{(2)}=-\frac{\lambda_2\lambda_1^*}{\Delta}. \]

Hence

\[\begin{split} \hat H^{\text{eff}}=-\frac{1}{\Delta} \begin{pmatrix} |\lambda_1|^2 & \lambda_1\lambda_2^*\\ \lambda_2\lambda_1^* & |\lambda_2|^2 \end{pmatrix} =-\frac{1}{\Delta}|\lambda\rangle\langle\lambda|, \end{split}\]

with \(|\lambda\rangle=\lambda_1|1\rangle+\lambda_2|2\rangle\).

Step 3. Diagonalize and identify bright/dark states. This is a rank-1 projector form, so eigenvalues are immediate:

\[ E_B=-\frac{|\lambda_1|^2+|\lambda_2|^2}{\Delta}, \qquad E_D=0. \]

A normalized bright state is

\[ |B\rangle= \frac{\lambda_1|1\rangle+\lambda_2|2\rangle}{\sqrt{|\lambda_1|^2+|\lambda_2|^2}}, \]

and a normalized dark state orthogonal to \(|B\rangle\) is

\[ |D\rangle= \frac{\lambda_2^*|1\rangle-\lambda_1^*|2\rangle}{\sqrt{|\lambda_1|^2+|\lambda_2|^2}}. \]

Step 4. Exact zero-mode check (not only perturbative).

\[ \hat H_0|D\rangle=0, \qquad \hat V|D\rangle=0, \]

so \((\hat H_0+\hat V)|D\rangle=0\) exactly.

Interpretation. First-order splitting is absent, but second-order virtual coupling generates an effective interaction with one bright mode and one protected dark mode.

Example: Spin-1 splitting from a degenerate manifold

Problem. Consider a spin-1 system in basis \(\{|+1\rangle,|0\rangle,|-1\rangle\}\) with

\[ \hat H(\lambda)=\hat H_0+\lambda\hat V, \qquad \hat H_0=(\hat S^z)^2, \qquad \hat V=\hat S^x+\hat S^z. \]

The operator matrices are

\[\begin{split} \hat S^x\simeq \frac{1}{\sqrt2} \begin{pmatrix} 0&1&0\\ 1&0&1\\ 0&1&0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \hat S^z\simeq \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\ 0&0&0\\ 0&0&-1 \end{pmatrix}, \end{split}\]

so

\[\begin{split} \hat H_0\simeq \begin{pmatrix} 1&0&0\\ 0&0&0\\ 0&0&1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \hat V\simeq \begin{pmatrix} 1&\frac{1}{\sqrt2}&0\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt2}&0&\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\\ 0&\frac{1}{\sqrt2}&-1 \end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

Unperturbed manifolds: \(E_1=1\) with \(\{|+1\rangle,|-1\rangle\}\) (degenerate), and \(E_0=0\) with \(|0\rangle\).

Step 1 (degenerate PT): construct effective blocks.

For the \(E_1\) manifold, to \(O(\lambda^2)\),

\[\begin{split} \hat H^{\text{eff}}_{1}\simeq \begin{pmatrix} 1+\lambda+\frac{\lambda^2}{2} & \frac{\lambda^2}{2}\\ \frac{\lambda^2}{2} & 1-\lambda+\frac{\lambda^2}{2} \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

(on the first-round corrected basis). For the \(E_0\) manifold,

\[ \hat H^{\text{eff}}_{0}\simeq(-\lambda^2). \]

At this stage, the original degeneracy is already lifted at \(O(\lambda)\).

Step 2 (second round: non-degenerate PT inside the lifted block).

In \(\hat H^{\text{eff}}_{1}\), the off-diagonal element is \(\lambda^2/2\), while the diagonal splitting is

\[ \Delta E'=(1+\lambda+\lambda^2/2)-(1-\lambda+\lambda^2/2)=2\lambda. \]

So the induced mixing scale is

\[ \frac{\lambda^2/2}{\Delta E'}=\frac{\lambda}{4}=O(\lambda). \]

Hence the second-round state correction is \(O(\lambda)\), but the induced energy correction is

\[ \frac{(\lambda^2/2)^2}{\Delta E'}\sim O(\lambda^3), \]

so it does not modify results through \(O(\lambda^2)\).

Therefore, up to \(O(\lambda^2)\),

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} E_{+1}&=1+\lambda+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}+O(\lambda^3),\\ E_{-1}&=1-\lambda+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}+O(\lambda^3),\\ E_0&=-\lambda^2+O(\lambda^3). \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

A representative first-order state structure is

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} |\pm1\rangle' &= |\pm1\rangle + \frac{\lambda}{\sqrt2}|0\rangle + O(\lambda^2),\\ |0\rangle' &= |0\rangle - \frac{\lambda}{\sqrt2}(|+1\rangle+|-1\rangle)+O(\lambda^2). \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Interpretation. This example shows why degenerate perturbation theory is naturally hierarchical: first build and diagonalize effective blocks, then (if needed) iterate with non-degenerate perturbation inside the lifted blocks.

Discussion

If diagonalizing \(V_{n\alpha,n\beta}\) still leaves repeated eigenvalues, first order is not enough. Then inspect second-order block terms (and symmetry constraints) to decide whether residual degeneracy is lifted.

Summary#

  • Degenerate perturbation theory is a block algorithm: solve each degenerate manifold before applying inter-manifold corrections.

  • First-order splitting comes from diagonalizing \(V_{n\alpha,n\beta}\) inside the manifold.

  • Second-order shifts come from virtual transitions to outside manifolds and back.

  • The same physics can be written in component form or projector form.

\[ \hat V=\mu\,(|1\rangle\langle 2|+|2\rangle\langle 1|)+\lambda\,(|3\rangle\langle 1|+|3\rangle\langle 2|+\mathrm{h.c.}), \]

with real \(\mu,\lambda\) and \(|\mu|,|\lambda|\ll\Delta\).

(a) Compute \(\hat P_d\hat V\hat P_d\) in \(\{|1\rangle,|2\rangle\}\), where \(\hat P_d\) projects onto the degenerate subspace. Read off the first-order splitting.

(b) Set \(\mu=0\). Build the second-order effective Hamiltonian \(\hat H^{(2)}_{\rm eff}\) in \(\{|1\rangle,|2\rangle\}\) from virtual transitions through \(|3\rangle\). Diagonalize and identify the bright state with shift \(-2\lambda^2/\Delta\) and the dark state with zero shift.

(c) Still with \(\mu=0\), show that the dark state is an exact zero-energy eigenstate of \(\hat H_0+\hat V\) (not only at second order), and explain in one sentence why.

(a) Use selection rules \(\Delta\ell=\pm1\), \(\Delta m=0\) to write the effective matrix structure.

(b) Explain why two states split linearly while two remain unsplit at first order.

(a) find eigenvalues,

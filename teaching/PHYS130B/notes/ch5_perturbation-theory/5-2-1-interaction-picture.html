5.2.1 Interaction Picture#

Prompts

  • What is the interaction picture and why is it useful for revealing the slow dynamics in time-dependent problems?

  • How does choosing a rotating reference frame (interaction picture) eliminate rapid oscillations from the free Hamiltonian?

  • In the interaction picture, the equation of motion becomes simpler — what role does the interaction-picture perturbation \(V_I(t)\) play?

  • What is the Dyson series, and why is time ordering essential for computing the evolution operator?

  • Can you give an example of how the time-ordered exponential \(\text{T} \mathrm{e}^{-\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\int V_I(t')dt'}\) encodes the physics of successive interactions?

Lecture Notes#

When quantum systems interact with external fields that change in time—whether oscillating electromagnetic radiation or engineered control pulses—the full dynamics become intractable. The interaction picture offers a brilliant mathematical perspective: by choosing a reference frame that rotates with the unperturbed evolution, we strip away the rapid oscillations and expose only the slow, physically significant interactions. This reformulation transforms time-dependent problems from impossible to solvable.

The Interaction Picture#

Definition and Transformations#

In the interaction picture, we separate the Hamiltonian into two parts:

  • Free/unperturbed part \(H_0\): evolution is rapid but exactly solvable (oscillates at natural frequencies \(\omega_n = E_n^{(0)}/\hbar\))

  • Interaction part \(V(t)\): slow compared to \(H_0\) (weak coupling regime)

Key Transformations

States transform as:

(212)#\[ |\psi_I(t)\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi_S(t)\rangle\]

Operators transform as:

(213)#\[ O_I(t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} O_S \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\]

Physical meaning: Multiplying by \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\) factors out the rapid oscillations from \(H_0\), leaving only the slow dynamics driven by \(V(t)\).

Equation of Motion in the Interaction Picture#

The interaction-picture state obeys a much simpler equation:

(214)#\[ \boxed{\mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = \lambda V_I(t) |\psi_I\rangle}\]

where the interaction-picture perturbation is:

(215)#\[ \boxed{V_I(t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} V(t) \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}}\]

Derivation: Interaction Picture Equation

Start with the Schrödinger equation:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi_S\rangle}{dt} = H(t)|\psi_S\rangle = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]|\psi_S\rangle\]

Transform to the interaction picture using \(|\psi_S\rangle = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\):

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\right) = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

Expand the left side using the product rule:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\left(-\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}H_0|\psi_I\rangle + \frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi_I\rangle}{dt}\right) = [H_0 + \lambda V(t)]\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

Simplify:

\[ H_0 \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle + \mathrm{i}\hbar \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = H_0 \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle + \lambda V(t)\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle\]

The \(H_0\) terms cancel, and multiply both sides by \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\):

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\mathrm{d}|\psi_I\rangle}{dt} = \lambda \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} V(t) \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}|\psi_I\rangle = \lambda V_I(t)|\psi_I\rangle\]

Physical content: \(V_I(t)\) is the perturbation as viewed from a frame rotating with the free evolution. Rapid oscillations (at natural frequencies \(\sim E_n^{(0)}/\hbar\)) are removed, revealing only the slow dynamics.

Eigenbasis Expansion and Resonance#

Expand \(V_I(t)\) in the eigenbasis of \(H_0\):

(216)#\[ V_I(t) = \sum_{n,m} \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{nm}t} V_{nm}(t) |n\rangle\langle m|\]

where the transition frequency is:

(217)#\[ \omega_{nm} = \frac{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}{\hbar}\]

Resonance Interpretation

The oscillatory factor \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{nm}t}\) encodes resonance:

  • When \(\omega_{nm} \approx 0\): Initial and final states have nearly equal energy. Coupling is strong, and transitions occur efficiently.

  • When \(|\omega_{nm}|\) is large: States are far apart in energy. Rapid oscillations suppress the coupling (off-resonant).

This insight motivates the rotating wave approximation: only near-resonant transitions (with \(|\omega_{nm}| \lesssim 1/T\)) contribute significantly to the dynamics over time \(T\).

The Evolution Operator and Dyson Series#

Formal Solution#

Define the interaction-picture evolution operator \(U_I(t)\) such that:

(218)#\[ |\psi_I(t)\rangle = U_I(t) |\psi_I(0)\rangle\]

This operator has the formal solution:

(219)#\[ \boxed{U_I(t) = \mathcal{T} \exp\left(-\frac{\mathrm{i}\lambda}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt' V_I(t')\right)}\]

This is the time-ordered exponential. Expanding it gives the Dyson series:

(220)#\[ \boxed{U_I(t) = \mathbb{I} + \frac{\lambda}{\mathrm{i}\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) + \left(\frac{\lambda}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}\right)^2 \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2) + \cdots}\]

Physical Meaning#

Interpreting the Dyson Series

Each term in the expansion represents a different “scattering” process:

  • Zeroth order (\(\lambda^0\)): No interaction; system stays in initial state.

  • First order (\(\lambda^1\)): One interaction event at time \(t_1\).

  • Second order (\(\lambda^2\)): Two interaction events at times \(t_1 > t_2\) (time-ordered).

  • Higher orders: Multiple interaction events, always ordered in time (\(t_1 > t_2 > t_3 > \ldots\)).

Each term can be visualized as Feynman diagrams in scattering theory.

Derivation: How Time Ordering Emerges#

Derivation

From the equation of motion:

\[ \mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial U_I}{\partial t} = \lambda V_I(t) U_I(t), \quad U_I(0) = \mathbb{I}\]

Integrate formally:

\[ U_I(t) = \mathbb{I} + \frac{1}{\mathrm{i}\hbar} \lambda \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) U_I(t_1)\]

This is a Volterra integral equation. Solve iteratively by substituting the expression for \(U_I\) back into the integral:

First order:

\[ U_I^{(1)}(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\mathrm{i}\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1)\]

Second order:

\[ U_I^{(2)}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}\right)^2 \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1) \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_2)\]

The integral limits automatically enforce time ordering: when substituting \(U_I(t_1)\) into the outer integral, the inner integration runs from \(0\) to \(t_1\), ensuring \(t_1 > t_2\). This pattern continues for higher orders.

Summing all orders:

\[ U_I(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{\lambda}{\mathrm{i}\hbar}\right)^n \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 \cdots \int_0^{t_{n-1}} dt_n \, V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2) \cdots V_I(t_n)\]

This is precisely the expansion of the time-ordered exponential.

Time Ordering and Causality#

The integral limits \(t > t_1 > t_2 > \ldots\) ensure causal time ordering: interactions are processed in sequence, with later times to the left.

Why Time Ordering Matters

In general, \([V_I(t_1), V_I(t_2)] \neq 0\) (operators do not commute). The order in which interactions occur is physical and affects the result. Time ordering enforces the natural causal sequence.

For operators \(A(t_1)\) and \(B(t_2)\) with \(t_1 > t_2\), the time-ordering operator \(\mathcal{T}\) is defined as:

\[ \mathcal{T}[A(t_1) B(t_2)] = A(t_1) B(t_2), \quad \mathcal{T}[B(t_2) A(t_1)] = A(t_1) B(t_2) \quad (t_1 > t_2)\]

Full Schrödinger-Picture Evolution#

The Schrödinger-picture evolution operator \(U_S(t)\) factorizes beautifully:

(221)#\[ \boxed{U_S(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} U_I(t)}\]

This decomposition is remarkably powerful:

  • The first factor \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\) handles the fast, high-frequency \(H_0\) oscillations.

  • The second factor \(U_I(t)\) (via the Dyson series) captures the slow interactions.

Together, they build up the full interacting dynamics without requiring direct solution of the complete Hamiltonian.

Perturbative Expansion and Transition Amplitudes#

Order-by-Order Structure#

Expand the evolution operator in powers of \(\lambda\):

(222)#\[ U_I(t) = U_I^{(0)}(t) + \lambda U_I^{(1)}(t) + \lambda^2 U_I^{(2)}(t) + \ldots\]

Zeroth order (\(\lambda = 0\)):

\[ U_I^{(0)}(t) = \mathbb{I} \quad \text{(no interaction)}\]

First order (\(\lambda^1\)): One interaction at time \(t_1\).

\[ U_I^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 V_I(t_1)\]

Second order (\(\lambda^2\)): Two interactions at times \(t_1, t_2\) with \(t_1 > t_2\).

\[ U_I^{(2)}(t) = -\frac{1}{\hbar^2} \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t_1} dt_2 V_I(t_1) V_I(t_2)\]

Higher orders follow this pattern: more interaction times, more nested integrals, time-ordered products.

Transition Amplitudes and Resonance#

The transition amplitude from state \(|i\rangle\) to \(|f\rangle\) is:

(223)#\[ \mathcal{A}_{i\to f}(t) = \langle f | U_S(t) | i \rangle\]

To first order in \(\lambda\):

\[ \mathcal{A}_{i\to f}^{(0)}(t) = \delta_{fi} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_f^{(0)} t/\hbar}\]
(224)#\[\mathcal{A}_{i\to f}^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar} \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}E_f^{(0)} t/\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_{fi}t_1} \langle f | V(t_1) | i \rangle\]

where \(\omega_{fi} = (E_f^{(0)} - E_i^{(0)})/\hbar\) is the transition frequency.

Virtual Processes and Higher Orders#

Higher-order terms in the Dyson series correspond to virtual intermediate states:

  • First order: Direct \(i \to f\) transition.

  • Second order: \(i \to n \to f\) (via intermediate state \(n\)), where state \(n\) is visited transiently.

  • Higher orders: Chains of intermediate transitions.

The time integrals in the Dyson series encode energy conservation on average: the system is not required to have fixed energy during intermediate steps (virtual processes), but the overall energy is conserved when integrated over all interaction times.

Summary#

Key Concepts

  • Interaction picture: Separates fast (solved by \(\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\)) and slow (perturbative \(V_I(t)\)) dynamics.

  • Interaction-picture perturbation: \(V_I(t) = \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} V(t) \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar}\), with oscillatory terms \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{nm}t}\) encoding resonances.

  • Evolution operator: \(U_I(t) = \mathcal{T} \exp(-\mathrm{i} \int V_I dt / \hbar)\) (time-ordered exponential).

  • Dyson series: Systematic expansion showing interaction as sequence of events at ordered times.

  • Full evolution: \(U_S(t) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}H_0 t/\hbar} U_I(t)\) factorizes free propagation and interactions.

  • Transition amplitudes: Depend on oscillatory factors \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{fi}t}\); resonance (\(\omega_{fi} \approx 0\)) enhances transitions.

  • Causality: Time ordering ensures that interactions occur in the correct physical sequence.

  • Virtual processes: Higher-order terms represent intermediate state transitions with average energy conservation.

Discussion: Limits of the Rotating Wave Approximation

The interaction picture separates \(H = H_0 + V\) and moves to a rotating frame where fast oscillations are removed. In the RWA, we keep only “resonant” terms.

  • For a driven two-level atom (\(\omega_0\) = atomic transition, \(\omega_L\) = laser), what is the timescale on which non-resonant (“counter-rotating”) terms become important?

  • If the detuning \(\Delta = \omega_L - \omega_0\) is very large, does the RWA break down? Explain physically.

  • Propose a calculation to quantify when the RWA error exceeds 10%.

  • Why does the rotating frame make the solution simpler—what physics does it obscure?

2. The interaction-picture perturbation

(a) Write the Schrödinger-picture perturbation \(V(t)\) in the energy eigenbasis:

\[V(t) = \sum_{m,n} V_{mn}(t) |m\rangle\langle n|\]

(b) Transform to the interaction picture. Show that:

\[V_I(t) = \sum_{m,n} V_{mn}(t) \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{mn}t} |m\rangle\langle n|\]

where \(\omega_{mn} = (E_m^{(0)} - E_n^{(0)})/\hbar\).

(c) Interpret this result: why do matrix elements acquire oscillatory phase factors at transition frequencies?

3. The time-evolution operator in the interaction picture

Define \(U_I(t, t_0)\) such that \(|\psi_I(t)\rangle = U_I(t, t_0)|\psi_I(t_0)\rangle\).

(a) Derive the equation of motion for \(U_I(t, t_0)\).

(b) Show that \(U_I\) satisfies the Dyson series (time-ordered exponential):

\[U_I(t, t_0) = \mathcal{T} \exp\left(-\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\int_{t_0}^{t} \lambda V_I(t') \mathrm{d}t'\right)\]

(c) Expand the time-ordered exponential to second order in \(\lambda\) and verify that it correctly satisfies the differential equation.

4. Rabi oscillations in a two-level system

A two-level atom with ground state \(|0\rangle\) (energy \(E_0 = 0\)) and excited state \(|1\rangle\) (energy \(E_1 = \hbar\omega_0\)) is driven by a resonant field \(V(t) = \hbar\Omega \sigma^x \cos(\omega_0 t)\), where \(\sigma^x = |0\rangle\langle 1| + |1\rangle\langle 0|\) and \(\Omega\) is the Rabi frequency.

(a) Decompose \(V(t)\) into rotating and counter-rotating terms. Which term dominates near resonance?

(b) In the interaction picture with \(H_0 = \hbar\omega_0 |1\rangle\langle 1|\), show that \(V_I(t) \approx \hbar\Omega \sigma^x\) (rotating-wave approximation).

(c) Write down the interaction-picture Schrödinger equation for the state \(|\psi_I(t)\rangle = a_0(t)|0\rangle + a_1(t)|1\rangle\).

(d) Solve to find the time evolution. Show that the population oscillates as:

\[P_1(t) = \sin^2(\Omega t)\]

6. Dyson series and time ordering

(a) Compute the first-order perturbation correction to \(U_I(2t_1, 0)\) by hand (integral without time-ordering).

(b) Compare with the time-ordered result from the Dyson series. Where does the difference appear, and why is time ordering essential for causality?

(c) If the two intervals had \(V(t)\) with different operators \(V_1(t)\) and \(V_2(t)\), would they commute? How does time-ordering handle the non-commutativity?

7. Eigenstate-perturbation correspondence

(a) Show that the interaction-picture amplitude for transition \(n \to m\) is:

\[a_m(t) = -\frac{\mathrm{i}}{\hbar}\int_0^t \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\omega_{mn}t'} V_{mn}(t') a_n(t') \mathrm{d}t'\]

(b) In the weak-coupling limit where \(a_n(t) \approx 1\) remains nearly constant, integrate to find the transition probability \(P_{n \to m}(t) = |a_m(t)|^2\) to first order in \(V\).

(c) For a constant coupling \(V_{mn} = V_0\), compute \(P_{n \to m}(t)\). Interpret the result in terms of resonance width and Fourier components of the coupling.

5.1.2 Non-Degenerate Perturbation Theory#

Prompts

  • What is the Hellmann-Feynman theorem, and why does it appear at the heart of perturbation theory? How does it relate energy changes to expectation values?

  • Derive the first-order energy correction and explain what it physically represents. Why is it just the expectation value of the perturbation?

  • In second order, why does the energy correction always lower the ground state energy? What does this tell you about the effect of perturbations?

  • What is state hybridization, and how does the size of energy gaps control whether two states mix strongly or weakly?

  • When does non-degenerate perturbation theory fail? What happens to the series when two levels approach degeneracy, and how should you handle this situation?

First-Order Energy Correction

The energy shift to first order in \(\lambda\) is simply the expectation value of the perturbation in the unperturbed state:

\[ \boxed{{E_n^{{(1)}} = \langle \psi_n^{{(0)}} | V | \psi_n^{{(0)}} \rangle}}\]

Physical interpretation:

  • The first-order correction is the diagonal matrix element of \(V\) in the unperturbed eigenbasis

  • It measures the average energy shift due to the perturbation

  • States with no diagonal coupling (e.g., odd-parity \(V\) acting on an even-parity state) experience zero first-order shift and must be treated to second order

  • From the Hellmann-Feynman theorem: \(\frac{{\mathrm{{d}}E_n}}{{\mathrm{{d}}\lambda}}\big|_{{\lambda=0}} = E_n^{{(1)}}\)

Example: For a qubit perturbed by \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) (which has zero diagonal elements in the computational basis), the first-order energy is exactly zero.

Hellmann-Feynman Theorem

A cornerstone result connecting energy derivatives to expectation values:

\[ \boxed{{\frac{{\mathrm{{d}}E_n(\lambda)}}{{\mathrm{{d}}\lambda}} = \langle \psi_n(\lambda) | \frac{{\partial H}}{{\partial \lambda}} | \psi_n(\lambda) \rangle}}\]

Physical meaning: The rate at which the eigenvalue changes with a parameter is exactly the expectation value of the parameter’s derivative in the eigenstate.

At \(\lambda = 0\) with \(\frac{{\partial H}}{{\partial \lambda}} = V\):

\[ \frac{{\mathrm{{d}}E_n}}{{\mathrm{{d}}\lambda}}\bigg|_{{\lambda=0}} = \langle \psi_n^{{(0)}} | V | \psi_n^{{(0)}} \rangle = E_n^{{(1)}}\]

Consequence: The first-order perturbation theory result follows directly from this theorem without expanding eigenstates explicitly.

Requires: The adiabatic continuity assumption — that eigenstates can be labeled continuously as \(\lambda\) varies (no level crossings in the parameter range of interest).

Lecture Notes#

The central strategy of non-degenerate perturbation theory is to surgically separate a quantum system’s evolution into two parts: the rapid, familiar oscillations of a solved Hamiltonian \(H_0\), and the slow, subtle rearrangement caused by a perturbation \(V\). This separation exposes a fundamental principle: energy gaps act as barriers to state mixing, with strong hybridization happening near resonance and exponential suppression far from it. The result is a systematic, order-by-order algorithm for computing energy shifts and state distortions without solving the full problem.

Problem Setup#

We consider the Hamiltonian:

(181)#\[ H(\lambda) = H_0 + \lambda V\]

Assumptions:

  1. The spectrum of \(H_0\) is discrete with no degeneracies: each eigenvalue appears exactly once.

  2. The coupling parameter \(\lambda\) is small enough that power series in \(\lambda\) converges.

  3. We know the complete spectrum of \(H_0\): \(\{E_n^{(0)}, |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\}\) for \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\)

  4. The perturbation \(V\) is Hermitian and labeled by the same index \(n\) throughout (“adiabatic continuity”).

Goal: Expand eigenvalues and eigenstates as power series:

(182)#\[ E_n(\lambda) = E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \lambda^2 E_n^{(2)} + \ldots\]
(183)#\[ |\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle = |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \lambda^2 |\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle + \ldots\]

Discussion: Perturbation Series Convergence

Rayleigh-Schrödinger perturbation theory gives

\[ E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle m|V|n\rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

For a harmonic oscillator with \(V = \lambda x^4\), compute \(E_0^{(2)}\) (ground state energy correction).

  • Does the series converge for all \(\lambda\), or only for \(\lambda\) below some threshold?

  • Compare the perturbative result to the exact ground state energy (available numerically). At what \(\lambda\) does the error exceed 5%?

  • Physical interpretation: large \(\lambda\) means the potential becomes very steep. Why does perturbation theory fail?

The Hellmann-Feynman theorem is a cornerstone of perturbation theory. It states:

(184)#\[ \boxed{\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda} = \langle \psi_n(\lambda) | \frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda} | \psi_n(\lambda) \rangle}\]

Physical meaning: The energy shifts with the perturbation parameter proportionally to the expectation value of the perturbation operator. Evaluated at \(\lambda = 0\) with \(\frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda} = V\):

(185)#\[ \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda}\right|_{\lambda=0} = \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle\]

This result shows that to first order, the energy shift is simply the expectation value of \(V\) in the unperturbed state—a direct consequence of the adiabatic continuity assumption.

Derivation

Consider the eigenvalue equation:

(186)#\[ H(\lambda) |\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle = E_n(\lambda) |\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle\]

Differentiate both sides with respect to \(\lambda\):

\[ \frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + H\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda} = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda} |\psi_n\rangle + E_n\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda}\]

Take the inner product with \(\langle \psi_n(\lambda)|\):

\[ \langle \psi_n|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + \langle \psi_n|H\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda} = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda}\langle\psi_n|\psi_n\rangle + E_n\langle\psi_n|\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda}\]

Use \(H^\dagger = H\) (Hermiticity) to rewrite \(\langle \psi_n|H = E_n\langle\psi_n|\):

\[ \langle \psi_n|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + E_n\langle\psi_n|\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda} = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda} + E_n\langle\psi_n|\frac{\partial |\psi_n\rangle}{\partial \lambda}\]

The \(E_n\) terms cancel:

(187)#\[ \langle \psi_n|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{d\lambda}\]

Discussion: Hellmann-Feynman Theorem

The Hellmann-Feynman theorem states \(\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} = \langle\psi_n(\lambda)|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle\), which appears to use only the expectation value in the perturbed state, not explicitly mentioning corrections to \(|\psi_n\rangle\).

  • Question 1: In the derivation, we took the inner product of the differentiated eigenvalue equation with \(\langle\psi_n|\). Show that the cross term \(\langle\psi_n|H\frac{\partial|\psi_n\rangle}{\partial\lambda}\) vanishes exactly (using Hermiticity). Why does this cancellation make the theorem exact to all orders, despite the existence of state corrections?

  • Question 2: Suppose we naively used the first-order state \(|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) to estimate \(\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}\) as \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda \text{(corrections)}\). Would this give the right answer? How do the neglected state corrections conspire to leave the energy derivative untouched?

  • Question 3: The adiabatic continuity assumption requires that we can label eigenstates continuously as \(\lambda\) varies. Explain why level crossings (where labeling becomes ambiguous) invalidate the theorem locally. Construct an example where \(\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}\) jumps discontinuously.

First-Order Energy Correction#

The first-order energy correction is obtained by expanding the eigenvalue equation to order \(\lambda^1\) and taking the inner product with the unperturbed state:

(188)#\[ \boxed{E_n^{(1)} = \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}\]

Physical interpretation: The first-order energy shift is simply the diagonal matrix element of the perturbation in the unperturbed basis. It is the expectation value of \(V\) in the unperturbed state. States with no diagonal matrix element (like \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) in the computational basis) experience zero first-order shift.

Derivation

Expand \(H|\psi_n\rangle = E_n|\psi_n\rangle\) to first order in \(\lambda\):

\[ (H_0 + \lambda V)(|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \ldots) = (E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \ldots)(|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \ldots)\]

Order \(\lambda^0\):

\[ H_0 |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle \quad \text{(unperturbed eigenvalue eq.)}\]

Order \(\lambda^1\):

\[ V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + H_0|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)} |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

Take the inner product with \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)}|\):

\[ \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle + \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | H_0 | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = E_n^{(0)} \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle + E_n^{(1)}\]

Use \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | H_0 = E_n^{(0)} \langle \psi_n^{(0)} |\) and the orthogonality condition \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = 0\):

\[ \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle = E_n^{(1)}\]

Normalization choice

We implicitly choose the normalization \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n(\lambda) \rangle = 1\) (independent of \(\lambda\)). Expanding to first order gives \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = 0\), so corrections are orthogonal to the unperturbed state.

First-Order State Correction#

While the first-order energy is diagonal, the state mixes with other eigenstates. The first-order state correction is:

(189)#\[ |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle\]

Physical interpretation: The perturbed state is a superposition of unperturbed states. The admixture of state \(m\) is:

  • Proportional to the coupling: \(\langle m | V | n \rangle\) (off-diagonal matrix element)

  • Inversely proportional to the energy gap: \(1/(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)})\) (strong near-resonance; exponentially suppressed far from resonance)

  • Sign-determined by gap: If state \(m\) is higher in energy, the coefficient is positive (state is pulled toward \(m\)); if lower, negative

This is the state hybridization principle: quantum states mix strongest when they are close in energy and coupled.

Derivation

Expand \(|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) in the eigenbasis of \(H_0\):

\[ |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} c_m^{(1)} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle\]

(We exclude \(m=n\) by the orthogonality condition \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = 0\).)

Insert into the order-\(\lambda^1\) equation:

\[ V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + H_0 \sum_m c_m^{(1)} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)} \sum_m c_m^{(1)} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

Rearrange:

\[ V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle = \sum_m c_m^{(1)} (E_m^{(0)} - E_n^{(0)}) |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

Project onto \(\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|\) for \(m \neq n\):

\[ \langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle = c_m^{(1)} (E_m^{(0)} - E_n^{(0)})\]

Solving for \(c_m^{(1)}\):

\[ c_m^{(1)} = \frac{\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_m^{(0)} - E_n^{(0)}} = \frac{\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{-(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)})}\]

Therefore:

\[ |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle\]

Second-Order Energy Correction#

The second-order energy correction captures “virtual transitions”: the system temporarily borrows strength from (or lends to) intermediate states before returning. The result is:

(190)#\[ \boxed{E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}}\]

Key features:

  1. The numerator is always non-negative (absolute value squared of coupling strength).

  2. The sign depends on the denominator: the energy difference \(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}\).

  3. For the ground state (\(n = 0\)): All intermediate states \(m\) satisfy \(E_m^{(0)} > E_0^{(0)}\), so all denominators are negative. Therefore \(E_0^{(2)} < 0\): ground state energy is always lowered by perturbation.

  4. For excited states: Mix of positive contributions (from higher states) and negative (from lower states). Net effect depends on the spectrum.

  5. Convergence: Series converges when \(\lambda |\langle m | V | n \rangle| \ll \min_m |E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}|\), i.e., perturbation is small compared to energy gaps.

This order contributes to level repulsion: nearby levels push each other apart. The ground state borrows energy from excited states (lowering itself), which raises the excited state energies.

Second-Order Energy Correction

The energy shift to second order in \(\lambda\) accounts for “virtual transitions” to intermediate states:

\[ \boxed{{E_n^{{(2)}} = \sum_{{m \neq n}} \frac{{|\langle \psi_m^{{(0)}} | V | \psi_n^{{(0)}} \rangle|^2}}{{E_n^{{(0)}} - E_m^{{(0)}}}}}}\]

Key features:

  1. Always negative for ground state: All denominators are negative (all intermediate states lie above), so \(E_0^{{(2)}} < 0\). Perturbations always lower the ground state energy.

  2. Level repulsion: Nearby levels are pushed apart by the perturbation (spacing increases).

  3. Resonance effect: Mixing is strongest when \(E_n^{{(0)}} \approx E_m^{{(0)}}\) (small denominator); exponentially suppressed for distant levels.

  4. Convergence criterion: Series converges when \(\lambda |V| \ll \min_m |E_n^{{(0)}} - E_m^{{(0)}}|\), i.e., perturbation strength is much smaller than energy gaps.

Virtual transitions picture: The system temporarily “borrows” strength from excited states (raising their energy) to lower the ground state energy, then returns to the original state.

Derivation

Derive from the order-\(\lambda^2\) eigenvalue equation. Project the order-\(\lambda^1\) equation:

\[ V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + H_0|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)} |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

onto \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\) after applying \((H_0 - E_n^{(0)})\) to \(|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) and using the orthogonality \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = 0\):

\[ \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = E_n^{(2)}\]

Substitute the first-order state correction:

\[ E_n^{(2)} = \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(1)} \rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_m^{(0)} \rangle \langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

Use the fact that \(\langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_m^{(0)} \rangle = \langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V^\dagger | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle^* = \langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle^*\) (by Hermiticity):

\[ E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

Discussion: Perturbation Theory at Degeneracy

Consider two unperturbed energy levels separated by a small gap \(\Delta E = E_2^{(0)} - E_1^{(0)}\). If the perturbation has a significant matrix element \(V_{12}\) between these levels, the first-order state correction includes a term:

\[ c_1^{(1)} \sim \frac{V_{12}}{\Delta E}\]

  • Question 1: For a given coupling \(|V_{12}|\), at what value of \(\Delta E\) does this become larger than \(c_1^{(0)} = 1\)? What does this mean physically?

  • Question 2: When \(\Delta E \to 0\) (degeneracy), the formula \(E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle m|V|n\rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\) appears to diverge. Why is this not a failure of perturbation theory’s validity, but rather a signal that degenerate perturbation theory (or diagonalization of the full 2×2 block) is required?

  • Question 3: Show that for exactly degenerate levels (\(\Delta E = 0\) exactly), the correct first-order treatment is to diagonalize \(V\) in the degenerate subspace. Construct a physical argument: why must we do this differently than for non-degenerate pairs?

Level Repulsion#

The second-order term exhibits level repulsion: nearby energy levels are pushed apart by the perturbation.

  • Ground state: \(E_0^{(2)} < 0\) because all denominators are negative (all intermediate states are higher), so the ground state energy is lowered.

  • First excited state: Receives positive contributions from ground state mixing (energy increases) and mixed contributions from higher states.

  • Net effect: The spacing between levels increases:

(191)#\[ \Delta E(\lambda) = [E_1(\lambda) - E_0(\lambda)] > [E_1^{(0)} - E_0^{(0)}] = \Delta E(0)\]

Level repulsion in words

Perturbation theory reveals that quantum levels repel each other. This is a fundamental consequence of the coupling structure \(|\langle m|V|n\rangle|^2\) (quadratic in coupling). The closer two levels are in energy, the stronger they couple, and the more they push apart. In non-degenerate systems, levels never cross—they execute an “avoided crossing.”

State Hybridization and Resonance#

The perturbed eigenstate is a hybrid of multiple unperturbed states:

\[ |\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle \approx |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle + O(\lambda^2)\]

Mixing strength depends on:

  1. Coupling matrix element: \(|\langle m|V|n\rangle|\) — larger coupling increases mixing.

  2. Energy gap: \(|E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}|\) — smaller gaps increase mixing exponentially (resonance effect).

Examples:

  • Covalent bonding in molecules: Atomic orbitals of nearby energy hybridize to form molecular orbitals (bonding and antibonding).

  • Spin-orbit coupling in atoms: Spin (\(\uparrow/\downarrow\)) and orbital angular momentum states mix when they are close in energy.

  • Stark effect in atoms: Electric field (\(V = eEz\)) mixes states of opposite parity that have nearby energy.

Example: The Qubit Revisited

For the qubit model \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x\):

Unperturbed basis:

\[\begin{split} |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad E_+^{(0)} = 1, \quad E_-^{(0)} = -1\end{split}\]

Perturbation matrix elements:

\[\begin{split} V = \hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \Rightarrow\langle 0|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 0, \quad \langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|1\rangle = 0, \quad \langle 0|\hat{\sigma}^x|1\rangle = 1\end{split}\]

Energy of state \(|0\rangle\) (labeled as \(+\) index):

\[ E_+^{(1)} = \langle 0|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 0\]
\[E_+^{(2)} = \frac{|\langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle|^2}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}} = \frac{|1|^2}{1 - (-1)} = \frac{1}{2}\]

Thus:

\[ E_+(\lambda) = 1 + 0 \cdot \lambda + \frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 + O(\lambda^3)\]

Compare with exact: \(E_+ = \sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 - \frac{1}{8}\lambda^4 + \ldots\) ✓ Matches through \(O(\lambda^2)\)!

State of \(|0\rangle\):

\[ |\psi_+^{(1)}\rangle = \frac{\langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}} |1\rangle = \frac{1}{2}|1\rangle\]

So:

\[ |\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle = |0\rangle + \frac{\lambda}{2}|1\rangle + O(\lambda^2)\]

Again, matches the exact expansion from Section 5.1.1! ✓

3. Selection Rules from Parity. For a particle in a 1D symmetric potential \(V_0(-x) = V_0(x)\), the eigenstates have definite parity. Show that if the perturbation \(\hat{V} = \lambda \hat{x}\) (odd parity), then \(E_n^{(1)} = 0\) for all \(n\). What does this imply about the leading correction?

4. Anharmonic Oscillator. For the harmonic oscillator with \(\hat{H}_0 = \frac{\hat{p}^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2\hat{x}^2\) and perturbation \(\hat{V} = \lambda \hat{x}^4\), compute the first-order energy correction \(E_n^{(1)}\) using \(\hat{x} = \sqrt{\hbar/(2m\omega)}(\hat{a}+\hat{a}^\dagger)\) and the matrix elements of \(\hat{x}^4\) in the number basis. (You may use \(\hat{x}^4\) in terms of \(\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger\) and keep only diagonal terms.)

5. Zeeman Effect (Weak Field). In a weak magnetic field \(B\) along \(z\), the perturbation to the hydrogen atom is \(\hat{V} = -\boldsymbol{\mu}\cdot\boldsymbol{B}\) where \(\boldsymbol{\mu} = -\frac{e}{2m_e}(\hat{L}_z + 2\hat{S}_z)\) (ignoring diamagnetic term). For states with definite \(j, m_j\) (good quantum numbers including spin-orbit coupling), write the first-order energy shift and explain why these are the correct basis states.

6. Validity Breakdown. Consider a 3-level system with unperturbed energies \(E_1 = 0\), \(E_2 = \Delta\), \(E_3 = 10\Delta\). The perturbation couples states 1 and 2 with matrix element \(V_{12}\), and states 2 and 3 with \(V_{23}\).

(a) State the validity conditions for perturbation theory applied to state 1.

(b) As \(\Delta \to 0\) with \(V_{12}\) fixed, which condition is violated? What is the appropriate fix?

7. Sum Rule. Show that the second-order ground-state energy shift satisfies

\[ E_0^{(2)} = -\sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{|\langle n^{(0)}|\hat{V}|0^{(0)}\rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_0^{(0)}} \leq 0\]

This proves that perturbations always lower the ground state energy to second order. What physical principle (related to the variational theorem) explains this?

8. Off-Diagonal Perturbation. A particle has \(\hat{H}_0\) with non-degenerate eigenvalues and a perturbation \(\hat{V}\) with \(\hat{V}_{nn} = 0\) for all \(n\) (no diagonal matrix elements). Show that the first-order energy correction vanishes for all states, and write the leading-order correction as a ratio that must be small for the expansion to be valid.

5.1.2 Non-Degenerate Perturbation Theory#

Prompts

  • What is the Hellmann-Feynman theorem, and how does it give the first-order energy correction as an expectation value without expanding eigenstates?

  • Derive the first-order state correction. Why does the admixture of state \(m\) scale as coupling/gap?

  • Why does the second-order energy correction always lower the ground state? Connect this to the variational principle.

  • When does non-degenerate perturbation theory break down? What signals the need for degenerate perturbation theory?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Non-degenerate perturbation theory provides a systematic expansion of eigenvalues and eigenstates in powers of a small parameter \(\lambda\), for the Hamiltonian \(H(\lambda) = H_0 + \lambda V\). The Hellmann-Feynman theorem is the organizing principle: the exact rate at which an eigenvalue changes with \(\lambda\) equals the expectation value of \(V\) in the exact eigenstate.

Hellmann-Feynman Theorem#

Hellmann-Feynman Theorem

For a parameter-dependent Hamiltonian \(H(\lambda)\) with eigenvalue \(E_n(\lambda)\) and normalized eigenstate \(|\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle\):

(77)#\[\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} = \langle \psi_n(\lambda) | \frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda} | \psi_n(\lambda) \rangle\]

At \(\lambda = 0\) with \(\partial H/\partial\lambda = V\), this immediately gives the first-order energy:

\[E_n^{(1)} = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}\bigg|_{\lambda=0} = \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle\]

Requires: adiabatic continuity — eigenstates can be tracked continuously as \(\lambda\) varies (no level crossings).

Derivation: Hellmann-Feynman Theorem

Start from the eigenvalue equation \(H(\lambda)|\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle = E_n(\lambda)|\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle\) and differentiate both sides with respect to \(\lambda\):

\[\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + H\frac{\partial|\psi_n\rangle}{\partial\lambda} = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + E_n\frac{\partial|\psi_n\rangle}{\partial\lambda}\]

Project onto \(\langle\psi_n|\) from the left. Using \(\langle\psi_n|H = E_n\langle\psi_n|\), the second term on the left becomes \(E_n\langle\psi_n|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle\), which cancels the second term on the right:

\[\langle\psi_n|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n\rangle + E_n\langle\psi_n|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle = \frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda}\underbrace{\langle\psi_n|\psi_n\rangle}_{=1} + E_n\langle\psi_n|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle\]

The \(E_n\langle\psi_n|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle\) terms cancel, leaving

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda} = \langle\psi_n(\lambda)|\frac{\partial H}{\partial\lambda}|\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle\]

Key insight: the state derivatives \(|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle\) drop out entirely — the energy derivative depends only on the current eigenstate and the explicit parameter dependence of \(H\).

First-Order Energy Correction#

First-Order Energy

(78)#\[\boxed{E_n^{(1)} = \langle \psi_n^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}\]

The first-order shift is the diagonal matrix element of \(V\) in the unperturbed basis — the expectation value of the perturbation in the unperturbed state.

If \(V\) has no diagonal elements (e.g., \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) in the computational basis), the first-order correction vanishes and the leading effect is second-order.

Derivation: First-Order Energy from the Eigenvalue Equation

Write the perturbative expansions \(E_n = E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \lambda^2 E_n^{(2)} + \cdots\) and \(|\psi_n\rangle = |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \cdots\). Substituting into \(H|\psi_n\rangle = E_n|\psi_n\rangle\):

\[(H_0 + \lambda V)(|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \cdots) = (E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \cdots)(|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \cdots)\]

Order \(\lambda^0\): \(H_0|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\) (the unperturbed problem).

Order \(\lambda^1\): \(V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + H_0|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\).

Project the order-\(\lambda^1\) equation onto \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\):

\[\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \underbrace{\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|H_0|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle}_{= E_n^{(0)}\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle} = E_n^{(0)}\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)}\]

The \(E_n^{(0)}\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) terms cancel (regardless of whether \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) vanishes), giving \(E_n^{(1)} = \langle\psi_n^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\).

Normalization convention: we choose \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = 0\) (the first-order correction is orthogonal to the unperturbed state). This fixes the phase ambiguity and ensures \(|\psi_n\rangle\) remains normalized through first order: \(\langle\psi_n|\psi_n\rangle = 1 + \lambda(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \text{c.c.}) + O(\lambda^2) = 1 + O(\lambda^2)\).

First-Order State Correction#

First-Order State

(79)#\[\boxed{|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} |\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle}\]

The admixture of state \(m\) is proportional to the coupling \(\langle m|V|n\rangle\) and inversely proportional to the energy gap \(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}\). States mix strongest when they are close in energy and strongly coupled.

Derivation: First-Order State Correction

From the order-\(\lambda^1\) equation:

\[V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + H_0|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + E_n^{(1)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

Rearrange: \((E_n^{(0)} - H_0)|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle - E_n^{(1)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\).

Expand \(|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\) in the unperturbed basis: \(|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m\neq n} c_m^{(1)}|\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle\) (the \(m=n\) component vanishes by our normalization convention). Projecting onto \(\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|\) with \(m\neq n\):

\[(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)})\,c_m^{(1)} = \langle\psi_m^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle - E_n^{(1)}\underbrace{\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle}_{=0}\]

Solving:

\[c_m^{(1)} = \frac{\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

This requires \(E_n^{(0)} \neq E_m^{(0)}\) for all \(m\neq n\) — the non-degeneracy condition. If any denominator vanishes, we must switch to degenerate perturbation theory (\S5.1.3).

Tip on the energy denominator: the energy of the state being corrected (\(E_n\)) always appears in the numerator position minus the energy of the admixed state (\(E_m\)).

Second-Order Energy Correction#

Second-Order Energy

(80)#\[\boxed{E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle \psi_m^{(0)} | V | \psi_n^{(0)} \rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}}\]

  • Ground state: all denominators are negative, so \(E_0^{(2)} \leq 0\) — perturbations always lower the ground state energy (consistent with the variational principle).

  • Level repulsion: nearby levels push each other apart through virtual transitions.

  • Convergence: requires \(\lambda|\langle m|V|n\rangle| \ll |E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}|\) for all \(m\).

Derivation: Second-Order Energy Correction

Method 1 (direct): From the order-\(\lambda^2\) equation, project onto \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\):

\[E_n^{(2)} = \langle\psi_n^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle\]

(The \(H_0|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle\) and \(E_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle\) terms cancel as before.) Substituting the first-order state:

\[E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m\neq n} \frac{\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|V|\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|V|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} = \sum_{m\neq n} \frac{|V_{mn}|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

Method 2 (Hellmann-Feynman): Differentiate \(\mathrm{d}E_n/\mathrm{d}\lambda = \langle\psi_n|V|\psi_n\rangle\) again:

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda^2} = \langle\partial_\lambda\psi_n|V|\psi_n\rangle + \langle\psi_n|V|\partial_\lambda\psi_n\rangle\]

Insert the resolution of identity \(\sum_m |\psi_m\rangle\langle\psi_m| = \hat{I}\), separate \(m=n\) from \(m\neq n\):

\[\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 E_n}{\mathrm{d}\lambda^2}\bigg|_{\lambda=0} = \sum_{m\neq n}\left(\frac{V_{nm}}{E_n - E_m}V_{mn} + V_{nm}\frac{V_{mn}}{E_n - E_m}\right) + V_{nn}\underbrace{\partial_\lambda\langle\psi_n|\psi_n\rangle}_{=0}\]

The \(m=n\) term vanishes because \(\partial_\lambda\langle n|n\rangle = 0\) (normalization is preserved). The result is \(\mathrm{d}^2 E_n/\mathrm{d}\lambda^2 = 2\sum_{m\neq n}|V_{mn}|^2/(E_n - E_m)\), giving \(E_n^{(2)} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{d}^2 E_n/\mathrm{d}\lambda^2 = \sum_{m\neq n}|V_{mn}|^2/(E_n - E_m)\).

Ground state bound: for the ground state (\(n=0\)), every denominator \(E_0 - E_m < 0\), so \(E_0^{(2)} \leq 0\). This is consistent with the variational principle: any trial state gives an energy \(\geq\) the true ground state energy.

Physical Intuitions#

The perturbation series has a clean physical interpretation at each order:

  • First-order energy = expectation value of the perturbation (average energy shift in the unperturbed state)

  • First-order state = hybridization with other levels, weighted by coupling/gap

  • Second-order energy = virtual transitions to intermediate states and back; always lowers the ground state

  • Breakdown: when \(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)} \to 0\) (degeneracy), denominators diverge → signal to switch to degenerate perturbation theory (\S5.1.3)

Example: Verification Against the Qubit Model

Problem. Verify perturbation theory on the qubit \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x\) from \S5.1.1.

Solution. Unperturbed: \(E_+^{(0)} = +1\) (\(|0\rangle\)), \(E_-^{(0)} = -1\) (\(|1\rangle\)). Since \(\langle 0|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 0\), the first-order energy vanishes: \(E_+^{(1)} = 0\). The second-order correction:

\[E_+^{(2)} = \frac{|\langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle|^2}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}} = \frac{1}{2}\]

So \(E_+(\lambda) = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 + O(\lambda^3)\), matching the exact result \(\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 - \tfrac{1}{8}\lambda^4 + \cdots\). \(\checkmark\)

The first-order state: \(|\psi_+^{(1)}\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}|1\rangle\), so \(|\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle \approx |0\rangle + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}|1\rangle\), again matching the exact eigenvector expansion. \(\checkmark\)

Derivation: Second-Order State Correction

Collecting the order-\(\lambda^2\) terms from the eigenvalue equation and projecting onto \(\langle\psi_m^{(0)}|\) with \(m\neq n\):

\[c_m^{(2)} = \sum_{l\neq n} \frac{V_{ml}\,V_{ln}}{(E_n - E_m)(E_n - E_l)} - \frac{V_{mn}\,V_{nn}}{(E_n - E_m)^2}\]

The full second-order state also acquires a correction along \(|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\) itself (from re-normalization):

\[|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle = \sum_{m\neq n} c_m^{(2)}|\psi_m^{(0)}\rangle - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{|V_{mn}|^2}{(E_n - E_m)^2}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\]

The last term ensures normalization through second order: \(\langle\psi_n|\psi_n\rangle = 1 + O(\lambda^3)\). It arises from differentiating \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle = 1\) and requiring \(\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle + \langle\psi_n^{(1)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = 0\), which gives

\[\langle\psi_n^{(0)}|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle = -\frac{1}{2}\langle\psi_n^{(1)}|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{|V_{mn}|^2}{(E_n-E_m)^2}\]

Discussion

The second-order formula \(E_n^{(2)} = \sum_m |V_{mn}|^2/(E_n - E_m)\) diverges when two levels become degenerate (\(E_n = E_m\)). Is this a failure of quantum mechanics, or just a signal that you need a different computational method? What is the correct procedure when the energy gap \(\Delta E\) approaches zero while the coupling \(V_{mn}\) stays finite?

Summary#

  • The Hellmann-Feynman theorem \(\mathrm{d}E_n/\mathrm{d}\lambda = \langle\psi_n|V|\psi_n\rangle\) is the organizing principle: energy derivatives are expectation values.

  • First-order energy: \(E_n^{(1)} = \langle n^{(0)}|V|n^{(0)}\rangle\) — expectation value of perturbation.

  • First-order state: admixture of other levels, weighted by coupling over energy gap.

  • Second-order energy: virtual transitions; always lowers the ground state; series diverges at degeneracy.

5.1.1 Toy Model#

Prompts

  • What is perturbation theory, and why is it so essential for quantum physics? When do we expand in powers of a small coupling?

  • For a qubit with \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x\), find the exact eigenvalues and eigenstates. Can you then expand these in powers of \(\lambda\) to extract perturbation corrections?

  • Why is the qubit model such a perfect pedagogical tool? What can we learn by comparing the perturbative series to the exact solution?

  • Under what conditions does the perturbative power series converge, and when does it break down? Can you identify when the expansion fails in the qubit example?

  • For an arbitrary two-level system, how would you decide whether to use perturbation theory or solve exactly? What rule of thumb guides this choice?

Lecture Notes#

Perturbation theory is the art of solving the unsolvable: when exact Hamiltonians resist solution, we leverage nearby solvable systems and expand the difference away. The qubit—a two-level system with an exact solution—becomes our laboratory for understanding how perturbative approximations work, how power series in a coupling constant reveal the structure of quantum states, and crucially, where and why those series break down.

What is Perturbation Theory?#

Important

Convergence condition: Perturbation theory requires \(\lambda \|V\| \ll \min(\Delta E)\), where \(\Delta E\) is the smallest unperturbed energy gap.

Caution

n

Finite radius of convergence: Even for analytic exact solutions, the perturbative series may diverge beyond \(|\lambda| = R\) due to singularities in the complex \(\lambda\)-plane.

Perturbation theory is a systematic approximation method that extends our knowledge of exactly solvable quantum systems to nearby regimes in parameter space. Rather than solving a complex Hamiltonian from scratch, we leverage solutions of a simpler (solvable) system and treat the difference as a small perturbation.

Central idea: Expand eigenvalues and eigenstates as power series in a small coupling parameter \(\lambda\).

The Fundamental Problem#

Given:

  • The unperturbed Hamiltonian \(H_0\) with known spectrum: \(\{E_n^{(0)}, |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle\}\)- A perturbation operator \(V\)- A small parameter \(\lambda \ll 1\)Find:

  • The spectrum of \(H(\lambda) = H_0 + \lambda V\) as a power series in \(\lambda\):

(164)#\[ E_n(\lambda) = E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \lambda^2 E_n^{(2)} + \ldots\]
(165)#\[ |\psi_n(\lambda)\rangle = |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda |\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \lambda^2 |\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle + \ldots\]

| Reason | Impact ||——– |——– || Exact solutions are rare | Most realistic quantum systems (atoms, molecules, condensed matter) have no closed-form solutions. Perturbation theory leverages the few that do. || Separated energy scales | Physics often occurs at distinct scales (e.g., atomic structure \(\approx\) eV, hyperfine splitting \(\approx\) mueV). Perturbation theory reveals this hierarchy. || Physical insight | Systematic expansion reveals how states hybridize, how levels interact, why certain quantities are small. || Practical calculations | Transition rates, response functions, scattering amplitudes are computed via perturbative series. |

Discussion: Two-Level Approximation

For a hydrogen atom in a weak electric field (Stark effect), we often use a two-level approximation truncating to \(n=1,2\). But the perturbation couples the \(n=1\) state to excited states beyond \(n=2\).

  • Estimate for what electric field strength the omitted states above \(n=2\) contribute >1% error to the ground state energy shift.

  • How would you systematically improve the calculation by including \(n=3\)? What changes in the perturbation matrix?

  • Physical insight: why does the two-level model sometimes work surprisingly well even when higher states are energetically close?

We consider a single qubit (two-level system) with an exact solution. This allows us to compare perturbative results with exact answers and understand where approximations break down.

Hamiltonian#

(166)#\[\begin{split} H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda \hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \lambda \\ \lambda & -1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) are Pauli matrices with \(\{ |0\rangle, |1\rangle\}\) as the computational basis:

(167)#\[\begin{split} \hat{\sigma}^z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad\hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Thus:

  • \(H_0 = \hat{\sigma}^z\) (unperturbed Hamiltonian)

  • \(V = \hat{\sigma}^x\) (perturbation)Physical meaning: \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) favors spin-up (energy \(+1\)) over spin-down (energy \(-1\)). The perturbation \(\lambda \hat{\sigma}^x\) induces spin flips between the two states.

Unperturbed Spectrum#

At \(\lambda = 0\):

(168)#\[ H_0 |0\rangle = |0\rangle, \quad H_0 |1\rangle = - |1\rangle\]

  • State \(|0\rangle\) has energy \(E_+^{(0)} = +1\) (excited state when labeled by decreasing energy)

  • State \(|1\rangle\) has energy \(E_-^{(0)} = -1\) (ground state)Notation: We use subscript \(+/-\) to label states in order of decreasing energy, so \(E_+^{(0)} > E_-^{(0)}\).

Exact Solution via Diagonalization#

Eigenvalue Equation#

Solve \(\det(H(\lambda) - E\mathbb{I}) = 0\):

\[\begin{split} \det\begin{pmatrix} 1-E & \lambda \\ \lambda & -1-E \end{pmatrix} = (1-E)(-1-E) - \lambda^2 = 0\end{split}\]
\[-1 - E + E + E^2 - \lambda^2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad E^2 = 1 + \lambda^2\]

Exact eigenvalues:

(169)#\[ E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\sqrt{1 + \lambda^2}\]

Exact Eigenstates#

For each eigenvalue, solve \((H - E)\psi = 0\). The normalized eigenstates are:

(170)#\[\begin{split} |\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(1 + \lambda^2 + \sqrt{1+\lambda^2})}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 + \sqrt{1+\lambda^2} \\ \lambda \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
(171)#\[\begin{split} |\psi_-(\lambda)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(1 + \lambda^2 + \sqrt{1+\lambda^2})}} \begin{pmatrix} \lambda \\ -(1 + \sqrt{1+\lambda^2}) \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

These expressions are exact for all \(\lambda \geq 0\).

Taylor Expansion of Exact Results#

Now expand the exact expressions for small \(\lambda\). We use:

(172)#\[ \sqrt{1 + x} = 1 + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{8} + \frac{x^3}{16} - \frac{5x^4}{128} + \ldots, \quad |x | < 1\]
(173)#\[ E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\sqrt{1 + \lambda^2} = \pm\left(1 + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} - \frac{\lambda^4}{8} + \frac{\lambda^6}{16} - \ldots\right)\]

Observation: The expansion contains only even powers of \(\lambda\). This is a consequence of symmetry: \(H(\lambda)\) is an even function under \(\lambda \to -\lambda\) (since the Hamiltonian matrix is real and symmetric), so eigenvalues must also be even functions.Identifying coefficients:

Discussion

Why do first-order corrections vanish?

You found that \(E_+^{(1)} = \langle 0 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 0 \rangle = 0\) and \(E_-^{(1)} = \langle 1 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 1 \rangle = 0\). This is not a coincidence—it follows from a deeper symmetry.

  • The matrix \(\hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) has zero diagonal elements. What does this tell you about the selection rules for transitions induced by this operator?

  • More generally, if the perturbation \(V\) commutes with some symmetry of the unperturbed system, which corrections can vanish?

  • Physical consequence: Since \(E^{(1)} = 0\), the first physical shift to the energy appears at second order. Why is this actually a blessing in practical calculations—what does it mean for experimental accuracy?

  • Sketch the energy curves \(E_\pm(\lambda)\) from \(\lambda=0\) to \(\lambda=0.5\). Confirm visually that both curves have zero slope at the origin.

This is a recurring pattern in perturbation theory: symmetries suppress certain corrections, and recognizing this saves enormous computational work.

(174)#\[ E_+^{(0)} = 1, \quad E_+^{(1)} = 0, \quad E_+^{(2)} = \frac{1}{2}, \quad E_+^{(3)} = 0, \quad E_+^{(4)} = -\frac{1}{8}, \ldots\]

State Series#

Expanding the eigenstates requires careful algebra. For the upper state \(|\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle\):

(175)#\[ |\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle = |0\rangle + \frac{\lambda}{2} |1\rangle - \frac{\lambda^2}{8} |0\rangle - \frac{3\lambda^3}{16} |1\rangle + O(\lambda^4)\]
(176)#\[ |\psi_-(\lambda)\rangle = |1\rangle - \frac{\lambda}{2} |0\rangle - \frac{\lambda^2}{8} |1\rangle + \frac{3\lambda^3}{16} |0\rangle + O(\lambda^4)\]

Again, the series contains only even powers in the “unperturbed” part (\(|0\rangle\) in \(|\psi_+\rangle\)) and odd powers in the “admixture” part (\(|1\rangle\) in \(|\psi_+\rangle\)). This reflects the parity of the coupling.

Power Series and Perturbative Coefficients#

Perturbation theory computes the coefficients \(E_n^{(k)}\) and \(|\psi_n^{(k)}\rangle\) without solving the system exactly. The key insight:

(177)#\[ E_n^{(k)} = \frac{1}{k!}\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}^k E_n(\lambda)}{d\lambda^k}\right |_{\lambda=0}\]

Method: Differentiate the eigenvalue equation \(k\) times and evaluate at \(\lambda = 0\). The derivatives of \(H_0\) and \(V\) are:

(178)#\[ \frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}\bigg |_{\lambda=0} = V, \quad \frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial \lambda^2}\bigg |_{\lambda=0} = 0, \quad \ldots\]

This turns the eigenvalue problem into a recursive algorithm that generates all orders of the perturbative expansion.

Convergence of the Perturbative Series#

Radius of Convergence#

For the qubit model, the exact solution is entire (analytic everywhere), but the perturbative expansion has finite radius of convergence \(R = 1\). This is determined by the nearest singularity in the complex \(\lambda\)-plane.Setting \(E^2 = 1 + \lambda^2 = 0\) gives \(\lambda = \pm i\). These are the branch points where the two eigenvalue sheets meet. The convergence radius is:

(179)#\[ R = |\lambda_{\text{singularity}} | = 1\]

Convergence Criterion#

Discussion

When is the perturbation “small”?

You have computed that for the qubit, perturbation theory converges for \(|\lambda| < 1\). But consider a hydrogen atom in an external magnetic field (Zeeman effect). The unperturbed splitting between \(n=1\) and \(n=2\) is roughly \(13.6\,\mathrm{eV}\), while the magnetic perturbation is \(\sim 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{eV}\) for a typical lab field.

  • Why does perturbation theory work brilliantly here, even though the gap \(\Delta E \gg |V|\) seems huge?

  • Now imagine ramping up the magnetic field until \(|V| \sim 0.1\,\Delta E\). At what field strength does the perturbation series start to fail?

  • How would you know experimentally (e.g., from spectroscopy) that you have entered the regime where perturbation theory breaks down?

The lesson: The absolute magnitude of \(\lambda\) or \(V\) matters far less than the ratio \(\lambda|V|/\Delta E\).

The perturbative power series converges for \(|\lambda | < 1\). More generally, for any quantum system:

(180)#\[ \text{Convergence requires} \quad \lambda \ll \frac{\min(\text{energy gaps})}{\ |V\ |}\]

Physical interpretation:

  • If \(\lambda V \sim \Delta E\) (energy gap), the perturbation is no longer “small”; strong mixing occurs.

  • For the qubit, the gap is \(\Delta E = 2\) and \(\ |V\ | = 1\) (largest eigenvalue of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\)), giving \(\lambda \lesssim 2\), consistent with \(R = 1\) as a rough bound.

Asymptotics of perturbative series

Many

quantum systems have divergent or asymptotic perturbative series. The series may have zero radius of convergence but still provides excellent approximations at small \(\lambda\) if truncated appropriately. The qubit is fortunate: it has a finite, nonzero convergence radius.

Graphical Insight: Level Crossing and Repulsion#

As \(\lambda\) increases from 0:

  • At \(\lambda = 0\): Two levels at \(E_+ = 1\) and \(E_- = -1\) (separated by gap \(\Delta E = 2\)).

  • For \(0 < \lambda < 1\): Levels curve smoothly; the perturbation couples them, inducing a quadratic splitting (from \(E^{(2)}\) terms).

  • At \(\lambda = 1\): Levels reach closest approach, with \(\Delta E = 0\) at complex \(\lambda = i\).

  • For \(\lambda > 1\): Perturbation series breaks down; exact solution still valid: \(E_\pm \approx \pm \lambda\) (grows linearly).The exact solution exhibits level repulsion: the two branches \(E_+(\lambda)\) and \(E_-(\lambda)\) never cross for real \(\lambda\), though they come close at large \(\lambda\). This is a hallmark of avoided crossings.

Key Takeaways#

  1. Perturbation theory expands eigenvalues/eigenstates as power series in \(\lambda\).

  2. The qubit model is an exactly solvable system where we can compare perturbative approximations with exact results.

  3. Taylor expansion of the exact solution reveals: - Energy has only even powers in \(\lambda\) (due to symmetry). - States have even powers in the “unperturbed” component and odd powers in admixtures. - Coefficients follow a pattern: \(E^{(0)}, E^{(2)}, E^{(4)}, \ldots\) (odd orders vanish).

  4. Convergence is controlled by the proximity to singularities in the complex \(\lambda\)-plane. For the qubit, \(R = 1\).

  5. Physical interpretation: Perturbation theory is valid when \(\lambda V \ll \Delta E\) (coupling strength much smaller than energy gaps).

Homework#

1. Perturbation Validity Condition. The perturbation expansion is valid when \(\lambda\) is small. For the qubit model, the unperturbed energy gap is \(\Delta E = E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)} = 2\).

(a) Write the convergence criterion \(\lambda \ll \Delta E / |V|\) for the qubit, where \(|V|\) is the operator norm of \(\hat{\sigma}^x\). What is the approximate radius of convergence?

(b) The exact solution has singularities at \(\lambda = \pm \mathrm{i}\). Verify that \(|\lambda_\text{singular}| = 1\) matches the geometric argument about energy gaps.

(c) For \(\lambda = 0.5\), is perturbation theory valid? For \(\lambda = 1.5\)?

2. First-Order Energy Correction by Direct Formula. Use the formula \(E_n^{(1)} = \langle n^{(0)} | \hat{V} | n^{(0)} \rangle\) to compute the first-order energy shift for both the upper (\(|0\rangle\)) and lower (\(|1\rangle\)) states of the qubit.

(a) Compute \(E_+^{(1)} = \langle 0 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 0 \rangle\).

(b) Compute \(E_-^{(1)} = \langle 1 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 1 \rangle\).

(c) Why do both vanish? What does this tell us about the symmetry of the problem?

3. Second-Order Energy Correction. The second-order energy correction is given by

\[ E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{|\langle m^{(0)} | \hat{V} | n^{(0)} \rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}}\]

For the qubit:

(a) Compute the matrix element \(\langle 1 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 0 \rangle\) and \(\langle 0 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 1 \rangle\).

(b) For the upper state (\(n = +\)), the only intermediate state is \(m = -\). Show that

\[E_+^{(2)} = \frac{|\langle 1 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 0 \rangle|^2}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}} = \frac{1}{4}\]

Wait, this doesn’t match the exact result \(E_+^{(2)} = 1/2\) from the lecture notes. Reconsider: is there a missing factor or convention difference? (This is a realistic problem-solving moment!)

(c) Verify the sign: \(E_+^{(2)} > 0\) and \(E_-^{(2)} > 0\). Give a physical explanation: does the second-order shift raise or lower both states?

4. Comparing Perturbation to Exact Results. The exact energies are \(E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\). Expand to second order:

\[E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\left(1 + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} - \frac{\lambda^4}{8} + O(\lambda^6)\right)\]

(a) Extract the perturbative coefficients: \(E_\pm^{(0)}, E_\pm^{(1)}, E_\pm^{(2)}\).

(b) For the upper state, compare \(E_+ \approx E_+^{(0)} + E_+^{(2)} \lambda^2\) (second-order perturbation theory) with the exact result at \(\lambda = 0.3, 0.5, 0.8\). Compute the absolute error for each.

(c) At what \(\lambda\) does the error exceed 10%?

5. First-Order State Mixing. The first-order state correction is

\[|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle m^{(0)} | \hat{V} | n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}} |m^{(0)}\rangle\]

For the upper state \(|\psi_+^{(1)}\rangle\):

(a) Write out the sum explicitly (there is only one intermediate state \(m = -\)).

(b) Compute the coefficient:

\[\frac{\langle 1 | \hat{\sigma}^x | 0 \rangle}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}} = \frac{1}{1 - (-1)} = \frac{1}{2}\]

(c) Therefore, \(|\psi_+^{(1)}\rangle = \frac{1}{2}|1\rangle\). Verify this matches the exact expansion in equation (eq-5a-state-series-plus).

(d) Interpret physically: what does this admixture mean? The perturbation \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) is a _____ operator.

6. Symmetry and Vanishing Corrections. Explain why \(E_+^{(1)} = E_-^{(1)} = 0\) using symmetry arguments.

(a) The Hamiltonian \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda \hat{\sigma}^x\) is invariant under the parity transformation \(\lambda \to -\lambda\). What does this imply for eigenvalues?

(b) If \(E_+(\lambda)\) is an even function, what is the \(\lambda^1\) coefficient?

(c) More generally, use the explicit forms of \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) and \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) to show that \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) has no diagonal elements. What does this imply for \(E^{(1)}\)?

7. Energy Denominator and Near-Degeneracy. The energy denominator \(E_n^{(0)} - E_m^{(0)}\) in the second-order formula can become problematic.

(a) For the qubit, \(\Delta E = E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)} = 2\). What happens to the perturbative series if the unperturbed gap shrinks?

(b) Consider a modified qubit with \(H_0 = \hat{\sigma}^z\) but now the gap is \(\Delta E = \epsilon\) (very small). Compute \(E_+^{(2)}\) as a function of \(\epsilon\). What is the limit \(\epsilon \to 0\)?

(c) This is why degenerate perturbation theory is needed for nearly degenerate or degenerate levels. Briefly explain what changes in the degenerate case.

8. Perturbation Series Convergence at Finite \(\lambda\). The radius of convergence is \(R = 1\), but the exact solution remains valid for all \(\lambda > 0\). Test the perturbation series truncation at different orders.

For \(\lambda = 0.8\) (safely within \(R = 1\)):

(a) Compute \(E_+^{(0)}, E_+^{(0)} + \lambda^2 E_+^{(2)}, E_+^{(0)} + \lambda^2 E_+^{(2)} + \lambda^4 E_+^{(4)}\) using coefficients from the lecture notes.

(b) Compare each truncation to the exact value \(E_+(0.8) = \sqrt{1 + 0.64}\).

(c) How many orders are needed to reach 5% accuracy? 1% accuracy?

(d) At \(\lambda = 1.2\) (beyond \(R = 1\)), compute the truncated series for orders 0 through 4 and compare to the exact value. Does the series oscillate or monotonically diverge?

5.1.1 Toy Model#

Prompts

  • Why do we need perturbation theory? What strategy does it use to approximate unsolvable quantum systems?

  • For the qubit \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x\), find the exact eigenvalues and Taylor-expand them. What do the expansion coefficients represent?

  • What is level repulsion? Why do the two eigenvalues of the qubit model never cross for real \(\lambda\)?

  • When does the perturbative series converge, and what sets the convergence radius? What happens beyond it?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Most quantum systems cannot be solved exactly. Perturbation theory provides a systematic approximation: start from a solvable Hamiltonian \(H_0\), add a small perturbation \(\lambda V\), and expand eigenvalues and eigenstates as power series in \(\lambda\). The qubit model \(H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x\) is the ideal testing ground — a \(2\times 2\) system with an exact solution that we can Taylor-expand and compare order-by-order.

The Qubit Hamiltonian#

(73)#\[\begin{split}H(\lambda) = \hat{\sigma}^z + \lambda\hat{\sigma}^x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \lambda \\ \lambda & -1 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]

Here \(H_0 = \hat{\sigma}^z\) is the unperturbed Hamiltonian with eigenstates \(|0\rangle\) (energy \(+1\)) and \(|1\rangle\) (energy \(-1\)), and \(V = \hat{\sigma}^x\) induces spin flips between them.

Exact Solution#

Diagonalizing:

\[\det(H - E\hat{I}) = (1-E)(-1-E) - \lambda^2 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad E^2 = 1 + \lambda^2\]

Exact Eigenvalues

(74)#\[E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\sqrt{1 + \lambda^2}\]

The energy gap \(\Delta E = 2\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\) never closes for real \(\lambda\) — the two levels repel rather than cross.

Derivation: Exact Eigenstates

To find the eigenstate \(|\psi_+\rangle\) with eigenvalue \(E_+ = \sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\), solve \((H - E_+\hat{I})|\psi\rangle = 0\):

\[\begin{split}\begin{pmatrix} 1 - \sqrt{1+\lambda^2} & \lambda \\ \lambda & -1 - \sqrt{1+\lambda^2} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} = 0\end{split}\]

From the first row: \((1 - \sqrt{1+\lambda^2})\,a + \lambda\,b = 0\), giving

\[\frac{b}{a} = \frac{\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} - 1}{\lambda}\]

Setting \(a = 1 + \sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\) and \(b = \lambda\) (clearing the denominator), the norm is

\[\mathcal{N}^2 = (1+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})^2 + \lambda^2 = 2(1+\lambda^2) + 2\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 2(1+\lambda^2+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})\]

so the normalized eigenstate is

\[|\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle = \frac{(1+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})|0\rangle + \lambda|1\rangle}{\sqrt{2(1+\lambda^2+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})}}\]

The other eigenstate \(|\psi_-\rangle\) follows from orthogonality (swap \(|0\rangle\leftrightarrow|1\rangle\) and flip the sign of \(\lambda\)):

\[|\psi_-(\lambda)\rangle = \frac{(1+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})|1\rangle - \lambda|0\rangle}{\sqrt{2(1+\lambda^2+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})}}\]

At \(\lambda = 0\): \(|\psi_+\rangle = |0\rangle\) and \(|\psi_-\rangle = |1\rangle\), as expected.

Taylor Expansion: Recovering the Perturbative Series#

Expanding \(\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\) for small \(\lambda\):

(75)#\[E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\left(1 + \frac{\lambda^2}{2} - \frac{\lambda^4}{8} + O(\lambda^6)\right)\]

Reading off the perturbative coefficients for the upper state:

\[E_+^{(0)} = 1, \quad E_+^{(1)} = 0, \quad E_+^{(2)} = \frac{1}{2}, \quad E_+^{(3)} = 0, \quad E_+^{(4)} = -\frac{1}{8}\]

All odd-order corrections vanish because \(H(\lambda)\) has a \(\lambda \to -\lambda\) symmetry (equivalently, \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) has zero diagonal elements in the \(|0\rangle, |1\rangle\) basis, so \(E_n^{(1)} = \langle n|\hat{\sigma}^x|n\rangle = 0\)).

The exact eigenstates expanded to low order:

(76)#\[|\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle = |0\rangle + \frac{\lambda}{2}|1\rangle - \frac{\lambda^2}{8}|0\rangle + O(\lambda^3)\]

The admixture of \(|1\rangle\) grows linearly with \(\lambda\), controlled by the ratio coupling/gap.

Derivation: Binomial Series for \(\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\)

Apply the generalized binomial series \((1+x)^\alpha = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k\) with \(\alpha = 1/2\) and \(x = \lambda^2\):

\[\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 + \tfrac{1}{2!}\tfrac{1}{2}(\tfrac{1}{2}-1)\lambda^4 + \tfrac{1}{3!}\tfrac{1}{2}(\tfrac{1}{2}-1)(\tfrac{1}{2}-2)\lambda^6 + \cdots\]

Computing each coefficient:

\[k=1:\quad \binom{1/2}{1} = \tfrac{1}{2}, \qquad k=2:\quad \binom{1/2}{2} = \tfrac{(1/2)(1/2-1)}{2!} = -\tfrac{1}{8}\]
\[k=3:\quad \binom{1/2}{3} = \tfrac{(1/2)(-1/2)(-3/2)}{3!} = \tfrac{1}{16}\]

So \(E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm(1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 - \tfrac{1}{8}\lambda^4 + \tfrac{1}{16}\lambda^6 - \cdots)\), confirming \(E_+^{(2)} = +\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(E_+^{(4)} = -\tfrac{1}{8}\).

For the eigenstates, expand \(|\psi_+(\lambda)\rangle\) by substituting \(\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 + \cdots\) into the exact formula and Taylor-expanding the normalization factor \(\mathcal{N}^{-1}\). At the numerator level:

\[(1+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2})|0\rangle + \lambda|1\rangle = (2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda^2 + \cdots)|0\rangle + \lambda|1\rangle\]
\[\mathcal{N}^2 = 2(1+\lambda^2+\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}) = 2(2 + \tfrac{3}{2}\lambda^2 + \cdots) = 4(1 + \tfrac{3}{4}\lambda^2 + \cdots)\]
\[\mathcal{N}^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \tfrac{3}{8}\lambda^2 + \cdots)\]

Combining: \(|\psi_+\rangle = |0\rangle + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}|1\rangle - \tfrac{\lambda^2}{8}|0\rangle + O(\lambda^3)\), matching the perturbative result.

Level Repulsion#

Level Repulsion

Two energy levels coupled by a perturbation \(V\) repel each other rather than crossing. The minimum gap at any \(\lambda\) is proportional to the matrix element \(|\langle 1|V|0\rangle|\). In the qubit model, \(\langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 1\), so the levels never touch.

This is a general phenomenon: for a \(2\times 2\) block with off-diagonal coupling \(v\), the eigenvalues are \(E_\pm = \bar{E} \pm \sqrt{\delta^2 + v^2}\), where \(\delta\) is the half-gap and \(\bar{E}\) the midpoint. The minimum splitting \(2|v|\) occurs at \(\delta = 0\).

Derivation: Level Repulsion from Second-Order Perturbation Theory

Level repulsion can be derived two ways: (i) from the exact 2×2 eigenvalue formula, and (ii) from the perturbative expansion. Both illuminate why coupling prevents level crossing.

Method 1: Exact 2×2 argument. For any Hermitian \(2\times 2\) matrix with energies \(E_0 > E_1\) (half-gap \(\delta = (E_0-E_1)/2 > 0\)) and off-diagonal coupling \(v\) (real for simplicity):

\[\begin{split} \hat{H} = \bar{E}\hat{I} + \begin{pmatrix} \delta & v \\ v & -\delta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad E_\pm = \bar{E} \pm \sqrt{\delta^2 + v^2} \end{split}\]

The eigenvalue gap is \(\Delta E = 2\sqrt{\delta^2 + v^2} \geq 2\delta\), with equality only when \(v = 0\). For any non-zero coupling, the gap strictly exceeds the unperturbed gap \(2\delta\). Levels can only cross if both \(v = 0\) and \(\delta = 0\) simultaneously — a codimension-2 condition that requires tuning two parameters.

Method 2: Second-order perturbation theory. Apply non-degenerate perturbation theory to \(\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 + \lambda\hat{V}\) with \(\hat{H}_0 = \hat{\sigma}^z\), \(\hat{V} = \hat{\sigma}^x\). Unperturbed: \(E_0^{(0)} = +1\), \(E_1^{(0)} = -1\), with \(\hat{V}_{10} = \langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 1\).

First-order corrections: \(E_n^{(1)} = \langle n|\hat{V}|n\rangle = \langle n|\hat{\sigma}^x|n\rangle = 0\) (diagonal Pauli matrix elements vanish). No first-order energy shift.

Second-order corrections:

\[ E_0^{(2)} = \frac{|\langle 1|\lambda\hat{V}|0\rangle|^2}{E_0^{(0)} - E_1^{(0)}} = \frac{\lambda^2 \cdot 1}{1 - (-1)} = +\frac{\lambda^2}{2} \]
\[ E_1^{(2)} = \frac{|\langle 0|\lambda\hat{V}|1\rangle|^2}{E_1^{(0)} - E_0^{(0)}} = \frac{\lambda^2 \cdot 1}{-1 - 1} = -\frac{\lambda^2}{2} \]

The gap grows:

\[ \Delta E = E_0 - E_1 = 2 + \lambda^2 + O(\lambda^4) \]

The upper level rises, the lower level falls — they repel. This matches the exact result \(2\sqrt{1+\lambda^2} = 2 + \lambda^2 - \frac{\lambda^4}{4} + \cdots\).

General rule. For any two levels with \(E_n > E_m\), the second-order energy denominators satisfy \(E_n - E_m > 0\) and \(E_m - E_n < 0\), so:

\[ E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{k \neq n}\frac{|V_{kn}|^2}{E_n - E_k} > 0 \quad (\text{from the }k=m\text{ term}), \qquad E_m^{(2)} = \sum_{k \neq m}\frac{|V_{km}|^2}{E_m - E_k} < 0 \quad (\text{from the }k=n\text{ term}) \]

The coupling \(V\) always pushes states of different energy apart at second order, regardless of the sign or magnitude of \(V\). This is the perturbative origin of the universal level repulsion phenomenon.

Convergence#

Convergence Criterion

The perturbative series converges when the perturbation is small compared to the energy gap:

\[\lambda \|V\| \ll \Delta E\]

For the qubit, the convergence radius is \(R = 1\), set by the branch points at \(\lambda = \pm\mathrm{i}\) in the complex plane where \(E^2 = 1 + \lambda^2 = 0\).

Derivation: Convergence Radius from Branch Points

The energy \(E_\pm(\lambda) = \pm\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\) is analytic in \(\lambda\) except where \(1+\lambda^2 = 0\), i.e., at \(\lambda = \pm\mathrm{i}\). These are branch points of the square root.

The Taylor series centered at \(\lambda = 0\) converges in the largest disk in the complex \(\lambda\)-plane that contains no singularity. The nearest singularity is at distance \(|\lambda| = 1\) from the origin, so the convergence radius is

\[R = \min_{\text{singularities}} |\lambda_*| = |\pm\mathrm{i}| = 1\]

Physical interpretation: at \(\lambda = \pm\mathrm{i}\) the two energy levels coalesce (\(E_+ = E_- = 0\)), forming an exceptional point — a non-Hermitian degeneracy (since \(H(\pm\mathrm{i})\) is not Hermitian). The perturbative series, built on the assumption that the two levels are distinct, cannot be continued past this point.

More generally, for a \(2\times 2\) system with gap \(\Delta E\) and coupling \(v\), the branch points are at \(\lambda_* = \pm\mathrm{i}\,\Delta E/(2v)\), giving convergence radius \(R = \Delta E/(2|v|)\), confirming the general criterion \(\lambda|v| \ll \Delta E\).

Beyond \(|\lambda| = R\), the series diverges even though the exact solution remains valid. Perturbation theory breaks down precisely when the coupling becomes comparable to the energy gap — the perturbation can no longer be treated as “small.”

Discussion

The first-order corrections \(E_\pm^{(1)} = 0\) vanish because \(\hat{\sigma}^x\) has zero diagonal elements. This is not a coincidence but a consequence of parity symmetry. More generally, when does symmetry guarantee that certain perturbative corrections vanish? Can you think of physical examples where recognizing this saves enormous computational effort?

Summary#

  • Perturbation theory expands eigenvalues as \(E_n(\lambda) = E_n^{(0)} + \lambda E_n^{(1)} + \lambda^2 E_n^{(2)} + \cdots\).

  • The qubit model provides an exact benchmark: \(E_\pm = \pm\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\) reproduces all perturbative coefficients when Taylor-expanded.

  • Level repulsion: coupled levels repel rather than cross; the gap is proportional to \(|\langle 1|V|0\rangle|\).

  • Convergence: the series converges for \(\lambda\|V\| \ll \Delta E\); breakdown signals that the perturbation is no longer “small.”

2. Verify that \(E_+^{(1)} = \langle 0|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle = 0\). Explain why this vanishing is guaranteed by the \(\lambda \to -\lambda\) symmetry of \(H(\lambda)\).

4. Write the first-order state correction \(|\psi_+^{(1)}\rangle = \frac{\langle 1|\hat{\sigma}^x|0\rangle}{E_+^{(0)} - E_-^{(0)}}|1\rangle\). Compare with the exact eigenstate expanded to \(O(\lambda)\).

5. The convergence radius is \(R = 1\), set by branch points at \(\lambda = \pm\mathrm{i}\). For \(\lambda = 0.5\) and \(\lambda = 0.8\), compare the second-order approximation \(E_+ \approx 1 + \lambda^2/2\) to the exact value. At what \(\lambda\) does the error exceed 10%?

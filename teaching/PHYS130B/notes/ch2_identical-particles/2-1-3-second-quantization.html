2.1.3 Second Quantization#

Prompts

  • What is the key difference between first-quantized and second-quantized descriptions of a many-body system? Why is the occupation-number representation more natural for identical particles?

  • Define the creation operator \(\hat{a}^\dagger\) and annihilation operator \(\hat{a}\). How do they act on Fock states for bosons versus fermions?

  • Why do boson operators satisfy commutation relations while fermion operators satisfy anticommutation relations? How does each algebra enforce the correct quantum statistics?

  • What is boson enhancement? If you apply \(\hat{b}^\dagger\) to a state with \(n\) bosons, why is the amplitude \(\sqrt{n+1}\) and what physical phenomenon does this explain?

  • Show that \((\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\) for fermions and explain why this is the Pauli exclusion principle in operator language.

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Second quantization reformulates quantum mechanics so that identical-particle statistics are automatic. Instead of labeling individual particles and then symmetrizing or antisymmetrizing by hand, we simply count how many particles occupy each single-particle mode. This shift from “particle labels” to “occupation numbers” handles variable particle number naturally and leads directly to the operator algebra of creation and annihilation.

First vs Second Quantization#

First quantization asks: “Which particle is in which state?” An \(N\)-particle state is a tensor product

\[\vert\Psi\rangle = \vert\psi_1\rangle \otimes \vert\psi_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\psi_N\rangle\]

that must be manually symmetrized (bosons) or antisymmetrized (fermions).

Second quantization asks: “How many particles occupy each state?” A state is specified by occupation numbers

\[\vert n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_D\rangle\]

where \(n_\alpha\) counts particles in single-particle mode \(\alpha\). Symmetry is built into the operator algebra—no manual (anti)symmetrization needed.

Fock Space#

Fock Space

The Fock space is the direct sum of all \(N\)-particle Hilbert spaces:

\[\mathcal{F} = \bigoplus_{N=0}^\infty \mathcal{H}_N\]

Its basis states are Fock states \(\vert n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_D\rangle\), labeled by occupation numbers \(\{n_\alpha\}\). These form a complete orthonormal basis:

\[\langle n_1', n_2', \ldots \vert n_1, n_2, \ldots \rangle = \prod_\alpha \delta_{n_\alpha' n_\alpha}\]

The vacuum \(\vert 0\rangle \equiv \vert 0, 0, \ldots, 0\rangle\) has no particles.

Creation and Annihilation Operators#

The creation operator \(\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha\) adds a particle to mode \(\alpha\); the annihilation operator \(\hat{a}_\alpha\) removes one. Their action on Fock states differs for bosons and fermions.

Bosonic Operators (\(\hat{b}_\alpha\), \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha\))#

\[\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle = \sqrt{n_\alpha + 1}\;\vert \ldots, n_\alpha + 1, \ldots\rangle\]
\[\hat{b}_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle = \sqrt{n_\alpha}\;\vert \ldots, n_\alpha - 1, \ldots\rangle\]

The \(\sqrt{n+1}\) and \(\sqrt{n}\) factors ensure normalization.

Derivation: Origin of the \(\sqrt{n}\) Factors

The \(\sqrt{n+1}\) and \(\sqrt{n}\) factors are uniquely determined by three requirements: (1) \(\hat{b}^\dagger\) is the adjoint of \(\hat{b}\), (2) \(\hat{n} = \hat{b}^\dagger \hat{b}\) has eigenvalue \(n\) on \(\vert n\rangle\), and (3) \(\hat{b}\vert 0\rangle = 0\).

From the number operator. Define \(\hat{b}^\dagger\vert n\rangle = f(n)\vert n+1\rangle\) for some function \(f(n)\) to be determined. Since \(\hat{b}^\dagger\) and \(\hat{b}\) are adjoints:

\[\langle n\vert \hat{b}\hat{b}^\dagger \vert n\rangle = \langle n+1\vert f(n)^* f(n) \vert n+1\rangle = \vert f(n)\vert^2\]

Using the commutation relation \(\hat{b}\hat{b}^\dagger = \hat{b}^\dagger\hat{b} + 1 = \hat{n} + 1\):

\[\langle n\vert (\hat{n} + 1)\vert n\rangle = n + 1\]

Equating: \(\vert f(n)\vert^2 = n + 1\), so \(f(n) = \sqrt{n+1}\) (choosing the positive real convention). Therefore:

\[\hat{b}^\dagger\vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\,\vert n+1\rangle\]

For the annihilation operator, use \(\hat{b} = (\hat{b}^\dagger)^\dagger\) and compute the matrix element:

\[\langle n-1\vert \hat{b}\vert n\rangle = \langle n\vert \hat{b}^\dagger\vert n-1\rangle^* = \sqrt{n}\]

giving \(\hat{b}\vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n}\,\vert n-1\rangle\). The entire Fock space is generated by repeated creation: \(\vert n\rangle = \frac{(\hat{b}^\dagger)^n}{\sqrt{n!}}\vert 0\rangle\).

Fermionic Operators (\(\hat{c}_\alpha\), \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\))#

With \(n_\alpha \in \{0,1\}\):

\[\begin{split}\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle = \begin{cases} (-1)^{P_\alpha} \vert \ldots, 1, \ldots\rangle & n_\alpha = 0 \\ 0 & n_\alpha = 1 \end{cases}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\hat{c}_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle = \begin{cases} (-1)^{P_\alpha} \vert \ldots, 0, \ldots\rangle & n_\alpha = 1 \\ 0 & n_\alpha = 0 \end{cases}\end{split}\]

where \(P_\alpha = \sum_{i<\alpha} n_i\) counts fermions in earlier modes. The phase \((-1)^{P_\alpha}\) enforces antisymmetry under particle exchange.

Algebraic Relations#

Commutation Relations (Bosons)

\[[\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = \delta_{\alpha\beta}, \quad [\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}_\beta] = 0, \quad [\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = 0\]

Derivation: Bosonic Commutation Relation

We verify \([\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\) by acting on an arbitrary Fock state. Consider a single mode first (\(\alpha = \beta\)):

\[\hat{b}\hat{b}^\dagger \vert n\rangle = \hat{b}\bigl(\sqrt{n+1}\,\vert n+1\rangle\bigr) = \sqrt{n+1}\cdot\sqrt{n+1}\,\vert n\rangle = (n+1)\vert n\rangle\]
\[\hat{b}^\dagger\hat{b} \vert n\rangle = \hat{b}^\dagger\bigl(\sqrt{n}\,\vert n-1\rangle\bigr) = \sqrt{n}\cdot\sqrt{n}\,\vert n\rangle = n\vert n\rangle\]

Subtracting: \([\hat{b}, \hat{b}^\dagger]\vert n\rangle = (n+1-n)\vert n\rangle = \vert n\rangle\). Since this holds for every basis state \(\vert n\rangle\), we conclude \([\hat{b}, \hat{b}^\dagger] = \hat{I}\).

For different modes (\(\alpha \neq \beta\)): the operators act on independent tensor factors. Since \(\hat{b}_\alpha\) and \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\beta\) act on different modes, they commute by the same tensor product argument as in §2.1.1. Hence \([\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = 0\) for \(\alpha \neq \beta\), and combining: \([\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\).

Similarly, \([\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta] = 0\) because applying two creation operators in either order gives the same result: \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{b}^\dagger_\beta \vert\ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots, n_\beta, \ldots\rangle = \sqrt{(n_\alpha+1)(n_\beta+1)}\,\vert\ldots, n_\alpha+1, \ldots, n_\beta+1, \ldots\rangle\) regardless of order.

Anticommutation Relations (Fermions)

\[\{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta\} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}, \quad \{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}_\beta\} = 0, \quad \{\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta\} = 0\]

Derivation: Fermionic Anticommutation Relation

We verify \(\{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\} = 1\) for a single mode with \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1\}\).

Case \(n_\alpha = 0\):

\[\hat{c}\hat{c}^\dagger \vert 0\rangle = \hat{c}\vert 1\rangle = \vert 0\rangle, \qquad \hat{c}^\dagger\hat{c}\vert 0\rangle = \hat{c}^\dagger \cdot 0 = 0\]

So \(\{\hat{c}, \hat{c}^\dagger\}\vert 0\rangle = 1 \cdot \vert 0\rangle\).

Case \(n_\alpha = 1\):

\[\hat{c}\hat{c}^\dagger \vert 1\rangle = \hat{c}\cdot 0 = 0, \qquad \hat{c}^\dagger\hat{c}\vert 1\rangle = \hat{c}^\dagger\vert 0\rangle = \vert 1\rangle\]

So \(\{\hat{c}, \hat{c}^\dagger\}\vert 1\rangle = 1 \cdot \vert 1\rangle\).

Both cases give \(\{\hat{c}, \hat{c}^\dagger\} = \hat{I}\) on the single-mode space.

For different modes (\(\alpha \neq \beta\)): we must verify \(\{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta\} = 0\). Acting on a Fock state \(\vert n_\alpha, n_\beta\rangle\):

\[\hat{c}_\alpha \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta \vert n_\alpha, n_\beta\rangle = (-1)^{P_\beta}\hat{c}_\alpha \vert n_\alpha, n_\beta + 1\rangle = (-1)^{P_\beta}(-1)^{P'_\alpha}\vert n_\alpha - 1, n_\beta + 1\rangle\]

where \(P'_\alpha\) is the parity count with the updated occupations. The key point is that adding a fermion to mode \(\beta\) (if \(\beta < \alpha\)) changes the parity count \(P_\alpha\) by 1, so \(P'_\alpha = P_\alpha + 1\). This extra sign flip ensures \(\hat{c}_\alpha \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta = -\hat{c}^\dagger_\beta \hat{c}_\alpha\), giving \(\{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta\} = 0\).

The anticommutation \(\{\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta\} = 0\) (for \(\alpha \neq \beta\)) follows similarly: the phase factors from the ordering convention produce a sign flip when the two creation operators are exchanged, so \(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{c}^\dagger_\beta = -\hat{c}^\dagger_\beta \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\). Setting \(\alpha = \beta\) gives \((\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\) — the Pauli exclusion principle.

Number Operator#

The number operator \(\hat{n}_\alpha = \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\alpha\) counts particles in mode \(\alpha\):

\[\hat{n}_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle = n_\alpha \vert \ldots, n_\alpha, \ldots\rangle\]

Fock states are eigenstates of every \(\hat{n}_\alpha\) simultaneously.

Derivation: Number Operator Eigenvalues Are Non-Negative Integers

We prove from the algebra that the eigenvalues of \(\hat{n}_\alpha = \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\alpha\) must be non-negative integers. The argument is parallel to the harmonic oscillator but applies to both bosons and fermions.

Setup. Let \(\vert n\rangle\) be an eigenstate of \(\hat{n}_\alpha\) with eigenvalue \(n\):

\[ \hat{n}_\alpha \vert n\rangle = n \vert n\rangle \]

Step 1: Eigenvalue is non-negative.

\[ n = \langle n \vert \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\alpha \vert n \rangle = \Vert \hat{a}_\alpha \vert n \rangle \Vert^2 \geq 0 \]

(norm squared is always non-negative). Equality \(n = 0\) iff \(\hat{a}_\alpha \vert n \rangle = 0\).

Step 2: Ladder operator action changes eigenvalue by \(\pm 1\).

From the (anti)commutation relations:

  • Bosons: \([\hat{n}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha] = [\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha] = \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha [\hat{b}_\alpha, \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha] = \hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha\)

  • Fermions: \([\hat{n}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha] = [\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha] = \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\{\hat{c}_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\} - \{\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\}\hat{c}_\alpha = \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\)

Similarly \([\hat{n}_\alpha, \hat{a}_\alpha] = -\hat{a}_\alpha\) for both types. Therefore:

\[ \hat{n}_\alpha (\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n\rangle) = (\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{n}_\alpha + \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha) \vert n\rangle = (n+1)(\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \vert n\rangle) \]
\[ \hat{n}_\alpha (\hat{a}_\alpha \vert n\rangle) = (\hat{a}_\alpha \hat{n}_\alpha - \hat{a}_\alpha) \vert n\rangle = (n-1)(\hat{a}_\alpha \vert n\rangle) \]

Step 3: Eigenvalues are non-negative integers.

Starting from any eigenvalue \(n \geq 0\), repeated application of \(\hat{a}_\alpha\) gives eigenvalues \(n, n-1, n-2, \ldots\). By Step 1, these must all be \(\geq 0\). The sequence must terminate when \(\hat{a}_\alpha \vert n_0 \rangle = 0\) for some \(n_0 \geq 0\).

For bosons: The condition \(\hat{b}_\alpha \vert n_0 \rangle = 0\) is only consistent with \(n_0 = 0\) (since the lowering step gives \(n_0 - 1\) but this must be non-negative, so \(n_0 = 0\)). Starting from \(\vert 0 \rangle\) and applying \(\hat{b}^\dagger_\alpha\) repeatedly: eigenvalues are \(n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) (all non-negative integers).

For fermions: The anticommutation relation \(\{\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\} = 2(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\) forces \((\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\), so the raising sequence terminates after one step. Starting from \(\vert 0 \rangle\): only \(\vert 0 \rangle\) and \(\vert 1 \rangle = \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha \vert 0 \rangle\) exist. Eigenvalues are \(n \in \{0, 1\}\) — the Pauli exclusion principle emerges directly from the algebra.

Boson Enhancement#

Apply \(\hat{b}^\dagger\) to a superposition of Fock states:

\[\hat{b}^\dagger \vert\psi\rangle = \hat{b}^\dagger \sum_n c_n \vert n\rangle = \sum_n c_n \sqrt{n+1}\;\vert n+1\rangle\]

The \(\sqrt{n+1}\) factor means it is more probable to add a boson to a mode that already contains many bosons. This “rich get richer” effect is the origin of stimulated emission—the transition rate into a mode with \(n\) bosons is proportional to \(n+1\) (stimulated + spontaneous). It also drives Bose-Einstein condensation.

Example: Stimulated Emission

Problem. A cavity mode contains \(n\) photons. A two-level atom in the excited state can emit a photon into the mode. Show that the emission matrix element is proportional to \(\sqrt{n+1}\).

Solution. The relevant operator is \(\hat{b}^\dagger\) acting on the photon Fock state \(\vert n\rangle\):

\[\hat{b}^\dagger \vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\;\vert n+1\rangle\]

The emission amplitude is \(\langle n+1\vert \hat{b}^\dagger \vert n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\), so the emission rate \(\propto n+1\). The “\(+1\)” corresponds to spontaneous emission (present even at \(n=0\)), while the “\(n\)” part is stimulated emission. This is why lasers work: a large photon population exponentially amplifies further emission.

Pauli Exclusion in Operator Language#

For fermions, the anticommutation relation \(\{\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha, \hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha\} = 0\) implies:

\[(\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\]

Attempting to create a second fermion in an occupied mode gives zero—the state is annihilated. This is the Pauli exclusion principle encoded directly in the algebra, not imposed by hand.

Discussion: Why Commutators vs Anticommutators?

Bosons obey commutation relations, fermions obey anticommutation relations. This is not an arbitrary choice—it is dictated by the symmetry of the wavefunction. Symmetric states require commuting creation operators; antisymmetric states require anticommuting ones. The spin-statistics theorem (a consequence of relativistic quantum field theory) links integer spin to bosonic statistics and half-integer spin to fermionic statistics. Can you see why \([\hat{b}^\dagger_1, \hat{b}^\dagger_2] = 0\) produces symmetric states while \(\{\hat{c}^\dagger_1, \hat{c}^\dagger_2\} = 0\) produces antisymmetric ones?

Derivation: Second-Quantized Form of a One-Body Operator

A one-body operator acts independently on each of \(N\) particles. In first quantization:

\[ \hat{T}^{(N)} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} t(\hat{x}_i, \hat{p}_i) \]

where \(t\) is a single-particle observable (kinetic energy, external potential, etc.). We show that the same physics is encoded in second quantization as

\[ \hat{T} = \sum_{\alpha,\beta} t_{\alpha\beta}\, \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\beta, \qquad t_{\alpha\beta} = \langle\alpha\vert t\vert\beta\rangle. \]

Step 1: Expand in a single-particle basis. Insert a resolution of the identity to write

\[ t = \sum_{\alpha,\beta} \vert\alpha\rangle \langle\alpha\vert t\vert\beta\rangle \langle\beta\vert = \sum_{\alpha,\beta} t_{\alpha\beta}\, \vert\alpha\rangle\langle\beta\vert. \]

Step 2: Sum over all particles. In the \(N\)-particle first-quantized space,

\[ \hat{T}^{(N)} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{\alpha,\beta} t_{\alpha\beta}\, \vert\alpha\rangle_i \langle\beta\vert_i. \]

Step 3: Identify the Fock-space image. Acting on a symmetrized or antisymmetrized \(N\)-particle state, the operator \(\sum_i \vert\alpha\rangle_i\langle\beta\vert_i\) removes one particle from mode \(\beta\) and places it in mode \(\alpha\), summing the contribution from each particle. By construction, this is exactly \(\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\beta\). Therefore

\[ \hat{T} = \sum_{\alpha,\beta} t_{\alpha\beta}\, \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\beta. \]

The (anti)symmetrization is handled automatically by the algebra of \(\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha\), \(\hat{a}_\alpha\).

Diagonal case. When the basis diagonalizes \(t\) with eigenvalues \(\varepsilon_\alpha\) (i.e., \(t_{\alpha\beta} = \varepsilon_\alpha \delta_{\alpha\beta}\)):

\[ \hat{T} = \sum_\alpha \varepsilon_\alpha\, \hat{n}_\alpha. \]

This is the free Hamiltonian: each particle in mode \(\alpha\) contributes energy \(\varepsilon_\alpha\). The identical-particle statistics enter only through the algebra—\([\hat{b},\hat{b}^\dagger]=1\) for bosons vs.\ \(\{\hat{c},\hat{c}^\dagger\}=1\) for fermions—while the Hamiltonian form \(\sum_\alpha \varepsilon_\alpha \hat{n}_\alpha\) is universal.

Summary#

  • Second quantization replaces particle labels with occupation numbers \(\{n_\alpha\}\), making identical-particle statistics automatic.

  • Fock space \(\mathcal{F} = \bigoplus_N \mathcal{H}_N\) spans all particle-number sectors; Fock states \(\vert n_1, n_2, \ldots\rangle\) form its orthonormal basis.

  • Creation/annihilation operators add and remove particles; commutators \([\hat{b},\hat{b}^\dagger]=1\) enforce bosonic statistics, anticommutators \(\{\hat{c},\hat{c}^\dagger\}=1\) enforce fermionic statistics.

  • Boson enhancement: the \(\sqrt{n+1}\) factor in \(\hat{b}^\dagger\vert n\rangle\) drives stimulated emission and Bose-Einstein condensation.

  • Pauli exclusion: \((\hat{c}^\dagger_\alpha)^2 = 0\) forbids double occupation, following automatically from anticommutation.

  • The number operator \(\hat{n}_\alpha = \hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha \hat{a}_\alpha\) counts particles per mode.

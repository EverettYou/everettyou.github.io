2.2.1 Angular Momentum Algebra#

Prompts

  • What are the fundamental commutation relations for angular momentum components, and why must they hold for any form of angular momentum?

  • Define the ladder operators \(J_+\) and \(J_-\). How do they connect eigenstates with different magnetic quantum numbers?

  • What is the Casimir operator \(J^2\), and why does it commute with all three components of \(\boldsymbol{J}\)?

  • What are the allowed eigenvalues of \(J^2\) and \(J_z\)? How does quantization arise purely from the algebra, without solving a differential equation?

  • Orbital angular momentum comes from \(\boldsymbol{L} = \boldsymbol{r} \times \boldsymbol{p}\), while spin has no classical meaning. How can they obey the same commutation relations?

  • If angular momentum quantization follows purely from commutation algebra, could there be other observables with similar algebra? What would they represent physically?

Lecture Notes#

Key Concept: Angular Momentum Algebra

Angular momentum is far more than a rotational property—it is a fundamental quantum number that governs atomic structure and the symmetry of space itself. All forms of angular momentum (orbital, spin, total) satisfy the same commutation relations, revealing a deep unity in quantum mechanics.

Angular Momentum Operators#

Orbital angular momentum is defined as:

(96)#\[ \boldsymbol{L} = \boldsymbol{r} \times \boldsymbol{p}\]

Spin angular momentum \(\boldsymbol{S}\) is an intrinsic angular momentum with no classical analog.

Total angular momentum:

\[ \boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{L} + \boldsymbol{S} \quad \text{(or more generally, any angular momentum)}\]

Despite their different origins, all forms satisfy the same fundamental commutation relations.

Commutation Relations for Angular Momentum

The three components of any angular momentum operator satisfy:

(97)#\[ [J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\]

where \(\epsilon_{ijk}\) is the Levi-Civita symbol (completely antisymmetric):

\[ \epsilon_{123} = \epsilon_{231} = \epsilon_{312} = +1, \quad \epsilon_{321} = \epsilon_{213} = \epsilon_{132} = -1, \quad \text{else} = 0\]

Explicit component relations:

(98)#\[ [J_x, J_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_z, \quad [J_y, J_z] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_x, \quad [J_z, J_x] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_y\]

These are cyclic in \((x, y, z)\).

Derivation: Angular Momentum Commutation Relations

For orbital angular momentum \(L_i = \epsilon_{ijk} r_j p_k\) (summation over repeated indices):

\[ [L_i, L_j] = [\epsilon_{imn} r_m p_n, \epsilon_{jpq} r_p p_q]\]

Using \([r_m, r_p] = 0\), \([p_n, p_q] = 0\), and the canonical relation \([r_m, p_q] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \delta_{mq}\):

\[ [r_m p_n, r_p p_q] = \mathrm{i}\hbar (\delta_{mq} p_n r_p - \delta_{np} r_m p_q)\]

Therefore:

\[ [L_i, L_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{imn} \epsilon_{jpq} (\delta_{mq} p_n r_p - \delta_{np} r_m p_q)\]

Using the identity \(\epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{imn} = \delta_{jm}\delta_{kn} - \delta_{jn}\delta_{km}\) to simplify:

\[ [L_i, L_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} L_k\]

For spin: The commutation relation is postulated based on rotational invariance, and all angular momenta are required to satisfy this same algebra.

Discussion: Orbital vs. Spin Angular Momentum

Orbital \(\boldsymbol{L}\) arises from position and momentum (classical limit possible). Spin \(\boldsymbol{S}\) is intrinsic with no classical analog. Yet both satisfy \([J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\).

Are they fundamentally the same kind of angular momentum, or do we just use the same word for two things? If a physicist measures total angular momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} + \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) and finds \(j = 1\), can they determine the contribution from orbital versus spin without additional measurements?

Why These Relations?#

For orbital angular momentum, these relations follow rigorously from the canonical position-momentum commutation relation \([x_i, p_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \delta_{ij}\). For spin, they are postulated based on rotational invariance. The commutation relations encode the fundamental structure of rotations in quantum mechanics.

Physical Meaning: The Geometry of Rotations

The commutation relations \([J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\) encode the geometry of rotations: the order of rotations matters. This is the quantum manifestation of the non-commutativity of 3D rotations.

The Casimir Operator#

Casimir Operator

The Casimir operator (or total angular momentum squared) is:

(99)#\[ J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2\]

A remarkable property is that \(J^2\) commutes with all components of \(\boldsymbol{J}\):

(100)#\[ [J^2, J_x] = 0, \quad [J^2, J_y] = 0, \quad [J^2, J_z] = 0\]

Why This Matters#

Because \([J^2, J_z] = 0\), we can find simultaneous eigenstates of both \(J^2\) and \(J_z\), labeled as \(|j, m\rangle\). This allows a clean labeling of quantum states by two quantum numbers.

Derivation: Commutation of Casimir with \(J_z\)

Proof (for \([J^2, J_z]\)):

\[ [J^2, J_z] = [J_x^2, J_z] + [J_y^2, J_z] + [J_z^2, J_z]\]

The last term is zero. For the first:

\[ [J_x^2, J_z] = J_x[J_x, J_z] + [J_x, J_z]J_x = J_x(-\mathrm{i}\hbar J_y) + (-\mathrm{i}\hbar J_y)J_x = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(J_x J_y + J_y J_x)\]
\[[J_y^2, J_z] = J_y[J_y, J_z] + [J_y, J_z]J_y = J_y(\mathrm{i}\hbar J_x) + (\mathrm{i}\hbar J_x)J_y = \mathrm{i}\hbar(J_y J_x + J_x J_y)\]

These cancel: \([J^2, J_z] = 0\). ✓

Simultaneous Eigenstates#

\[ |j, m\rangle\]

with eigenvalues:

Eigenvalues of Angular Momentum

\[ J^2 |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) |j, m\rangle\]
(101)#\[J_z |j, m\rangle = \hbar m |j, m\rangle\]

where \(j\) is the angular momentum quantum number and \(m\) is the magnetic quantum number (projection along \(z\)).

Ladder Operators (Raising and Lowering)#

Ladder Operators

The raising operator \(J_+\) and lowering operator \(J_-\) are defined as:

(102)#\[ J_+ = J_x + \mathrm{i} J_y, \quad J_- = J_x - \mathrm{i} J_y\]

Inverse relations:

\[ J_x = \frac{1}{2}(J_+ + J_-), \quad J_y = \frac{1}{2\mathrm{i}}(J_+ - J_-)\]

Commutation Relations for Ladder Operators#

(103)#\[ [J_z, J_+] = \hbar J_+, \quad [J_z, J_-] = -\hbar J_-\]
(104)#\[ [J_+, J_-] = 2\hbar J_z\]
(105)#\[ [J^2, J_\pm] = 0\]

Action of Ladder Operators on Eigenstates

(106)#\[ J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{j(j+1) - m(m+1)} |j, m+1\rangle\]
(107)#\[ J_- |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{j(j+1) - m(m-1)} |j, m-1\rangle\]

Physical interpretation:

  • \(J_+\) raises the \(z\)-projection of angular momentum: \(m \to m+1\).

  • \(J_-\) lowers the \(z\)-projection: \(m \to m-1\).

  • The coefficient \(\sqrt{\cdots}\) is a normalization factor ensuring that \(|j, m+1\rangle\) is normalized if \(|j, m\rangle\) is.

Derivation: Raising Operator Action

We prove: \(J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{j(j+1) - m(m+1)} |j, m+1\rangle\)

Key relations:

  • \(J_z |j, m\rangle = \hbar m |j, m\rangle\)

  • \(J^2 |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) |j, m\rangle\)

  • \([J_z, J_+] = \hbar J_+\)

  • \(J^2\) commutes with \(J_\pm\)

Argument: Let \(|\psi\rangle = J_+ |j, m\rangle\). We determine what state it is.

Apply \(J_z\):

\[ J_z |\psi\rangle = J_z J_+ |j, m\rangle = (J_+ J_z + [J_z, J_+]) |j, m\rangle = J_+ J_z |j, m\rangle + \hbar J_+ |j, m\rangle\]
\[= J_+ (\hbar m) |j, m\rangle + \hbar J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar(m+1) J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar(m+1) |\psi\rangle\]

So \(|\psi\rangle\) is an eigenstate of \(J_z\) with eigenvalue \(\hbar(m+1)\), meaning it is proportional to \(|j, m+1\rangle\).

Apply \(J^2\):

\[ J^2 |\psi\rangle = J^2 J_+ |j, m\rangle = J_+ J^2 |j, m\rangle = J_+ (\hbar^2 j(j+1)) |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) |\psi\rangle\]

So \(|\psi\rangle = |j, m+1\rangle\) (up to normalization).

Normalization: To find the coefficient, use the norm:

\[ \langle j, m | J_-^\dagger J_- |j, m\rangle = \langle j, m | J_+ J_- |j, m\rangle\]

Using \(J_+ J_- = J^2 - J_z^2 + \hbar J_z\):

\[ \langle j, m | (J^2 - J_z^2 + \hbar J_z) |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) - \hbar^2 m^2 + \hbar^2 m = \hbar^2(j(j+1) - m^2 + m)\]
\[= \hbar^2(j(j+1) - m(m-1)) = \hbar^2((j-m)(j+m+1))\]

Therefore:

\[ J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)} |j, m+1\rangle \quad ✓\]

which can also be written as \(\hbar\sqrt{j(j+1) - m(m+1)}\).

Normalization Coefficients#

The square root coefficients can be rewritten using \(j(j+1) - m(m+1) = (j-m)(j+m+1)\):

\[ J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)} |j, m+1\rangle\]

Similarly, \(j(j+1) - m(m-1) = (j+m)(j-m+1)\):

\[ J_- |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j+m)(j-m+1)} |j, m-1\rangle\]

Angular Momentum Quantization#

Derivation from Commutation Relations#

The allowed values of angular momentum quantum numbers arise purely from the algebra of commutation relations, without solving any differential equations!

Key observation: For a state with angular momentum quantum number \(j\), the eigenvalue equation \(J_z |j, m\rangle = \hbar m |j, m\rangle\) requires that:

\[ m \in \{-j, -j+1, \ldots, j-1, j\}\]

That is, \(m\) ranges from \(-j\) to \(+j\) in integer steps. This has \(2j+1\) possible values.

For physical systems, the requirement of single-valuedness (the wavefunction must return to itself after a \(2\pi\) rotation) forces:

\[ j \in \{0, \frac{1}{2}, 1, \frac{3}{2}, 2, \ldots\}\]

Both integer and half-integer values are allowed in quantum mechanics (unlike classical mechanics, where angular momentum is always an integer multiple of \(\hbar\)).

Integer vs. Half-Integer#

  • Integer \(j\) (\(j = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\)): examples are orbital angular momentum, photons, pions.

  • Half-integer \(j\) (\(j = 1/2, 3/2, \ldots\)): examples are electron spin, quarks, neutrinos.

The distinction has profound consequences through the spin-statistics theorem: integer-\(j\) particles are bosons; half-integer-\(j\) particles are fermions.

Matrix Representations#

Spin-1/2 (Two-Dimensional Representation)#

For \(j = 1/2\), there are \(2j + 1 = 2\) basis states: \(|1/2, +1/2\rangle\) and \(|1/2, -1/2\rangle\).

Pauli Matrix Representations

The angular momentum operators are represented as:

\[\begin{split} J_z = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \hat{\sigma}^z\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}J_+ = \hbar \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}J_- = \hbar \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}J_x = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \hat{\sigma}^x, \quad J_y = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ \mathrm{i} & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \hat{\sigma}^y\end{split}\]

where \(\hat{\sigma}^i\) are the Pauli matrices.

General Pattern#

For a representation with angular momentum quantum number \(j\), the operators are \((2j+1) \times (2j+1)\) matrices in the basis \(\{|j, -j\rangle, |j, -j+1\rangle, \ldots, |j, j\rangle\}\).

All such representations are irreducible (cannot be block-diagonalized while respecting the angular momentum algebra).

Orbital Angular Momentum (Specific Case)#

In Quantum Mechanics#

Orbital angular momentum \(\boldsymbol{L} = \boldsymbol{r} \times \boldsymbol{p}\) with components:

(108)#\[ L_x = y p_z - z p_y, \quad L_y = z p_x - x p_z, \quad L_z = x p_y - y p_x\]

In the position representation:

\[ L_z = -\mathrm{i}\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi} \quad \text{(in spherical coordinates)}\]

Spherical Harmonics as Eigenfunctions#

The eigenfunctions of \(L^2\) and \(L_z\) are the spherical harmonics \(Y_\ell^m(\theta, \phi)\):

\[ L^2 Y_\ell^m = \hbar^2 \ell(\ell+1) Y_\ell^m\]
\[L_z Y_\ell^m = \hbar m Y_\ell^m\]

where \(\ell \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) (always integer) and \(m \in \{-\ell, \ldots, \ell\}\).

Note

Orbital angular momentum can only have integer quantum numbers. Half-integer is forbidden for \(L\) (a consequence of requiring single-valuedness for the spatial wavefunction on a sphere).

Useful Relations and Identities#

Useful Commutators#

\[ [J_x, J_x] = 0 \quad \text{(self-commutation always zero)}\]

For different components:

\[ [J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\]

Useful Products#

\[ J_+ J_- = J^2 - J_z^2 + \hbar J_z\]
\[J_- J_+ = J^2 - J_z^2 - \hbar J_z\]

Derivation: Ladder Product Relations

These can be verified by expanding \(J_\pm = J_x \pm \mathrm{i} J_y\) and using \([J_x, J_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_z\).

Starting from \(J_\pm = J_x \pm \mathrm{i} J_y\):

\[ J_+ J_- = (J_x + \mathrm{i} J_y)(J_x - \mathrm{i} J_y) = J_x^2 - \mathrm{i} J_x J_y + \mathrm{i} J_y J_x + J_y^2\]
\[= J_x^2 + J_y^2 + i(J_y J_x - J_x J_y) = J_x^2 + J_y^2 - i[J_x, J_y]\]
\[= J_x^2 + J_y^2 - i \cdot \mathrm{i}\hbar J_z = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + \hbar J_z\]

Since \(J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2\), we have \(J_x^2 + J_y^2 = J^2 - J_z^2\), giving:

\[ J_+ J_- = J^2 - J_z^2 + \hbar J_z\]

The second relation follows similarly.

Completeness of Ladder Operators#

Every state \(|j, m\rangle\) can be reached from \(|j, j\rangle\) (the state with maximum \(m\)) by repeatedly applying \(J_-\):

\[ |j, m\rangle \propto (J_-)^{j-m} |j, j\rangle\]

Similarly, every state can be reached from \(|j, -j\rangle\) by applying \(J_+\) repeatedly.

Expectation Values#

For a state \(|j, m\rangle\):

\[ \langle J_x \rangle = \langle J_y \rangle = 0 \quad \text{(no preferred $x$ or $y$ direction)}\]
\[\langle J_z \rangle = \hbar m\]
\[\langle J^2 \rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1)\]

Summary#

  • Angular momentum algebra: All angular momenta satisfy \([J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\).

  • Casimir operator \(J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2\) commutes with all components; simultaneous eigenstates exist: \(|j, m\rangle\).

  • Eigenvalues: \(J^2 |j, m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) |j, m\rangle\) and \(J_z |j, m\rangle = \hbar m |j, m\rangle\).

  • Quantization: \(j \in \{0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, \ldots\}\) and \(m \in \{-j, -j+1, \ldots, j-1, j\}\) (integer steps).

  • Ladder operators \(J_\pm = J_x \pm \mathrm{i} J_y\) shift \(m\) by one unit: \(J_\pm |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j\mp m)(j\pm m+1)} |j, m\pm 1\rangle\).

  • Orbital angular momentum always has integer \(\ell\); spin can be half-integer.

  • Representation matrices: For spin-1/2, use Pauli matrices; general \(j\) requires \((2j+1) \times (2j+1)\) matrices.

Homework#

1. Verify commutation relations. Show that the commutation relations \([J_x, J_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_z\), \([J_y, J_z] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_x\), and \([J_z, J_x] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_y\) are cyclic. What does this symmetry tell you about rotations in three dimensions?

2. Casimir operator commutation. Verify that \([J^2, J_z] = 0\) by expanding \(J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2\) and using the fundamental commutation relation \([J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\). Why is this result important for finding simultaneous eigenstates?

3. Ladder operator algebra. Using the definitions \(J_+ = J_x + \mathrm{i} J_y\) and \(J_- = J_x - \mathrm{i} J_y\), derive the relation \([J_+, J_-] = 2\hbar J_z\). (Hint: expand the commutator and use \([J_x, J_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar J_z\).)

4. Action of raising operator. Consider the state \(|1, 1\rangle\) (spin-1). Use the formula \(J_+ |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)} |j, m+1\rangle\) to explain why \(J_+ |1, 1\rangle = 0\). What is the physical meaning of this result?

5. Spin-1/2 matrix representations. For a spin-1/2 system, verify that the Pauli matrices satisfy the angular momentum commutation relations. Specifically, show that \([\sigma^x, \sigma^y] = 2\mathrm{i}\sigma^z\) and interpret this result.

6. Normalization of ladder operator states. Given \(J_- |j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j+m)(j-m+1)} |j, m-1\rangle\), find the coefficient when \(J_-\) acts on \(|1/2, 1/2\rangle\) (the spin-up state for spin-1/2). Verify your result using the explicit matrix representation from the lecture.

7. Expectation values in angular momentum eigenstates. In a state \(|j, m\rangle\), compute \(\langle J_x \rangle\) and \(\langle J_y \rangle\) using the ladder operators. Explain why these expectation values are zero despite \(J_x\) and \(J_y\) being non-zero operators. (Hint: \(J_x = \frac{1}{2}(J_+ + J_-)\) and use \(\langle j, m | J_+ | j, m \rangle\).)

8. Integer vs. half-integer angular momentum. Explain why orbital angular momentum \(L\) (which arises from \(L_z = -\mathrm{i}\hbar \partial_\phi\)) can only have integer quantum numbers \(\ell = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\), while spin \(S\) can be half-integer. What is the role of single-valuedness of the wavefunction in restricting \(\ell\)? How does this connect to the spin-statistics theorem mentioned in the lecture?

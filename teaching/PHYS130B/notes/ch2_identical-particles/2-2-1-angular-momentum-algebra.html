2.2.1 Angular Momentum Algebra#

Prompts

  • What are the fundamental commutation relations for angular momentum, and how do they encode the geometry of 3D rotations?

  • Define the Casimir operator \(\hat{J}^2\) and explain why it commutes with all components of \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}}\). Why does this allow simultaneous eigenstates \(\vert j, m\rangle\)?

  • How do the ladder operators \(\hat{J}_\pm\) connect eigenstates with different \(m\)? Derive their normalization coefficients.

  • Show that angular momentum quantization (\(j = 0, \frac{1}{2}, 1, \ldots\)) follows purely from the algebra, without solving a differential equation.

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

In Chapter 1, we studied a single qubit — the spin-1/2 system with two eigenstates. This raises a natural question: what about higher spin? What constrains the possible values of angular momentum, and how do they arise?

The answer is remarkable: angular momentum quantization follows entirely from commutation relations, without solving any differential equation. All forms of angular momentum — orbital (\(\boldsymbol{L} = \boldsymbol{r} \times \boldsymbol{p}\)), spin (\(\boldsymbol{S}\)), and total (\(\boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{L} + \boldsymbol{S}\)) — satisfy the same algebra. This algebra is the Lie algebra of SU(2), the group of rotations we met in §1.3.3. The physical content is simple: rotations in 3D do not commute, and angular momentum operators are their generators.

Angular Momentum Commutation Relations#

Defining Relation

The three components of any angular momentum operator \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = (\hat{J}_x, \hat{J}_y, \hat{J}_z)\) satisfy:

(34)#\[ [\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} \hat{J}_k \]

Explicitly:

\[ [\hat{J}_x, \hat{J}_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \hat{J}_z, \quad [\hat{J}_y, \hat{J}_z] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \hat{J}_x, \quad [\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_x] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \hat{J}_y \]

These are cyclic in \((x, y, z)\). The Levi-Civita symbol \(\epsilon_{ijk}\) is \(+1\) for even permutations of \((1,2,3)\), \(-1\) for odd, and \(0\) if any index repeats.

For orbital angular momentum, these follow from the canonical commutation relation \([\hat{r}_i, \hat{p}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \delta_{ij}\). For spin, they are postulated — the requirement is that angular momentum generates rotations, so it must satisfy the Lie algebra of SO(3).

Derivation: Orbital Angular Momentum Commutators

Starting from \(\hat{L}_i = \epsilon_{imn} \hat{r}_m \hat{p}_n\) and the canonical commutation relation \([\hat{r}_m, \hat{p}_q] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \delta_{mq}\), we compute \([\hat{L}_x, \hat{L}_y]\) explicitly. With \(\hat{L}_x = \hat{y}\hat{p}_z - \hat{z}\hat{p}_y\) and \(\hat{L}_y = \hat{z}\hat{p}_x - \hat{x}\hat{p}_z\):

\[ [\hat{L}_x, \hat{L}_y] = [\hat{y}\hat{p}_z - \hat{z}\hat{p}_y,\; \hat{z}\hat{p}_x - \hat{x}\hat{p}_z] \]

Expanding the four commutator terms using \([\hat{A}\hat{B}, \hat{C}\hat{D}] = \hat{A}[\hat{B}, \hat{C}]\hat{D} + \hat{C}[\hat{A}, \hat{D}]\hat{B} + \ldots\) and noting that operators acting on different coordinates commute:

\[ = [\hat{y}\hat{p}_z, \hat{z}\hat{p}_x] - [\hat{y}\hat{p}_z, \hat{x}\hat{p}_z] - [\hat{z}\hat{p}_y, \hat{z}\hat{p}_x] + [\hat{z}\hat{p}_y, \hat{x}\hat{p}_z] \]

The middle two terms vanish (no conjugate pairs share a coordinate). The surviving terms give:

\[ = \hat{y}[\hat{p}_z, \hat{z}]\hat{p}_x + \hat{x}[\hat{z}, \hat{p}_z]\hat{p}_y = \hat{y}(-\mathrm{i}\hbar)\hat{p}_x + \hat{x}(\mathrm{i}\hbar)\hat{p}_y = \mathrm{i}\hbar(\hat{x}\hat{p}_y - \hat{y}\hat{p}_x) = \mathrm{i}\hbar \hat{L}_z \]

The other two relations follow by cyclic permutation \((x \to y \to z \to x)\). \(\checkmark\)

General form. In index notation with \(\hat{L}_i = \epsilon_{imn}\hat{r}_m\hat{p}_n\), the product of Levi-Civita symbols satisfies the contraction identity \(\epsilon_{imn}\epsilon_{jpq} = \delta_{ij}(\delta_{mp}\delta_{nq} - \delta_{mq}\delta_{np}) + \text{cyclic}\), which reduces the general commutator to \([\hat{L}_i, \hat{L}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{L}_k\).

Connection to Rotations

The commutation relations encode the geometry of 3D rotations: rotating first about \(x\) then about \(y\) differs from \(y\) then \(x\). Mathematically, \([\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} \hat{J}_k\) is the Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\) — the same algebra that appeared in §1.3.3 as the algebra of SU(2) generators. The angular momentum operators \(\hat{J}_i\) generate rotations via \(\hat{U}(\boldsymbol{n}, \theta) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\theta \boldsymbol{n} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{J}}/\hbar}\).

Casimir Operator \(\hat{J}^2\)#

Casimir Operator

The total angular momentum squared:

(35)#\[ \hat{J}^2 = \hat{J}_x^2 + \hat{J}_y^2 + \hat{J}_z^2 \]

commutes with every component:

(36)#\[ [\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_i] = 0 \quad \text{for } i = x, y, z \]

Because \([\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_z] = 0\), we can find simultaneous eigenstates of both operators, labeled \(\vert j, m\rangle\).

Derivation: \([\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_z] = 0\)

\[ [\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_z] = [\hat{J}_x^2, \hat{J}_z] + [\hat{J}_y^2, \hat{J}_z] \]

Using \([\hat{A}^2, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] + [\hat{A}, \hat{B}]\hat{A}\):

\[ [\hat{J}_x^2, \hat{J}_z] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar(\hat{J}_x \hat{J}_y + \hat{J}_y \hat{J}_x), \quad [\hat{J}_y^2, \hat{J}_z] = +\mathrm{i}\hbar(\hat{J}_y \hat{J}_x + \hat{J}_x \hat{J}_y) \]

These cancel exactly. \(\checkmark\)

Ladder Operators#

Ladder Operators

The raising and lowering operators:

(37)#\[ \hat{J}_+ = \hat{J}_x + \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y, \quad \hat{J}_- = \hat{J}_x - \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y \]

satisfy:

\[ [\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_\pm] = \pm\hbar \hat{J}_\pm, \quad [\hat{J}_+, \hat{J}_-] = 2\hbar \hat{J}_z, \quad [\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_\pm] = 0 \]

Derivation: Ladder Operator Commutation Relations

\([\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_\pm] = \pm\hbar \hat{J}_\pm\): Using \(\hat{J}_+ = \hat{J}_x + \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y\):

\[ [\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_+] = [\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_x] + \mathrm{i}[\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_y] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\hat{J}_y + \mathrm{i}(-\mathrm{i}\hbar\hat{J}_x) = \hbar(\hat{J}_x + \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y) = +\hbar\hat{J}_+ \]

Similarly \([\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_-] = -\hbar\hat{J}_-\). This means \(\hat{J}_\pm\) shifts the \(\hat{J}_z\) eigenvalue by \(\pm\hbar\).

\([\hat{J}_+, \hat{J}_-] = 2\hbar\hat{J}_z\):

\[ [\hat{J}_+, \hat{J}_-] = [\hat{J}_x + \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y,\, \hat{J}_x - \mathrm{i}\hat{J}_y] = -\mathrm{i}[\hat{J}_x, \hat{J}_y] + \mathrm{i}[\hat{J}_y, \hat{J}_x] = -2\mathrm{i}[\hat{J}_x, \hat{J}_y] = -2\mathrm{i}(\mathrm{i}\hbar\hat{J}_z) = 2\hbar\hat{J}_z \]

\([\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_\pm] = 0\): Follows from \([\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_x] = [\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_y] = 0\), since \(\hat{J}_\pm\) is a linear combination of \(\hat{J}_x\) and \(\hat{J}_y\). \(\checkmark\)

The key property: \(\hat{J}_+\) raises \(m\) by one, \(\hat{J}_-\) lowers it by one.

Ladder Operator Action

(38)#\[ \hat{J}_+ \vert j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j-m)(j+m+1)}\;\vert j, m+1\rangle \]
(39)#\[ \hat{J}_- \vert j, m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{(j+m)(j-m+1)}\;\vert j, m-1\rangle \]

The coefficient vanishes at the boundaries: \(\hat{J}_+ \vert j, j\rangle = 0\) and \(\hat{J}_- \vert j, -j\rangle = 0\).

Derivation: Normalization Coefficients

Let \(\vert\psi\rangle = \hat{J}_+ \vert j, m\rangle\). From \([\hat{J}_z, \hat{J}_+] = \hbar \hat{J}_+\):

\[ \hat{J}_z \vert\psi\rangle = \hbar(m+1)\vert\psi\rangle \]

From \([\hat{J}^2, \hat{J}_+] = 0\): \(\hat{J}^2 \vert\psi\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1)\vert\psi\rangle\). So \(\vert\psi\rangle \propto \vert j, m+1\rangle\).

For the norm, first derive the identity \(\hat{J}_- \hat{J}_+ = \hat{J}^2 - \hat{J}_z^2 - \hbar\hat{J}_z\). Writing \(\hat{J}_x = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}_+ + \hat{J}_-)\) and \(\hat{J}_y = \frac{1}{2\mathrm{i}}(\hat{J}_+ - \hat{J}_-)\):

\[ \hat{J}_x^2 + \hat{J}_y^2 = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}_+\hat{J}_- + \hat{J}_-\hat{J}_+) = \hat{J}^2 - \hat{J}_z^2 \]

Using \([\hat{J}_+, \hat{J}_-] = 2\hbar\hat{J}_z\) gives \(\hat{J}_+\hat{J}_- = \hat{J}_-\hat{J}_+ + 2\hbar\hat{J}_z\), so:

\[ \hat{J}_-\hat{J}_+ = \hat{J}^2 - \hat{J}_z^2 - \hbar\hat{J}_z, \qquad \hat{J}_+\hat{J}_- = \hat{J}^2 - \hat{J}_z^2 + \hbar\hat{J}_z \]

Then:

\[ \|\hat{J}_+ \vert j, m\rangle\|^2 = \langle j,m\vert \hat{J}_- \hat{J}_+ \vert j,m\rangle = \hbar^2[j(j+1) - m(m+1)] = \hbar^2(j-m)(j+m+1) \]

Taking the square root gives the coefficient. \(\checkmark\)

Angular Momentum Quantization#

The quantization of \(j\) and \(m\) follows purely from the algebra — no differential equation needed.

Quantization Rules

(40)#\[ j \in \{0,\; \tfrac{1}{2},\; 1,\; \tfrac{3}{2},\; 2,\; \ldots\}, \qquad m \in \{-j,\; -j+1,\; \ldots,\; j-1,\; j\} \]

For each \(j\), there are \(2j+1\) states. The eigenvalues are:

\[ \hat{J}^2 \vert j,m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1)\vert j,m\rangle, \qquad \hat{J}_z \vert j,m\rangle = \hbar m \vert j,m\rangle \]

Derivation: Why \(j\) Is Quantized

For fixed \(j\), the ladder operators generate a chain of states with \(m\) values differing by integers. Since \(\langle \hat{J}_x^2 + \hat{J}_y^2\rangle \geq 0\) implies \(m^2 \leq j(j+1)\), the chain must terminate: there exist \(m_{\max}\) and \(m_{\min}\) with \(\hat{J}_+ \vert j, m_{\max}\rangle = 0\) and \(\hat{J}_- \vert j, m_{\min}\rangle = 0\).

From the norm formula: \(m_{\max} = j\) and \(m_{\min} = -j\). Since \(m\) changes in integer steps, we need \(m_{\max} - m_{\min} = 2j \in \mathbb{Z}\), so \(j\) is a non-negative integer or half-integer. \(\checkmark\)

Integer vs. half-integer: Orbital angular momentum (\(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{r}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{p}}\)) only admits integer \(\ell\) because the wavefunctions \(Y_\ell^m(\theta,\phi)\) must be single-valued on the sphere. Spin has no spatial wavefunction, so half-integer \(j\) is allowed. The spin-statistics theorem (proved in QFT) links this to particle statistics: integer spin \(\to\) bosons, half-integer spin \(\to\) fermions.

Discussion

Angular momentum quantization follows from algebra alone — no differential equation, no specific Hamiltonian. Does this mean any operator satisfying \([\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{J}_k\) must have quantized eigenvalues? Could there be physical systems where the algebra is deformed (e.g., \(q\)-deformed angular momentum)? What would change?

Matrix Representations#

For a given \(j\), the operators act on the \((2j+1)\)-dimensional space spanned by \(\{\vert j, m\rangle : m = -j, \ldots, j\}\).

Spin-1/2: Pauli Matrices

For \(j = 1/2\), the two basis states are \(\vert\uparrow\rangle = \vert\tfrac{1}{2}, +\tfrac{1}{2}\rangle\) and \(\vert\downarrow\rangle = \vert\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}\rangle\). The angular momentum operators are \(\hat{J}_i = \frac{\hbar}{2}\hat{\sigma}^i\):

\[\begin{split} \hat{J}_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{J}_x = \frac{\hbar}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{J}_y = \frac{\hbar}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ \mathrm{i} & 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

These are the Pauli matrices from §1.1.3, now understood as the \(j = 1/2\) representation of angular momentum.

Spin-1: 3×3 Matrices

For \(j = 1\), three basis states \(\vert 1, +1\rangle\), \(\vert 1, 0\rangle\), \(\vert 1, -1\rangle\):

\[\begin{split} \hat{J}_z = \hbar\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{J}_x = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \hat{J}_y = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\mathrm{i} & 0 \\ \mathrm{i} & 0 & -\mathrm{i} \\ 0 & \mathrm{i} & 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

The factors of \(\sqrt{2}\) come from the ladder operator formula with \(j = 1\).

General pattern: For spin-\(j\), the operators are \((2j+1) \times (2j+1)\) matrices. Each representation is irreducible — it cannot be block-diagonalized while preserving the algebra.

Summary#

  • All angular momenta satisfy the same commutation relations \([\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{J}_k\) — the Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\).

  • The Casimir operator \(\hat{J}^2\) commutes with all components, enabling simultaneous eigenstates \(\vert j, m\rangle\).

  • Quantization (\(j = 0, \frac{1}{2}, 1, \ldots\); \(m = -j, \ldots, j\)) follows from algebra alone.

  • Ladder operators \(\hat{J}_\pm\) shift \(m\) by \(\pm 1\) with known normalization.

  • Representations: spin-1/2 recovers Pauli matrices; spin-1 gives \(3\times 3\) matrices; general \(j\) gives \((2j+1)\)-dimensional irreducible representations.

2.2.3 Addition of Angular Momenta#

Prompts

  • When combining two angular momenta \(j_1\) and \(j_2\), what are the selection rules for allowed total angular momentum values?

  • Explain the difference between the uncoupled basis \(|j_1, m_1\rangle |j_2, m_2\rangle\) and the coupled basis \(|J, M\rangle\). When would you use each one?

  • What are Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, and how do they serve as a change-of-basis transformation between these two representations?

  • For two spin-1/2 particles, explicitly write the singlet state and all three triplet states in terms of the uncoupled basis.

  • Explain how spin-orbit coupling causes orbital and spin angular momentum to preferentially align or anti-align. Why isn’t \(L_z\) a good quantum number in this regime?

  • In the hydrogen atom, the energy levels depend on \(J\) but not separately on \(L\) and \(S\). Does this mean orbital and spin angular momentum have “merged” into a single entity, or are they still distinct? How would you design an experiment to tell?

Lecture Notes#

When multiple angular momenta combine—orbital and spin, or spin and spin—their coupling rules determine the allowed energy levels and symmetries of atoms and molecules. This section reveals how the composition of angular momenta generates the rich spectroscopy of the atom and the basis for chemical bonding.

Total Angular Momentum#

When two particles (or subsystems) each carry angular momentum, the total angular momentum is their vector sum:

(114)#\[ \boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{J}_1 + \boldsymbol{J}_2\]

The components add:

\[ J_x = J_{1x} + J_{2x}, \quad J_y = J_{1y} + J_{2y}, \quad J_z = J_{1z} + J_{2z}\]

Both \(\boldsymbol{J}_1\) and \(\boldsymbol{J}_2\) satisfy angular momentum commutation relations, so their sum automatically satisfies:

\[ [J_i, J_j] = \mathrm{i}\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\]

The Tensor Product Hilbert Space#

If particle 1 has angular momentum quantum numbers \(j_1, m_1\) and particle 2 has \(j_2, m_2\), the combined system lives in a Hilbert space that is a tensor product:

\[ \mathcal{H}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{H}_{j_1} \otimes \mathcal{H}_{j_2}\]

This space has dimension \((2j_1 + 1)(2j_2 + 1)\).

Coupled and Uncoupled Bases#

The Two Perspectives#

The uncoupled basis \(|j_1, m_1\rangle |j_2, m_2\rangle\) is an eigenbasis of \(\hat{J}_{1z}\), \(\hat{J}_{2z}\) (and the squares \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}}_1^2\), \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}}_2^2\)). It has \((2j_1 + 1)(2j_2 + 1)\) states.

The coupled basis \(|J, M\rangle\) is an eigenbasis of \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}}^2_\text{tot}\) and \(\hat{J}_{z,\text{tot}}\). It accounts for the fact that the total angular momentum is a single quantum object.

These bases span the same Hilbert space but provide different perspectives:

  • Uncoupled: we know the individual angular momenta \(j_1, j_2\) and their \(z\)-components \(m_1, m_2\).

  • Coupled: we know the magnitude and \(z\)-component of the total angular momentum \(J, M\).

Triangle Rule

When adding angular momenta \(j_1\) and \(j_2\), the total \(J\) ranges from \(|j_1 - j_2|\) to \(j_1 + j_2\) in integer steps.

\[ |j_1 - j_2| \le J \le j_1 + j_2\]

For each allowed \(J\), the magnetic quantum number ranges as \(-J \le M \le J\).

Physical interpretation: The total angular momentum must satisfy a triangle inequality—the same rule that governs vectors in classical mechanics.

Derivation: Triangle Rule

The triangle rule \(j \in \{|j_1 - j_2|, |j_1 - j_2| + 1, \ldots, j_1 + j_2\}\) follows from the requirement that quantum numbers must be integers or half-integers.

Key constraint: For a given value of \(j\), the magnetic quantum number must satisfy:

\[ m \in \{-j, -j+1, \ldots, j-1, j\}\]

In the uncoupled basis, the total \(z\)-component is:

\[ m_{\text{total}} = m_1 + m_2\]

Maximum value of \(m_{\text{total}}\):

\[ m_{\text{max}} = j_1 + j_2\]

For a coupled state \(|j_1, j_2; j, m\rangle\), the maximum value of \(m\) is \(j\) itself. Therefore:

\[ j_{\text{max}} = j_1 + j_2\]

Dimension matching:

  • Uncoupled basis: \((2j_1 + 1)(2j_2 + 1)\) states

  • Coupled basis: \(\sum_{j} (2j + 1)\) where \(j\) ranges over allowed values

These must be equal. With the triangle rule, we get exactly the right count:

\[ \sum_{j=|j_1-j_2|}^{j_1+j_2} (2j + 1) = (2j_1 + 1)(2j_2 + 1) \quad ✓\]

Thus the minimum is \(j_{\text{min}} = |j_1 - j_2|\), giving the full range.

Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients: Change of Basis#

Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients

The Clebsch-Gordan coefficients \(\langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle\) are the overlaps that express the coupled basis in terms of the uncoupled basis:

\[ |J, M\rangle = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle |j_1, m_1\rangle |j_2, m_2\rangle\]

Equivalently:

\[ |j_1, m_1\rangle |j_2, m_2\rangle = \sum_{J, M} \langle J, M | j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 \rangle |J, M\rangle\]

This is a unitary transformation between the two bases. Clebsch-Gordan coefficients are nonzero only when:

  • \(M = m_1 + m_2\) (conservation of \(m\))

  • \(|j_1 - j_2| \leq J \leq j_1 + j_2\) (triangle rule)

  • The coefficients are real numbers (not complex)

Properties and Selection Rules#

  1. Orthonormality:

    \[ \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J', M' \rangle = \delta_{JJ'} \delta_{MM'}\]

  2. Selection rules: CG coefficients vanish unless \(M = m_1 + m_2\) and triangle rule is satisfied.

  3. Computational access: CG coefficients are typically:

    • Tabulated in physics references

    • Computed by algebra systems (Mathematica, SymPy)

    • Evaluated online via databases (NIST, etc.)

For simple cases, they can be derived algebraically.

Derivation: Clebsch-Gordan Orthonormality

Both \(|j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\) and \(|J, M\rangle\) are orthonormal bases. Expanding using CG coefficients:

\[ |J, M\rangle = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle |j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\]

Take the inner product of two such states:

\[ \langle J', M' | J, M \rangle = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J', M' \rangle \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle\]

Since both sides must equal \(\delta_{JJ'} \delta_{MM'}\):

\[ \delta_{JJ'} \delta_{MM'} = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J', M' \rangle \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | J, M \rangle\]

This is the orthonormality relation for CG coefficients.

Example: Two Spin-1/2 Particles#

Triangle Rule Applied#

For \(j_1 = 1/2\) and \(j_2 = 1/2\):

\[ J \in \{|1/2 - 1/2|, \ldots, 1/2 + 1/2\} = \{0, 1\}\]

So we can have:

  • Singlet (\(J=0\)): 1 state with \(M = 0\)

  • Triplet (\(J=1\)): 3 states with \(M = -1, 0, +1\)

Total: \(1 + 3 = 4\) states, which matches \((2 \cdot 1/2 + 1)(2 \cdot 1/2 + 1) = 2 \times 2 = 4\). ✓

The Four Uncoupled Basis States#

\[ |\uparrow\uparrow\rangle = |+\rangle_1 |+\rangle_2, \quad |\uparrow\downarrow\rangle = |+\rangle_1 |-\rangle_2, \quad |\downarrow\uparrow\rangle = |-\rangle_1 |+\rangle_2, \quad |\downarrow\downarrow\rangle = |-\rangle_1 |-\rangle_2\]

Triplet States (\(J = 1\))

The triplet (\(J = 1\)) consists of three symmetric combinations:

\[ |1, 1\rangle = |\uparrow\uparrow\rangle\]
\[|1, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle + |\downarrow\uparrow\rangle)\]
(115)#\[|1, -1\rangle = |\downarrow\downarrow\rangle\]

Each triplet state is symmetric under particle exchange: \(P_{12} |1, M\rangle = |1, M\rangle\).

Singlet State (\(J = 0\))

The singlet (\(J = 0\)) is the antisymmetric combination:

(116)#\[ |0, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - |\downarrow\uparrow\rangle)\]

The singlet state is antisymmetric under particle exchange: \(P_{12} |0, 0\rangle = -|0, 0\rangle\).

Physical Implications: Singlet and Triplet States#

Spin Correlations in the Singlet#

The singlet state has a remarkable property:

If we measure the spin of particle 1 along \(z\) and find \(\uparrow\), then particle 2 is guaranteed to be \(\downarrow\) (and vice versa).

This is maximal entanglement: the two spins are perfectly anticorrelated.

Bell’s Inequality and Non-locality#

The singlet state can be used to test Bell’s inequality, demonstrating that quantum mechanics exhibits stronger correlations than any classical hidden-variable theory allows. This is a fundamental test of the non-classical nature of quantum mechanics.

In Atomic and Molecular Physics#

Helium atom ground state: Two electrons in the \(1s\) orbital must form a spin singlet (to satisfy the Pauli exclusion principle, as the spatial part is symmetric).

\[ \Psi_{He, \text{ground}} = \phi_{1s}(\boldsymbol{r}_1) \phi_{1s}(\boldsymbol{r}_2) \times |0, 0\rangle\]

The two electron spins are paired (antiparallel).

Hydrogen molecule \(H_2\): Two electrons can pair in a singlet state, lowering the energy and stabilizing the bond.

Discussion

Two spin-1/2 particles are prepared in the uncoupled state \(|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle = |+, -\rangle_{12}\) (particle 1 up, particle 2 down). This is a superposition of \(J=0\) (singlet) and \(J=1\) (triplet) in the coupled basis.

An experiment measures \(S_z^{(1)}\) (spin of particle 1 along \(z\)) and gets \(+\hbar/2\). Before this measurement, could we have determined whether the total spin was \(J=0\) or \(J=1\)? After the measurement, can we?

What does the answer tell us about whether total angular momentum “belongs to” the system independent of which observables we measure?

Spin-Orbit Coupling#

The Interaction Hamiltonian#

An electron moving in an electromagnetic field experiences a spin-orbit interaction proportional to:

Spin-Orbit Coupling

(117)#\[ H_{SO} = \lambda \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{L}\) is orbital angular momentum, \(\boldsymbol{S}\) is spin, and \(\lambda\) is a coupling constant that depends on the electric field gradient.

Why the Coupled Basis is Essential#

The spin-orbit interaction \(\boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\) is diagonal in the coupled basis \(|\ell, s; j, m_j\rangle\) but not in the uncoupled basis \(|\ell, m_\ell; s, m_s\rangle\).

Using coupled basis states simplifies calculations enormously because:

  • In the uncoupled basis, the interaction mixes many states.

  • In the coupled basis, each state evolves independently.

Fine Structure: An Example#

For an electron in an atom with orbital angular momentum \(\ell\):

\[ j \in \{\ell - 1/2, \ell + 1/2\} \quad \text{(for } \ell \geq 1\text{)}\]

The energy splitting between \(j = \ell + 1/2\) and \(j = \ell - 1/2\) is the fine structure—a small but measurable correction to atomic energies.

Example: Hydrogen \(2p\) level splits into \(2p_{3/2}\) and \(2p_{1/2}\).

Summary#

  • Total angular momentum: \(\boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{J}_1 + \boldsymbol{J}_2\); satisfies the same commutation relations.

  • Uncoupled basis: \(|j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\) diagonalizes \(J_{1z}, J_{2z}\) but not \(J^2\).

  • Coupled basis: \(|j_1, j_2; j, M\rangle\) diagonalizes \(J^2, J_z\).

  • Triangle rule: \(j \in \{|j_1 - j_2|, \ldots, j_1 + j_2\}\); determines allowed total angular momenta.

  • Clebsch-Gordan coefficients: Real unitary transformation between bases; provide the change of basis.

  • Two spin-1/2 particles: Singlet (\(j=0\), antisymmetric) and triplet (\(j=1\), symmetric) states with explicit forms.

  • Spin-orbit coupling \(\boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\) is naturally diagonal in coupled basis; causes fine structure in atoms.

Consider two particles with angular momenta \(j_1 = 1\) and \(j_2 = 3/2\). Use the triangle rule to determine all allowed values of the total angular momentum quantum number \(j\). For each allowed \(j\), specify the number of possible magnetic quantum numbers \(m_j\).

2. Dimension Counting (Conceptual Check)

Show that the total number of states in the uncoupled basis equals the total number of states in the coupled basis for the \(j_1 = 1\) and \(j_2 = 3/2\) system from Problem 1. That is, verify:

\[ (2j_1 + 1)(2j_2 + 1) = \sum_{j} (2j + 1)\]

where the sum runs over all allowed \(j\) values from the triangle rule.

3. Two Spin-1/2: Explicit CG Coefficients (Derivation)

For two spin-1/2 particles, derive the Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for the transformation from uncoupled to coupled basis by showing that:

\[ |j_1, j_2; j, m\rangle = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 | j, m \rangle |j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\]

Explicitly compute the coefficient \(\langle 1/2, +1/2; 1/2, -1/2 | 1, 0 \rangle\) by normalizing the state \((|\uparrow\downarrow\rangle + |\downarrow\uparrow\rangle) / \sqrt{2}\).

4. Singlet and Triplet Construction (Physical Reasoning)

For two identical fermions (e.g., two electrons), the total wavefunction must be antisymmetric under particle exchange. If the spatial part \(\psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1, \boldsymbol{r}_2)\) is symmetric, explain why the spin part must be antisymmetric. Which of the singlet or triplet states satisfies this requirement? Sketch how this constraint affects possible ground states.

5. Spin-Orbit Interaction and Energy Shifts (Show That)

The spin-orbit interaction is \(H_{SO} = \lambda \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\). Show that in the coupled basis \(|\ell, 1/2; j, m_j\rangle\), we can write:

\[ \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2} \left[ j(j+1) - \ell(\ell+1) - \frac{3}{4} \right]\]

For a hydrogen atom \(2p\) electron (\(\ell = 1\)), compute the energy shift for both \(j = 3/2\) and \(j = 1/2\) states and determine which is lower in energy.

6. CG Coefficient Selection Rules (Derivation)

Using the fact that \(J_z |j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle = \hbar(m_1 + m_2) |j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\) and \(J_z |j_1, j_2; j, m\rangle = \hbar m |j_1, j_2; j, m\rangle\), prove that CG coefficients vanish unless \(m = m_1 + m_2\).

7. Triplet State Under Particle Exchange (Physical Application)

Show that each of the three triplet states \(|1, 1\rangle\), \(|1, 0\rangle\), and \(|1, -1\rangle\) (from Section 6) is symmetric under the exchange operator \(P_{12}\): \(P_{12} |1, m\rangle = |1, m\rangle\). Explain how this relates to the Pauli exclusion principle for identical fermions.

8. Fine Structure in Alkali Atoms (Application)

An electron in a \(p\)-orbital (\(\ell = 1\)) experiences spin-orbit coupling with constant \(\lambda\). The spin-orbit energy is \(\Delta E = \lambda \boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\). Using the result from Problem 5, compute the fine-structure splitting \(\Delta E_{3/2} - \Delta E_{1/2}\) and explain why the \(j = 3/2\) state is higher in energy (for a non-filled subshell). [Hint: Use \(\hbar = 1\) in natural units.]

9. Inverse Transformation (CG Algebra)

Write the uncoupled basis state \(|1/2, +1/2; 1/2, -1/2\rangle\) (first spin-up, second spin-down) as a linear combination of coupled basis states \(|1/2, 1/2; j, m\rangle\). Use the orthogonality and completeness of CG coefficients. [Hint: Invert the transformation from Problem 3.]

10. Coupled vs. Uncoupled for a Real Problem (Conceptual)

Suppose you are computing matrix elements of the operator \(\boldsymbol{L} \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\) between states of a hydrogen atom. Would you find it easier to work in the uncoupled basis \(|n, \ell, m_\ell, m_s\rangle\) or the coupled basis \(|n, \ell, 1/2; j, m_j\rangle\)?

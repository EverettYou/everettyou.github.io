2.2.3 Addition of Angular Momenta#

Prompts

  • Explain the difference between uncoupled and coupled bases. When is each one useful?

  • State the triangle rule for adding \(j_1\) and \(j_2\). Verify the dimension count for two spin-1/2 particles.

  • What are Clebsch-Gordan coefficients? Derive the singlet and triplet states for two spin-1/2 particles.

  • How does spin-orbit coupling \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) lead to fine structure? Why is the coupled basis essential?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

When two systems each carry angular momentum — whether orbital and spin, or spin and spin — the total angular momentum \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{J}}_1 + \hat{\boldsymbol{J}}_2\) obeys addition rules with no classical analog. The combined Hilbert space admits two natural bases: the uncoupled basis \(\vert j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2\rangle\) (individual projections known) and the coupled basis \(\vert J, M\rangle\) (total angular momentum known). The Clebsch-Gordan coefficients provide the unitary change of basis, and the triangle rule determines which total angular momenta are allowed.

Two Bases for the Same Space#

The combined system lives in \(\mathcal{H}_{j_1} \otimes \mathcal{H}_{j_2}\), with dimension \((2j_1+1)(2j_2+1)\).

Uncoupled basis \(\vert j_1, m_1\rangle\vert j_2, m_2\rangle\): eigenstates of \(\hat{J}_{1z}\) and \(\hat{J}_{2z}\) separately. Good quantum numbers: \(m_1, m_2\).

Coupled basis \(\vert J, M\rangle\): eigenstates of \(\hat{J}^2_{\text{tot}}\) and \(\hat{J}_{z,\text{tot}}\). Good quantum numbers: \(J, M\).

Both span the same space and are related by a unitary transformation.

Triangle Rule

When adding angular momenta \(j_1\) and \(j_2\), the allowed total angular momentum is:

(43)#\[ J \in \{\vert j_1 - j_2\vert,\; \vert j_1 - j_2\vert + 1,\; \ldots,\; j_1 + j_2\} \]

Dimension check: \(\displaystyle\sum_{J=\vert j_1-j_2\vert}^{j_1+j_2}(2J+1) = (2j_1+1)(2j_2+1)\).

Derivation: Triangle Rule

Step 1: Maximum \(J\). The highest possible \(M = m_1 + m_2\) is \(j_1 + j_2\) (when both projections are maximal). Only one uncoupled state achieves this: \(\vert j_1, j_1\rangle\vert j_2, j_2\rangle\). This must be \(\vert J_{\max}, J_{\max}\rangle\) with \(J_{\max} = j_1 + j_2\).

Step 2: Counting by subtraction. At \(M = j_1 + j_2 - 1\), there are two uncoupled states (one from lowering each \(m\)), but one belongs to the \(J = j_1 + j_2\) multiplet. The remaining state starts a new multiplet with \(J = j_1 + j_2 - 1\). Continuing: at each step, the number of uncoupled states with a given \(M\) exceeds those already assigned, until we reach \(M = \vert j_1 - j_2\vert\), after which no new multiplets appear.

Step 3: Minimum \(J\). The process terminates at \(J_{\min} = \vert j_1 - j_2\vert\), where the state count is exactly exhausted.

Step 4: Dimension check. The total number of states is:

\[ \sum_{J=\vert j_1 - j_2\vert}^{j_1+j_2}(2J+1) \]

Setting \(k = J - \vert j_1-j_2\vert\) and summing the arithmetic series gives \((2j_1+1)(2j_2+1)\), matching the dimension of \(\mathcal{H}_{j_1}\otimes\mathcal{H}_{j_2}\). \(\checkmark\)

Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients#

Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients

The CG coefficients \(\langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 \vert J, M\rangle\) express the coupled basis in terms of the uncoupled basis:

(44)#\[ \vert J, M\rangle = \sum_{m_1, m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 \vert J, M\rangle\;\vert j_1, m_1\rangle\vert j_2, m_2\rangle \]

Properties:

  • Selection rule: nonzero only if \(M = m_1 + m_2\) and the triangle rule is satisfied

  • Reality: all CG coefficients are real

  • Orthonormality: \(\sum_{m_1,m_2} \langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 \vert J, M\rangle\langle j_1, m_1; j_2, m_2 \vert J', M'\rangle = \delta_{JJ'}\delta_{MM'}\)

In practice, CG coefficients are looked up in tables or computed by algebra systems. For simple cases they can be derived by applying ladder operators starting from the stretched state \(\vert J_{\max}, J_{\max}\rangle = \vert j_1, j_1\rangle\vert j_2, j_2\rangle\).

Derivation: Recursion Relations for Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients

The CG coefficients can be computed systematically via recursion. The idea: apply \(\hat{J}_-\) to the expansion of \(\vert J,M\rangle\) and project onto uncoupled states.

Notation. Write \(C(m_1, m_2\,|\,M) \equiv \langle j_1,m_1;j_2,m_2\vert J,M\rangle\) (suppressing fixed \(j_1, j_2, J\)).

Step 1: Apply the total lowering operator.

Start from \(\vert J, M\rangle = \sum_{m_1+m_2=M} C(m_1,m_2\,|\,M)\,\vert j_1,m_1;j_2,m_2\rangle\) and apply \(\hat{J}_- = \hat{J}_{1-} + \hat{J}_{2-}\):

\[ \hat{J}_-\vert J,M\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{J(J+1)-M(M-1)}\,\vert J,M-1\rangle \]

On the right side, \(\hat{J}_{1-}\) acts on particle 1 and \(\hat{J}_{2-}\) on particle 2:

\[ (\hat{J}_{1-}+\hat{J}_{2-})\vert j_1,m_1;j_2,m_2\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{j_1(j_1+1)-m_1(m_1-1)}\,\vert j_1,m_1{-}1;j_2,m_2\rangle + \hbar\sqrt{j_2(j_2+1)-m_2(m_2-1)}\,\vert j_1,m_1;j_2,m_2{-}1\rangle \]

Step 2: Project onto \(\langle j_1,m_1';j_2,m_2'\vert\) with \(m_1'+m_2' = M-1\).

Equating the \(\vert j_1,m_1';j_2,m_2'\rangle\) component on both sides:

\[ \boxed{\sqrt{J(J+1)-M(M-1)}\;C(m_1',m_2'\,|\,M{-}1) = \sqrt{j_1(j_1+1)-m_1'(m_1'{+}1)}\;C(m_1'{+}1,m_2'\,|\,M) + \sqrt{j_2(j_2+1)-m_2'(m_2'{+}1)}\;C(m_1',m_2'{+}1\,|\,M)} \]

This is the lowering recursion relation — given the column \(M\), it determines column \(M-1\).

Step 3: Starting condition.

For each \(J\), the unique state with \(M = J\) and \(m_1 = j_1\) (maximum) has: $\( C(j_1,\,J-j_1\,|\,J) = 1 \quad \text{(positive by convention, normalized)} \)$

Applying the recursion downward (decreasing \(M\)) generates all \(C(m_1', m_2'\,|\,M)\) for \(M = J-1, J-2, \ldots, -J\).

Step 4: Verification for \(j_1 = j_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\), \(J = 1\).

Start: \(C(+\tfrac{1}{2},+\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,1) = 1\). Apply recursion with \(M=1, m_1' = -\tfrac{1}{2}, m_2' = +\tfrac{1}{2}\):

\[ \sqrt{1 \cdot 2 - 1 \cdot 0}\;C(-\tfrac{1}{2},+\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,0) = \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{4}-(-\tfrac{1}{2})(+\tfrac{1}{2})}\; C(+\tfrac{1}{2},+\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,1) + 0 \]
\[ \sqrt{2}\;C(-\tfrac{1}{2},+\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,0) = 1 \implies C(-\tfrac{1}{2},+\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,0) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \]

By symmetry (or applying with \(m_1' = +\tfrac{1}{2}, m_2' = -\tfrac{1}{2}\)): \(C(+\tfrac{1}{2},-\tfrac{1}{2}\,|\,0) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\). This gives \(\vert 1,0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle + \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle)\), confirming the result in the key example. \(\checkmark\)

Key Example: Two Spin-1/2 Particles#

For \(j_1 = j_2 = 1/2\): \(J \in \{0, 1\}\), giving \(1 + 3 = 4 = 2 \times 2\) states.

Triplet States (\(J = 1\), Symmetric)

\[ \vert 1, +1\rangle = \vert\uparrow\uparrow\rangle \]
\[ \vert 1, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle + \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle) \]
(45)#\[ \vert 1, -1\rangle = \vert\downarrow\downarrow\rangle \]

All three are symmetric under particle exchange: \(\hat{P}_{12}\vert 1, M\rangle = +\vert 1, M\rangle\).

Singlet State (\(J = 0\), Antisymmetric)

(46)#\[ \vert 0, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle) \]

The singlet is antisymmetric: \(\hat{P}_{12}\vert 0, 0\rangle = -\vert 0, 0\rangle\). It has zero total spin and is maximally entangled — measuring one particle’s spin along any axis completely determines the other’s.

Derivation: Two Spin-1/2 Clebsch-Gordan Coefficients

Stretched state. The highest-weight state is unique: \(\vert 1, 1\rangle = \vert\uparrow\uparrow\rangle\).

Lowering to \(M = 0\). Apply \(\hat{J}_- = \hat{J}_{1-} + \hat{J}_{2-}\) to \(\vert 1, 1\rangle\):

\[ \hat{J}_-\vert 1,1\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{2}\,\vert 1, 0\rangle \]
\[ (\hat{J}_{1-} + \hat{J}_{2-})\vert\uparrow\uparrow\rangle = \hbar\vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle + \hbar\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle \]

Equating: \(\vert 1, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle + \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle)\). Lowering again gives \(\vert 1, -1\rangle = \vert\downarrow\downarrow\rangle\).

Singlet by orthogonality. At \(M = 0\) there are two uncoupled states: \(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle\) and \(\vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle\). The \(J = 0\) state must be orthogonal to \(\vert 1, 0\rangle\) within this subspace:

\[ \vert 0, 0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle) \]

Verification. Check \(\hat{J}_+\vert 0,0\rangle = 0\): \((\hat{J}_{1+} + \hat{J}_{2+})\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - \vert\downarrow\uparrow\rangle) = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\uparrow\uparrow\rangle - \vert\uparrow\uparrow\rangle) = 0\). \(\checkmark\)

Physical consequences:

  • Helium ground state: Two electrons in the \(1s\) orbital have a symmetric spatial wavefunction, so the spin part must be the antisymmetric singlet — the electrons are spin-paired.

  • Pauli exclusion: Identical fermions in the same spatial state must form a spin singlet.

Discussion

The uncoupled state \(\vert\uparrow\downarrow\rangle\) is an equal superposition of singlet and triplet (\(M = 0\)). If you measure \(\hat{J}^2_{\text{tot}}\) on this state, you get \(J = 0\) or \(J = 1\) with equal probability. Before measuring, does the system “have” a definite total spin? What does this tell us about the relationship between individual and collective quantum numbers?

Application: Spin-Orbit Coupling#

Spin-Orbit Interaction

An electron in an atom experiences a coupling between orbital and spin angular momentum:

(47)#\[ \hat{H}_{SO} = \lambda\,\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}} \]

Using \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} + \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\), we can write \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}^2 - \hat{L}^2 - \hat{S}^2)\), which is diagonal in the coupled basis \(\vert\ell, s; j, m_j\rangle\):

\[ \langle \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}} \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}[j(j+1) - \ell(\ell+1) - s(s+1)] \]

Derivation: \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) in Terms of \(\hat{J}^2\)

From \(\hat{\boldsymbol{J}} = \hat{\boldsymbol{L}} + \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\), square both sides:

\[ \hat{J}^2 = (\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} + \hat{\boldsymbol{S}})^2 = \hat{L}^2 + \hat{S}^2 + 2\hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{S}} \]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{S}} = \hat{L}_x\hat{S}_x + \hat{L}_y\hat{S}_y + \hat{L}_z\hat{S}_z\) (the cross terms \(\hat{L}_i\hat{S}_j\) with \(i \neq j\) cancel in the expansion because \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\) and \(\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) act on different Hilbert spaces and thus commute). Solving:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{S}} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{J}^2 - \hat{L}^2 - \hat{S}^2) \]

In the coupled basis \(\vert \ell, s; j, m_j\rangle\), all three operators on the right are diagonal, giving:

\[ \langle \hat{\boldsymbol{L}}\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{2}[j(j+1) - \ell(\ell+1) - s(s+1)] \quad \checkmark \]

For an electron (\(s = 1/2\)) with orbital quantum number \(\ell \geq 1\), two values are allowed: \(j = \ell + 1/2\) and \(j = \ell - 1/2\). Their energy splitting is the fine structure — a small but measurable correction. Example: the hydrogen \(2p\) level splits into \(2p_{3/2}\) and \(2p_{1/2}\), a preview of perturbation theory in §5.1.

Derivation: Wigner–Eckart Theorem

The Wigner–Eckart theorem generalizes the selection rule \(\Delta m = q\) to a precise factorization of all matrix elements of tensor operators. It is one of the most powerful tools in quantum mechanics, reducing complicated multi-index matrix element computations to a single reduced matrix element times a known Clebsch–Gordan coefficient.

Spherical tensor operators. An operator \(\hat{T}^{(k)}_q\) (\(-k \le q \le k\)) is a spherical tensor of rank \(k\) if it transforms under rotations the same way as \(\vert k, q\rangle\):

\[ [\hat{J}_z,\, \hat{T}^{(k)}_q] = \hbar q\, \hat{T}^{(k)}_q, \qquad [\hat{J}_\pm,\, \hat{T}^{(k)}_q] = \hbar\sqrt{k(k+1) - q(q\pm 1)}\; \hat{T}^{(k)}_{q\pm 1}. \]

Examples: scalar operators have \(k=0\); vector components \(\hat{V}_{\pm1} = \mp(\hat{V}_x \pm \mathrm{i}\hat{V}_y)/\sqrt{2}\) and \(\hat{V}_0 = \hat{V}_z\) form a \(k=1\) tensor; the \(n=2\) spherical harmonics form a \(k=2\) tensor.

Step 1: Selection rule from \(\hat{J}_z\).

Sandwich \([\hat{J}_z, \hat{T}^{(k)}_q] = \hbar q\,\hat{T}^{(k)}_q\) between \(\langle j', m'\vert\) and \(\vert j, m\rangle\):

\[ (m' - m - q)\, \hbar \langle j',m'\vert \hat{T}^{(k)}_q \vert j,m\rangle = 0. \]

Therefore \(\langle j',m'\vert \hat{T}^{(k)}_q \vert j,m\rangle = 0\) unless \(m' = m + q\).

Step 2: Recursion from \(\hat{J}_\pm\).

Sandwich \([\hat{J}_\pm, \hat{T}^{(k)}_q]\vert j,m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{k(k\!+\!1)-q(q\pm 1)}\,\hat{T}^{(k)}_{q\pm 1}\vert j,m\rangle\) with \(\langle j',m'\vert\), and rewrite \(\langle j',m'\vert\hat{J}_\pm\) using \(\hat{J}_\pm^\dagger = \hat{J}_\mp\):

\[ \sqrt{j'(j'\!+\!1) - m'(m'\!\mp\! 1)}\,\langle j', m'\!\mp\! 1\vert \hat{T}^{(k)}_q\vert j,m\rangle + \sqrt{j(j\!+\!1) - m(m\!\pm\! 1)}\,\langle j',m'\vert \hat{T}^{(k)}_q\vert j, m\!\pm\! 1\rangle = \sqrt{k(k\!+\!1) - q(q\!\pm\! 1)}\,\langle j',m'\vert \hat{T}^{(k)}_{q\pm 1}\vert j,m\rangle. \]

Step 3: Identifying the Clebsch–Gordan recursion.

These are exactly the same recursion relations satisfied by the Clebsch–Gordan coefficients \(\langle j,m;k,q\vert j',m'\rangle\)! (Compare the recursion in the previous derivation box.) Since both \(\langle j',m'\vert\hat{T}^{(k)}_q\vert j,m\rangle\) and \(\langle j,m;k,q\vert j',m'\rangle\) satisfy the same linear recursion with the same boundary conditions (up to \(j'\)-dependent normalization), they are proportional.

Step 4: The Wigner–Eckart theorem.

The proportionality constant can depend only on \(j\), \(j'\), and \(k\) — it is independent of \(m\), \(m'\), and \(q\). It defines the reduced matrix element \(\langle j'\Vert\hat{T}^{(k)}\Vert j\rangle\):

\[ \langle j',m'\vert\hat{T}^{(k)}_q\vert j,m\rangle = \frac{\langle j,m;\,k,q\,\vert j',m'\rangle}{\sqrt{2j'+1}}\,\langle j'\Vert\hat{T}^{(k)}\Vert j\rangle. \]

(The \(1/\sqrt{2j'+1}\) normalization is a convention chosen so that \(\langle j'\Vert\hat{T}^{(k)}\Vert j\rangle\) is symmetric in \(j\leftrightarrow j'\) for Hermitian operators.)

Step 5: Selection rules and physical applications.

The CG coefficient \(\langle j,m;k,q\vert j',m'\rangle\) vanishes unless (i) \(m' = m+q\) and (ii) \(\vert j - k\vert \le j' \le j + k\) (triangle rule). These are the angular momentum selection rules: any operator of rank \(k\) can only connect states satisfying both conditions.

Example — electric dipole transitions in atoms. The dipole operator \(\hat{r}\) is a rank-\(k=1\) tensor (vector). So radiative transitions require \(\vert\Delta l\vert = 0\) or \(1\) (triangle rule with \(k=1\)), \(m' = m, m\pm 1\) (from \(q=0,\pm 1\) components), and parity change. These are the standard electric dipole selection rules, now proven from first principles rather than guessed.

Summary#

  • Two bases: uncoupled (\(m_1, m_2\) known) vs. coupled (\(J, M\) known), related by CG coefficients.

  • Triangle rule: \(J \in \{\vert j_1 - j_2\vert, \ldots, j_1 + j_2\}\); dimension count: \(\sum(2J+1) = (2j_1+1)(2j_2+1)\).

  • Clebsch-Gordan coefficients: real, unitary, nonzero only when \(M = m_1 + m_2\) and triangle rule holds.

  • Two spin-1/2: triplet (\(J = 1\), symmetric, 3 states) + singlet (\(J = 0\), antisymmetric, maximally entangled).

  • Spin-orbit coupling: \(\hat{\boldsymbol{L}} \cdot \hat{\boldsymbol{S}}\) is diagonal in the coupled basis; causes atomic fine structure.

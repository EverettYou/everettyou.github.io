2.3.1 Exchange Statistics#

Prompts

  • Why can particles in 3D only be bosons or fermions, while 2D allows any exchange phase?

  • What is the braid group, and how does it differ from the permutation group?

  • A charge-flux composite \((q, \Phi)\) is exchanged with an identical copy. Walk through how the Aharonov-Bohm effect produces the exchange phase \(\theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = q\Phi / 2\hbar\).

  • How does the charge-flux model unify braiding statistics, exchange statistics, and the spin-statistics relation into a single formula?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

In 3D, identical particles must be bosons or fermions — these are the only two options allowed by quantum mechanics. But in 2D, the topology of particle worldlines is richer: exchanging two particles twice is not topologically trivial, so any exchange phase is allowed. This lesson explores why 2D topology opens the door to anyons and develops the charge-flux composite as a concrete toy model for anyonic statistics.

Why 2D Is Special#

In 3D, exchanging two identical particles twice is the same as doing nothing — the particles can always “go around” each other and return to the original configuration. This forces the exchange phase to satisfy \(\mathrm{e}^{2\mathrm{i}\theta} = 1\), giving only two possibilities:

Exchange Statistics in 3D

\[\text{Bosons: } \theta = 0 \quad (\text{no phase}), \qquad \text{Fermions: } \theta = \pi \quad (\text{phase } {-1})\]

These are the only options in three spatial dimensions.

Derivation: Why Only \(\theta = 0\) or \(\pi\) in 3D

The allowed exchange phases are determined by the topology of configuration space. For \(N\) identical particles in \(d\) dimensions, the configuration space is

\[ \mathcal{C}_N = (\mathbb{R}^{dN} \setminus \Delta) / S_N \]

where \(\Delta\) removes coincident points (particles cannot overlap) and \(S_N\) quotients by permutations (particles are identical). Quantum statistics is classified by the first fundamental group \(\pi_1(\mathcal{C}_N)\): each topologically distinct loop in configuration space corresponds to a distinct exchange process, and the wavefunction must pick up a well-defined phase for each.

In 3D (\(d \geq 3\)): The fundamental group is the permutation group, \(\pi_1(\mathcal{C}_N) = S_N\). The key fact is that for \(d \geq 3\), any loop where particle 1 goes around particle 2 can be continuously deformed to the trivial loop — the extra spatial dimensions provide room to “lift” the path over the obstacle. Since the exchange operation \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}\) satisfies \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}^2 = I\) in \(S_N\), the phase must satisfy

\[ \mathrm{e}^{2\mathrm{i}\theta} = 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \theta = 0 \text{ (boson) or } \theta = \pi \text{ (fermion)} \]

In 2D (\(d = 2\)): The fundamental group is the braid group, \(\pi_1(\mathcal{C}_N) = B_N\). In a plane, one particle cannot “go over” another — the path around the obstacle is topologically nontrivial and cannot be shrunk to a point. The braid group has generators \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_i\) that satisfy \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_i \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{i+1} \hat{\mathcal{P}}_i = \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{i+1} \hat{\mathcal{P}}_i \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{i+1}\) (the Yang-Baxter equation) but not \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_i^2 = I\). The one-dimensional representations of \(B_N\) are \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_i \mapsto \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\theta}\) for any \(\theta \in [0, 2\pi)\), giving abelian anyons. Higher-dimensional representations give non-abelian anyons.

In 2D, the situation changes. When one particle moves around another in a plane, it traces out a loop that cannot be shrunk to a point — the other particle is always “in the way.” Clockwise and counterclockwise exchanges are topologically distinct, so in general \(\hat{\mathcal{P}} \neq \hat{\mathcal{P}}^{-1}\). Exchanging twice is no longer trivial. The exchange phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\theta}\) can take any value, not just \(\pm 1\).

Braiding vs. Permutation

In 3D, particle exchange is described by the permutation group \(S_N\). Exchanging twice gives the identity: \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{12}^2 = I\).

In 2D, particle exchange is described by the braid group \(B_N\). Exchanging twice does not give the identity — the worldlines of the particles form a braid that cannot be untangled.

Think of particle worldlines as strings extending in time. In 2D+1D spacetime:

  • Two strings can wind around each other, forming knots and braids

  • The order in which you braid matters (for non-abelian anyons)

  • The number of windings is a topological invariant — it cannot be changed by smooth deformations

Definition: (Abelian) Anyons

Particles in 2D with exchange phase \(\theta \neq 0, \pi\) are called anyons (from “any-ons,” because \(\theta\) can be any value). Under a single counterclockwise exchange, the many-body state \(\vert\Psi\rangle\) acquires a phase:

\[|\Psi\rangle \to \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\theta} |\Psi\rangle\]

  • \(\theta = 0\): boson

  • \(\theta = \pi\): fermion

  • \(0 < \theta < \pi\): anyon (fractional statistics)

Abelian vs. Non-Abelian

If exchanging particles only produces a phase \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\theta}\), the anyons are abelian — different exchanges commute.

If exchanging particles acts as a unitary matrix on a degenerate ground-state space, i.e. \(|\Psi\rangle \to U |\Psi\rangle\), the anyons are non-abelian — the order of exchanges matters.

Charge-Flux Composite#

A useful toy model for abelian anyons is the charge-flux composite \((q, \Phi)\): a point charge \(q\) bound to a thin magnetic flux tube \(\Phi\). The Aharonov-Bohm effect then generates statistical phases.

Aharonov-Bohm Phase

When a charge \(q\) completes a closed loop encircling a magnetic flux \(\Phi\), it accumulates a phase:

(48)#\[\theta_{\mathrm{AB}} = \frac{q\,\Phi}{\hbar}\]

Derivation: Aharonov–Bohm Phase from the Path Integral

We derive the Aharonov–Bohm phase accumulated by a charged particle moving along a closed loop \(\mathcal{C}\) encircling a magnetic flux tube carrying flux \(\Phi\).

Setup. Outside the flux tube the magnetic field vanishes, \(\boldsymbol{B} = 0\), but the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is non-zero and satisfies

\[ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A} \cdot \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r} = \Phi \]

by Stokes’ theorem applied to any surface bounded by \(\mathcal{C}\).

Minimal coupling in the Lagrangian. For a particle of charge \(q\) moving in an electromagnetic field, the Lagrangian contains the minimal-coupling term:

\[ L = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{\boldsymbol{r}}^2 + \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r}) \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{r}} \qquad (\text{Gaussian units}) \]

or equivalently in SI units \(q\boldsymbol{A}\cdot\dot{\boldsymbol{r}}\).

Path integral phase. In the path integral, each path \(\boldsymbol{r}(t)\) contributes a weight \(\exp(\mathrm{i}S[\boldsymbol{r}]/\hbar)\). The electromagnetic contribution to the action along a path is

\[ S_{\mathrm{em}} = q\int \boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r}(t))\cdot\dot{\boldsymbol{r}}\,\mathrm{d}t = q\int_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r} \]

This is purely a line integral along the path and does not depend on the speed of travel.

Closed loop. For a closed loop \(\mathcal{C}\) encircling the flux tube, all paths that wind around the tube once receive the same additional phase factor regardless of their detailed shape:

\[ \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}S_{\mathrm{em}}/\hbar} = \exp\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}q}{\hbar}\oint_{\mathcal{C}} \boldsymbol{A}\cdot\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{r}\right) = \exp\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{i}q\Phi}{\hbar}\right) \]

Physical consequence. Even though \(\boldsymbol{B} = 0\) along every path, the vector potential \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is non-zero and gives a gauge-invariant (observable) phase when integrated around the loop. This phase is \(\theta_{\mathrm{AB}} = q\Phi/\hbar\), independent of the loop’s size or shape as long as it winds once around the flux tube.

This single formula unifies three kinds of statistics for charge-flux composites:

Three Kinds of Statistics

  • Braiding statistics. When one composite \((q_1, \Phi_1)\) moves in a full closed loop around another \((q_2, \Phi_2)\), the charge \(q_1\) encircles the flux \(\Phi_2\), accumulating an Aharonov-Bohm phase:

    (49)#\[ \theta_{\mathrm{br}} = \frac{q_1 \Phi_2}{\hbar} \]

  • Exchange statistics. Exchanging two identical composites \((q, \Phi)\) is a half-braid, so the exchange phase is:

    (50)#\[ \theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = \frac{\theta_{\mathrm{br}}}{2} = \frac{q\,\Phi}{2\hbar} \]

  • Spin-statistics relation. A \(2\pi\) self-rotation of a composite produces the same phase as an exchange. The spin quantum number is:

    (51)#\[ s = \frac{\theta_{\mathrm{ex}}}{2\pi} = \frac{q\,\Phi}{4\pi\hbar} \]

Derivation: Exchange Phase Is Half the Braiding Phase

Consider two identical charge-flux composites \((q, \Phi)\) at positions \(\boldsymbol{r}_1\) and \(\boldsymbol{r}_2\). We want to show that exchanging them (a half-braid) gives exactly half the phase of a full braid.

Full braid (braiding phase): Composite 1 makes a complete counterclockwise loop around composite 2, returning to \(\boldsymbol{r}_1\). The charge \(q_1\) encircles the flux \(\Phi_2\), accumulating the Aharonov-Bohm phase \(\theta_{\mathrm{br}} = q\Phi/\hbar\).

Half-braid (exchange phase): In an exchange, composite 1 moves to position \(\boldsymbol{r}_2\) while composite 2 simultaneously moves to \(\boldsymbol{r}_1\), with each tracing a semicircular arc. In the center-of-mass frame, the relative coordinate \(\boldsymbol{r} = \boldsymbol{r}_1 - \boldsymbol{r}_2\) traces a full semicircle (angle \(\pi\)), so the relative angle changes by \(\Delta\phi = \pi\).

But a full braid corresponds to \(\Delta\phi = 2\pi\) in the relative coordinate. Since the Aharonov-Bohm phase is proportional to the subtended angle:

\[ \theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = \frac{\Delta\phi_{\text{exchange}}}{\Delta\phi_{\text{braid}}} \cdot \theta_{\mathrm{br}} = \frac{\pi}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{q\Phi}{\hbar} = \frac{q\Phi}{2\hbar} \]

Equivalently, the charge \(q_1\) sweeps through a solid angle that subtends exactly half the flux \(\Phi_2\) during an exchange, accumulating half the full Aharonov-Bohm phase.

“Charge around flux” vs “flux around charge”

When composite A encircles composite B, one can equivalently view this as charge \(q_1\) moving around flux \(\Phi_2\), or flux \(\Phi_1\) moving around charge \(q_2\). These are two descriptions of the same topological interaction (the same linking of worldlines), not two separate contributions to be summed. The braiding phase is \(q_1\Phi_2/\hbar\), counted once.

Recovering Bosons and Fermions

  • Bosons: \(q\Phi/(2\hbar) = 0 \pmod{2\pi}\), so \(\theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = 0\) and \(s \in \mathbb{Z}\) (integer spin).

  • Fermions: \(q\Phi/(2\hbar) = \pi \pmod{2\pi}\), so \(\theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = \pi\) and \(s \in \mathbb{Z} + \tfrac{1}{2}\) (half-integer spin).

  • Anyons: any other value gives fractional statistics and fractional spin.

Discussion: Why Does Spin = Exchange?

The spin-statistics relation \(\theta_s = \theta_{\mathrm{ex}}\) follows naturally in the charge-flux picture: both self-rotation and exchange involve a charge sweeping past a flux in a half-braid topology. But in 3D, the spin-statistics theorem is proven from relativistic quantum field theory (CPT symmetry). Is the charge-flux argument a “derivation” of spin-statistics, or merely a consistency check? Can you think of a topological reason why self-rotation and exchange must give the same phase in 2D?

Derivation: Spin-Statistics Relation for Charge-Flux Composites

We show that a \(2\pi\) self-rotation of a charge-flux composite \((q, \Phi)\) produces the same phase as an exchange with an identical composite, establishing \(\theta_s = \theta_{\mathrm{ex}}\).

Self-rotation: Rotate the composite by \(2\pi\) about its center. The charge \(q\) is displaced from the flux \(\Phi\) by an infinitesimal distance (the composite has internal structure). During the \(2\pi\) rotation, the charge traces a small loop that encloses the flux exactly once in a half-winding sense. More precisely, in the frame co-rotating with the composite, the charge sweeps an angle of \(2\pi\) around the flux tube.

However, the key geometric identity is that a \(2\pi\) self-rotation of a point-like composite is topologically equivalent to an exchange of two identical composites. To see this, consider two composites and imagine the following continuous deformation:

  1. Start with two composites at \(\boldsymbol{r}_1\) and \(\boldsymbol{r}_2\).

  2. Exchange them via a half-braid (each moves along a semicircle).

  3. The relative coordinate \(\boldsymbol{r} = \boldsymbol{r}_1 - \boldsymbol{r}_2\) rotates by \(\pi\).

  4. Now shrink composite 2 onto composite 1 — the half-braid becomes a \(\pi\) self-rotation.

Since a half-braid = exchange = \(\pi\) rotation of the relative coordinate, and the phase is \(\theta_{\mathrm{ex}} = q\Phi/(2\hbar)\), we identify the spin as

\[ s = \frac{\theta_{\mathrm{ex}}}{2\pi} = \frac{q\Phi}{4\pi\hbar} \]

and the \(2\pi\) rotation phase (topological spin) is \(\theta_s = 2\pi s = q\Phi/(2\hbar) = \theta_{\mathrm{ex}}\), confirming the spin-statistics relation.

Derivation: Mutual Statistics Phase Between Charge-Flux Composites

Two different charge-flux composites, composite 1 = \((q_1, \Phi_1)\) and composite 2 = \((q_2, \Phi_2)\), acquire a phase when one executes a full counterclockwise loop around the other. This mutual statistics phase has two independent contributions.

Contribution 1: Charge of composite 1 around flux of composite 2. As composite 1 moves along a closed loop encircling composite 2, its charge \(q_1\) orbits the flux \(\Phi_2\). By the Aharonov–Bohm effect (eq. (48)):

\[ \varphi_1 = \frac{q_1\,\Phi_2}{\hbar} \]

Contribution 2: Flux of composite 1 around charge of composite 2. The flux tube \(\Phi_1\) is rigidly attached to composite 1 and is dragged along the same path. In the rest frame of composite 2, this is equivalent to its charge \(q_2\) tracing a full counterclockwise loop around flux \(\Phi_1\). By AB again:

\[ \varphi_2 = \frac{q_2\,\Phi_1}{\hbar} \]

Total mutual statistics phase (full counterclockwise braid):

\[ \varphi_{12} = \frac{q_1\Phi_2 + q_2\Phi_1}{\hbar} \]

Note: both contributions have the same sign — when composite 1 goes counterclockwise around composite 2, charge 1 sees flux 2 counterclockwise AND from composite 2’s perspective charge 2 also encircles flux 1 counterclockwise.

Special cases:

  1. Identical composites \((q_1=q_2=q, \Phi_1=\Phi_2=\Phi)\): Full braid phase \(= 2q\Phi/\hbar\). A single exchange is a half-braid, giving exchange phase \(\theta = q\Phi/\hbar\) — consistent with the self-statistics derived from the AB effect and spin-statistics theorem.

  2. Pure charge vs. pure flux \((q_1,0)\) and \((0, \Phi_2)\): Mutual phase \(= q_1\Phi_2/\hbar\) (only charge sees flux, no flux-on-charge contribution). This is the elementary AB setup.

  3. Recovering bosons and fermions: \(q\Phi = 0\) gives \(\theta = 0\) (bosons); \(q\Phi = \pi\hbar\) gives \(\theta = \pi\) (fermions). \(\checkmark\)

  4. Semion (\(\theta = \pi/2\)): \(q\Phi = \pi\hbar/2\), the statistics of the \(e\) and \(m\) anyons in the toric code. Their composite \(\varepsilon = e \times m\) has total phase \(\pi/2 + \pi/2 = \pi\) — a fermion, consistent with §2.3.3.

Physical picture: Statistics are electromagnetic in origin. The mutual statistics phase is entirely due to the Aharonov–Bohm interaction between the charge of one composite and the flux of the other. This is why anyons are possible in 2D: the flux tube and the particle it’s bound to cannot be separated by any local deformation, making the mutual phase topologically robust.

Discussion: Can Anyons Exist in 3D?

In 3D, the topology of configuration space forces \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}^2 = I\), seemingly ruling out anyons. But what about particles confined to a 2D layer embedded in 3D (like electrons in a quantum well)? Are these “truly 2D” for the purposes of exchange statistics? What physical conditions must be met for anyonic behavior to emerge?

2.1.1 Tensor Product#

Prompts

  • What is a tensor product of two Hilbert spaces, and how do basis states combine?

  • For a two-particle system where each particle has \(d\) basis states, how many basis states does the combined system have, and why?

  • What is a product state, and how does it differ from an entangled state?

  • How do operators acting on individual particles act on multi-particle states?

  • Explain why the dimension of an \(N\)-particle Hilbert space grows exponentially with \(N\). Is there a fundamental limit?

  • If you entangle two qubits in a Bell state, can you later separate them back into a product state? Why or why not?

Lecture Notes#

Key Concept: Tensor Product

When quantum systems combine, their state space is not a simple union—it is a tensor product that encodes all possible correlations between subsystems. For subsystems A and B with Hilbert spaces \(\mathcal{H}_A\) and \(\mathcal{H}_B\), the composite system lives in \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\).

Combining Two Single-Qubit Systems#

Each qubit has a 2-dimensional Hilbert space with basis states \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\).

The two-qubit Hilbert space has dimension \(2 \times 2 = 4\). The basis states are formed by tensoring single-qubit states:

\[ |00\rangle = |0\rangle_A \otimes |0\rangle_B, \quad |01\rangle = |0\rangle_A \otimes |1\rangle_B, \quad |10\rangle = |1\rangle_A \otimes |0\rangle_B, \quad |11\rangle = |1\rangle_A \otimes |1\rangle_B\]

More compactly, we label two-qubit states as \(|ij\rangle\) where \(i,j \in \{0,1\}\), or sometimes write \(|i\rangle \otimes |j\rangle\) to emphasize the tensor product structure.

Orthonormality:

(56)#\[ \langle i_1 j_1 | i_2 j_2 \rangle = \langle i_1 | i_2 \rangle \langle j_1 | j_2 \rangle = \delta_{i_1 i_2} \delta_{j_1 j_2}\]

Discussion: Schmidt Decomposition

For any bipartite state \(|\psi\rangle_{AB}\), the Schmidt decomposition writes it as \(|\psi\rangle = \sum_k \sqrt{\lambda_k} |u_k\rangle_A |v_k\rangle_B\). Does separability depend on how we partition the system into subsystems A and B? A state might appear entangled under factorization \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\), but separable under a different partition \(\mathcal{H}_{A'} \otimes \mathcal{H}_{B'}\).

Key question: Is entanglement observer-dependent—a property of our choice of subsystem partition—or is there a physically preferred factorization based on dynamics or spatial separation?

General Case: \(N\) Particles with \(d\) States Each#

For \(N\) quantum particles, each having access to \(d\) single-particle basis states \(|\alpha\rangle\) (where \(\alpha = 1, 2, \ldots, d\)), the combined Hilbert space is:

\[ \mathcal{H}_1 \otimes \mathcal{H}_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathcal{H}_N\]

A complete orthonormal basis consists of all product states:

\[ |\alpha_1\rangle |\alpha_2\rangle \cdots |\alpha_N\rangle = |\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N\rangle\]

where each \(\alpha_i \in \{1, 2, \ldots, d\}\).

Hilbert Space Dimension

The total Hilbert space dimension is:

(57)#\[ \dim(\mathcal{H}_{\text{total}}) = d^N\]

This dimension grows exponentially with the number of particles.

Example: Exponential Growth and Physical Limits

For \(N = 10\) particles with \(d = 2\) (qubits), the Hilbert space has \(2^{10} = 1024\) dimensions. For \(N = 300\) and \(d = 2\), we get \(2^{300} \approx 10^{90}\) dimensions—larger than the number of atoms in the observable universe!

This exponential growth is both a source of quantum computing’s power and a reason why classical simulation of quantum systems becomes fundamentally intractable.

Note: Physical Interpretation

The exponential dimension reflects a deep fact about quantum mechanics: specifying a general \(N\)-particle quantum state requires listing \(d^N\) complex amplitudes. No classical computer can store this for large \(N\) without enormous overhead. Quantum computers exploit this exponential space for computational advantage.

Product States and Entanglement#

Product State (Separable State)

A product state is a state that can be written as a tensor product of single-particle states:

(58)#\[ |\psi angle = |\psi_1 angle \otimes |\psi_2 angle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\psi_N angle\]

For a product state:

  • Particle independence: measurement on particle \(i\) does not affect the state of particle \(j\) (\(j eq i\)).

  • Factorizable expectation values: \(\langle A_1 \otimes B_2 angle = \langle A_1 angle \langle B_2 angle\) where \(A_1\) acts on particle 1 and \(B_2\) acts on particle 2.

Entangled States#

Most states in the tensor product space cannot be written as a simple product. These are entangled states.

Example: The Bell State (Two Qubits)

\[ |\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\]

This state cannot be written as \(|\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle\) for any \(|\psi_A\rangle, |\psi_B\rangle\). If we measure qubit A and find it in state \(|0\rangle\), then qubit B is definitely in state \(|0\rangle\) (not a superposition). The measurement on A instantaneously affects the state of B—perfect correlation.

General Multi-Particle State#

Any state in the \(N\)-particle Hilbert space can be expanded as:

(59)#\[ |\psi\rangle = \sum_{\alpha_1=1}^d \sum_{\alpha_2=1}^d \cdots \sum_{\alpha_N=1}^d c_{\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N} |\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N\rangle\]

The coefficients \(c_{\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N}\) are complex amplitudes, with normalization \(\sum_{\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N} |c_{\alpha_1 \ldots \alpha_N}|^2 = 1\).

Operators on Multi-Particle Systems#

Single-Particle Operators#

An operator that acts on only one particle is extended to the full space by tensoring with identity on other particles.

For particle \(i\) with operator \(A_i\):

(60)#\[ \hat{A}_i = \mathbb{I}_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathbb{I}_{i-1} \otimes A_i \otimes \mathbb{I}_{i+1} \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathbb{I}_N\]

Action on product states:

\[ \hat{A}_i |\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N\rangle = |\alpha_1 \ldots \alpha_{i-1}\rangle (A_i |\alpha_i\rangle) |\alpha_{i+1} \ldots \alpha_N\rangle\]

Example: For a two-qubit system, the Pauli \(X\) operator acting on qubit 2:

\[\begin{split} X_2 = I \otimes X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \otimes I\end{split}\]
\[X_2 |01\rangle = |0\rangle \otimes (X|1\rangle) = |0\rangle \otimes |0\rangle = |00\rangle\]

Two-Body Interactions#

An operator acting on pairs of particles takes the form:

\[ V_{ij} = \mathbb{I}_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes V_{ij} \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathbb{I}_N\]

where \(V_{ij}\) couples particles \(i\) and \(j\).

Example: Spin-spin coupling

\[ H_{int} = J \boldsymbol{S}_1 \cdot \boldsymbol{S}_2\]

This couples the spins of particles 1 and 2, energetically favoring or disfavoring alignment depending on the sign of \(J\).

Commutation of Single-Particle Operators#

Operators acting on different particles always commute:

(61)#\[ [A_i, B_j] = 0 \quad \text{for } i \neq j\]

This is because they act on orthogonal subspaces of the tensor product.

Basis States and Notation Conventions#

Standard Notation#

For an \(N\)-particle system, we use several interchangeable notations:

\[ |\alpha_1 \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_N\rangle = |\alpha_1\rangle |\alpha_2\rangle \cdots |\alpha_N\rangle = |\alpha_1\rangle \otimes |\alpha_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\alpha_N\rangle\]

The commas are often omitted for brevity when there is no ambiguity.

Position and Momentum Basis#

For continuous quantum systems (particles in space), basis states are labeled by position or momentum:

\[ |\boldsymbol{r}_1 \boldsymbol{r}_2 \ldots \boldsymbol{r}_N\rangle \quad \text{(position basis)}\]

A many-body wavefunction is:

\[ \psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1, \boldsymbol{r}_2, \ldots, \boldsymbol{r}_N) = \langle \boldsymbol{r}_1 \boldsymbol{r}_2 \ldots \boldsymbol{r}_N | \psi \rangle\]

Discrete Basis (Modes)#

For systems with discrete single-particle spectrum (e.g., trapped particles, cavity modes), label basis states by mode indices:

\[ |\phi_i\rangle \quad \text{for } i = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]

A two-particle state might be:

\[ |\phi_i \phi_j\rangle = |i, j\rangle\]

Example: Two-Particle Hilbert Space

Two Distinguishable Qubits

For two distinguishable particles (labeled A and B), each a qubit, the complete basis is:

\[ \{|00\rangle, |01\rangle, |10\rangle, |11\rangle\}\]

Any two-qubit state:

\[ |\psi\rangle = c_{00} |00\rangle + c_{01} |01\rangle + c_{10} |10\rangle + c_{11} |11\rangle\]

with \(|c_{00}|^2 + |c_{01}|^2 + |c_{10}|^2 + |c_{11}|^2 = 1\).

Product State Example

The state \(|\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle = \left(\frac{|0\rangle + |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}\right)_A \otimes |0\rangle_B\) expands as:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |10\rangle)\]

This factorizes: knowing particle A’s state constrains particle B to \(|0\rangle\) always.

Entangled State Example

The Bell state \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) has a beautiful property: measuring either qubit in the computational basis collapses both.

  • If we measure qubit A and get outcome “0”, qubit B must be in state \(|0\rangle\).

  • If we measure qubit A and get outcome “1”, qubit B must be in state \(|1\rangle\).

The state cannot be factored into independent pieces; the qubits are maximally correlated.

Key Insights#

Hilbert Space Dimension#

The exponential growth \(d^N\) means that:

  • Specifying a general \(N\)-particle quantum state requires \(O(d^N)\) complex numbers.

  • This exponential complexity is why classical computers struggle to simulate quantum systems but also why quantum computers can be powerful.

Distinguishability vs. Indistinguishability#

The tensor product as formulated here assumes distinguishable particles (e.g., “particle A” and “particle B” are labeled).

For identical particles, not all states in \(\mathcal{H}_1 \otimes \mathcal{H}_2 \otimes \cdots\) are physical. Only symmetric (bosons) or antisymmetric (fermions) combinations are allowed. This dramatically reduces the effective Hilbert space dimension.

Entanglement as a Computational Resource#

Entanglement—correlations that cannot be explained by local product states—is a key resource in quantum computing and quantum information. It allows:

  • Quantum cryptography (secure key distribution).

  • Quantum teleportation (moving a quantum state).

  • Exponential speedups in some algorithms (quantum computers).

Summary#

  • Tensor product \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\) combines single-particle Hilbert spaces.

  • Dimension scaling: An \(N\)-particle system with \(d\) single-particle states has dimension \(d^N\).

  • Basis states: Form complete set by tensoring all single-particle basis state combinations.

  • Product states factor into single-particle states; most states are entangled.

  • Single-particle operators act via identity on other particles; different-particle operators commute.

  • Exponential growth is foundational to quantum advantage and scaling challenges.

The Hilbert space of system A has dimension \(d_A = 3\) (with basis \(|1\rangle, |2\rangle, |3\rangle\)) and system B has dimension \(d_B = 4\) (with basis \(|1\rangle, |2\rangle, |3\rangle, |4\rangle\)). Write down the dimension of \(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B\) and list all 12 basis states explicitly.

2. Determine whether the two-qubit state

\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{2}(|00\rangle + |01\rangle + |10\rangle + |11\rangle)\]

is a product state. If it is, factor it as \(|\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle\).

3. Show that the Bell state \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) cannot be written as a product state \(|\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle\) for any single-qubit states \(|\psi_A\rangle\) and \(|\psi_B\rangle\).

4. The operator \(\hat{\sigma}^z\) acts on qubit A. Write the explicit \(4 \times 4\) matrix for \(\hat{\sigma}^z \otimes \hat{\mathbb{I}}\) in the ordered basis \(\{|00\rangle, |01\rangle, |10\rangle, |11\rangle\}\).

5. Compute \((\hat{\sigma}^x \otimes \hat{\sigma}^x)|\Phi^+\rangle\) where \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\). What is the result, and what does it tell you about this Bell state?

6. Using the tensor product inner product rule \(\langle \alpha_1 \alpha_2 | \beta_1 \beta_2 \rangle = \langle \alpha_1 | \beta_1 \rangle \langle \alpha_2 | \beta_2 \rangle\), evaluate \(\langle 00 | 11 \rangle\), \(\langle 01 | 01 \rangle\), and \(\langle 10 | 01 \rangle\). Explain why the four states \(\{|00\rangle, |01\rangle, |10\rangle, |11\rangle\}\) form an orthonormal basis.

7. For a product state \(|\psi\rangle = |\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle\), prove that the expectation value of a two-particle operator factorizes: \(\langle \psi | \hat{A} \otimes \hat{B} | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi_A | \hat{A} | \psi_A \rangle \langle \psi_B | \hat{B} | \psi_B \rangle\).

8. For \(N\) qubits (\(d = 2\)), how many independent real parameters are needed to specify (a) a general normalized state in \(\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}\), and (b) a general normalized product state \(|\psi_1\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\psi_N\rangle\)? What does the difference represent physically?

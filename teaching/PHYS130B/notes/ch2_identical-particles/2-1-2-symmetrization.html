2.1.2 Symmetrization#

Prompts

  • What does it mean for particles to be identical in quantum mechanics? Why does indistinguishability constrain the wavefunction to be either symmetric or antisymmetric?

  • How do you construct a properly symmetrized (bosonic) or antisymmetrized (fermionic) multi-particle state from single-particle wavefunctions? What happens when two fermions are placed in the same state?

  • What are the insertion and deletion rules \(\rhd_\pm\) and \(\lhd_\pm\)? How do they provide a systematic procedure for adding or removing a particle while preserving symmetry?

  • Why does the Pauli exclusion principle follow automatically from antisymmetry? What physical consequences does it have for atomic structure and the stability of matter?

Lecture Notes#

Overview#

Particles of the same type (electrons, photons, etc.) are absolutely identical in quantum mechanics—they carry no identity tags and are completely interchangeable. This indistinguishability is not a practical limitation but a fundamental principle that restricts which quantum states are physical: multi-particle states must be either symmetric (bosons) or antisymmetric (fermions) under particle exchange.

Analogy. Money in a bank account is identical. You cannot ask “Is this the dollar I deposited today or yesterday?”—dollars are indistinguishable. Identical particles are the same: two electrons have no labels. This simple observation has profound consequences for the structure of matter.

Identical Particles#

Identical Particles

Particles of the same type have identical mass, charge, spin, and no inherent labels. This indistinguishability is a fundamental principle that constrains which quantum states are physical.

Physical requirement. Let \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) be the permutation operator that swaps particles \(i\) and \(j\). For identical particles, all observables must be invariant under permutation: \(\langle\Psi\vert \hat{O}\vert\Psi\rangle = \langle\Psi\vert \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^\dagger \hat{O}\,\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle\) for any observable \(\hat{O}\). This requires the state to be an eigenstate of every transposition:

\[\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = \lambda\,\vert\Psi\rangle\]

Since \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^2 = \hat{I}\), the eigenvalue satisfies \(\lambda^2 = 1\), so \(\lambda = \pm 1\).

Symmetry Constraint

Under particle exchange, the many-body state must satisfy:

  • Symmetric (\(\lambda = +1\), bosons): \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = +\vert\Psi\rangle\)

  • Antisymmetric (\(\lambda = -1\), fermions): \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = -\vert\Psi\rangle\)

The choice is determined by particle spin (integer → boson, half-integer → fermion) via the spin-statistics theorem.

Derivation: Exchange Eigenvalues Are \(\pm 1\)

Step 1: Involution property. The permutation operator \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) swaps particles \(i\) and \(j\). Swapping twice returns to the original state, so \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^2 = \hat{I}\).

Step 2: Eigenvalue constraint. Let \(\vert\Psi\rangle\) be an eigenstate with eigenvalue \(\lambda\): \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\vert\Psi\rangle = \lambda\vert\Psi\rangle\). Applying \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) again:

\[\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^2\vert\Psi\rangle = \lambda^2\vert\Psi\rangle = \vert\Psi\rangle\]

so \(\lambda^2 = 1\), giving \(\lambda = +1\) (symmetric) or \(\lambda = -1\) (antisymmetric).

Step 3: Why must the state be an eigenstate? For identical particles, every observable \(\hat{O}\) commutes with \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\): \([\hat{O}, \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}] = 0\). This means the Hamiltonian preserves the symmetric and antisymmetric subspaces separately, so a system that starts in one subspace stays there forever. Nature chooses one subspace for each particle species.

Step 4: Unitarity. Since \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^2 = \hat{I}\) and \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) preserves inner products (it just relabels particles), \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}\) is both unitary and Hermitian: \(\hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^\dagger = \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij} = \hat{\mathcal{P}}_{ij}^{-1}\).

Discussion: Indistinguishability and Entanglement

A symmetrized or antisymmetrized state is always an entangled superposition—you cannot assign a definite single-particle state to a specific particle. Is this “entanglement” the same as in a Bell pair, or is it fundamentally different? Can you design an experiment that distinguishes indistinguishability-entanglement from preparation-entanglement?

Symmetric and Antisymmetric States#

Let \(\{\vert\alpha\rangle\}\) be an orthonormal basis for the single-particle Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). A generic \(N\)-particle state in \(\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}\) is

(31)#\[\vert\Psi\rangle = \sum_{\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N} \Psi_{\alpha_1 \cdots \alpha_N}\;\vert\alpha_1\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_N\rangle\]

where \(\Psi_{\alpha_1 \cdots \alpha_N}\) is the many-body amplitude. For identical particles, only the symmetric or antisymmetric subspace of \(\mathcal{H}^{\otimes N}\) is physical.

Bosonic (Symmetric) States#

For \(N\) bosons placed in distinct single-particle states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_N\rangle\), the symmetrized state is:

(32)#\[\vert\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N\rangle_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(2)}\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle\]

The sum (all signs positive) is the ket-space analog of the permanent. Multiple bosons can occupy the same state.

Example: Two Bosons

Problem. Construct the symmetric state for two bosons in orthogonal states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \vert\alpha_2\rangle\).

Solution.

\[\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \bigl(\vert\alpha_1\rangle\vert\alpha_2\rangle + \vert\alpha_2\rangle\vert\alpha_1\rangle\bigr)\]

If both bosons occupy the same state \(\vert\alpha\rangle\): \(\vert\alpha, \alpha\rangle_+ = \vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\) (already symmetric).

Normalization with Repeated States

More generally, when \(n_\alpha\) bosons share the same single-particle state, the permanent acquires \(n_\alpha !\) identical terms, which should be further normalized by \(\sqrt{n_\alpha !}\). So for \(N\) bosons with occupation numbers \(\{n_\alpha\}\), the normalization factor is:

\[\mathcal{N}_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N! \prod_\alpha n_\alpha!}}\]

Derivation: Normalization of Symmetrized States

Distinct states. For \(N\) bosons in mutually orthogonal states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_N\rangle\), the unnormalized symmetrized state is \(\vert\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle\). Its norm squared is:

\[\langle\tilde{\Psi}\vert\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = \sum_{\pi, \pi'} \prod_{k=1}^N \langle\alpha_{\pi'(k)}\vert\alpha_{\pi(k)}\rangle = \sum_{\pi, \pi'} \prod_{k=1}^N \delta_{\pi'(k),\pi(k)}\]

The product of Kronecker deltas equals 1 only when \(\pi' = \pi\). Since there are \(N!\) permutations, \(\langle\tilde{\Psi}\vert\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = N!\), so the normalization factor is \(1/\sqrt{N!}\).

Repeated states. When \(n_\alpha\) bosons share state \(\vert\alpha\rangle\), different permutations \(\pi\) and \(\pi'\) can produce the same tensor product state (permutations that only rearrange particles within the same mode are invisible). For each mode \(\alpha\), there are \(n_\alpha!\) such redundant permutations, so each distinct term appears \(\prod_\alpha n_\alpha!\) times. The norm squared becomes \(\langle\tilde{\Psi}\vert\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = N! \prod_\alpha n_\alpha!\), giving the normalization factor:

\[\mathcal{N}_+ = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N! \prod_\alpha n_\alpha!}}\]

Fermionic case. For antisymmetrized states with distinct single-particle states, the norm calculation is similar: \(\langle\tilde{\Psi}\vert\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = \sum_{\pi,\pi'} \mathrm{sgn}(\pi)\,\mathrm{sgn}(\pi') \prod_k \delta_{\pi'(k),\pi(k)} = \sum_\pi [\mathrm{sgn}(\pi)]^2 = N!\). The normalization is again \(1/\sqrt{N!}\). No repeated-state correction is needed because fermionic states with \(n_\alpha \geq 2\) vanish identically.

Fermionic (Antisymmetric) States#

For \(N\) fermions placed in distinct single-particle states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_N\rangle\), the antisymmetrized state is:

(33)#\[\vert\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \mathrm{sgn}(\pi)\;\vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(2)}\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle\]

This is the ket-space analog of the Slater determinant. Antisymmetry is automatic: swapping any two particle labels flips the sign. If \(\vert\alpha_i\rangle = \vert\alpha_j\rangle\) for \(i \neq j\), the state vanishes—this is the Pauli exclusion principle.

Derivation: Slater Determinant Representation

The antisymmetrized state can be written as a determinant. Define the \(N \times N\) matrix \(M\) with entries \(M_{ij} = \vert\alpha_j\rangle_i\) (particle \(i\) in state \(\alpha_j\)). The determinant formula gives:

\[\det M = \sum_{\pi \in S_N} \mathrm{sgn}(\pi)\;\vert\alpha_{\pi(1)}\rangle_1 \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(2)}\rangle_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes \vert\alpha_{\pi(N)}\rangle_N\]

which is exactly the unnormalized antisymmetrized state. So:

\[\begin{split}\vert\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_N\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \begin{vmatrix} \vert\alpha_1\rangle_1 & \vert\alpha_2\rangle_1 & \cdots & \vert\alpha_N\rangle_1 \\ \vert\alpha_1\rangle_2 & \vert\alpha_2\rangle_2 & \cdots & \vert\alpha_N\rangle_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \vert\alpha_1\rangle_N & \vert\alpha_2\rangle_N & \cdots & \vert\alpha_N\rangle_N \end{vmatrix}\end{split}\]

Why antisymmetry is automatic: Swapping two particles (rows) flips the sign of a determinant. Setting two states equal (columns) makes the determinant vanish—this is Pauli exclusion as a property of linear algebra.

For wavefunctions, the Slater determinant takes the familiar form:

\[\begin{split}\Psi(\boldsymbol{r}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{r}_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \begin{vmatrix} \psi_1(\boldsymbol{r}_1) & \psi_2(\boldsymbol{r}_1) & \cdots & \psi_N(\boldsymbol{r}_1) \\ \psi_1(\boldsymbol{r}_2) & \psi_2(\boldsymbol{r}_2) & \cdots & \psi_N(\boldsymbol{r}_2) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \psi_1(\boldsymbol{r}_N) & \psi_2(\boldsymbol{r}_N) & \cdots & \psi_N(\boldsymbol{r}_N) \end{vmatrix}\end{split}\]

where \(\psi_j(\boldsymbol{r}_i) = \langle\boldsymbol{r}_i\vert\alpha_j\rangle\) are single-particle wavefunctions.

Example: Two Fermions

Problem. Construct the antisymmetric state for two fermions in states \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \vert\alpha_2\rangle\).

Solution.

\[\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \bigl(\vert\alpha_1\rangle\vert\alpha_2\rangle - \vert\alpha_2\rangle\vert\alpha_1\rangle\bigr)\]

Check: Swap particle labels gives \(-\vert\alpha_1, \alpha_2\rangle_-\). Set \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle = \vert\alpha_2\rangle\) gives zero.

Pauli Exclusion Principle#

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two identical fermions can occupy the same single-particle quantum state. The fermionic occupation number satisfies \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1\}\).

(Bosons have no such restriction: \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\))

This follows directly from antisymmetry: if \(\vert\alpha_i\rangle = \vert\alpha_j\rangle\), the antisymmetrized state has two identical columns in the Slater determinant and vanishes. The consequences are far-reaching: atomic shell structure, chemical bonding, degeneracy pressure in white dwarfs and neutron stars, and the stability of matter itself.

Proof: Pauli Exclusion Principle

Start with the two-fermion antisymmetric state:

\[ \vert\alpha_1,\alpha_2\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\bigl(\vert\alpha_1\rangle\vert\alpha_2\rangle-\vert\alpha_2\rangle\vert\alpha_1\rangle\bigr). \]

If both fermions try to occupy the same single-particle state, \(\alpha_1=\alpha_2\equiv\alpha\), then

\[ \vert\alpha,\alpha\rangle_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\bigl(\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle-\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\bigr) =0. \]

So that two-fermion state does not exist: this is Pauli exclusion.

For many fermions, the same idea is unchanged. The \(N\)-fermion state is totally antisymmetric, so exchanging any two particles flips the sign. If two particles are in the same single-particle state, exchanging them does not change the ket itself, so it must satisfy

\[ \vert\Psi\rangle = -\vert\Psi\rangle, \]

hence \(\vert\Psi\rangle=0\). Therefore no two fermions can occupy the same single-particle state.

Boson and Fermion Hilbert Space Dimensions

How many independent \(N\)-particle states are there when the particles are identical? The answer follows from counting distinct occupation configurations.

Setup. Let there be \(d\) single-particle modes (orthonormal states) \(\vert\alpha_1\rangle, \ldots, \vert\alpha_d\rangle\). Distribute \(N\) identical particles among them.

Fermionic case (antisymmetric subspace):

Antisymmetry requires all occupation numbers \(n_\alpha \in \{0,1\}\): no two fermions can share a state (Pauli exclusion). A basis state is fully determined by choosing which \(N\) of the \(d\) modes are occupied — an unordered selection of \(N\) from \(d\) without repetition. The number of such choices is:

\[ \dim\mathcal{H}_{\text{antisym}}^{(N)} = \binom{d}{N} = \frac{d!}{N!(d-N)!} \]

This is the number of linearly independent Slater determinants. For \(N > d\), there are no valid configurations: \(\binom{d}{N} = 0\).

Bosonic case (symmetric subspace):

Occupation numbers \(n_\alpha \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) with \(\sum_\alpha n_\alpha = N\). A basis state is an unordered selection of \(N\) from \(d\) modes with repetition (bosons can pile into the same mode). The number of such distributions is given by the “stars and bars” formula:

\[ \dim\mathcal{H}_{\text{sym}}^{(N)} = \binom{N+d-1}{N} = \frac{(N+d-1)!}{N!\,(d-1)!} \]

Proof by stars and bars: Represent \(N\) particles as stars \(\star\) and use \(d-1\) bars \(\vert\) to divide them among \(d\) modes. For example, with \(d=3\) modes and \(N=4\) particles: \(\star\star\vert\star\vert\star\) means \((n_1,n_2,n_3)=(2,1,1)\). The total number of arrangements of \(N\) stars and \(d-1\) bars among \(N+d-1\) positions is \(\binom{N+d-1}{N}\). \(\square\)

Consistency check: For one particle (\(N=1\)), both subspaces must have dimension \(d\) (a single particle cannot be “symmetrized”):

  • Fermionic: \(\binom{d}{1} = d\) ✓

  • Bosonic: \(\binom{d}{1} = d\) ✓

Two-particle qubit example (\(d=2\), \(N=2\)):

  • Bosonic: \(\binom{3}{2}=3\) states: \(\vert\alpha_1\alpha_1\rangle\), \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\alpha_1\alpha_2\rangle+\vert\alpha_2\alpha_1\rangle)\), \(\vert\alpha_2\alpha_2\rangle\) (the spin triplet).

  • Fermionic: \(\binom{2}{2}=1\) state: \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\vert\alpha_1\alpha_2\rangle-\vert\alpha_2\alpha_1\rangle)\) (the spin singlet).

  • Check: \(3 + 1 = 4 = d^N = 2^2\) — the symmetric and antisymmetric subspaces together span the full tensor product. \(\checkmark\)

Large-\(N\) asymptotics:

  • Fermionic: \(\binom{d}{N} \leq \binom{d}{d/2} \sim 2^d/\sqrt{d}\) — bounded even as \(N\to d\).

  • Bosonic: \(\binom{N+d-1}{N} \sim N^{d-1}/(d-1)!\) for fixed \(d\), \(N\to\infty\) — grows polynomially.

These count how much of the “exponentially large” \(d^N\)-dimensional tensor product space is physically accessible for indistinguishable particles.

Insertion and Deletion Rules#

To add or remove a particle while preserving symmetry, we define insertion (\(\rhd_\pm\)) and deletion (\(\lhd_\pm\)) operations on first-quantized states. Let \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) be a single-particle state, let \(1\in\mathbb{C}\) denote the scalar unit of the zero-particle space (so \(\vert\alpha\rangle \equiv 1\otimes\vert\alpha\rangle \equiv \vert\alpha\rangle\otimes 1\)), and let \(\vert\Psi\rangle = \vert\alpha_1\rangle \otimes \vert\alpha_2\rangle \otimes \cdots\) be a generic tensor product state. The subscript \(\pm\) denotes symmetrization (+, bosons) or antisymmetrization (−, fermions).

Insertion and Deletion Rules

Insertion \(\rhd_\pm\) (adds a particle symmetrically/antisymmetrically):

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm 1 &= \vert\alpha\rangle, \\ \vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle) &= \vert\alpha\rangle \otimes \vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \rhd_\pm \vert\Psi\rangle) \end{split} \end{split}\]

Deletion \(\lhd_\pm\) (removes a particle symmetrically/antisymmetrically):

\[\begin{split} \begin{split} \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm 1 &= 0, \\ \vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm (\vert\beta\rangle \otimes \vert\Psi\rangle) &= \delta_{\alpha\beta}\,\vert\Psi\rangle \pm \vert\beta\rangle \otimes (\vert\alpha\rangle \lhd_\pm \vert\Psi\rangle) \end{split} \end{split}\]

Both operations are linear in the state being acted on.

The recursive structure means insertion places \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) at every possible position with alternating signs (for fermions) or all-positive signs (for bosons). Deletion removes \(\vert\alpha\rangle\) from every position where it appears, with the same sign pattern.

Example: Bosonic Insertion into a Two-Particle State

Start from the symmetric two-particle state

\[ \vert\beta\gamma\rangle_+ \equiv \vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle + \vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle. \]

By linearity,

\[ \vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_+\vert\beta\gamma\rangle_+ =\vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_+(\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle) +\vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_+(\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle). \]

Using the insertion rule on each term,

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_+\vert\beta\gamma\rangle_+ ={}&\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle +\vert\beta\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle +\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\\ &+\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle +\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle +\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle. \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

So the bosonic result is the sum over all 6 permutations (up to overall normalization).

Example: Fermionic Insertion into a Two-Particle State

Start from the antisymmetric two-particle state

\[ \vert\beta\gamma\rangle_- \equiv \vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle - \vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle. \]

By linearity,

\[ \vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_-\vert\beta\gamma\rangle_- =\vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_-(\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle) -\vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_-(\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle). \]

Using the insertion rule,

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \vert\alpha\rangle\rhd_-\vert\beta\gamma\rangle_- ={}&\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle -\vert\beta\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle +\vert\beta\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\\ &-\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle +\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle\vert\beta\rangle -\vert\gamma\rangle\vert\beta\rangle\vert\alpha\rangle. \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

So the fermionic result is the alternating sum over all 6 permutations, with signs given by permutation parity (up to overall normalization).

The insertion and deletion rules \(\rhd_\pm\) and \(\lhd_\pm\) are the first-quantized building blocks for adding and removing particles. When properly normalized, they yield creation (\(\hat{a}^\dagger_\alpha\)) and annihilation (\(\hat{a}_\alpha\)) operators—the central objects of second quantization, developed in §2.1.3 Second Quantization.

Summary#

  • Identical particles have no labels; the many-body state must be symmetric (bosons, \(\lambda=+1\)) or antisymmetric (fermions, \(\lambda=-1\)) under particle exchange.

  • Symmetric states (permanent): sum over all permutations with positive signs; bosons allow unlimited occupation.

  • Antisymmetric states (Slater determinant): alternating-sign sum; the Pauli exclusion principle (\(n_\alpha \in \{0,1\}\)) follows automatically.

  • Insertion/deletion rules \(\rhd_\pm\), \(\lhd_\pm\) recursively add/remove particles while preserving symmetry. When normalized, they become the creation and annihilation operators of second quantization (§2.1.3).
